    Amrapali

    Long ago, the powerful Licchavi nobles who ruled over ancient Vaishali took a remarkably keen interest in the maintenance of their state gardens.

    One day, the best of the royal gardeners was watering the plants when he saw something strange. "What's that under the mango tree?" he wondered. "Why, it's a lady! What an extra-ordinary beauty!"

    He went up to her. "Who are you, fair lady? What is your name? Where have you come from?"

    "I don't have a name. I come from nowhere and have nowhere to go," the mysterious maiden replied softly.

    "Are you going to live in this garden?" the gardener asked.

    "Yes, if you do not mind."

    "I certainly don't mind. But this garden belongs to the nobles. They should be informed," he said.

    Leaving her in the garden, he rushed to the nobles. "A strange lady has appeared in the state garden! In all Vaishali there is no one as beautiful as she is!"

    Intrigued, the nobles rushed out to meet her. When they reached the garden and saw her, they were absolutely mesmerised. "The gardener knew what he was talking about! She is the most enchanting lady I have ever seen!"

    But almost immediately, a fierce, ugly fight broke out among the proud nobles. "Amrapali shall be mine!" one yelled, naming her after the mango tree (Amra) where she was found.

    "No! You'll have to take her from me first!" another shouted, drawing his sword.

    Suddenly, the maiden stepped forward. "Stop!" she commanded. "Do I have no choice in the matter? Can I not have my say?"

    The nobles immediately felt ashamed. "We have been selfish, thoughtless! You choose one among us, Amrapali. The one you choose, shall marry you."

    Amrapali looked at them. "If I choose one of you, I will no doubt be making him very happy. But I will be making all the others incredibly jealous and angry. So I will not marry any of you."

    The elders of Vaishali, who had hurried to the garden on hearing the clash, completely supported her. "A wise decision, indeed! Amrapali cannot belong to one person. She shall belong to all of Vaishali."

    "And we could have a grand house built here for her," they agreed.

    As soon as the magnificent house was ready, they presented it to her. "I shall ever remain indebted to the people of Vaishali for the love they have shown me," Amrapali smiled.

    To entertain the nobles of Vaishali, Amrapali, who was an excellent, natural dancer, began to give public dance performances. "Such perfect rhythm! She is matchless!" the nobles cheered. "No other kingdom can boast of such an accomplished dancer!"

    But her peaceful life was soon interrupted. One evening, when the nobles were watching her perform, a messenger rushed in in a total panic.

    "Master! I have bad news! Vaishali is in terrible danger!" the messenger gasped. "The Magadhan army is heading towards Vaishali!"

    "What! That monster King Bimbisara again!" the nobles roared, immediately leaving for war.

    As Amrapali watched the young men of Vaishali ride to the battlefield, her heart sank. "Why do men fight? What do they gain by killing one another?" she wept. "One moment they appreciate beauty and art; the next, they thirst for blood."

    A few days later, while the war raged outside the city, a handsome stranger called on her. "I have come a long way to see you dance. Will you dance for me, fair one?"

    Amrapali danced for him, and the stranger was absolutely captivated. "No wonder the Licchavis are proud of her. We don't have a dancer to equal her in all Magadha," he thought.

    "Where do you stay in Vaishali?" Amrapali asked when the dance was over.

    "I am a stranger here. I have not yet found a place to stay," he replied.

    "Then you are welcome to stay at my house."

    The stranger stayed with her for a week, and they fell deeply in love. But then, he sighed. "Amrapali, I must take your leave. I have to go back."

    "I love you. Why don't you come away with me?" the stranger pleaded.

    Amrapali laughed lightly. "You amuse me. You are a total stranger. I don't even know your name!"

    The man looked deeply into her eyes. "I am Bimbisara."

    "What! The dreaded King of Magadha!" Amrapali gasped, backing away in horror. "There is no room for love in the hearts of ambitious men! How can you talk of love, when this very moment because of you hundreds of men are dying on the battlefield?"

    "If I give up my ambition to conquer Vaishali and cease hostilities forthwith, will you marry me?" Bimbisara pleaded desperately.

    "No! I cannot!" Amrapali wept. "I belong to Vaishali! I have given my pledge."

    Without another word, the utterly dejected Bimbisara turned and left. Amrapali watched him go, completely heartbroken. "Why do I feel sad to see the dreaded enemy of Vaishali go?" she sobbed.

    A few days later, the nobles of Vaishali returned, cheering loudly. "That coward, Bimbisara, called off the battle and retreated!"

    Why did he do it? Was it for love of me? Amrapali wondered silently.

    "Amrapali, we are back. We would like to see you complete the dance you began when we left," the nobles requested.

    But as Amrapali danced, her movements were entirely lifeless. Her performance was a total failure. It was evident to everyone that her heart was simply not in it.

    Meanwhile, Amrapali sat brooding for weeks. "What is the matter with me? Why did I find no joy in dancing for the nobles today?"

    The next morning, as she walked in her garden, she saw a young boy carrying a single, exquisitely beautiful pink lotus. "What a beautiful lotus! How fresh it is! Will you sell that lotus to me, my lad?" she asked.

    "Pardon me, lady. This lotus is not for sale," the boy bowed. "Haven't you heard? Lord Buddha, the Enlightened One who knows the cause of suffering, has come to Vaishali and is camping at Kotigrama. I am taking this flower to him."

    "Lord Buddha! I will go to him," Amrapali decided instantly.

    She travelled to Kotigrama and sat at the very back of the massive crowd, listening to Lord Buddha with rapt attention.

    "Out of desire is born grief," Lord Buddha preached gently. "Out of desire alone is born fear. Free yourself from desire and the grief and the fear will wither away."

    His words fill me with peace! His voice is so soft! His words so soothing! Amrapali thought, tears of relief streaming down her face.

    She began to experience a totally new kind of joy. She went to Kotigrama daily to hear him speak. His very presence stills my restless mind. How I wish he would visit my home and fill it with his being! she wished desperately. But will the Lord visit the house of an unworthy person like me?

    Soon it was time for Lord Buddha to leave Vaishali. "When will I see you again, Master?" Amrapali wept as he departed.

    Even after Lord Buddha had left, Amrapali continued to visit Kotigrama every single evening, sitting under the tree where he used to preach. "This is where the Master used to sit. I can still feel his presence here," she smiled peacefully.

    Many months passed. And Amrapali patiently waited for the return of Lord Buddha. "My Master will come to Vaishali again. Perhaps today, perhaps tomorrow, but he will come."

    One evening, she heard joyous news. "My Master is back! I can't believe my eyes!" she wept, running up to Lord Buddha and falling at his feet.

    "Arise, my child," Lord Buddha smiled warmly.

    "Master, will you honour me by eating at my house tomorrow?" Amrapali begged.

    "I will come, my child," Lord Buddha agreed.

    Amrapali sped homewards to Vaishali in her chariot to prepare for the coming of her Master. Suddenly, a few chariots carrying the nobles of Vaishali came charging from the opposite direction.

    "Where are you speeding to?" Amrapali called out.

    "We heard that Lord Buddha has arrived. We are off to invite him to eat with us at the palace tomorrow," the nobles replied proudly.

    "But the Lord has already agreed to dine at my house tomorrow," Amrapali smiled.

    The nobles were furious. "We have been forestalled! But not all is lost. Not yet. She can easily be bought off," they plotted.

    They turned their chariots around. "Amrapali, if you let Lord Buddha have his first meal at Vaishali with us, we will give you all the gold you want."

    "No. Even if you offer me the entire kingdom of Vaishali, I won't give up the honour of serving the Lord!" Amrapali refused firmly.

    "We will go to the Lord. He cannot refuse the nobles of Vaishali," they scoffed, driving away.

    Later, at Kotigrama, the nobles bowed before the Buddha. "Lord, please honour us by dining with us tomorrow."

    "I am sorry. I can't," Lord Buddha replied calmly. "I have already agreed to go to Amrapali's house tomorrow."

    The next day, Lord Buddha went to Amrapali's house.

    "I have come, Amrapali," Lord Buddha smiled.

    "Lord, you do me great honour," Amrapali wept, washing his feet.

    Later, as she served the monks a lavish meal, she felt a profound sense of peace. "Lord, why do I feel such immense joy as I serve you?"

    "Amrapali, you have begun to know the true joy of giving," Lord Buddha replied.

    After the meal, Amrapali bowed deeply before the Enlightened One. "Lord, permit me to grant my garden and my massive house to the Sangha (the monastic order)."

    "So be it, child. It could be used as a monastery," Lord Buddha accepted her noble gift.

    Then, happily leaving behind her worldly life, her immense wealth, and all its heartaches, Amrapali began to lead a simple, peaceful life of profound renunciation, finding at last the deep peace of mind she had always craved.
