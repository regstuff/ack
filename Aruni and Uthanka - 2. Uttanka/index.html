    Uttanka

    When Sage Gautama worshipped Agni, the god of fire, he always chose his devoted disciple Uttanka to assist him.

    "O Agni, I worship you for the sake of my Guru, who is my only god," Uttanka would pray.

    "My dear Uttanka," Gautama told him one day, "I know what you are thinking. May the divine Agni come to your aid when you need him most."

    The years passed. One day, Gautama called his disciples to him. "You have acquired mastery of the Vedas. You may now go and set up ashrams of your own." One by one, they came to Gautama to be blessed before departing.

    Uttanka watched them leave. "Ah! Now it is my turn," he thought. "What dakshina should I offer my Guru? I'll leave it to him to decide."

    But over the years, Gautama had become so dependent on Uttanka that even though hundreds of disciples came, finished their studies, and left, Uttanka had to stay on. "No, not you, Uttanka," the sage would say. "I cannot permit you to go. Not yet. I need you here."

    "Will I ever set up an ashram of my own?" Uttanka wondered sadly. "But how can I go when my Guru needs me?"

    So Uttanka, serving Gautama and his family, grew old without either his Guru or himself realizing it.

    One day, returning with a heavy pot of water, he thought, "I am very hungry. After I've set down this water, I'll have something to eat." But as soon as he reached the hut, Gautama's wife called out to him.

    "Ah! You're back, Uttanka! Please fetch me some firewood. There is none left."

    "Food can wait. Her orders must be obeyed first," Uttanka told himself, turning back toward the forest.

    "When will I be free to set up my own ashram and retire to a life of meditation and peace?" he wondered. Out of sheer fatigue, he threw the wood on the ground instead of putting it down gently.

    As he returned to the hermitage an hour later, containing his hunger, he noticed something strange. "Ah! My back aches. It never did before. Is my devotion to my master weakening? This load feels heavier than usual."

    He caught his reflection and gasped. "What's that? A lock of silver hair! It is my own! I have grown old! Decrepit!"

    While Gautama patiently listened, Uttanka poured out his woes. "And I have not yet tasted worldly happiness. Though I have lovingly served you for many years, you have not yet permitted me to go."

    "What's the matter, my son? Please calm down and speak out," Gautama said gently.

    "Old age has overtaken me without my being aware of it," Uttanka replied.

    "My son, I kept you here because of my love for you. But if you wish to go, you have my permission and my blessings," Gautama assured him. "And... if, with the strength of your penances, you become a young man, I shall give my daughter to you in marriage."

    And lo! The very next moment, Uttanka regained his lost youth. True to his word, Gautama placed his daughter's hand in Uttanka's and solemnized the marriage.

    After the marriage, Uttanka approached his Guru. "What guru dakshina should I give you before I go away with my wife?"

    "Your excellent conduct as my disciple has been fee enough. I need no other," Gautama smiled.

    "If it pleases you, you may do so," the sage added.

    "Then, with your permission, may I bring something for mother?" Uttanka asked.

    Uttanka went to the sage's wife. "What shall I bring for you, mother?"

    "If you must, then bring me the celestial earrings worn by the wife of King Saudasa," she replied.

    When Gautama heard of this later, he was alarmed. "Oh! It was not wise to demand such a fee. Saudasa has been cursed by Vasishtha and is now a cannibal. He is sure to kill Uttanka."

    "Oh, I didn't know that!" his wife cried in dismay. "But, my Lord, surely he can be protected from coming to any harm by your grace?"

    "And my prayers! He can and he will," Gautama promised.

    Meanwhile, Uttanka trudged on till he came to a remote forest. He spotted a fierce, wild-looking man. "That must be Saudasa!"

    When he saw Uttanka, Saudasa got up. "Ah! I am lucky. You have come just in time, for it's the sixth hour of the day and I'm hungry."

    "Wait, O King!" Uttanka said. "I have come on an errand for my Guru. It's against dharma to injure anyone who is on such a mission."

    "But this is the only hour when I am permitted to eat," Saudasa reasoned. "Besides, I am hungry. So I will not let you go."

    "Then let us make a pact. After I have completed my errand, I will offer myself to you."

    "All right. What is the errand?" Saudasa asked.

    "I have come to beg from you the earrings worn by your wife!"

    Saudasa was taken aback. "The celestial earrings! But they belong to my wife. Please ask for something else. Anything. I'll give it to you."

    "It is the earrings I'll have and nothing else," Uttanka insisted. "If our pact means anything to you, if you are one who keeps his word..."

    Saudasa sighed. "All right! Go to my queen and tell her that I command her to give the earrings to you."

    Uttanka went to the queen with Saudasa's message.

    "If it is the command of my husband, and for his good, I will obey. For no other reason would I part with these earrings," she said, handing them over.

    "In what way are these earrings different?" Uttanka asked.

    "These earrings free the wearer from hunger and thirst and fear of any kind," the queen explained. "They protect the one they adorn from poison, fire or any other danger. But beware—the Nagas are waiting for an opportunity to steal them. So take care of them. Hide them in the folds of your deerskin."

    Uttanka returned to Saudasa. "I have got the earrings. I will give them to my Guru's wife and then return to you, as I promised."

    "There is, however, one question I'd like to ask you before I go," Uttanka added. "What is it, my friend?"

    "The learned say that one who does not take the advice of a friend is a fool. And one who wrongs a friend is a villain. And you, O King, have become my friend today."

    "That is true," Saudasa agreed.

    "Would you, a friend, advise me to return to you, a cannibal?" Uttanka asked slyly.

    Saudasa knew he was trapped. "Well, as a friend I would say you'd be a fool to come back!"

    "That's all I wanted to hear, my friend. Good day!" Before Saudasa could change his mind, Uttanka left him and hurried back to the hermitage.

    After he had gone some distance, Uttanka felt weary. "I feel weak from hunger. I must find something to eat." As he looked around, his eyes fell on a bilva tree. "Ah! Ripe bilva fruit! But what shall I do with the earrings? If I leave them on the ground, a Naga might slink away with them. I'll put the deerskin on this branch. The earrings should be safe here."

    As he climbed the bilva tree, some of the fruit fell on the deerskin. The branch broke, and the deerskin fell to the ground with a heavy thud. Uttanka looked down in horror. "My deerskin! It's fallen off the branch!"

    He scrambled down. "The earrings! Ah! The celestial earrings! I must be quick." But it was too late. A snake slithered out of the grass. "It's a Naga! He's got the earrings!"

    Uttanka chased the Naga, but before he could catch up with it, it had slid safely into an anthill.

    A furious Uttanka began to poke at the anthill with his staff. "I'll retrieve those earrings somehow!" Burning with anger and a desire for revenge, Uttanka dug the earth with his staff for thirty-five days.

    On the thirty-sixth day, an old man stood before him. "What are you doing?"

    "I am digging my way to the nether regions to retrieve the earrings a Naga stole from me," Uttanka panted.

    "It's a vain pursuit, my son. Give it up," the old man advised. "The region of the Nagas is thousands of yojanas away from here. You can't reach it by digging with your staff."

    "If I can't recover the earrings, I shall give up my life," Uttanka declared.

    The next moment, the old man transformed into a glorious figure on a chariot. "Since you are steadfast in your resolve, I shall aid you. Hold up your staff," said Lord Indra.

    "There! Now your staff is charged with the force of thunder. You shall soon achieve your purpose," Indra said, and vanished.

    With a few strokes of his empowered staff, Uttanka rent the earth and, following the path it yielded, soon reached the subterranean region of the Nagas.

    "How shall I get through the gates? And when I do, where shall I look for the Naga who stole the earrings?" Uttanka wondered.

    Suddenly, a blazing steed stood before him. To his amazement, the creature spoke to him. "Blow hard at me!"

    Overcome by awe, Uttanka did not move. "Do not hesitate, my son," the horse urged. "You have often done it at your Guru's hermitage."

    "At the hermitage? I don't understand. Who are you?"

    "I am the fiery Agni. You have often worshipped me with your Guru. I have come to help you. Do as I say without delay."

    Uttanka did as he was told and blew hard. Suddenly the steed blazed out in flames. Thick clouds of black smoke issued from its body, rose high over the wall, and poured into the palaces of the Nagas.

    "Aa-ah! I am blinded!" the Nagas cried out in the darkness. "I can't see anything!"

    With streaming eyes and scorched bodies, the Nagas slowly made their way through the suffocating smoke to the gates of the palace. Vasuki, their king, gave an order: "Throw open the gates!"

    When they saw Uttanka standing there, the Nagas, led by Vasuki, fell at his feet and worshipped him.

    "O Holy One, why have you caused this affliction to befall us?" Vasuki pleaded.

    "One of you has stolen the earrings I was taking to my Guru's wife," Uttanka said sternly.

    The next moment, a Naga bowed before Uttanka. "Forgive me, O Holy One. I am the culprit." He then brought out the earrings and returned them. "Take the earrings, but please rid us of this scourge."

    Uttanka took the earrings and turned to Agni. "O Agni, let them know peace once more."

    As soon as the smoke dispersed, Uttanka took his leave of the Nagas and safely returned to the ashram of his Guru with his prize.
