    Lokamanya Tilak

    On July 23, 1856, Gangadhar Pant Tilak, the headmaster of the local Marathi school in Ratnagiri, was pacing up and down the veranda of his house. His wife was about to have a baby. He had three daughters, but like other parents of his time, he longed for a son.

    "It's a girl!" his mother teased him. "Poor Dada! I will tell him the truth."

    "Aunty's teasing you, Dada! It's a boy!" his daughter corrected.

    Whoever he looked like, the child, Bal Gangadhar Tilak, was destined to become great. Bal had his early education at home, under the strict supervision of his father.

    "Dada, this sum is too simple. Give me a more difficult one," young Bal would say.

    When Bal was ten years old, his father was transferred to Pune. "This is a godsend! Bal can now go to the best of schools," his father rejoiced.

    Shortly after they came to Pune, Bal's mother died. It was his aunt who now looked after him.

    Bal was no doubt intelligent. But he was, to his teacher's annoyance, equally stubborn. One day at school, the teacher entered and took one look at the class. The floor was littered with peanut shells.

    "What's going on here? You ought to be ashamed of yourselves! All right. This time I'll let you off. Now clean up the mess, and get back to your work," the teacher ordered.

    As the boys bent down to pick up the shells, the teacher's eye fell on Bal. "Hey, you! Do you think you are a prince? Come on, get to work."

    "I'm sorry, sir, but I won't," Bal refused firmly. "I didn't eat in class. I didn't make the mess. So why should I take the punishment?"

    "You impudent, obstinate child! I'll teach you to talk back to your teacher!" the teacher shouted, bringing down his cane. But Bal would not submit to unfair treatment.

    He joined another school. But though he was an excellent student he did not do well in examinations. "Why do you always pick the most difficult questions, Bal? If you answered the easy ones, you'd get full marks!" a friend asked.

    "It's not marks that I'm after. It's knowledge. So there's no point in attempting what I already know," Bal replied.

    When Bal was fifteen, he was married to a ten-year-old girl named Satyabhama, as child marriage was common in those days. That same year, Bal's father died. "Bal, you must get over your grief and concentrate on your studies," his uncle advised.

    And get over it he did. He was seventeen when he joined the Deccan College. On the first day as the boys assembled at the mess for lunch, a boy named Daji Abaji Khare commented on Tilak's traditional dress.

    "Well, well, Tilak! How orthodox can we get! A silk dhoti—it reminds me of my father and grandfather!" Khare laughed.

    "What's wrong with it? It's our traditional dress! Don't the Europeans have their formal dress for dinners?" Tilak retorted.

    After the meal, Khare approached him. "You are out-spoken, aren't you, Tilak? I have much to learn from you. And I, from you, Khare." And thus began the lifelong friendship of Tilak and Khare.

    "I want to improve my physique, Khare," Tilak said later. "Come to the gym with me, then. I'll make you a pehlwan (wrestler)," Khare promised.

    Of all sports, swimming was Bal's favourite. One day he amazed Khare by floating on his back for hours with a loaf of bread held aloft in one hand. Tilak's diet and health no doubt improved, but when the First Year Arts results were announced, he failed.

    "I've failed. Hm...m...m... I have achieved one goal—a robust physique. But... at the cost of my other goal. I'll have to strike a balance between books and sports," he realized.

    He worked hard and graduated in Arts. Soon after, Tilak took up the study of Law. Around that time he struck up a friendship with Gopal Ganesh Agarkar, an M.A. student and a budding champion of social reforms.

    "Without educating the people, we cannot hope to introduce social reforms," Tilak argued. "I don't see what impact social reforms imposed by an alien government can have on the people?"

    "We must have political freedom first!" Tilak insisted.

    The idea was soon put into shape. With the help of Vishnushastri Chiplunkar, the two friends founded a school: The New English School.

    Tilak's words set Chiplunkar thinking. "I've found a way to reach the adults! Why don't we start a weekly?"

    "Why one, let's have two! One in English and the other in Marathi!"

    And so the two weeklies—Kesari in Marathi and The Mahratta in English—were born.

    "We are lucky to catch them young. And if we don't succeed in inculcating self-respect and love for the country in them... we will succeed! If only we could reach the adults too!"

    In the papers, Tilak exposed the Diwan of Kolhapur for plotting against the adopted prince. The Diwan sued the editors, and Tilak and Agarkar were jailed for sedition.

    The views of the people of Pune were openly expressed in the rousing welcome given to Tilak and Agarkar on their release from jail. "Anyway, Tilak and Agarkar don't deserve to be imprisoned for publishing what they believed to be a genuine document," the people said.

    Soon after their release, the friends formed the Deccan Education Society and the Fergusson College was started.

    But Tilak soon found himself at odds with some of his colleagues. "Did you see the morning papers? Ranade and Gokhale, I can understand. But Tilak! I never dreamt that Tilak would have tea from a Christian kitchen!" the orthodox people complained when Tilak was tricked by a friend into attending a mission meeting.

    "We must expel them from the community unless they are prepared to perform the purification ceremony," they demanded. Tilak agreed to perform the ceremony to maintain peace, but Agarkar and others refused.

    "We must lead the people, not blindly follow their superstitions," Agarkar argued. The differences grew, and eventually, Tilak left the Deccan Education Society to dedicate himself entirely to the Kesari and Mahratta.

    To achieve mass awakening, he set the tradition of publicly celebrating the Ganesha festival. Later he celebrated the birth anniversary of Shivaji at Raigad.

    Around that time, the government raised the excise duty on Indian cloth, to help the cloth from Britain to sell better.

    "For Swadeshi (indigenous) cloth, it's too expensive! Why should we buy it when imported cloth is available for the same price?" the people complained.

    Tilak, however, did not sit back helpless. "If we don't buy Swadeshi cloth, who will? How will our people, our trade survive? If you love your country and your people, don't buy British cloth! Boycott it!"

    Once when famine broke out in Maharashtra, farmers started leaving their villages. "I must reach them! I must instil courage into them! They must know what their rights in their homeland are!"

    "The crops have failed and you have neither food nor money. Are you going to lose the roof above your head too? You have a right to live! Can't you fight for your rights even in the grip of death?" Tilak urged them to demand their rights.

    Tilak did not believe in sending petitions to the government as some of his colleagues in the Indian National Congress, the Moderates, did. His methods were different.

    While Tilak was busy with the struggle to secure famine relief for the farmers, the plague struck Pune. The government was at first indifferent and took no measures to arrest the spread of the dreaded disease.

    Finally when it did act, it did so with a vengeance. One Charles Rand was appointed Plague Commissioner.

    "I want every house in Pune disinfected. Put anyone who is ill in hospital and take the relatives to the camp outside the town—by force if necessary. That's all. You may go," Rand ordered his soldiers.

    The overzealous soldiers did not respect the feelings of the citizens. They handled even babies in their cradles without any mercy.

    Tilak raised his voice against the inhuman treatment. "The plague is more merciful to us than its human prototype now reigning in the city..." he wrote in Mahratta. He set up his own segregation camp for the terrified victims and a free kitchen for the poor.

    Normalcy slowly returned but the wounds inflicted by the thoughtless soldiers did not heal. An angry man, Damodar Hari Chaphekar, shot at Rand.

    There was a massive hunt for the unknown culprit. When Rand died of the bullet wound, the government swung into action. "Tilak has been arrested."

    "The government has lost its senses," the people whispered.

    Tilak was held on a charge of sedition. "Does praising Shivaji Maharaj amount to sedition? Tilak has taught us to fight for our rights. Is that sedition?"

    As expected the court found Tilak guilty. The sentence was eighteen months' rigorous imprisonment. When the news reached his wife, she fell seriously ill.

    Tilak too fell ill in prison. He lost 21 pounds! However, a Muslim warder tried to be of help to him. "Dadasaheb, I know you don't like food cooked with onion and garlic. Have some betel nut. It might help."

    "You are kind but you are breaking prison rules. You shouldn't," Tilak replied.

    Tilak spent his time in jail reading and writing. While reading the translation of the Rig Veda, a thought struck him. "Many days passed before the sun rose... How could this be true... unless... unless the Aryans had once lived in the Arctic! Yes! That's it!" It was this idea that later led him to write the book—The Arctic Home in the Vedas.

    At last on September 6, 1898, Tilak was released. The temple bells of Pune rang out echoing the joy of the people.

    Five years later, the plague broke out again in Pune. This time it claimed Tilak's eldest son, Vishwanath. Bearing his grief with stoicism, Tilak worked with extra zeal.

    Then in July 1905, Lord Curzon announced the partition of Bengal. The partition came into force on October 16, 1905.

    "Their aim is to disunite the Bengali Muslims and Hindus," Tilak warned. "But they will not succeed."

    The partition only gave further impetus to the Swadeshi movement. Students made a huge bonfire of foreign clothes. Tilak started a glass factory at Talegaon and a co-operative store for the sale of Swadeshi goods in Bombay.

    Since Swaraj was the goal of Tilak and his friends, like Lala Lajpatrai of Punjab and Aurobindo Ghose of Bengal, they came to be known as Nationalists.

    The government tried in vain to curb the Nationalists. Lala Lajpatrai was deported to Mandalay in Burma.

    When Lajpatrai was released in November 1907, the Nationalists wanted him as President of the Surat Congress. The Moderates, however, wanted Dr. Rashbehari Ghosh.

    At the Congress session in Surat, Tilak was not allowed to speak before Dr. Ghosh's election. "I've a right to address the delegates. You can't stop me!" Tilak declared. The Congress split into two groups.

    Around this time, revolutionaries who believed in violence became active. On April 30, 1908 the first bomb was thrown at the British residents of Muzaffarpur. The explosion echoed through the country. The government swung into action and several journalists were jailed.

    A few days later, Tilak was arrested. The charge was sedition, the offence, an article he had written.

    Tilak contended that while he deplored the use of bombs the only way to prevent such occurrences was to reform the administration. He made a fervent appeal to the jury.

    When the jury found him guilty, Tilak proclaimed, "I maintain that I am innocent... It may be the will of Providence that the cause which I represent may prosper more by my sufferings than by my remaining free."

    Tilak was sentenced to transportation for six years. In Bombay a six-day strike was observed and all the textile mills were closed. The police fired indiscriminately at the demonstrators.

    Tilak was deported to Mandalay. Tilak spent five years and eight months in Mandalay. His sole companions were books and birds.

    Tilak's prolonged detention totally unnerved his wife and she died when he was in Mandalay. "I'm sorry. It must be a great loss to you," the jailor comforted him.

    "One of us had to face the other's death. I am glad it is I. I only wish I could have been with her when the end came," Tilak replied stoically.

    He began to write a treatise on the Gita—The Gita Rahasya.

    On June 17, 1914 at 2:00 A.M. there was a knock at Gaikwad Wada, Tilak's residence. The whole household woke up. Tilak was back!

    The news spread. Friends came running to his house. "Lala Lajpatrai, Bipin Chandra Pal and Aurobindo have gone into self-imposed exile. The press is gagged. National schools are closed," they told him.

    "Somebody has to do the work I am doing. If no one does it, the British will rule as they like," Tilak declared.

    Tilak took up the cause of Home Rule, the people's right to form their own government. He toured all over India and spoke to the people. "Home Rule is the only remedy. Insist on your rights. India is your own home, isn't it? Then why not manage it yourselves?"

    Tilak by then had come to be revered as 'Lokamanya' (Respected by the people) by people all over India. When he went to Lucknow to attend the Congress session, following the unification of the Moderates and Nationalists, he was taken out in procession.

    At the Lucknow Congress, he made his famous declaration: "Swaraj is my birthright and I shall have it."

    On August 1, 1920, exactly eight months after his return to India from a visit to England to present India's case for Home Rule, Tilak died. But the cause for which he fought was taken up by millions of his countrymen.

    "Britishers, Quit India!"

    And Tilak's lifelong struggle at last bore fruit on August 15, 1947, when India became independent.
