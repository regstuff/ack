    How Friends Are Parted

    There was once a rich merchant who was travelling alone through a deep forest in a cart loaded with goods for sale. "If all goes well and I sell these goods, I will make a lot of money," he thought happily.

    But all did not go well. His heavy cart hit a rut, and one of his two bulls stumbled and fell with a painful cry.

    "Oh, no!" the merchant groaned. He examined the poor animal. "His foreleg is broken. I'll have to leave him here." Unable to wait, the merchant abandoned the injured bull in the middle of the dense jungle.

    I am all alone in this forest, the bull thought sadly. Well, if I'm fated to live, I will; if not, I'll die.

    Fortunately, the forest was full of tender, lush grass. As the days passed, the bull fed on the grass, his leg healed, and he grew incredibly strong and healthy. Ah! I am happier here than I ever was with my master, he realized, roaming freely.

    In the same forest lived two cunning jackals named Damanaka and Karataka. They had recently fallen from the favor of King Lion and were hungry.

    "We have fallen from King Lion's favour. No more feasts for us!" Karataka complained.

    "Mark my words," Damanaka schemed, watching the Lion pass by. "By our efforts, we will win back the confidence of our King."

    Nearby, the Lion approached a pool of water for a grand feast. Just as he bent down to drink, a terrifying, thunderous sound echoed through the trees. MO-O-W-M-O-O!

    It was the bull bellowing with all his might, though the Lion did not know what it was. Terrified that a terrible beast had entered his forest, the Lion ran back to his cave to hide.

    Damanaka sprang to his feet. "Here is our chance!"

    He confidently entered the Lion's den. "A servant should serve his master. That is why I have come," Damanaka said smoothly.

    "Hush! So you saw me," the Lion admitted, embarrassed. "Don't tell anyone yet. But we may have to leave this forest. Didn't you hear the thundering roar of a mighty beast?"

    "My brother and I can find a way to help you," Damanaka promised. The Lion happily fed the jackals a royal feast in return for their help.

    Damanaka and Karataka went to find the source of the noise. "I do know what it was," Damanaka laughed to his brother. "It was the bellowing of a bull! Ha! Ha! A bull, my brother, whom even you and I could kill!"

    "Then why didn't you tell him so and forgo that fine feast?" Karataka asked.

    "Because now we can tell him, and earn another feast!" Damanaka sneered.

    They found the bull grazing peacefully. "O bull, I am the keeper of this forest," Damanaka lied. "Don't you know you can't come here without permission? Ask Karataka, the King's general, who commands you to appear before him."

    The innocent bull, terrified by the thought of a King Lion, fell at Karataka's feet. "O mighty general, what would you have me do?"

    "You must go and fall at the imperial feet of King Lion," Karataka ordered.

    Damanaka ran ahead to the Lion's den. "Your majesty! I have seen him. He is as dangerous as your majesty feared. But after much persuasion, he is willing to be your friend."

    When the bull arrived, the Lion graciously welcomed him. "Come, come, my good friend! We are equals," the Lion smiled. The bull was pleasantly surprised and lived in the forest as the honored guest of his majesty.

    One day, the Lion went out to hunt, leaving the bull in charge. When he returned with a fresh kill, the jackals approached for their share.

    "No, Damanaka," the Lion said. "You will have to receive your provisions from the bull. He is my treasurer now."

    Days rolled by. The Lion and the bull became inseparable friends, constantly seen together.

    "The bull has become too powerful," Karataka growled.

    "So what? There is nothing that cunning cannot accomplish," Damanaka replied maliciously. "We must turn the King and the bull against each other."

    Damanaka went to the Lion alone. "Your majesty, I have something very unpleasant to tell you. My lord, the bull is a traitor. He plans to seize your kingdom."

    "But why should he? He enjoys more power than any other minister," the Lion said in disbelief.

    "That is why! He now wants to be more powerful than even you," Damanaka lied smoothly. "You will have proof soon. He plans to come here with his horns lowered, as bulls do when they attack. Will you believe me then?"

    Immediately, Damanaka ran to the bull, putting on a tragic face. "Ah! There's nothing but misery for those who serve wicked masters. I should not betray my King... but I had promised you protection. His majesty's heart is turned against you. He has made secret plans to kill you and let us feast on your flesh."

    "But why? What have I done to displease him?" the bull cried.

    "Don't waste time. Think of what you can do to save your life," Damanaka urged. "Go now to him. When he glares at you with his tail stiff, his mouth agape, and his paw raised, you'll see for yourself."

    Damanaka ran ahead to the Lion and waited. Soon, the bull arrived near the den. Remembering Damanaka's warning, the Lion lowered himself, glaring fiercely with a raised paw, ready to defend himself.

    Seeing the Lion's terrifying, aggressive stance, the bull believed Damanaka's lie. To defend his own life, he lowered his horns and charged.

    "He lowered his horns and charged!" the Lion roared, leaping forward.

    A brutal fight ensued, but the mighty Lion quickly overpowered the bull and killed him. As the Lion stood staring at the carcass of his innocent friend, a weird sound caught his ears.

    It was the gleeful howling of Damanaka and Karataka. "What's that I hear?" the Lion realized in horror. "The gleeful howl of the jackals! Ah, I have let the cunning jackals outwit me and part me from a true friend!"
