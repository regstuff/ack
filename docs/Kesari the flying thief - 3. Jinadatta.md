# Jinadatta

Jinadatta, a rich but generous merchant of Pottanapura, had fallen on bad days. His business was failing. 

"Why don't you go to my father and ask him for a loan?" his wife, Purna, suggested. 

"No! No, Purna. I can't take money from your father," Jinadatta refused, his pride hurt. 

"Why not? It's only a loan. You will repay it when business improves. He won't refuse. I know my father," she insisted. 

"What if he refuses?" Jinadatta worried, but finally agreed. "If you are so confident... well... all right... I'll go." 

"You must go today! I'll get some food ready for the journey," Purna smiled, going into the kitchen. She came out with a small packet. "Here you are! Just enough food to last you till you reach father's house." 

Jinadatta set out. Towards noon he sat under a tree by a tank and opened his lunch packet. "Roasted gram powder! Good Purna does pamper me! She's probably given me all that was left in the house," he thought affectionately. 

Just then, a monk approached. "A monk! How lean and feeble he looks. He must have been fasting." 

"Holy sir, please honour me by accepting a portion of my meal," Jinadatta offered. The monk accepted, and the two shared the meagre meal. 

When they finished, the monk went his way and Jinadatta continued his journey. 

When he reached his father-in-law's house, the reception was lukewarm. "Why it's Jinadatta! Come in, come in," his father-in-law said, eyeing his clothes. "But you seem to have fallen on bad days. These shabby clothes... How thin you have become! Have you been ill?" 

"Not ill, but... I have had a spate of bad luck in business. We have lost everything but the house. I have come to you for a loan, to start afresh," Jinadatta explained. 

"Oh! I see! But... Well, Jinadatta. I've just invested all my spare money. I am so-o-o sorry but... I hate to refuse you but... at the moment... I will not be able to help you, I'm afraid." 

Shocked by the blunt refusal, Jinadatta walked away in a daze. "He won't refuse, I know my father," Purna had said. "Poor Purna! If I tell her the truth it will break her heart. She was so sure that her father would help us." 

He sat down by a river, feeling utterly defeated. He noticed a bundle his father-in-law had given him as a parting gift. "What's that gleaming on the ground?" 

He opened it. "Pebbles! Oh! They are just pebbles," he sighed. "This should save her from the first shock. Later, when she finds out that this bundle contains only pebbles... I can always say that there must have been a mistake somewhere." 

When Jinadatta reached home, Purna greeted him joyfully. "Oh! What a big bundle! I can see that father has been quite generous." 

"Purna, I'm going out for a while after I've washed and changed," Jinadatta said quickly, trying to avoid the moment of truth. 

When Jinadatta, who had gone out to pacify his creditors, returned, he found a feast waiting for him. "Wh-what's all this?" he asked in astonishment. 

"Your favourite dishes!" Purna smiled. 

"You know we already owe the grocer a lot of money. Why did you borrow..." 

"Borrow? Why should I borrow?" Purna laughed. "One precious stone was enough to pay..." 

"Which... what precious stone?" Jinadatta stammered. 

"From the bundle father gave us." 

"Where is the bundle?" Jinadatta asked, rushing into the room. "But... but... I don't believe it!" 

The bundle was filled with gleaming, priceless gems! "Your father didn't give me anything!" he tried to explain, but Purna wouldn't believe it. 

Yes, Jinadatta's bad days were certainly over. Now whether it was his generosity to the monk or his kind heart or Purna's faith in her father's goodness that turned mere pebbles into precious stones, is not known. But Jinadatta never again sat to a meal without first offering some of it to a mendicant.
