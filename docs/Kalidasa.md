# Kalidasa

Kalidasa, one of the greatest poets the world has ever known, lost his parents when he was barely five years old. He was brought up by a kind-hearted but unlettered cowherd. Kalidasa grew into a handsome youth, but he was an uneducated rustic. He spent his free time at the village temple dedicated to Kali, earning him the name 'Kalidasa'. 

Now it so happened that the King of Varanasi had a beautiful and talented daughter, Princess Vasanti. She was very proud of her knowledge. The King was worried about finding a husband equal to her in accomplishment. He suggested Vararuchi, a great scholar. 

But Vasanti challenged Vararuchi to a mute debate. "I will ask questions only through gestures and you must answer them," she decreed. 

On the appointed day, Vasanti raised one of her fingers. "What, in the name of God, does that mean!" Vararuchi wondered. Thinking she meant to poke out one of his eyes, he raised two fingers in response, meaning he would poke out both of hers! 

Vasanti, however, interpreted his gesture philosophically. "He understands me! His answer is correct. What he says is that, besides God, the individual self is also a true and eternal being. That makes it two, and not one." 

As her second question, Vasanti held up her five fingers. Vararuchi, thinking she wanted to slap him, showed a fist, meaning he would box her. Vasanti again misinterpreted it. "Ah, the perfect answer! Though the physical elements are five, they become one in the human body." 

Princess Vasanti acknowledged defeat and was married to Vararuchi. But after the ceremony, Vararuchi fumbled over a simple Sanskrit word for 'camel'. Vasanti was shocked. "Aren't you a scholar?" 

"No. I am only a cowherd," he confessed. 

"Get out! Get out of my sight!" Vasanti screamed, throwing him out. 

Vararuchi felt cheated and humiliated. Seeking revenge, he wandered into the forest where he saw a strange sight—a man sitting on a branch and cutting the very branch he was seated on! It was the brash Kalidasa. As expected, Kalidasa fell from the tree. 

"I could use this boor to pay her back with a trick of my own!" Vararuchi plotted. He and his friends dressed Kalidasa as a pandit and took him to the palace, instructing him not to speak. 

They presented him to Vasanti for a mute debate. When Vasanti raised one finger, Kalidasa thought she wanted to poke out his eye, so he raised two fingers. The same misunderstanding occurred, and Vasanti accepted defeat. 

But when Vasanti discovered she had married an idiot, she was furious. "Go away! Only a great scholar can be a fit husband for me—and you are only a country bumpkin!" she raged, driving him out. 

Kalidasa was deeply humiliated by Vasanti's words. "How can I become a scholar? Ah, there's the temple of Mother Kali. I'll ask her to grant me knowledge," he resolved. 

Inside the temple, he meditated and stayed without food or drink for many days. Finally, in desperation, he drew a sword to sacrifice himself at Kali's feet. 

"Don't do it, son! I am pleased with you. I grant your wish. You shall be endowed with genius," Mother Kali appeared and blessed him. 

Kalidasa returned home glowing with happiness and spoke to Vasanti in chaste Sanskrit. "Asti kashchit vagvisheshah? (Is there any special speech?)" she asked, surprised. 

"Yes. By the grace of Mother Kali, I have learnt Sanskrit," he proudly replied. He then told her he would begin three great works with each of the words she had just uttered ("Asti", "Kashchit", "Vag"), honoring her as his guru who set him on the path of knowledge. 

Vasanti was enraged that he considered her his guru and not his wife. "May you meet your end at the hands of a woman!" she cursed him. 

After this, Kalidasa led the life of a wanderer. He wrote beautiful poetry in Sanskrit, including the famous *Meghaduta*. From time to time, he stopped at various courts. His favourite was the court of King Bhoja at Dhara. 

One day, King Bhoja challenged his scholars: "Can anyone compose a verse ending with 'Taatam, tatam, tam, tatatam, tatam, tah'?" All the scholars were perplexed by the seemingly meaningless syllables. 

But Kalidasa, a ready wit, composed a poem describing a golden bowl of sandal-paste falling down the stairs, making that exact sound. King Bhoja awarded him the prize and embraced him as a friend. 

Kalidasa lived at the court and wrote magnificent plays, including *Malavika-Agnimitra* and the incredibly successful *Shakuntala*. He also wrote epic poems like *Kumarasambhava* and *Raghuvamsha*. 

However, King Bhoja and Kalidasa eventually had a misunderstanding. Bhoja wanted a poem in his praise, but Kalidasa refused. "You are a great and noble king, but the praise of mortals is not the aim of poetry." Kalidasa left Dhara. 

King Bhoja missed his friend. He devised a plan to find him. He announced a reward of a lakh of gold coins for anyone who composed a 'new' poem, but instructed his scholars to falsely claim they had heard every submitted poem before, thereby rejecting them all. 

Kalidasa, hearing of this trick, helped a poor villager by composing a poem for him to present to the King. The poem stated: "Ninety-one crores of gold coins did your father once borrow from me. Isn't it time now, O King Bhoja, to return the money?" 

The King's scholars were trapped. If they claimed they had heard it before, the King would have to pay the massive debt! The King gladly accepted it as a new poem, paid the reward, and recognized Kalidasa's wit. He went to Kalidasa's village, apologized, and they were reunited. 

Some time later, after another quarrel, Kalidasa set sail for Lanka. Avoiding the court of King Kumaradasa so Bhoja wouldn't find him, Kalidasa lived in the house of a dancer. 

King Kumaradasa offered a reward to anyone who could complete a specific verse. The dancer brought the verse to Kalidasa, and he easily completed it. 

But the dancer was greedy. "The King will surely give an award for this verse. Why should I let Kalidasa have the money?" she thought. She stabbed Kalidasa in the back, killing him, fulfilling Vasanti's curse. 

The dancer took the poem to King Kumaradasa, claiming she wrote it. But the King recognized the genius. "It is rather like Kalidasa's work. Did you really write those lines?" he demanded. He interrogated her, threatening her with the dungeon, and she confessed. 

Heartbroken, Kumaradasa arranged a grand funeral for the poet. As he witnessed the flames enveloping Kalidasa's body, the King was so overcome by grief that he leaped into the funeral pyre and was consumed in the blaze alongside his great friend.
