# Karttikeya

In ancient times, the Devas and the Asuras were forever at war, and often it was the Asuras who were defeated. After one crushing defeat, an Asura king named Akhirsen went to his daughter, Maya, a sorceress. 

"The Devas are victorious again. O the shame of it!" he lamented. 

"Don't worry, father. I will not rest till I teach the Devas a lesson!" Maya vowed. Taking the form of a beautiful woman, she went to the forest where the great Sage Kashyapa sat deep in meditation. "I will marry this sage and bring forth children more powerful than the Devas," she plotted. 

Her magic changed the place into a pretty garden where she danced and sang melodiously till the sage looked up. "Please let me serve you as your wife, O holy one," she requested. The virtuous sage, ignorant of her designs, accepted her. 

In due course, a son was born to them. "You, my mighty Surapadman, shall be the lord of the three worlds," Maya declared. Later, she gave birth to two more sons, Simhamukha and Taraka. 

When the boys came of age, Kashyapa told them, "I am going away, my sons. Take care of your mother, lead a virtuous life, and be devoted to the Lord." 

"Now is the time to act. Propitiate Lord Shiva and obtain boons which will make you invincible," Maya instructed them. 

The three left home and performed severe penances to win the grace of Shiva. But when their efforts did not bear fruit, Surapadman, the eldest, jumped into the sacrificial fire. 

He was saved from the flames by Shiva himself. "Why are you sacrificing yourself?" Shiva asked. 

"Lord, I want to be the master of the universe. I want to have a body that will not perish," Surapadman prayed. 

"No one can have an immortal body. But you will be invincible and rule the universe for a long time to come. You shall not be defeated by any power except mine," Shiva granted the boon. 

Intoxicated with triumph, the three brothers returned home and then began ravaging kingdom after kingdom. They eventually attacked Devaloka, routed the Devas, and took them captive. 

"We'll make them our slaves. Indra will be our fisherman, and Vayu our sweeper," Surapadman decreed from his throne, mocking the captive gods. He also imprisoned Indra's son, Jayanta. Surapadman then had the magnificent city of Mahendrapuri built by Vishwakarma to serve as his capital. 

The desperate Devas approached Lord Brahma. "Shiva had promised to help us. That was long ago. Let's go to Kailas once more and pray to him," Brahma advised. 

At Kailas, Shiva listened to their pleas. "Be patient! Your miseries will soon end because their days are numbered," he assured them. 

Then Shiva assumed a majestic form with six faces. From his faces, six divine sparks shot forth, dazzling the Devas with their splendour. "The child born out of these sparks will slay the Asuras," Shiva proclaimed. 

"Vayu, you and Agni carry these sparks to Ganga. She'll carry them to the Sharavana (forest of reeds) on the Udaya mountain," Shiva instructed. 

Ganga brought the sparks to the Sharavana, where they turned into six beautiful babies resting on lotus flowers. Six celestial nymphs, the Krittikas, happened to pass by and cuddled the infants. 

Shiva and Parvati soon arrived. "My children!" Parvati cried. As she stepped closer and embraced them, the six babies merged into one child with six heads and twelve arms. 

"Peace! Peace! As Parvati's son he will be named Skanda and as the son of the Goddess of the forest, Sharavana; as the Krittikas' son, Karttikeya; as Ganga's Kumara; as Agni's son, Mahasena; and as mine, Guha," Shiva declared. 

Soon, nine divine beings emerged from the lake. "Those are your Ganas—Veerabahu and his companions," Shiva told Karttikeya. 

Shiva took Karttikeya to Mount Kailasa. When the time came to subdue the Asuras, Shiva gave him a divine weapon. "You shall lead the Devas to victory. Crush the Asuras and liberate the Devas. Here is the matchless Vel, your spear. May success be yours!" 

Armed with the spear, Karttikeya set out. His first obstacle was the Asura Krauncha, who took the form of a massive mountain to block his path. Without a word, Karttikeya hurled his spear, shattering the mountain and destroying Krauncha. 

When the news reached Taraka, he mocked, "Krauncha destroyed? By a slip of a boy? Let me go and size him up." 

Taraka confronted Karttikeya but found his weapons useless against the divine spear. Karttikeya hurled his spear, and Tarakasura was slain. 

Next, Karttikeya sent Veerabahu as his envoy to Surapadman, asking him to release Jayanta and stop persecuting the Devas. Surapadman haughtily refused and threatened Veerabahu, who fought his way out and returned with the message of war. 

Karttikeya's army marched to Mahendrapuri. On the first day, Surapadman's son, Banukopan, caused great damage to the Deva army but was eventually slain by Veerabahu. 

The next day, Simhamukha led the attack. He used a magical missile that wound itself like a rope around Veerabahu and the Deva army, hurling them far away. Karttikeya countered the move and then faced Simhamukha. 

After a fierce battle, Karttikeya hurled the 'Indrayudha' (thunderbolt) at Simhamukha. As he fell, Simhamukha's ego shattered. "Now I see you in all your glory. O Lord, bless me." 

"You shall serve Goddess Kali as her vehicle," the merciful Karttikeya blessed him. 

The next day, Surapadman himself led the Asuras. He used sorcery to create an illusion of darkness and revived his dead army. Karttikeya used a special astra to destroy the revived Asuras and dispel the darkness. 

"I'll change my form and attack him," Surapadman decided. He assumed the form of a huge bird and attacked. Karttikeya, using Indra in the form of a peacock as his mount, fought back in the sky. 

Surapadman escaped and took the form of a massive tree. "Learn, O blind one! Darkness cannot hide before light!" Karttikeya hurled his spear, cutting the tree in two. 

Surapadman emerged in his natural form and charged. Quick as lightning, Karttikeya hurled his spear, fatally striking the Asura king. 

As he fell, a great change came over Surapadman. "Lord, pardon me. I repent for my evil acts. Lord, I seek refuge in you," he gasped. 

Karttikeya looked at the fallen Asura with compassion. "Surapadman, repentance washes away all one's sins. You shall serve me as my vehicle and also as my emblem," he promised. 

Thus, Surapadman assumed two forms—one of a peacock to serve as a vehicle to the Lord, and another of a cock to adorn Karttikeya's flag post. The Devas rejoiced and bowed to Karttikeya in reverence.
