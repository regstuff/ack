# Agastya

Agastya is perhaps the most famous of the Rishis of India, revered in both the North and the South. Though almost a dwarf in size, he possessed incredible spiritual strength and wisdom. 

One day, Sage Agastya came upon a strange and disturbing sight in a forest. Several men were hanging upside down from the branches of a tree over a deep, dark pit. 

"Who could they be?" Agastya wondered. 

"We are your ancestors," the men cried out pitifully. 

"Why are you hanging here like this?" Agastya asked in horror. 

"Because you are not yet married!" they explained. "Our souls will have no peace till we are assured of the continuance of our line. A son born of you can redeem us. And you too will escape this hell."

"O revered ones, I shall act for your redemption," Agastya promised solemnly. 

As Agastya walked away, he pondered his vow. "The mother of my son shall be the most worthy of women." 

He searched long and hard in all the three worlds but could not find the ideal woman. "I shall have to create her, taking in all that is most beautiful," he decided. Using his immense spiritual powers, Agastya created a beautiful baby girl. 

At that time, the King of Vidarbha was performing severe penances to have a child. "The child I have created shall take birth as the daughter of this king," Agastya decreed. 

When the Queen of Vidarbha gave birth, the King was overjoyed. The Brahmans were deeply impressed by the extraordinary beauty of the child. "She shall be called Lopamudra."

Lopamudra grew up into a stunningly beautiful and virtuous young woman. The King began searching for a worthy husband for her. Meanwhile, Agastya realized it was time. "Lopamudra must now be ready for marriage. I shall go to her father and ask for her hand."

When Agastya approached the King of Vidarbha, the King was deeply troubled. *I wish to marry and beget a son. Will you give me your daughter?* Agastya had asked. *How can I give my beautiful daughter to an ascetic living in the forest?* the King worried. *But I dare not displease Agastya.*

Just then, Lopamudra herself entered the room. "Father, why do you hesitate? I am willing to marry the great sage."

Relieved, the King agreed, and Agastya married Lopamudra. After the wedding, Agastya looked at her rich clothing. "Lopamudra, your royal robes do not befit a sage's wife. Discard them."

Without a moment's hesitation, Lopamudra cast off her costly silks and jewels. "Henceforth I shall wear only bark and skin and rags, my lord."

They left Vidarbha and travelled to Agastya's remote hermitage at Gangotri in the Himalayas. The days passed, and Lopamudra served her husband excellently through his severe penances. 

"Dear wife, you have pleased me well. You will soon become my equal in spiritual merit," Agastya praised her one day. 

"My lord, I too wish to be pleased," Lopamudra replied gently. "We are householders. It is certainly not wrong for us to possess wealth. I would like to live as I did in my father's house."

"Then I will go out in search of wealth. Wait here for me," Agastya agreed. 

Agastya first went to King Srutarva, who was renowned for his riches. "O great one, what can I do for you?" the King bowed. 

"I have come to you for wealth. Give me what you can spare," Agastya said. 

Srutarva was generous but honest. "I have no wealth to spare, O Sage. However, you may take as much as you want from what I have."

Agastya, using his spiritual insight, saw that the King's accounts were perfectly balanced. "If I take anything from this King, I will be depriving others," Agastya realized. He turned to the King. "Let us go and see if King Brihadastha can help me."

But King Brihadastha, too, had no wealth to spare without harming his subjects. "Perhaps King Trasadasyu would be able to help," Brihadastha suggested. "Let us all go to him."

However, King Trasadasyu was also in the same position. "My revenue and expenditure are equal," the King explained. "But I shall come to your aid if you need it."

"No. I do not want to acquire wealth by depriving others," Agastya refused. 

The three Kings conferred with each other. "There is an Asura called Ilvala who has a great deal of wealth. Let us go to him," they suggested. 

Ilvala was a wealthy but wicked Asura. He and his brother Vatapi deeply hated Brahmans and had vowed to kill as many as they could using a dark, magical trick. 

"Vatapi, I hear that the great Brahman sage, Agastya, is coming here today," Ilvala laughed wickedly. "Get ready to change your form! I'll become a goat as usual. You will kill and cook me... and feed you to the Brahman. Then when I call you... I'll tear his stomach and come rushing out... and another hated Brahman, the greatest of them all, will be destroyed!" 

When Agastya and the three Kings arrived, Ilvala pretended to be a gracious host. "Welcome, O Sage, and you, O Kings. Come, I have cooked a special meal in your honour."

The three Kings, who knew of Ilvala's deadly trick, were terrified. "Alas! I never thought he would dare do it to the great sage," they whispered. "We must warn Agastya."

But Agastya calmly stopped them. "Do not worry. I will be safe."

Agastya sat down and heartily ate the massive meal of cooked meat. "Hm..m.m! The tastiest meal I have ever eaten!" he smiled, finishing the very last morsel. 

*And the last, you hated Brahman,* Ilvala thought gleefully. He stepped forward and shouted the magical command. "Vatapi, O Vatapi, come out!"

Agastya patted his stomach and let out a massive, thunderous burp. *BURRRRRP.*

Ilvala waited for his brother to burst out of the sage's stomach. Nothing happened. He panicked. "Vatapi! O Vatapi! Come out. I, Ilvala, am calling you!"

Agastya burst into laughter. "Ha! Ha! How can he come out? I have already digested him."

Realizing his brother was gone forever and his magic had failed against the great sage, Ilvala accepted utter defeat. Trembling, he fell to his knees. "Why have you come here? What can I do for you?"

"We know that you are wealthy. These Kings and I need wealth," Agastya commanded. "Give us what you can without depriving any other."

Ilvala was quiet for a moment, silently calculating. "I shall give the Kings 10,000 cows each and as many gold coins. To Agastya - 20,000 cows and as many gold coins. I will also give him my golden chariot and horses," he thought to himself. 

He looked up at Agastya. "If you can guess what I intend to give you, that will be yours."

Agastya, with his divine spiritual insight, smiled. "You intend to give the Kings 10,000 cows each and as many gold coins. And to me, 20,000 cows, as many coins, and your golden chariot and horses."

Ilvala bowed his head. "You have won them all. Take them."

Agastya and the Kings loaded the vast treasure onto the magnificent golden chariot and rode away. "I have heard that Ilvala's horses are the most fleet-footed in the land," one King remarked. In no time at all, they reached Agastya's hermitage at Gangotri. The Kings took their share and returned to their cities. 

Agastya went to his wife. "I have brought what you wanted, Lopamudra. We will now live as you wish."

A few years later, Lopamudra gave birth to a radiant son. "At last the promise I made to my ancestors is fulfilled," Agastya smiled, his lineage secure. 

One day, while Agastya, Lopamudra, and their son were living peacefully at Gangotri, a massive crisis threatened the world. The towering Vindhya Mountain, located in central India, became deeply jealous of Mount Meru. 

"O glorious sun, you go around Mount Meru every day and honour him," the Vindhya Mountain complained to the Sun God. "I too would like to be honoured in the same manner."

"My path has been assigned to me since creation," the Sun replied patiently. "I do no special honour to Meru."

The Sun's dismissal infuriated the arrogant mountain. "Then I shall grow taller and block your path!" 

The Vindhya Mountain began to grow rapidly, stretching higher and higher into the sky. The Devas (gods) in heaven were extremely alarmed. "This unchecked growth of Vindhya will soon plunge the earth in absolute darkness! It will upset the balance of creation."

They pleaded with the mountain, but Vindhya was stubborn. "That is not my concern," it scoffed, continuing to grow. 

"Let us hasten to Agastya," the desperate Devas finally decided. "He is the only one who can help us."

The Devas rushed to Agastya's hermitage and begged for his intervention. "Do not worry. I will do all I can," Agastya promised. He turned to his wife. "Come, dear one. Let us go and see if we can stop him. It is our duty."

Agastya and his family travelled to the foot of the massive, looming Vindhya Mountain. When the mountain saw the great, revered Sage approaching, it respectfully bowed its peak down low to allow him to pass. 

"O great mountain, I wish to cross over you to the southern country," Agastya said softly. "Wait till I return."

"O great Sage, I will stop increasing my size till you return from the south," the mountain promised, remaining bowed. 

Agastya, Lopamudra, and their son crossed over the bowed mountain and stepped into the beautiful, lush lands of South India. 

"Lopamudra, we will not return to the north," Agastya smiled. "Then Vindhya will never be able to grow any taller."

And so, Agastya and his family settled in the country south of the Vindhya Mountain, bringing his immense wisdom and grace to the southern lands forever. 

Some time later, a ferocious group of underwater Asuras known as the Kalkeyas, led by the terrible demon Vritra, began terrorizing the three worlds. The Devas, desperately needing help, sought the advice of Lord Brahma. 

"Go to the great sage Dadhichi and ask him for his bones," Brahma instructed Indra, the King of the Devas. "Take the bones to the divine architect Twashtri and ask him to make a weapon for you with them."

Sage Dadhichi willingly sacrificed his life, and Twashtri forged his indestructible bones into the supreme weapon, the *Vajra* (Thunderbolt). Armed with the Vajra, Indra and the Devas attacked the Asuras. A terrifying battle ensued, and Indra finally hurled the mighty Vajra at Vritra. 

*BOOM!* 

The Vajra struck Vritra perfectly, and the great Asura leader fell dead. 

Panic-stricken without their leader, the surviving Kalkeyas turned and fled. "We are doomed! Run! Run into the deep ocean. They will not find us here!"

The Kalkeyas hid deep in the dark, impenetrable trenches of the ocean. "We must kill all good men on earth," they plotted underwater. "Their death will destroy the whole universe. We must do our work by night, so we will not be found out."

Under the cover of darkness, the Kalkeyas began a terrifying campaign of slaughter, emerging from the sea at night to murder sages and good men, and vanishing back into the depths before dawn. 

"The good men on earth are being destroyed," the Devas wept to Lord Vishnu. "We do not know who the culprits are."

"The Kalkeyas have taken shelter under the sea," Vishnu revealed. "They are the culprits. They cannot be killed as long as they hide in the ocean bed."

"What should we do?" Indra despaired. 

"You must find out some means to dry up the entire ocean," Vishnu advised. 

"But who can accomplish such an impossible task?" Indra gasped. 

Then, the Devas realized there was only one being in all the three worlds capable of such a feat. "Who else, but Agastya!"

The hopeful Devas travelled south to Agastya's hermitage. "Great one, you consumed the evil Vatapi. You prevented Vindhya from plunging the world in eternal darkness. So we have come to you for help."

"What do you want me to do?" Agastya asked calmly. 

"We want you to drink the ocean! Then we shall be able to kill our enemies, the evil Kalkeyas."

"I shall do what you desire as it will benefit the world," Agastya agreed at once. "Array yourselves for war while I drink the ocean."

Agastya walked to the edge of the roaring sea, scooped water into his hands, and simply began to drink. In a matter of moments, the waters receded, and the entire ocean bed became completely dry and visible. 

The Kalkeyas, suddenly stripped of their watery shield, gasped in sheer terror. "Wh... what! Where is all the water going? RUN!"

But the Devas were well prepared, waiting on the newly exposed sea floor. "Attack! Do not spare a single one of them!" Indra commanded. 

A terrible battle ensued, and without the cover of the ocean, the Kalkeyas were quickly and utterly vanquished. 

Agastya watched the victory of the Devas with deep happiness. "My task is accomplished," the great sage smiled. "I may now go back to my penances. Peace be with you."

Thus, once more, the just and wise Sage Agastya had suppressed evil and protected the good of the universe.
