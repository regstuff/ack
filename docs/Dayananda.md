# Dayananda: His Strength Was His Virtue

On the night of Maha Shivaratri in 1839, in the town of Tankara near Morvi in Gujarat, fourteen-year-old Mool Shankar kept a strict vigil alongside his father, Karshanji Tiwari, in the local Shiva temple. As midnight approached, the tired worshippers dozed off one by one. 

Alone and watchful, young Mool Shankar stared at the Shiva Linga. Suddenly, he saw a swarm of rats scurry across the sacred stone, boldly devouring the food offerings. 

*What is this?* the boy thought in shock. *If this idol cannot even protect its own offerings from mere rodents, how can it be the Lord of the Universe?*

Awakening his father, Mool Shankar asked, "Father, why do we worship an idol that is helpless against rats?"

"Stop asking foolish questions!" his father snapped. "Repeat the mantra!"

Unable to quell the doubt in his heart, Mool Shankar slipped out of the temple and walked home.

A few years later, cholera struck Tankara, claiming the lives of Mool Shankar's beloved younger sister and his uncle. Watching his family weep in agony, the nineteen-year-old youth was deeply shaken by the reality of death. *Is death the end of all?* he pondered. *I must find the immortal Truth and the means to liberate mankind from sorrow.*

When his father insisted on arranging his marriage, twenty-one-year-old Mool Shankar left a quiet farewell note for his parents and escaped into the night, renouncing his family's vast wealth.

Wandering as an ascetic across India, he took the vow of lifelong celibacy (*Naishthik Brahmachari*) under a wandering monk who named him Shuddha Chaitanya. Later, on the banks of the sacred Narmada River, Swami Poornananda initiated him into the order of sanyasa, bestowing upon him the name **Swami Dayananda Saraswati** ("One who delights in compassion").

Dayananda traveled extensively through Kashmir, the Himalayas, and Kashi, studying Yoga and scriptures. Wherever he went, his heart bled for the suffering masses—oppressed by rigid caste discrimination, child marriage, superstition, and religious exploitation by corrupt priests. 

At the edge of a Himalayan cliff, overcome by desolation, Dayananda contemplated leaping into the abyss. But a voice within stopped him: *Dying in desolation serves no purpose. Why not use this body to lighten the misery of the world?*

In Mathura, thirty-six-year-old Dayananda sought out Swami Virjananda, a blind, saintly master of Vedic Sanskrit. Virjananda looked at the books of medieval grammar Dayananda carried and said, "Throw those secondary books into the Yamuna and come back to me. The original Vedas are the only pure source of knowledge!"

For two and a half years, Dayananda served his blind Guru with unwavering devotion, fetching water, sweeping the floor, and mastering the Vedas. When his studies were completed, Dayananda offered a small bowl of cloves as his *Guru Dakshina*.

Swami Virjananda pushed the cloves aside gently. "Dayananda, I ask for a different dakshina. Go into the world and spread the pure light of the Vedas to a weary nation. That will be the most fitting gift to your teacher."

Fired by his Guru's command, Swami Dayananda launched a relentless campaign for social and spiritual reform. At the Haridwar Kumbh Mela in 1867, he raised the banner of Vedic revival before thousands of pilgrims. 

"Idol worship, untouchability, child marriage, and the oppression of women have no sanction in the Vedas!" Swami Dayananda proclaimed in his thunderous voice. "God is one, formless, and omnipresent! All human beings—regardless of caste or gender—have equal right to education and the sacred thread of initiation!"

His revolutionary teachings outraged conservative orthodoxy. Reactionary priests threw stones, mud, and insults at him. In Anupshahar, an opponent handed him a poisoned *pan*. Using his advanced Yogic techniques of internal cleansing (*Kriyas*), Dayananda regurgitated the poison unharmed. 

When the local British magistrate arrested the culprit, Dayananda requested his immediate release: "I have come to free men from bondage, not to imprison them. Forgive him."

On October 22, 1869, the famous Kashi Shastrartha (great debate) was organized in Varanasi. Twenty-seven renowned orthodox pundits lined up to challenge the lone reformer before a crowd of thousands. With unmatched erudition, Dayananda cited original Vedic texts, refuting every argument for idol worship and birth-based caste. When rowdies disrupted the assembly, the Maharaja of Kashi later visited Dayananda's camp to apologize.

On April 10, 1875, Swami Dayananda established the **Arya Samaj** in Bombay to unite Indians across caste barriers, promote social justice, and inspire national self-respect. In the same year, he authored his masterpiece, *Satyartha Prakash* ("The Light of Truth").

Traveling through Punjab, Swamiji won millions of followers. Possessing superhuman physical strength, he once bare-handedly grabbed the wooden wheel of a fast-moving horse carriage belonging to a arrogant nobleman, bringing the galloping horses to a dead halt!

In 1883, Swami Dayananda visited the princely state of Jodhpur. Observing Maharaja Jaswant Singh spending his time in debauchery with a courtesan named Nanhijan, Swamiji fearlessly admonished the King: "You are a king, a lion among men! Why do you waste your life in a dancer's den?"

Ashamed, the Maharaja reformed his ways. But the enraged courtesan, backed by British spies suspicious of Dayananda's nationalist influence, bribed the royal cook, Jagannath, to mix crushed glass into Swamiji's nightly glass of milk.

Swamiji drank the milk and fell mortally ill. When the cook broke down in tears of repentance at his bedside, Dayananda showed boundless compassion. Handing the cook a bag of gold coins, Swamiji whispered, "Run away before the Maharaja finds out, or you will be executed."

Shifted to Ajmer for treatment, Swami Dayananda's condition deteriorated. On October 30, 1883, as the sun set over the hills of Ajmer, the great reformer opened his eyes, smiled serenely, and recited his final prayer: *"O Lord, May Thy Will be done! Om!"* before taking his last breath.

Swami Dayananda Saraswati's legacy of fearless truth, educational reform, and national awakening laid the foundational bedrock for India's freedom struggle.
