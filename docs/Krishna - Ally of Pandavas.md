# Krishna: An Ally of the Pandavas

Sage Shukadeva narrated the exploits of Krishna and Balarama to Pareekshit. 

"Once while Balarama was journeying on Mount Raivataka, he met an ape called Dvivida. Dvivida, who was said to possess the strength of ten thousand elephants, was enraged by the slaying of his friend, Narakasura. 'I must avenge the death of my dear friend. This is where that Krishna, the slayer of my friend, lives. I will ravage this whole area,' the ape roared." 

Dvivida created havoc, throwing huge boulders on houses and churning the seawater until coastal villages were submerged. He even harassed women on the Raivataka hill. Balarama confronted him. Dvivida uprooted trees and rocks to attack, but Balarama parried the blows and crushed him barehanded, hitting him a fatal blow on the collar bone. 

Sometime later, Prince Duryodhana arranged a Swayamvara for his daughter, Lakshmana. Samba, the son of Krishna and Jambavati, attended and boldly carried the bride away. 

Karna, Duryodhana, and other Kaurava warriors gave chase. "At last the Kaurava warriors overpowered Samba... Samba was tied up and taken captive to Hastinapura." 

Sage Narada conveyed this news to the Yadavas in Dwaraka. The Yadavas prepared for war, but Balarama intervened. "Let us avoid any fights in the family. I myself will go to Hastinapura and persuade them to free Samba." 

Balarama went to Hastinapura and sent a message through Uddhava. The Kauravas welcomed Balarama, but when he delivered King Ugrasena's request to release Samba, they turned arrogant. "Ha! You Yadavas really think too much of yourself... Just because you are related to us by marriage, it does not make you our equal in status." 

Enraged by the insult, Balarama struck his powerful plough into the ground. "Today I will render the earth free from these vain and foolish Kauravas. I will drag their city into the Ganga!" 

The earth began to shake, and the city walls trembled. Panic-stricken, the Kauravas rushed out, begging for mercy. "They hurriedly released Samba. Here is your Samba and Lakshmana, his wife." 

Balarama returned to Dwaraka with the newly-weds, where they were joyously received. 

One day, Narada visited Dwaraka to see how Krishna managed his thousands of wives. He was astounded to find Krishna present in every palace simultaneously—playing chess in one, performing a yajna in another, and in serious discussion in yet another! 

While Krishna was in his council hall, Sudharma Sabha, a messenger arrived. "O Lord, you are the saviour of all. I have come to seek your help... He was the messenger sent on behalf of the thousands of kings held captive by Jarasandha in Girivraja." 

Just then, Narada arrived and conveyed Yudhishthira's wish to perform the Rajasooya Yajna. Krishna advised Yudhishthira, "First you must subdue all the kings of the land... We should attack Jarasandha first." 

Krishna, Bhima, and Arjuna went to Girivraja disguised as Brahmins. They demanded a duel from Jarasandha. "We are not Brahmins in search of alms. We are Kshatriyas keen on fighting," Krishna revealed. 

Jarasandha laughed and chose to fight Bhima. "Like two mighty elephants fighting, Bhima and Jarasandha were pitched against one another." They wrestled barehanded for twenty-seven days. 

On the twenty-eighth day, Bhima confided, "O Krishna, I cannot defeat Jarasandha." 

To give Bhima a hint, Krishna took up a twig and tore it into two halves, throwing them in opposite directions. Understanding the hint (which referred to how Jarasandha was born from two halves joined together), Bhima felled Jarasandha and tore his body into two! 

Jarasandha's son, Sahadeva, was installed as king, and the imprisoned kings were finally freed. They joyously accepted Yudhishthira's supremacy. 

During the Rajasooya Yajna in Indraprastha, Yudhishthira offered the first worship to Krishna, guided by Sahadeva's advice. 

Shishupala, the Prince of Chedi, could not bear this. "How can you people be misled by a young fool like Sahadeva? With so many learned venerable sages present here, how can you select a cowherd like Krishna for the first worship?" he shouted insultingly. 

When Shishupala lunged at him, Krishna calmly released his Chakra and cut off his head. 

The Rajasooya Yajna was successfully completed. However, Duryodhana burned with envy seeing the wealth and glory of the Pandavas. Wandering in the magical palace built by Maya, he was confused by illusions, falling into pools he thought were floors and bumping into doors he thought were open. 

"Many people including Bhima and the queens burst into laughter at Duryodhana's plight." Humiliated and mocked, Duryodhana's heart was filled with bitterness and a thirst for revenge.
