# Bhagat Singh: The Cheerful Young Martyr

On September 27, 1907, in the quiet village of Banga near Lahore, a baby boy was born into the household of Sardar Kishan Singh. The family was steeped in patriotism—Kishan Singh and his younger brother Swaran Singh were in British custody for political agitation, while another brother, Ajit Singh, was serving an exile in Mandalay. On the day of the child's birth, news arrived that Ajit Singh had been released. Rejoicing at the auspicious coincidence, his grandmother named the infant Bhagat, meaning "the fortunate one."

Growing up in an atmosphere of patriotic sacrifice, young Bhagat was acutely sensitive to his family's suffering. Watching his aunt weep for her exiled husband, the boy rushed to embrace her. "Auntie, don't weep," he promised earnestly. "When I grow up, I will drive the British out and bring Uncle home."

"The British are very strong, child," his mother, Vidyavati, cautioned. "They have guns and cannons."

"Don't worry," the boy replied with conviction. "I too will get guns."

One evening, Kishan Singh strolled through his fields with a friend and found young Bhagat digging small holes in the soil, carefully dropping pebbles into them. "What are you planting, Bhagat?" his father asked in amusement.

"Rifles!" the child answered proudly. "To free my country."

In 1916, Bhagat entered the D.A.V. High School in Lahore. He was deeply drawn to the memory of Shaheed Kartar Singh Sarabha, a revolutionary who had walked to the gallows at the age of twenty. Carrying Sarabha's photograph in his pocket, Bhagat would often tell his classmates, "He is my hero."

In 1919, when Bhagat was twelve years old, the British government passed the tyrannical Rowlatt Act, authorizing arrest and imprisonment without trial. Protests erupted nationwide, culminating in the horrific Jallianwalla Bagh massacre in Amritsar, where General Dyer ordered troops to fire upon a peaceful, unarmed crowd. 

Shocked by the news, young Bhagat took a train to Amritsar. Standing amidst the bullet-ridden walls of Jallianwalla Bagh, he filled a small glass vial with the blood-soaked earth. Returning home, he placed the jar near his bed, vowing to dedicate his life to avenging the martyrs.

In the early 1920s, Bhagat joined the National College in Lahore, founded by Lala Lajpat Rai. There he befriended fellow patriots Sukhdev and Bhagwati Charan Vohra. Together, they staged nationalist plays such as *Rana Pratap* to awaken the illiterate masses.

When Bhagat turned sixteen, his family pressed him to marry. Unwilling to bind himself to domestic life, Bhagat left a letter for his father: *"My life has been dedicated to the noblest cause, that of the freedom of the country. Therefore, there is no rest or worldly desire that can lure me now."*

He fled to Kanpur, meeting the great journalist-patriot Ganesh Shankar Vidyarthi. Working at Vidyarthi's nationalist press, *Pratap*, Bhagat joined the Hindustan Republican Association (HRA). He threw himself into revolutionary work, distributing anti-British leaflets at public fairs and outwitting police chasers with daring agility.

Returning to Lahore when his grandmother fell ill, Bhagat founded the *Naujawan Bharat Sabha* in 1926 to unite youth across communal lines. In September 1928, he met Chandra Shekhar Azad in Delhi to reorganize the movement into the Hindustan Socialist Republican Association (HSRA).

Events reached a climax on October 30, 1928, when the Simon Commission arrived in Lahore. A massive, peaceful demonstration led by Lala Lajpat Rai marched toward the railway station, chanting "Simon, Go Back!" 

A heavy police force under Superintendent James A. Scott blocked the marchers and ordered a brutal lathi charge. Scott himself struck Lala Lajpat Rai repeatedly on the head and chest. Standing battered before the crowd, Lalaji declared in a booming voice: *"I declare that every blow hurled at us today will prove a nail in the coffin of the British Empire!"*

Eighteen days later, Lalaji succumbed to his injuries. Outraged by the insult to the nation, the HSRA resolved to avenge his death. On December 17, 1928, Bhagat Singh and Rajguru took position outside the Lahore Police Station. When an officer mounted a motorcycle, believing him to be Scott, Rajguru and Bhagat fired a fatal volley of bullets. Only later did they learn they had killed Assistant Superintendent J.P. Saunders.

With the entire Punjab police force on high alert, Bhagat executed a masterly escape. He shaved off his beard and hair, donned an elegant suit and felt hat, and boarded a first-class train compartment with Durga Bhabhi (wife of revolutionary Bhagwati Charan Vohra) posing as his wife, while Rajguru dressed as their servant. They passed through heavy police cordons undetected.

In early 1929, the British government sought to push the draconian Trade Disputes Bill and Public Safety Bill through the Central Legislative Assembly in Delhi. To protest these anti-labor measures, the HSRA decided to strike a dramatic blow—not to kill, but to awaken the nation.

On April 8, 1929, as the Assembly met in Delhi, Bhagat Singh and Batukeshwar Dutt sat in the visitors' gallery. As the President stood up to pass the bill, Bhagat tossed two low-intensity noise bombs into an empty area on the floor. Smoke filled the chamber as leaflets rained down from the gallery, carrying the opening words: *"It takes a loud voice to make the deaf hear."*

Instead of fleeing, the two young men stood their ground, shouting "Inquilab Zindabad!" (*Long Live the Revolution!*) and voluntarily offered their wrists to the arresting officers, intending to use the court trial to spread their message nationwide.

During the trial, lawyer Asaf Ali read out Bhagat Singh's ringing statement: *"We dropped the bomb to register our protest on behalf of those who had no other means left to give expression to their heart-rending agony... Our sole purpose was to make the deaf hear."*

Sentenced to life imprisonment, Bhagat was transferred to Lahore Central Jail to face trial for the Saunders murder. In prison, discovering that Indian political prisoners were subjected to filthy food and brutal treatment compared to European convicts, Bhagat launched a historic hunger strike. 

"Outside the jail we fight for freedom," Bhagat told his fellow inmates. "Inside the jail we must fight for our honor." 

For sixty-three days, the prisoners refused food. When his close comrade Jatin Das died a martyr during the strike, public outrage forced the British authorities to concede jail reforms.

When his father filed a court petition arguing that Bhagat was innocent and absent from Lahore during Saunders' death, Bhagat wrote a stern letter to his father: *"My life is not so precious as you may think it to be... You have proven weak at a critical moment. I want you to know that your conduct has pained me deeply."*

On October 7, 1930, the Special Tribunal sentenced Bhagat Singh, Sukhdev, and Rajguru to death by hanging. When a sympathetic state advocate suggested filing a mercy petition, Bhagat smiled calmly. "There is no need. It is better to die bravely than to cringe before the enemy. I am the moth of the flame of liberty."

On the evening of March 23, 1931, the jailor entered Bhagat's cell ahead of schedule. Bhagat was engrossed in a biography of Lenin. "Sardarji, we have come to lead you to the gallows," the official said quietly.

"Wait a moment," Bhagat smiled, turning a page. "Let one revolutionary meet another."

Moments later, Bhagat Singh, Sukhdev, and Rajguru walked hand-in-hand to the gallows, singing patriotic songs that echoed through the dark corridors of Lahore Jail. Standing on the trapdoor, Bhagat called out to the British Deputy Commissioner: "Well, Mr. Magistrate, you are lucky today. You shall see how Indian revolutionaries embrace death with pleasure for the sake of their supreme ideal!"

As the hangman pulled the lever, the three young martyrs embraced death with smiles on their lips. Bhagat Singh was just twenty-three years old, but his sacrifice ignited a fire across India that ultimate brought the British Raj to its knees.
