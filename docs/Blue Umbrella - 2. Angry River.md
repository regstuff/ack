# Angry River

In the middle of the Great River, which began high in the snow-capped Himalayas and flowed down toward the distant sea, lay a tiny, green island. On this island stood a small mud hut sheltered by a fifty-year-old Peepul tree. Here lived twelve-year-old Sita with her grandparents. 

Sita's grandfather was a gentle fisherman, and her grandmother had taught her all the household chores. Her only companion was a small rag doll named Mumta, to whom Sita whispered all her thoughts and secrets.

One monsoon morning, Sita's grandmother fell gravely ill. "I must take her to the hospital in Shahganj across the river," her grandfather told Sita anxiously. "I will have to sell our goats to pay the doctor. You will have to stay on the island alone for a few days."

Sita looked at the dark storm clouds gathering over the mountains. "I'm not afraid, Grandfather, but what if the river rises?"

"If the water reaches the island, bring the goats and hens inside," Grandfather instructed. "And if the river floods the hut, climb up into the branches of the ancient Peepul tree!"

Sita watched the small wooden boat shrink to a tiny speck on the broad river as her grandfather rowed away. 

That night, torrential rain hammered down. The wind howled through the Peepul leaves, and the river roared like a enraged beast. Sita lay awake on her charpoy, clutching Mumta to her chest.

By morning, muddy brown floodwaters were tearing away at the mud banks of the island. Dead trees, drowned cattle, and debris swept past in the raging current. Realizing the danger, Sita brought the hens inside and packed their few valuables—Grandfather's hookah, grandmother's brass vessels, and their silver ornaments—into a heavy iron trunk.

Suddenly, water gushed over the threshold of the hut. The mud walls began to crumble under the force of the flood. 

"The Peepul tree!" Sita cried out. Dropping everything except her rag doll Mumta, Sita scrambled out of the collapsing hut and climbed up into the sturdy lower branches of the ancient Peepul tree.

From her perch in the branches, Sita watched in sorrow as her home dissolved into the churning brown floodwaters. Soon, the entire island was submerged, and the Peepul tree stood alone against the fury of the deluge.

The raging river tore at the roots of the tree, washing away the stones and earth holding it to the riverbed. With a terrible groan of timber and a flurry of falling leaves, the fifty-year-old Peepul tree uprooted and plunged into the river with a massive splash!

Sita screamed and clung with all her strength to a thick branch as the giant tree drifted into the main current, spinning through the violent, muddy waters. 

For hours, as rain lashed down, Sita's arms ached with fatigue. She watched helplessly as distant boats swept past in the storm, unable to hear her faint cries.

Just as her strength was about to fail, the drifting tree struck a submerged sandbank near a bend in the river. 

"Hellooooo!" a clear voice called out through the rain.

Sita looked up to see a small wooden boat rowing toward her through the waves. At the oars was a young, dark-skinned village boy named Krishan. "Here, take my hand!" he shouted, pulling Sita out of the branches into his boat.

As the boat glided into the safety of a flooded mangrove forest, Krishan handed Sita two sweet ripe mangoes. "I haven't tasted a mango in over a year!" Sita smiled, eating eagerly.

"My name is Krishan," the boy introduced himself. "I live near the foothills. The current swept my boat past your island, and I saw you in the tree."

As dusk fell, Krishan tied the boat to a high mangrove branch. Rain pattered softly on the water. Taking out a slender bamboo flute, Krishan began to play a sweet, haunting melody that lulled the exhausted girl into a peaceful sleep.

At sunrise, the storm cleared and the sun broke through the clouds. Rowing downstream for an hour, they reached a hill village where generous folk offered them warm food and shelter.

From there, an old farmer offered Sita a ride on his slow bullock cart to Shahganj. Bidding farewell to Krishan, Sita boarded the cart, keeping his bamboo flute wrapped in her shawl.

Arriving in the busy lanes of Shahganj, Sita searched frantically among the crowds until she spotted a familiar turbaned figure—her grandfather!

"Grandfather!" Sita cried out, running into his arms.

"Sita! You are alive!" the old man wept, embracing her tightly. "I was so worried when the river flooded!"

Sita looked into his tear-filled eyes. "Is Grandmother...?"

Grandfather nodded silently. Her grandmother had passed away peacefully in the hospital.

Three days later, as the floodwaters receded, Sita and her grandfather returned to their island. The hut and the majestic Peepul tree were gone, leaving only bare rocks and river silt. 

"The Peepul tree was a part of our island and our life," Grandfather sighed.

"Yes, Grandfather," Sita said softly. "It was the tree that saved me... and the boy."

Together, they set to work. Using jute bags and fresh thatch, they built a sturdy new home. In the damp soil where the Peepul tree had once stood, Sita carefully planted a fresh mango seed, watering it every day.

As weeks passed, the river cleared of mud, flowing calm, deep, and blue under the mountain skies. One bright afternoon, while her grandfather was out fishing, Sita sat on the rocks playing a sweet tune on Krishan's bamboo flute.

A soft footfall sounded behind her. Sita turned to see Krishan standing on the bank with a warm smile.

"I thought you'd never come back!" Sita laughed.

"I promised I'd teach you how to play properly," Krishan grinned, sitting beside her on the rocks.

Gazing out over the tranquil blue waters, Sita smiled. The river had taken much away, but it had also given her life, hope, and a lasting friendship.
