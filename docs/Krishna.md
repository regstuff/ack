# Krishna: The Childhood of the Eighth Avatar of Vishnu

Krishna is the most endearing character in Indian mythology. He is at once the common cowherd engaging the milk-maids in playful banter and the supreme intellectual engaged in the exposition of the Gita philosophy. 

Vasudeva, a nobleman, had married Princess Devaki of Mathura. As he was taking his bride home, Devaki's cruel cousin, Prince Kamsa, was driving the chariot. Suddenly, a heavenly voice thundered, "Kamsa, you shall soon die. The eighth child born to Devaki will slay you!"

Enraged, Kamsa drew his sword to kill Devaki on the spot. "If she lives to have that eighth child! I'll slay her before then!" he roared. 

"Kamsa! Don't!" cried Vasudeva, jumping in front of the blade. "Your sister has done you no harm. As for the children who are yet to be born, I shall give each one to you, the moment it is born. I promise."

Kamsa lowered his sword with a sneer. "I'll spare Devaki, but I'll make sure that you keep your word." He imprisoned the newlyweds in a heavily guarded palace. 

True to his cruel word, Kamsa visited them every time a baby was born. "Devaki, give me the baby," he demanded. 

"No!" Devaki wept as Kamsa seized the infants, dashing them to the ground. 

"I am helpless, Devaki!" Vasudeva mourned as they lost six of their children. The seventh child was miraculously carried away to Rohini, Vasudeva's second wife in Gokul, and Kamsa was told the baby was still-born. 

"Good," Kamsa laughed. "The next will be the eighth one—the slayer of mighty Kamsa. Ha! Have Vasudeva and Devaki put in chains."

Then came the eighth child. On a dark, heavily raining midnight in the month of Shravan, a beautiful baby boy was born. "What a beautiful baby he is!" Devaki whispered. "And he knows he must not cry!"

"How I long to hold my son... but these chains..." Vasudeva sighed. Suddenly, with a sharp clank, the chains fell off his wrists. "Look! The chains have fallen off. And the doors have opened! It's a miracle!"

"Devaki! Quick! Give the child to me," Vasudeva said urgently. "I'll take him to our friend, Nanda, in Gokul."

Vasudeva placed the baby in a basket and stepped out into the storm. The palace guards had fallen into a deep, magical slumber. He reached the banks of the raging river Yamuna, which was in spate. However, as Vasudeva approached, the waters parted, allowing him to cross safely. When he reached Nanda's house, he found that Nanda's wife Yashoda had just given birth to a baby girl. Vasudeva swiftly swapped the babies, leaving his son and taking the girl back to the Mathura prison.

The moment Vasudeva re-entered the cell, the doors slammed shut, and the heavy chains wrapped around him once more. 

"Did you hear that? It's the wail of a new-born baby!" a guard yelled, waking up. "Come! Let's report this to the king!"

Kamsa rushed into the cell. "The eighth child! I will be there in a moment."

"Spare the baby, Kamsa," Vasudeva pleaded. "What harm can a girl do you?"

Ignoring him, Kamsa seized the baby girl by her legs. Just as he was about to dash her to the ground, she escaped his grip, flew into the sky, and her voice echoed down: "The one who shall destroy you still lives!"

In Gokul, Nanda and Yashoda rejoiced at the birth of their beautiful boy, whom they named Krishna.

Desperate to eliminate the threat, Kamsa sent the wicked demoness Putana to slaughter every newborn in the region. Smearing her breasts with poison, she went to Gokul disguised as a beautiful woman. 

"What a sweet baby! May I hold it?" she asked Yashoda.

Flattered, Yashoda handed the baby over. Putana fed him her poisoned milk, but the divine infant not only drank the milk—he sucked the life right out of the demoness. Putana collapsed dead in her true, hideous form, leaving the villagers stunned and baby Krishna perfectly safe.

As the years went by, Krishna grew into an adorable but incredibly mischievous boy. He and his older brother Balarama played on the banks of the Yamuna with the other cowherd boys (gwalas). Krishna loved fresh butter and constantly raided the neighbors' kitchens. 

"Krishna has eaten all the butter in my house," a frustrated gopika complained, dragging the boy to his mother. 

"My Krishna did that? I don't believe it," Yashoda defended him. "Why, I gave him a whole pot of butter only a short while ago."

Another day, the gopikas tried hanging their butter pots out of reach from the ceiling. "What shall we do?" the boys wondered. 

"I have an idea," Krishna grinned. They formed a human pyramid, and Krishna effortlessly climbed up to drink the milk.

"Yashoda! Krishna was drinking the milk straight from my cow!" another woman shouted. 

Krishna turned to his mother with innocent eyes. "But mother, the cow gave it to me."

"And the butter on your face? Who gave that to you?" Yashoda asked sternly.

"The gwala boys smeared it on my face. All out of spite," Krishna lied smoothly. 

"Krishna, you are becoming naughtier day by day. I will have to punish you!" Yashoda tied him to a heavy mortar. "Let's see you move now."

"Balarama will untie me. I'll go to him," Krishna thought. He began to crawl, dragging the heavy mortar behind him. The mortar caught between two massive trees. Krishna pulled with all his might, and with a tremendous crack, uprooted both mighty trees. 

"He uprooted those two mighty trees! This boy is a marvel!" the villagers gasped. Frightened by these strange happenings, the people of Gokul decided to pack up and move across the forest to Vrindavan.

In Vrindavan, Krishna herded the cattle and played his flute. His melodious music enchanted the entire village. "His music fills me with a strange yearning," the gopikas would sigh.

One day, a massive, angry bull charged through the fields. "It's Hastin! Run, Krishna!" the boys screamed.

"I won't," Krishna smiled. "I'll tame him." As the bull snorted fiercely and charged, Krishna nimbly leaped onto its back. "Quiet, my friend. I won't hurt you," he said gently, calming the beast.

Another time, Krishna found his friends crying. "My cows are dead. They drank water from Kaliya's pool," one wept. Kaliya was a massive, venomous multi-headed serpent who had poisoned a section of the river.

"Our cows will not be safe as long as Kaliya lives," Krishna said, marching toward the river.

"Krishna! Come back! Kaliya will kill you!" his friends shrieked, but Krishna dove straight into the venomous waters. Kaliya wrapped his massive coils around the boy, trying to crush him. Krishna effortlessly slipped free, subdued the serpent, and leaped onto its many hoods, dancing triumphantly on the monster's heads. Defeated, Kaliya begged for mercy, and Krishna ordered him to take his family and leave the pool forever.

As time passed, Krishna began to question the villagers' traditions. Seeing them prepare a grand offering out of fear, he asked, "We worship Indra because we are afraid of him. We ought to worship Mount Govardhana, whom we love!"

Persuaded by the young boy, the villagers made their offerings to the mountain instead. This infuriated Indra, the god of rain, who unleashed a terrifying, apocalyptic thunderstorm upon Vrindavan.

"Indra is angry. We shouldn't have worshipped Govardhana," the villagers panicked.

"Come my people, follow me," Krishna called out. He walked to the base of the mountain. "Look! It's rising!" 

With a casual push of his little finger, Krishna lifted the entire Mount Govardhana into the air like an umbrella. "Bring your cattle with you and take shelter. It is dry here." The villagers huddled in awe under the floating mountain for seven days and nights, until Indra's fury was spent and the humbled god retreated.

News of these miracles finally reached Kamsa in Mathura. "You fool! Why have you let him live so long? Didn't I order you to have him killed?" he raged at his commander, Pradyota.

"I have tried everything. Even the mad bull, Arishta, and the wild horse, Keshi," Pradyota trembled.

"Have it announced that in a fortnight I will perform the bow sacrifice... and invite all, including Krishna," Kamsa schemed. "You must see to it that he does not return alive."

He summoned the wise Akrura to fetch the boys from Vrindavan. Nanda, Krishna's foster father, was deeply suspicious. "No, Akrura. I don't trust Kamsa. I will not send my son."

Akrura was forced to reveal the truth. "Nanda! Krishna is the son of Prince Vasudeva."

When Yashoda heard this, she was heartbroken. "Krishna, did you hear that? Akrura says that Devaki is your mother. I am your mother, aren't I?"

Krishna wiped her tears. "Please don't be upset, mother. I shall always look upon you as my mother. But... I seek the permission of my elders to go to Mathura!" Reluctantly, the village elders agreed, knowing the Yadava chiefs would protect him.

As Krishna and Balarama arrived in Mathura, a massive crowd followed them to the sacrificial hall. At the gates, Kamsa's commander stood guard over a colossal, sacred bow. 

"Sir, I am Krishna. May I inspect the bow?" Krishna asked politely.

"Certainly! Do step in," Pradyota grinned, thinking, *So this is the boy I have to kill.*

Krishna lifted the massive weapon effortlessly. "Is this the bow? It doesn't look very heavy." He pulled the string back until, with a deafening crack, the sacred bow snapped in two. 

"Victory to Krishna! He is the saviour!" the jubilant crowd roared. 

When Pradyota delivered the news, Kamsa was terrified. The next morning, as the boys approached the wrestling arena, Kamsa unleashed a gigantic, maddened elephant upon them. "Watch out, Krishna! The elephant...!" the crowd screamed. The beast seized Krishna in its trunk, but the boy wrestled free, lifted the monstrous animal by its own trunk, hurled it into the air, and killed it.

Inside the arena, Kamsa announced, "Krishna, Kamsa has heard much about your strength and valour. He has invited you to wrestle... with me!"

"It's an honour, Chanura. I am ready," Krishna replied to the giant wrestler. 

Kamsa pitted Balarama against another brute, Mushtika. The crowd was outraged at the unfair match, but Balarama quickly overpowered Mushtika, killing him. 

Meanwhile, the massive Chanura rushed madly at Krishna, trying to crush him in his mighty arms. But Krishna nimbly slipped away. As Chanura charged again, Krishna dashed him to the ground with a sickening thud. "Chanura is dead! Victory to Krishna!" the arena erupted.

Furious and panicked, Kamsa ordered his soldiers to kill the boys. But Krishna leapt over the guards, vaulted into the royal balcony, and seized Kamsa by the hair. Taking the tyrant's own sword, Krishna threw him to the ground and killed him. 

The crowd was stunned into silence as Krishna stood over the fallen king and lifted the crown from Kamsa's head.

He then marched down into the palace dungeons and broke the chains of his true parents, Vasudeva and Devaki. Finally, he sought out Kamsa's imprisoned father, Ugrasena. 

"My lord! I have brought you what rightfully belongs to you," Krishna said, handing him the crown. 

This was only the beginning. Krishna lived long to triumph over all evil, and his great deeds are remembered to this day.
