# Buddha: The Enlightened One

In the foothills of the Himalayas, the Sakya clan ruled over the prosperous kingdom of Kapilavastu, governed by King Suddhodana. One night, his chief queen, Mayadevi, had a celestial dream: a magnificent white elephant with six tusks entered her womb from the heavens.

Ten months later, while traveling to her parents' home, Queen Mayadevi passed through the serene Lumbini grove. Feeling the onset of labor beneath a flowering Sala tree, she gave birth to a radiant prince on the full moon night of Vaisakha. 

King Suddhodana rushed to Lumbini and brought the mother and child back to the palace. The aged hermit Asita came to inspect the infant. Looking at the baby's auspicious markings, Asita wept. 

"Why do you weep, holy sage?" Suddhodana asked anxiously.

"I weep because I am old and will not live to hear his great teachings," Asita replied softly. "This boy will either become a chakravartin—a king of kings—or a supreme enlightened saint who will lead mankind out of darkness."

On the fifth day, the prince was named Siddhartha ("He who has achieved his goal"). Seven days after his birth, Queen Mayadevi passed away, and Siddhartha was lovingly raised by her sister, Prajapati.

As Siddhartha grew, King Suddhodana, fearing the prophecy of renunciation, surrounded the prince with every conceivable luxury and pleasure, shielding him from all sights of pain and decay. 

Yet young Siddhartha possessed a deeply compassionate heart. One morning while walking in the royal gardens, a wounded swan fell at his feet, struck by an arrow. Siddhartha gently pulled out the arrow, bathed the bird's wound, and cradled it in his lap. 

Moments later, his cousin Devadatta arrived with his bow. "That is my bird! I shot it; give it to me!"

"I will not," Siddhartha replied quietly. "You tried to take its life, while I saved it."

The dispute was brought before the council of elders, who ruled wisely: "The life of a creature belongs to the one who saves it, not to the one who seeks to destroy it."

To anchor the prince to worldly life, King Suddhodana arranged his marriage to Princess Yashodhara, daughter of the Sakyan noble Dandapani, after Siddhartha demonstrated his unmatched skills in archery and horsemanship. Years of domestic bliss followed, culminating in the birth of their son, Rahula.

However, Siddhartha's inquiring spirit could not be confined within golden walls. One day, he asked his charioteer, Channa, to drive him through the streets of Kapilavastu. On four successive journeys, Siddhartha encountered the Four Sights that altered the course of human history: an old man bent with age, a sick man groaning in agony, a dead body being carried to the cremation ground, and finally, a serene wandering ascetic with a peaceful smile.

"Does everyone grow old, fall ill, and die, Channa?" Siddhartha asked in deep anguish.

"Yes, my Lord," Channa answered. "It is the inevitable fate of all living beings."

That night, looking at his sleeping wife Yashodhara and infant son Rahula, Siddhartha made his great resolve. *I must find a way to end human suffering and death,* he thought. 

Summoning Channa at midnight, Siddhartha mounted his horse Kanthaka and rode out of Kapilavastu into the dark forest. At the edge of the kingdom, he stripped off his royal jewels, cut his long hair with his sword, exchanged his silks for a beggar's coarse robes, and sent Channa back to the palace.

Siddhartha wandered to Rajagriha, the capital of Magadha. Seeing the majestic monk begging for alms, King Bimbisara offered him high rank and half his kingdom. But Siddhartha refused gently: "I seek not earthly kingdoms, O King, but the truth that liberates men from sorrow."

He studied under famous philosophers Alara Kalama and Uddaka Ramaputta, but found their doctrines incomplete. Wandering to the forests of Urubilva near Gaya, Siddhartha joined five ascetics in extreme mortification, reducing his daily food to a single grain of hemp. 

After years of severe fasting, his body became emaciated like a skeleton. One day, while bathing in the Nairanjana river, he collapsed from weakness. Realizing that extreme self-mortification only weakened the mind without revealing truth, Siddhartha accepted a bowl of sweet rice-milk (*kheer*) from Sujata, a village herdsman's daughter. Disgusted by his abandonment of asceticism, the five hermits deserted him.

Restored in strength, Siddhartha walked to a grand Pipal tree (now known as the Bodhi Tree) in Bodh Gaya. Sitting cross-legged beneath its branches, he vowed: *"Let my body wither, let my skin and bones dissolve, but I shall not rise from this seat until I attain Supreme Enlightenment!"*

Through the night, Maras—the forces of desire, fear, and illusion—assailed his mind with terrifying storms and tempting visions. But Siddhartha remained unshaken. At dawn on the full moon of Vaisakha, total awakening dawned upon his mind. Prince Siddhartha became the **Buddha**—the Enlightened One.

Traveling to Deer Park in Sarnath near Banaras, Buddha sought out the five ascetics who had deserted him. Struck by his luminous aura, they fell at his feet and became his first disciples, marking the founding of the Sangha. Buddha preached his First Sermon, expounding the **Four Noble Truths** (Life is suffering; Suffering arises from desire; Suffering can be ended; The path to end suffering is the Eightfold Path).

Returning to Urubilva, Buddha converted the great fire-worshipper Kasshyapa after spending a night unharmed in a sacred fire hut guarded by a venomous serpent. 

When King Bimbisara prepared a grand animal sacrifice in Rajagriha, Buddha walked into the royal court carrying a wounded, lame lamb in his arms. "O King," Buddha spoke softly, "the slaughter of innocent animals cannot bring spiritual merit. All living beings cherish life." Moved to tears, Bimbisara abolished animal sacrifice and gifted the Veluvana bamboo grove to the Sangha.

In Shravasti, a grief-stricken mother named Krisha Gotami brought her dead child to Buddha, begging him to restore his life. Buddha instructed her gently: "Fetch a handful of mustard seeds from a house where no child, parent, or servant has ever died." Krisha Gotami searched every home in the city, only to learn that every family had suffered bereavement. Returning to Buddha, she realized the universal law of impermanence and joined the order of nuns.

Years later, Buddha returned to Kapilavastu. His father King Suddhodana, wife Yashodhara, and son Rahula embraced his Dhamma. Young Rahula was ordained into the Sangha.

Even as his influence spread across India, Buddha faced hostility from his envious cousin Devadatta. Devadatta plotted to assassinate the Master, hurling a giant boulder down a cliff (which split in two, missing Buddha) and releasing the maddened, man-killing elephant Nalagiri in Rajagriha. But as Nalagiri charged, Buddha extended his hand in boundless loving-kindness, and the fierce elephant knelt submissively at his feet.

For forty-five years, Lord Buddha walked the dusty roads of India, teaching kings, peasants, courtesans, and robbers alike. At the age of eighty, surrounded by his weeping disciples in Kushinagar, the Master delivered his final words: *"Decay is inherent in all compounded things. Strive on with diligence."* He then entered Parinirvana, leaving behind a light that continues to illuminate the world.
