# Bhanumati: Granddaughter of Krishna

When Pradyumna, the valiant son of Krishna and Rukmini, returned to Dwaraka after defeating the formidable asura Vajranabha, he brought with him his new bride, Princess Prabhavati. The royal city celebrated with grand festivities, and King Ugrasena organized a sacred sacrifice at Mount Raivataka to invoke divine blessings upon the newly wedded couple.

Early the next morning, Krishna set out for Raivataka accompanied by his family, kinsmen, and retinue. While the elders gathered at the sacrificial grounds, young Princess Bhanumati—the lively granddaughter of Krishna and daughter of Bhanu—stole away into the nearby woods with her playmates.

As Bhanumati ran playfully through the dense foliage, she bumped blindly into a meditating ascetic, knocking over his holy water pot. The hermit was none other than Sage Durvasa, notorious throughout the three worlds for his fiery temper.

"You ill-mannered child!" Durvasa roared in fury. "How dare you interrupt my meditation? For this insolence, you shall be abducted by a demon—the traditional foe of your family!"

Sage Narada, who happened to be passing by, was shocked by the harshness of the curse. "O Durvasa, how can a wise soul like you curse an innocent child for a minor accident?" Narada chided gently.

Regret filled Durvasa's heart, but the words could not be recalled. "A curse once uttered cannot be revoked," Durvasa admitted sorrowfully. "However, I bless her that she shall remain unsullied through her ordeal, marry a virtuous hero, and live as a happy housewife."

Frightened by the encounter, Bhanumati and her companion hurried back to the sacrificial enclosure, resolving to keep the incident secret. *Let us not breathe a word of this to anyone,* Bhanumati thought. *Besides, what the sage said cannot come to pass. Dwaraka, guarded by my grandfather Krishna and my uncles, is impregnable.*

Years rolled by, and Bhanumati grew into a maiden of extraordinary beauty and charm. Her father, Bhanu, planned to host a grand Swayamvara for her upon returning from a pleasure cruise along the coast with Krishna and the Yadava warriors.

Meanwhile, in the demon stronghold of Shatpura, Nikumbha—the brother of the slain Vajranabha—sat brooding in hatred. His spies brought him golden news: "Lord! Krishna and the Yadava warriors have left Dwaraka on a pleasure cruise. Only the aged Ugrasena and Vasudeva remain behind with the young maidens!"

Seeing his opportunity to avenge his brother's death, Nikumbha flew to Dwaraka under cover of darkness. In the secluded royal gardens, as Bhanumati gathered flowers with her maidens, Nikumbha swooped down, seized the princess, and cast a spell of invisibility over them both.

Hearing the maidens' frantic screams, Ugrasena and Vasudeva rushed out with drawn swords, but the abductor had already vanished into thin air.

Word was rushed to Krishna's pleasure barge. Incensed by the outrage, Krishna, Arjuna—who was visiting Dwaraka—and Pradyumna immediately mounted Garuda and set off in fierce pursuit toward Shatpura.

Once far beyond Dwaraka's borders, Nikumbha rendered himself visible again. Bhanumati, recovering from her faint, glared at the demon. "Coward!" she spat. "Do you think you will escape the wrath of my father, my uncles, and my grandfather?"

"Once we reach Shatpura, it is their lives they will have to beg for!" Nikumbha sneered. But his boast turned to dread as a massive shadow covered the sky. Looking up, he saw Garuda soaring above, bearing Krishna and Arjuna, with Pradyumna following swiftly in his flying chariot.

Panic-stricken, Nikumbha held Bhanumati in front of his chest as a human shield. Seeing the demon's ploy, Krishna and Pradyumna held back their maces, but Arjuna drew his bow. With unmatched precision, Arjuna loosed a stream of arrows that pierced Nikumbha's arms and shoulders without brushing a single hair of Bhanumati.

Bhanumati fainted from terror as Nikumbha used dark sorcery to vanish once more. But Pradyumna invoked a mystic spell taught to him by his wife Mayavati, exposing the invisible demon to plain sight. 

Desperate to escape, Nikumbha transformed into a monstrous yellow vulture and took flight toward Mount Gokarna, carrying Bhanumati in his sharp talons. Arjuna relentlessly rained arrows upon the bird until, mortally weakened, the vulture dropped down toward the river below.

Pradyumna swooped down in his chariot, caught the falling princess safely, and bore her away toward Dwaraka, leaving Krishna and Arjuna to finish the demon.

Cornered at the narrow entrance of a cave near Shatpura, Nikumbha unleashed his final illusion, splitting his body into thousands of identical demon forms that swarmed the sky. Unfazed by the trick, Krishna, Arjuna, and Pradyumna invoked their own divine powers, multiplying themselves into thousands of forms to engage every duplicate demon.

Using his divine vision, Krishna pierced through the illusion and identified the true Nikumbha. Raising his right hand, Krishna summoned the glowing Sudarshana Chakra and launched it through the air. The sacred discus severed the demon's head from his body, ending Nikumbha's evil reign forever.

The heroes returned to Dwaraka in triumph. However, Bhanu met them at the palace gates in tears. "The demon is slain, but my daughter was abducted! Her reputation is tarnished—who will marry her now?"

Just then, Sage Narada appeared before the royal assembly. "Do not lament, Bhanu," Narada smiled. "Durvasa's curse has run its course, but his blessing remains. Send an emissary to bring the Pandava prince Sahadeva."

When Sahadeva arrived in Dwaraka, he was wedded to Bhanumati in a grand celestial ceremony. Durvasa's ancient curse yielded its ultimate blessing, as Bhanumati departed for her new home as the cherished queen of the heroic youngest Pandava.
