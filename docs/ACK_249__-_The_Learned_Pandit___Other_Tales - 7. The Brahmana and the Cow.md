# The Brahmana and the Cow

There was once an old Brahmana who owned a beautiful, sprawling garden filled with rare flowers and fruit trees. He loved it dearly and spent an immense amount of time tending to it with his own hands. 

But he was incredibly selfish. He did not want to share his beautiful garden with anyone. Not even the birds! 

"Parrots in my garden! Sitting on my sunflowers! How dare they!" he would shout, waving his arms wildly. "Get away, you wretched birds!"

The mischievous boys of the village liked to tease the grumpy old man. They sat on his garden wall and laughed at him. "Be a sport, sir. Parrots love to eat sunflower seeds. Why did you shoo them away?"

"Besides, you are a Brahmana, sir. You must not be rude to your guests," another boy teased. 

"Guests! Humph! I don't want any pesky birds... or brats in or near my garden," the old man yelled, chasing them off with a stick. "Run off! Off with you!"

And so the days went by. Then, one day, the Brahmana walked out to check on his new saplings. "Let me go and see how tall my mango saplings have grown," he smiled. 

But when he reached the flowerbeds, his jaw dropped. "A stray cow in my garden! And she's eating my saplings!"

In a blind fit of rage, the Brahmana picked up a heavy, wooden stick and began to beat the cow mercilessly. "You wicked beast!" he screamed, striking her again and again. 

The cow was already old, thin, and very hungry. The vicious beating gave her such a terrible fright that she collapsed onto the ground and dropped down, dead. 

The Brahmana dropped his stick in shock. "O God! What have I done? I've killed a cow," he realized in horror. 

Word quickly spread, and the Brahmana had to face the angry villagers. 

"You have committed the greatest of sins!" the village headman scolded him. "You have put your garden above everything else, even the life of a sacred cow. The cow gives us her sweet and nourishing milk, and in return you kill her?"

"What kind of hand is yours that it can kill a cow? We're going now, but we'll see you later," the villagers threatened, leaving the disgraced old man alone. 

"They will throw me out of the village. I'm sure they will. What shall I do?" the Brahmana panicked, pacing nervously. 

Suddenly, an idea struck him. "Ah! I've got it. It was my hand that killed the cow. And Lord Indra is supposed to be the presiding deity of the hand. If it is Indra who guides the hand, it is not I who killed the cow, but he! He is to blame! Yes, I'll tell the villagers that."

So the next day, at the bustling village market, he loudly proclaimed his innocence. "It was not I! It was Indra. It was he who made my hand kill the cow."

This news spread like wildfire, until it reached the heavens and the ears of Lord Indra himself. 

"I'd better assume a disguise and go and see this Brahmana," Indra sighed, stepping down from his celestial throne. 

Taking the form of an old man, Indra walked casually into the Brahmana's garden. 

"Sir, I am a stranger to this town," Indra greeted the Brahmana. "I was just passing this way and couldn't help noticing your garden. It is very beautiful."

The Brahmana was highly flattered. "It is kind of you to say so, sir. I have tended it as if it were my own child."

"I can see that. How well this path has been laid! Did you lay it?"

"Yes, sir. I laid it myself," the Brahmana boasted. 

"What a charming fountain! Did you set it up yourself?"

"Yes, I set it up with my own hands."

"How tall these fruit trees have grown! Did you plant them yourself?" Indra continued. 

"Yes, sir," the Brahmana puffed his chest out proudly. "From seed to fruit, it was all my work."

Suddenly, the old man's disguise vanished in a flash of divine light, and the mighty Lord Indra stood before the trembling Brahmana in all his glory. 

"O Brahmana," Indra thundered. "If you take all the credit for setting up your beautiful garden with your own hands, then you should take the blame for killing the cow with those same hands too! Why blame poor Indra for your own wicked temper?"
