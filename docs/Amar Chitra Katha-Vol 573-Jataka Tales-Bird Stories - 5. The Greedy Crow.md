# The Greedy Crow

A gentle pigeon once made its home in a small basket hanging in the bustling kitchen of a rich merchant of Varanasi. The merchant's cook was a kind man who happily let the pigeon live there. 

One day, a greedy crow flew past the window. "Mm-m! Fish! I must find my way into this kitchen and make it my home," the crow plotted, smelling the delicious food. "But how should I go about it?"

Just then, his eye fell on the pigeon peacefully resting in its basket. As the pigeon flew out in search of grass seeds, the crow quickly followed him. 

After a while, the pigeon noticed him. "Why do you follow me, friend?" 

"I like you," the crow lied smoothly. "I would like to feed with you and be your friend."

"But we do not eat the same food," the pigeon pointed out in confusion. 

"That doesn't matter," the crow insisted. "While you peck grass seeds, I'll look for worms."

"All right. Follow me then, but don't disturb me," the gentle pigeon agreed. 

*I've won the first round!* the crow thought gleefully. 

Towards evening, as the pigeon flew back into the merchant's kitchen, the crow followed him right inside. 

The kind cook looked up in surprise. "Why! Our pigeon has brought a friend! I must hang up another basket for him."

*Second round won!* the crow cheered as he settled into his new, comfortable basket. And so, the crow, too, began to live in the kitchen. 

Then, one evening as the two birds returned home, the master of the house walked into the kitchen. "I am having a grand banquet tomorrow," he ordered the cook. "Clean and cut all this fish tonight."

The crow peered over the edge of his basket, practically drooling at the massive pile of fresh fish. *Look at his mouth water!* the pigeon noticed worriedly. 

*What luck! I've already eaten my fill today. Tomorrow I'll feast—not on worms, but on perfectly fried fish!* the crow planned. 

That night, the crow pretended to be in terrible agony, groaning and clutching his stomach. "Aa-a-ah! Oo-oh!"

"What's the matter with you?" the concerned pigeon asked. 

"I don't know! It must be the worms I ate today! I have a terrible stomachache," the crow lied. 

"Or is it the fish you want to eat tomorrow?" the pigeon asked suspiciously. 

The next morning, the pigeon got ready to leave. "Come on, let's go."

"I'm not coming today," the crow groaned. "You go alone. I have a terrible stomachache."

"Nonsense! It's the fish, isn't it?" the pigeon sighed. "Take my advice. It is highly dangerous to touch men's food. Come, let's go. Up with you!"

"What! And give up what I came here for in the first place! Never!" the crow thought stubbornly. He turned to the pigeon. "I do have a stomachache. You go."

"All right, I'm going. But take care," the pigeon warned, flying out the window. 

As soon as the pigeon flew out, the cook entered and set to work. "These pieces I'll fry... And these I'll put into the curry," the cook muttered. 

*Ah! The best bits are to be fried! I'll settle for fried fish!* the crow watched eagerly. 

When the food was ready, the cook covered the steaming dishes with heavy lids. "I'll go out and rest for a while till the maid comes for the food," he yawned, walking out of the kitchen. 

*It is high time you went out. I cannot wait any longer,* the crow thought impatiently. 

As soon as the cook's back was turned, the crow swooped down from his basket. *I'll take a large piece of fried fish and fly back to my basket. I can eat it there in peace without being found out.*

He landed on the counter and grabbed the handle of the lid with his beak. But the lid was heavier than he expected. As he tried to lift it, the lid slipped. 

*CLANNG! CLANGGG!* The heavy metal lid crashed loudly onto the floor. 

"Oh! Oh! What have I done!" the crow panicked. 

Hearing the crash, the cook immediately turned around and rushed back into the kitchen. "What was that?"

He saw the crow standing over his master's precious banquet. "You thief! Is this how you repay me for sheltering you?" the furious cook roared. 

Before the startled crow could even take wing, the cook pounced on him, grabbing him firmly in his strong hands. 

"I'll pluck you clean and soak you in a mixture of sour buttermilk and burning spices!" the angry cook yelled. And he did exactly that, tossing the plucked, burning bird back into its basket. 

That evening, when the pigeon flew in, he saw the dying crow's horrific plight. 

"Alas, my greedy friend," the pigeon sighed sadly. "If only you had listened to me! That should teach you never to be greedy again!"
