# Hanuman to the Rescue

When Rama, the exiled prince of Ayodhya, attacked Lanka, Ravana, the king of Lanka, himself rode out one day to the battlefield. In the course of the day's battle, he hurled his spear at Rama's brother, Lakshmana. 

Even as Lakshmana fell, Rama came forward and drove away the mighty Rakshasa. 

Rama knelt by his fallen brother in grief. "Oh, my noble brother... where will I find another like you? Lakshmana! Lakshmana!" 

"Save him, Sushena. You are a great physician," Rama pleaded. 

"Life has not left him as yet. I could easily revive him with the herb called Vishalya Karani," Sushena replied. "But I must get it before daybreak." 

"Where is this herb to be found?" 

"It grows on either side of the river which flows down one of the peaks of Mount Gandhamadana. But it would take eighteen years to go there and come back!" 

"I could do it in much less than eighteen hours," Hanuman volunteered. 

"Go, Hanuman. Look for a golden creeper with blue flowers," Sushena instructed. "Cast aside your anxieties, Lord Rama. Lakshmana will be saved." 

Then Hanuman, the son of the wind, made himself larger and larger, and with one great leap rose high up into the sky. 

As he flew along, supported by the wind, Ravana saw him from Lanka. "Where does Hanuman go at this hour? Ah! The Vishalya Karani! The only herb that can save Lakshmana!" 

Ravana immediately summoned his uncle, Kalanemi. Kalanemi was a fearful monster with four heads, eight eyes and eight arms. He was also a magician. 

"Yes, uncle Kalanemi, I need your help. Go to Mount Gandhamadana and destroy Hanuman with your magic. If Lakshmana does not inhale the fumes of the herb before daybreak, he will die and then Rama too will die of grief," Ravana ordered. 

Kalanemi was reluctant. "Oh what use would that be to a dead man? For I shall surely meet my death at his hands if I go after him. No, Ravana! I cannot go to Gandhamadana. The son of the wind is cunning. He'll dash out my brains." 

"Go at once!" Ravana roared. 

"What a predicament. I am dead if I go and dead if I don't," Kalanemi whimpered, "Y-yes." 

Kalanemi used his magic to transport himself to Mount Gandhamadana. There he transformed himself into a hermit, created a beautiful hermitage out of thin air, and sat down outside it. 

When Hanuman arrived, he greeted the hermit. "I am in great haste, sir. I have no time for food and drink. I am looking for a herb called Vishalya Karani." 

"Go and bathe in that river, my child and then you can refresh yourself on fruits," the disguised Kalanemi suggested smoothly. "If one fails to persuade his guest to eat, he is sure to be doomed." 

"All right. I'll do as you say," Hanuman agreed. 

"The crocodile will tear him to pieces... and I shall go to Ravana and claim the reward," Kalanemi thought wickedly as Hanuman waded into the river. 

The crocodile saw him from afar, moved forward silently, and lunged forward. But Hanuman saw it approaching. Caught in its jaws, Hanuman wrestled with the beast, pulled it out of the water, and flung it to the shore. Then he pounced on it and tore open its breast. 

As he stepped away from its carcass, a beautiful maiden appeared from the body. "I am a celestial dancer. Once when I was dancing, my foot accidentally touched the person of Indra. He was so enraged that he turned me into a crocodile... and decreed that I should live in this river till I was liberated by you." 

"Beware of the false hermit, O son of the wind!" she warned him before vanishing. 

Hanuman rushed back to the false hermit. "You're back!" Kalanemi stammered. 

"I'll do what the crocodile couldn't. Throw aside your fruit, false one... and prepare for your death," Hanuman growled. 

The earth shook as the combatants wrestled with one another. Hanuman flung his adversary to the ground, coiled his tail round him, and hurled him into the sky. The Rakshasa shot through the heavens like a comet... and landed right back in Lanka, at Ravana's court! 

Ravana was furious. "It's Kalanemi! He has failed in his mission." Ravana then commanded the Sun God to rise before the appointed hour. 

Some time later, Hanuman, who was searching frantically for the herb, was startled to see a rosy glow on the horizon. "What! Is the sun ready to rise? I must stop him at once!" 

The Sun God's charioteer was startled to see a huge monkey dropping down from the sky and barring the road to the east. Hanuman darted forward, caught hold of the chariot, and whirled it round and round. "I shall burn him and fling him down to earth," the Sun God warned. 

"Listen, O Deva, I am the son of the wind. I have come to get a herb which could save Lakshmana's life," Hanuman pleaded. "Do not rise before Lakshmana is restored to health, I beg you." 

"My sympathies are with Rama, O son of the wind. But how can I save myself from Ravana's wrath if I disobey him?" the Sun God asked. 

"I'll tell you how. Come closer..." Hanuman said. Then, Hanuman caught him in a warm embrace, and before he knew what was happening, bundled him under his arm! 

Hanuman rushed back to Gandhamadana. "Not much time left. Sushena said it's a golden creeper with blue flowers... but I can't see any such plant here." 

As he wandered, he came across some Gandharvas. They teased him and pulled his hair. Hanuman was enraged. He made himself as huge as a hill and struck back at his tormentors. Seven huge lion-faced Rakshasas joined the fight, but Hanuman coiled his tail around them and flung them down. 

Pandemonium broke out among the Gandharvas until Hanuman suddenly remembered his mission. "I can't waste time fighting these people. Rama and the others must be waiting anxiously for my return." 

"Well, if I can't find the herb... I'll take the whole mountain with me," Hanuman decided. 

He dug his hands under the mountain, uprooted it, and lifted it above his head. With a mighty leap, he soared into the sky, carrying the entire mountain. 

Rama and everyone else were amazed to see Hanuman descending with a huge mountain. Later, when he had lowered his load, Sushena himself went up with him and gathered the plants he wanted. 

The physician made a paste out of the herbs and held it to Lakshmana's nose. As the fumes entered his nostrils, Lakshmana stirred. 

"Lakshmana is safe. Victory to Rama!" the army cheered. 

"I am indebted to you, O son of the wind. Now please take the mountain back," Rama said gently. 

"I'll do that at once," Hanuman replied, picking up Mount Gandhamadana and setting off across the sky to restore it to its original place. He even healed the Gandharvas he had wounded during their battle. 

Then Hanuman sped back to Lanka. "What is that shining under your arm, son of the wind?" Rama asked when he returned. 

"Oh, that is the sun. Ravana had ordered him to rise before the appointed hour. So I had to hold him prisoner," Hanuman smiled. 

"How extraordinary! Let him go at once," Rama marvelled. 

Hanuman lifted his arm and let the Sun go. They all paid homage to the Sun God, and he departed to rise from his usual place in the east. 

"You have saved Lakshmana's life, Hanuman. I am ever indebted to you," Rama said, embracing the great monkey hero.
