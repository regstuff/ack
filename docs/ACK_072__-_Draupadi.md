# Draupadi: The Queen of the Pandavas

King Drupada of Panchala performed a grand *yagna* (fire sacrifice) to obtain a mighty son who could defeat his enemies. As the flames of the sacrificial fire leapt high, an excellent, handsome, and armor-clad youth sprang from the fire. "Let the boy be called Dhrishtadyumna," the Brahmans declared. 

But then, to the amazement of all, there arose from the same fire a breathtakingly beautiful, dark-skinned maiden, wafting the fragrance of a blue lotus in bloom. 

"This girl is an exceptional woman," a celestial voice echoed. "She will bring about the destruction of the Kauravas." 

She was named Krishnaa, but the world would come to know her as Draupadi, the daughter of Drupada. 

From the moment she was born, one thought obsessed King Drupada: her marriage. *Arjuna, the valiant Pandava, would have been an ideal husband for my daughter,* the King thought sadly. *If only he, his mother, and his brothers had survived that fateful fire at Varanavata.*

Hoping to find a peerless archer, Drupada had a heavy, massive bow crafted. "I shall have a heavy bow made which only Arjuna or his peer can wield," he declared. "Then a target to challenge an ace archer like him. He who hits the mark shall wed my sister," his son Dhrishtadyumna announced.

Unbeknownst to Drupada, the Pandavas and their mother Kunti had survived the fire and were living in hiding at Ekachakrapura, disguised as humble Brahmans. Hearing of the grand *swayamvara* (marriage contest), the brothers were eager to attend.

On their way to Panchala, the Pandavas met the great Sage Vyasa. He told them a secret of Draupadi's past. "In an earlier birth, she was the daughter of a rishi. Fated to remain unmarried, she performed severe penances to Lord Shiva. When Shiva appeared, she was so overwhelmed with eagerness that she repeated her request five times: 'An accomplished husband... an accomplished husband...'"

Vyasa smiled. "Shiva granted her wish: 'So be it. You shall have five husbands from among the Bharata princes.' When the girl was confounded, Shiva explained that his words could not be revoked, but the event would be postponed to her next life. You, the five Pandavas, are the five husbands Draupadi is destined to have."

At the *swayamvara*, princes and kings from all over India tried and failed to string the massive bow. Even the great warrior Karna stepped forward. *It's Karna! We may consider the mark as already hit,* the Pandavas thought in dismay. But as Karna strung the bow and took aim, Draupadi stood up gracefully.

"I will never marry one of the *suta* (charioteer) caste!" she announced firmly. Insulted, Karna threw down the bow and walked away.

Finally, Arjuna, dressed as a humble Brahman, stepped forward. With noble confidence, he effortlessly strung the bow and hit the target. Full of joy, Draupadi stepped forward and garlanded the handsome Brahman.

The brothers returned to the potter's house where they were staying. "Look mother! See what we have brought today!" they called out from the door.

Kunti, busy with her prayers and thinking they had brought alms, replied without looking. "Whatever it is, share it equally and enjoy it."

When she came out and saw Draupadi, she gasped. "Alas! Yudhishthira, what have I said? Arjuna won her. It is proper that he marry her."

"No, mother. And commit the sin of ignoring my mother's orders?" Yudhishthira insisted. Knowing it was destined by the gods, he declared, "Then Draupadi shall be a wife to all of us."

When the news reached Hastinapura that the Pandavas were alive and allied with the powerful King Drupada, the Kuru elders advised King Dhritarashtra to be just. He invited the Pandavas back and granted them half the kingdom. They built a glorious new capital at Khandavaprastha (Indraprastha) and ruled virtuously.

But Duryodhana, burning with envy, plotted with his wicked uncle, Shakuni. "Yudhishthira is an addict to the game of dice, and I am a master at cheating," Shakuni smiled. "We will invite him, and we will win."

Bound by royal honor, Yudhishthira accepted the challenge. As Shakuni rolled his loaded dice, Yudhishthira lost his wealth, his kingdom, his brothers, and finally himself.

"O King, there is still one dear stake you hold on to," Shakuni sneered. "Draupadi. Stake her and win yourself back."

In a tragic lapse of judgment, Yudhishthira agreed, and lost again. A jubilant Duryodhana sent a messenger to fetch Draupadi. When she refused to come, realizing the illegality of a slave staking his wife, Duryodhana ordered his brutal brother Duhshasana to drag her in.

Duhshasana broke into her chamber, grabbed her by her long, beautiful hair, and dragged the weeping Queen into the royal assembly. 

"The Kuru elders have lost their reason!" Draupadi challenged the silent kings. "Why else do they look silently on this great crime?" She looked desperately at her husbands, but Yudhishthira cast his eyes down in shame. 

"The very clothes she is wearing belong to us! She is our slave," Duhshasana laughed, moving to strip her of her sari.

"O Krishna, save me!" Draupadi prayed, closing her eyes.

A miracle occurred. As Duhshasana pulled at her sari, endless yards of exquisite silk materialized, constantly keeping her covered. Frustrated and exhausted by the miles of cloth, Duhshasana finally collapsed. 

Quivering with rage, Bheema swore a terrifying oath. "I shall, in the battlefield, tear open the breast of this villain... and drink his life blood!"

Frightened by evil omens that suddenly echoed through the hall, Dhritarashtra intervened. He granted Draupadi boons, allowing her to free her husbands from slavery. But the cruel game of dice resumed. The Pandavas lost again, and were sentenced to twelve years of exile in the forest, followed by a thirteenth year in disguise.

Before leaving, an enraged Draupadi vowed, "Thirteen years hence, the wives of those villains will languish in desolate widowhood! I will know no peace till his hands are ripped off."

Life in the forest was harsh. To aid Draupadi in feeding the many sages who visited them, the Sun God Surya gave her the *Akshaya Patra*—a magical vessel that provided inexhaustible food until Draupadi herself had eaten.

Duryodhana, seeking to humiliate them further, intentionally sent the incredibly ill-tempered Sage Durvasa and his ten thousand disciples to visit the Pandavas *after* Draupadi had finished her meal. Knowing she had no food left, Draupadi prayed to Lord Krishna.

Krishna suddenly appeared. "Draupadi, I am hungry. Fetch the vessel." He found a single grain of rice and a scrap of vegetable stuck to the side, and ate it. "May the soul of the universe be satiated with it."

Instantly, down at the river, Sage Durvasa and his ten thousand disciples suddenly felt painfully full, as if they had eaten a massive feast. Embarrassed that they could not eat the meal they requested, they quietly fled the forest, and Draupadi was saved from the sage's curse.

For their thirteenth year in disguise, the Pandavas and Draupadi served in the court of King Virata. Draupadi worked as a maid, *Sairandhri*, to Queen Sudheshna. 

Ten months passed peacefully until Keechaka, the Queen's powerful and arrogant brother, became smitten with Draupadi. Despite her warnings that she was protected by invisible Gandharvas, he continually harassed her. When the Queen sent her to his room for wine, he chased her and kicked her brutally in the royal court.

That night, a weeping Draupadi went to Bheema. "I will kill Keechaka this very night," Bheema promised, his eyes blazing. "Agree to meet him tonight in the dark, empty dance-hall."

When the lustful Keechaka entered the dark hall expecting Draupadi, he was met by the crushing grip of Bheema, who silently and brutally ended his life.

The fourteen years of exile and disguise finally ended. When Duryodhana refused to return even five villages to the Pandavas, the catastrophic Mahabharata war was declared. 

For eighteen bloody days, the battle raged. Bheema fulfilled his terrible oath, cornering Duhshasana, breaking his limbs for dragging Draupadi by the hair, and drinking his blood. 

On the final day, Bheema faced Duryodhana in a mace duel. As the fight dragged on, Krishna subtly slapped his own thigh. Bheema caught the cue. Remembering the day Duryodhana had insultingly slapped his thigh and invited Draupadi to sit on it, Bheema swung his mace low, shattering Duryodhana's thighs and winning the war.

Their oaths fulfilled and their honor restored, the victorious Pandavas blew their conches. Yudhishthira was crowned Emperor, and Draupadi, her flowing hair finally tied, reigned by his side as the cherished and powerful Queen.
