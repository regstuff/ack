# Kanwal and Kehar

Mahmud Shah, the Sultan of Gujarat, was being entertained by a group of dancers from Marwar. Among them, a dancer in a red dress caught his eye. "She is so agile and graceful. I must speak to her after the dance is over," he thought. 

Later, he approached her. "Your beauty bewitches me. Will you consent to be mine?" 

"No, I cannot! I cannot!" the dancer replied, rushing back to her mother, Jawahar. 

"How dare you refuse an offer from the King of Gujarat? Have you gone mad?" her mother scolded. 

"You know, mother, I'm betrothed to Kehar," she replied firmly. She was Kanwal, the daughter of the famous dancer Jawahar. 

When Jawahar conveyed this to Mahmud Shah, he was furious. "Kehar! He's just a petty chieftain under Mahmud Shah's command," the Sultan mocked. 

"My Kehar is a lion among men. Mahmud Shah is just a jackal," Kanwal retorted proudly. 

"You called me a jackal! You'll regret this. I'll throw your lion in a cage, bound in chains," Mahmud Shah threatened, placing Kanwal under the watch of his guard, Falooda Khan. 

The next day, Mahmud Shah challenged his men to capture Kehar. Jalal, a chieftain, accepted the challenge. He devised a plan with Arabkhan, an ace swimmer. 

At a big mela on the banks of the river Sabarmati, Jalal invited Kehar for water games. Arabkhan tried to drown Kehar by dragging him under the water. But Kehar overpowered him, giving him a severe beating before dragging him out of the water. Jalal had to pretend it was all a game, but Kehar suspected treachery. 

The next morning, Kehar asked Mahmud Shah for permission to return to his jagir. "Stay a little longer, Kehar! Go after Holi, next week," the Sultan insisted, secretly hatching another plan. 

On Holi day, Kehar arrived with his friends Khetji and Sangji at Mahmud Shah's palace. They were led into an enclosed courtyard and asked to remove their weapons. Suddenly, Mahmud Shah's men attacked. Kehar was outnumbered, bound in iron chains, and thrown into a cage. His friends managed to escape. 

Mahmud Shah placed the cage in Kanwal's courtyard. "Come and have a look at your lion! Is he worth pining over? Ask Kanwal to be mine, Kehar, and I will free you," he taunted. 

"You cheat! I will kill you just as Bheema killed Jarasandha," Kehar roared. 

Refusing food and drink, Kehar languished in the cage. Kanwal brought him delicacies, but he ignored her. Desperate, Kanwal sent a letter to his friends, Khetji and Sangji. 

They sent a reply: "O brave Rajput! ... How will you fulfil your vow of killing Mahmud if you starve? Rouse yourself, recall all your fighting skills and escape!" 

Kanwal read the letter to Kehar. "How right they are. Let's eat the food you've brought, Kanwal," he finally agreed. 

They planned an escape for the next new moon night. Kanwal's maid, Tuna, drugged the wine of the guard, Falooda Khan. When he collapsed, Kanwal gave Kehar a file and a dagger. Kehar cut his chains, broke the lock, and emerged from the cage. He killed the awakening Falooda Khan and escaped over the fort walls using the terrace. 

Kehar reached his home and met his friends. They decided it was too dangerous to stay in Gujarat. "Let's leave at once for Mewar across the border," Sangji suggested. 

They reached Bethun, a border town in Mewar, and sought refuge with the village chief, Gango, a Bhil. "I place both myself and the Bhils of my sixty villages at your service," Gango offered generously. 

With Sangji, Khetji, and Gango's men, Kehar began attacking royal posts across the border in Gujarat. He looted the rich and distributed the wealth among the poor. 

Mahmud Shah, enraged, sent Jalal to capture Kehar. When Jalal arrived in Baria, Kehar devised a plan. He accepted a meal invitation from Bhoonga, a cunning man who informed Jalal's soldiers. 

When the soldiers arrived at Bhoonga's house, Kehar charged out the backdoor, mounted Jalal's own horse, beheaded Jalal in a swift stroke, and sped away. 

Mahmud Shah proclaimed a huge reward for capturing Kehar. Meanwhile, Kanwal hatched a plan. She sent a message to Kehar through her maidservant: "Moonghda's marriage party will be leaving from Ajmer for Ahmedabad. Join it and come here." 

Kehar disguised himself as a Sadhu, accompanied by four armed men, and joined the marriage procession, safely entering Ahmedabad. 

Kanwal then changed her strategy with Mahmud Shah. "Sir, I am tired of waiting for Kehar... I am willing to marry you," she told him. "But I have a few conditions. I wish to be married on the same day as Moonghda... Secondly, I wish to be led to the altar from the Great Dome, amid noisy fireworks... And thirdly, my mother should be permitted to come right up to the Great Dome in a palanquin." 

Mahmud Shah eagerly agreed to all conditions. At night, fully armed, Kehar got into the palanquin at Kanwal's mother's house and was carried into the women's quarters at the Great Dome. 

As the deafening fireworks and blinding celebrations began, Mahmud Shah walked into the bride's quarters, expecting Kanwal. 

"Mahmud!" a voice called out. It was Kehar. 

"Here we are, face to face at last. Let's see now who's the lion and who's the jackal," Kehar challenged. 

They unsheathed their swords and fought bravely. Since they were well matched, they discarded their weapons and fought barehanded. Finally, Kehar overpowered Mahmud Shah and killed him. The noise of the fireworks completely masked the sounds of their struggle. 

"Kanwal, you are mine now! Let's go," Kehar said. 

Both got into the palanquin, which was brought back to Kanwal's mother's place. From there, the pair mounted horses kept ready and rode off into the night towards Bethun, free at last.
