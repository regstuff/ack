# Ashtavakra

Kahoda was the favourite disciple of Uddalaka, a renowned scholar in the Vedas. Uddalaka was so pleased with Kahoda's devotion and hard work that he offered him his daughter's hand in marriage. "You shall have my daughter, Sujata, for a wife," Uddalaka promised. 

Kahoda and Sujata were married and lived happily in Sage Uddalaka's hermitage. A few months later, Sujata became pregnant. 

"I shall spare no pains to teach him all I know," Kahoda vowed. "I must expose my child to the sacred influence of the hymns even while he is in the womb." 

So Sujata sat by her husband as he recited verses from the scriptures, and the child in her womb silently absorbed what the learned father recited. 

One day as Kahoda sat among his disciples, instructing them, a voice suddenly interrupted him. "That's not the way to chant that hymn, father." 

Kahoda was stunned. "Who was that? Where did that voice come from?" 

"It must have been the voice of our unborn child," Sujata realized. 

Furious with his son for putting him to shame before his disciples, Kahoda cursed him. "Such impertinence! And from one yet unborn! O vain soul! May you be born deformed—may your body be twisted in eight places!" 

The months rolled on, and the time for Sujata's confinement drew near. Knowing they would need money to provide for the child, Kahoda made a decision. "I will go to King Janaka of Mithila. He is generous to learned Brahmans. Besides, he is at the moment performing a grand yagna." 

When Kahoda reached Mithila, Janaka welcomed him but gave him a warning. "A scholar named Bandhi has come to Mithila. No one can defeat him in debate. Think it over carefully. Though you will be well rewarded if you win... you will be drowned in the river if you don't." 

"Let me try, O King. I accept the challenge," Kahoda insisted. 

The debate began, but it did not continue for long. Kahoda was no match for the brilliant Bandhi. 

"Bandhi has won again," the crowd whispered. "Poor Kahoda will be drowned like all the others." 

When the tragic news reached Sujata, she broke down. "Alas! My desire for wealth has cost me my husband and my child its father." 

A few days later, Sujata had a baby. Because his body was deformed in eight places due to the curse, he was named Ashtavakra. Around the same time, Uddalaka's wife was also blessed with a son, whom they named Shwetaketu. 

Growing up under the tender, loving care of Uddalaka, Ashtavakra soon began to look upon his grandfather as his father. The years rolled on, and Uddalaka taught Ashtavakra and Shwetaketu everything he knew. When the boys were twelve years old, Uddalaka proudly told Ashtavakra, "You have mastered the Vedas. I am proud of you, my son." 

But one day, during a minor squabble, Shwetaketu snapped at Ashtavakra, "He is not your father." 

Deeply humiliated and confused, Ashtavakra ran to his mother. "Mother, where is my father? Oh! Why do you weep? Tell me!" 

"Your father is dead," Sujata finally confessed, and she told him all that had happened in Mithila with Bandhi. 

Ashtavakra's eyes blazed with determination. "I must defeat Bandhi and have him drowned." 

He convinced Shwetaketu to go with him to Mithila. "King Janaka has not been able to complete the yagna he started twelve years ago," Ashtavakra explained. 

When the boys reached the gates of Janaka's palace, the guards stopped them. "The king is performing a yagna. I have been ordered to permit only old learned Brahmans to see him," the guard said, looking at the two twelve-year-olds. 

"To possess true knowledge one need not be grey-haired or bald," Ashtavakra retorted sharply. "It is wrong to judge a man's merit by his age. I may be young but I have observed the sacred vows and have mastered the Vedas." 

The guards were impressed by his intelligence and took him to the King. Ashtavakra immediately issued his challenge. "O King, I have heard of Bandhi's challenge and the fate of those who failed to meet it. I have come to challenge Bandhi and to see him destroyed." 

Janaka was astonished. "You speak of defeating Bandhi because you are ignorant of his skill in debate. No one has been able to defeat him." Seeing that Ashtavakra was adamant, the King tested him with a few riddles. 

"What is it that does not move when born?" the King asked. 
"An egg," Ashtavakra replied instantly. 
"Which animal does not close its eyes while it is asleep?" 
"The fish." 

Impressed by the bold, prompt answers, the King summoned Bandhi. The debate began. But in no time, Ashtavakra had him in a tight corner, and Bandhi had to admit defeat. 

"Bandhi has met his match," the King announced. "Let Ashtavakra decide his fate." 

"Bandhi had those whom he defeated thrown into the river. Let him meet with the same fate today. Drown him! Seize him!" Ashtavakra commanded. 

Bandhi calmly stepped forward. "O King! I am the son of Varuna, the Lord of the Hydrosphere. The sages I defeated are not dead. My father was performing a yagna for which he needed sages from the Earth. The challenge was a means of sending them to him. The yagna must be over by now and they should return. Let us go to the river." 

At the river, Bandhi jumped into the water. Then, as the crowd watched in amazement, one by one, the sages emerged from the water. 

As Kahoda came out, he saw his son. "Ashtavakra, my son!" he cried, rushing to embrace him. 

Ashtavakra fell at the feet of his father. "My blessings on you, Ashtavakra," Kahoda said, beaming with pride. "We regard Ashtavakra as the most brilliant scholar among us. Sons have often been known to excel fathers in achievements." 

Then, Kahoda turned to his son. "Ashtavakra, take a quick plunge into the river and come out." 

Ashtavakra did as he was told. A second later, as he emerged from the water, a miracle happened. "Mother! Father! Look! My body! It's no longer deformed!" he shouted joyfully. His twisted limbs had become straight and tall. 

They all returned to Uddalaka, who rejoiced to see his grandson cured. The hermitage once again became the haven of peace it used to be.
