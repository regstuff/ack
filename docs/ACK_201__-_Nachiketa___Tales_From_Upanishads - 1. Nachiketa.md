# Nachiketa

Thousands of years ago, there lived a sage named Vajashrava. His young son, Nachiketa, was deeply curious about the world. Nachiketa would watch his father perform sacred *yajnas* (fire sacrifices) with wide eyes. 

"Father, when will you let me worship the sacred fire?" little Nachiketa asked. 

"Soon after your initiation, son," his father smiled. 

Nachiketa was sent to a *gurukula*, a forest school, where he learned to recite sacred hymns and tend to his guru's cattle. But as he grew older, the boy began to notice the suffering in the world. He watched old, weak cows struggle to graze, and he wept when a beautiful deer he had befriended suddenly dropped dead. 

"Why is he so lazy today?" Nachiketa asked his friend.

"He is dead, Nachiketa. There is no life left in him," his friend replied. 

"But death is not the end. There is life after death," Nachiketa insisted. 

"That's a lie," his friend argued. "Death is the end."

Troubled by this debate, Nachiketa asked his guru. "It is a difficult question, my son," the wise guru said. "Each one must discover the truth for himself."

Soon after, Vajashrava summoned his son home to witness the great *Vishvajit* sacrifice. "It is one of the greatest sacrifices," a fellow student explained to Nachiketa. "For the one who performs it gives away absolutely everything he possesses."

When Nachiketa arrived, he was shocked by what he saw. His father was giving away sick, old, and dying cows as charity to the visiting Brahmans. *What a poor offering this is!* Nachiketa thought. *By giving away these pathetic cows, father will surely go to the dark world of the joyless.*

Desperate to save his father's soul, Nachiketa stepped forward. "But what can my poor father do? This is all he possesses!" he realized. *But I, too, belong to my father. I am hale and hearty. By offering myself, I can save my father.*

"To whom will you give me, father?" Nachiketa asked aloud. 

Vajashrava ignored him, irritated by the interruption. Nachiketa asked again. And again. 

"He is trying my patience!" Vajashrava exploded in anger. "I will give you to Yama, the God of Death!"

The sage immediately regretted his angry words, but Nachiketa calmly accepted his fate. "Father, do not grieve. Men of old have passed away, and those of days to come will also pass away. Let me go to the abode of death with your blessing."

Nachiketa travelled to the dark realm of Yama. But the God of Death was not home. For three days and three nights, the young boy waited patiently at the gates without a single drop of water or morsel of food. 

When Yama finally returned, he was horrified to find a Brahman boy starved at his doorstep. "Hope, expectations, sacrifices, and good deeds are all snatched away from him who does not offer hospitality to a Brahmana," Yama panicked. "Since you have come as a guest and received no hospitality for three days, you may choose three boons to atone for my sin."

"For my first boon," Nachiketa said, "Grant my father peace of mind and make him cheerful. Let him greet me happily when I return."

"He shall have it," Yama agreed. 

"For my second boon, teach me the sacred fire sacrifice by which no hunger or thirst is felt in heaven."

"I will do so gladly," Yama smiled, teaching him the complex ritual. "Henceforth, such a sacrifice will be known as the Nachiketa Sacrifice."

"For my third boon," Nachiketa said firmly, "Some people believe there is life after death; others say there isn't. Tell me who is right. Instruct me in the mysteries of life after death."

Yama became deeply thoughtful. "Even the gods are not certain about this. It is not easily understood. Ask for something else! Ask for cattle, elephants, gold, an empire, long life... look at these lovely celestial maidens! I will give you anything, but do not ask me about life after death."

"I am not interested in things that bring momentary happiness," Nachiketa replied with steadfast faith. "I want that which will bring everlasting bliss. I do not choose any other boon."

Impressed by the boy's immunity to temptation, Yama finally relented. "Two paths are open to men. The wise take the good path and realise the Self. The foolish choose the path of pleasure and go through the never-ending cycle of birth and death."

Yama began to teach Nachiketa the great secret of the universe. "When a man is dead, it is the body that perishes, not the Self. The Self is never born, nor does it die."

"But aren't the body and the Self the same?" Nachiketa asked. 

"No, they are not. The Self resides in the body as a master does in his chariot," Yama explained, pointing to a grand chariot. "The intellect is the driver. The mind is the reins. And the sense organs—the eyes, ears, nose, tongue, and skin—are like the horses that pull the chariot."

"Men are pulled in different directions by their sense organs," Yama continued. "Just as horses are held in check by the reins, so also the sense organs are held in check by the mind. The man who cannot control his senses won't reach his goal. But when all desires have been given up, and the mind is controlled, man becomes immortal, and realizes Brahman—the ultimate truth."

Armed with the supreme knowledge of the universe, Nachiketa thanked Yama, returned to the land of the living, and was joyously reunited with his father. As he grew older, his renown spread, and many seekers of truth came to his hermitage for guidance.
