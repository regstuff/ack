# Jnaneshwar

One morning, towards the end of the thirteenth century, SiddhOPant Kulkarni, the chief officer of Alandi, saw a young boy deep in meditation on the bank of the river Indrayani. 

"I am Vitthal, the son of Govind Pant and Nirabai of Apegaon. I left home on a pilgrimage seven years ago," the boy explained when he opened his eyes. 

SiddhOPant took young Vitthal home. That night, Lord Panduranga appeared to SiddhOPant in a dream. "Give Rukmini to Vitthal. Their children will be great spiritual teachers." 

The marriage of Vitthal and Rukmini was celebrated with great pomp. For some years, Vitthal led a happy life. But then tragedy struck; both his parents died in quick succession. SiddhOPant took them to Alandi to help Vitthal overcome his grief. 

However, Vitthal felt restless. "I have no peace of mind. Permit me to take up Sanyasa," he pleaded with Rukmini repeatedly. But she would not agree. 

One day, Vitthal went out to wash his hands... and did not return. He had gone to Kashi. There, he met Swami Ramananda. "I have neither parents nor wife. Please initiate me into the path of Sanyasa," he lied. Ramananda initiated him, calling him Chaitanya Swami. 

Months went by. One day, Swami Ramananda visited Alandi. Rukmini went to make obeisance to him. "Arise, daughter. May you be blessed with many children who will guide mankind," he blessed her. 

"Why child! What's the matter?" he asked when she wept. 

"My husband has deserted me. Vitthal of Alandi? Your husband? But..." Ramananda remembered his first meeting with Vitthal Pant and realized he had been deceived. 

When Ramananda returned to his hermitage at Kashi, he confronted Chaitanya Swami. "My words cannot prove false. Rest assured, daughter. Your husband will come back to you," he had promised Rukmini. He ordered Vitthal, "Go back to Alandi and lead the life of a householder." 

Vitthal obeyed, but the orthodox Brahmans of Alandi took a different view. "Once having renounced the world, a man cannot return to his family. Vitthal and his wife must be excommunicated." 

Vitthal and Rukmini had to leave the village. They lived in a small hut near the river bank. Four children were born to them: Nivritti, Jnanadev, Sopan, and Mukta. 

From a very young age, little Jnanadev took a keen interest in the Bhagavad Gita. But Vitthal was worried. "My children are so bright and religious. Yet I cannot perform their thread ceremonies so long as we remain excommunicated." 

He approached the Brahmans. "Please take us back into the fold." 

"We don't have the authority to do that. Please go to Paithan and obtain the sanction of the learned scholars there," they replied. 

The family went to Tryambak. There, a tiger attacked them. Nivritti was separated and lost his way. He found a cave and entered it, meeting a Sadhu, Gahininath. The holy one initiated him into the Natha cult. Nivritti then found his way back to his family and, in a secluded spot, initiated his brother Jnanadev. 

Soon after, they all went to Paithan, where Vitthal pleaded with the Brahman scholars. "If he is Jnanadev (God of Knowledge), where is the need for him to learn?" a scholar mocked. 

Just then, a village water-man prodded his buffalo past them. "Faster Jnana, faster." 

"What is the difference between your Jnanadev and that buffalo, Jnana?" a Brahman asked. 

"There is no difference at all. As the assembly of Brahmans burst into laughter, Jnanadev's soft voice was heard. All are equal." 

"Wait! You say there is no difference between us and that buffalo. Can that buffalo recite the Vedas?" 

Jnanadev walked up to the buffalo, placed his hand on its back, and asked it to recite. To the utter amazement of the gathering, the buffalo recited the Vedas in a deep, resonant voice. 

The Brahmans were overwhelmed with awe. "We are grateful to you. But we propose to live as ascetics without the sacred thread. Then allow us to give you a letter of commendation at least." 

As the party proceeded to Pandharpur, the buffalo followed them. On their way, they stopped at Nevase. A funeral procession passed by. A woman fell at Jnanadev's feet. "May you enjoy long life with your husband and children," he blessed her. 

"Sir, my husband is dead. I am on my way to become a Sati," she cried. 

"He who made me utter those blessings would surely breathe life into your husband," Jnanadev said. He told the pall-bearers to place the body on the ground. "Rise, O Satchidananda!" 

The dead man came back to life! "As the Gita tells you, the soul is immortal... Jnanoba, explain the Gita in Marathi. Let the multitude benefit," Nivritti commanded. 

Jnanadev began to explain the Bhagavad-Gita in chaste and simple Marathi, later known as the 'Jnaneshwari'. 

The only one who was blind to his greatness was a Brahman named Visoba. When Mukta went to buy an earthenware pan to make sweet pancakes, Visoba broke every pan in the shop to prevent her. 

Mukta ran home weeping. "Why do you cry? Fry the pancakes on my back," Jnanadev offered. The pancakes began sizzling. "NO! IMPOSSIBLE! IT IS COOKED! HE IS A GREAT SOUL!" Visoba, who was secretly watching, realized. He became Jnanadev's disciple. 

One day, Yogi Changdev, riding on a tiger, came to meet them. Jnanadev, his brothers and sister were sitting on a wall. "Look! It's Yogi Changdev on a tiger! He's coming to meet us! Let's welcome him. Wait! You don't have to get down. Move wall, move forward! Towards the sage!" Jnanadev commanded. 

The lifeless wall moved! Changdev was wonderstruck, jumped off the tiger, and sought Jnanadev's teachings. 

A few months later, the siblings travelled to Kashi. Mudgalacharya was performing a sacrifice and an elephant was to choose the most worthy man to be honoured. The elephant chose Jnanadev. 

The Brahmans grumbled. Namdev, a great saint accompanying them, intervened. "Let's go to the temple then. The one from whose hand Lord Vishweshwara will eat the purodasha is the worthiest." 

At the temple, Jnanadev prayed and offered the purodasha. The next moment, it disappeared! The Lord had accepted it. Jnanadev was taken in procession to the sacrificial site and offered the seat of honour. 

On his return to Alandi, at the tender age of twenty-one, Jnanadev attained the state of blissful consciousness and withdrew from the world, leaving behind a legacy as the founder of the Bhakti movement in Maharashtra.
