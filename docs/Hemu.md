# Hemu

Hemu was born in a small town called Rewari, in modern Haryana. As his father was a grocer, he was brought up to be a shopkeeper. 

"Welcome, Sethji. What can I do for you?" young Hemu would ask the customers. 

But Hemu had ambitions beyond the grocery store. "Father has no head for business. I'll have to take matters into my own hands or die a miserable grocer," he thought. 

One day, he told his father, "I'm sorry, father. Our stock is finished. We did, but a stranger came here yesterday and bought up all of it." 

"You should have kept some in stock! I'll buy my supplies elsewhere in future!" the angry customer yelled. 

"Hemu! You don't plan to sell supplies to the army!" his father gasped, realizing his son's plan. 

"I do, father. It's the only way of attracting the Sultan's attention," Hemu replied confidently. "I don't plan to remain a grocer for the rest of my life." 

Hemu patiently waited for his customer to come. "Hemu, I hear the Sultan has sent reinforcements to Ferozepore to crush the rebels," a friend told him. "That's good news! Ferozepore isn't far away." 

Then one day, the customer he had been waiting for arrived. It was an army officer. "Welcome, Faujdar Sahib. May I have the honour of serving the Sultan and your noble self?" Hemu offered. 

"Do you have enough grain to supply the Sultan's army?" the officer asked. 

"Enough and more, sir. I've been saving it for you ever since I heard the army had entered our district." 

A few days later, Hemu set out for Agra, the capital of Sultan Islam Shah. "Take care, my son. Life at court is full of intrigue. Don't trust anyone," his father warned him. 

Sultan Islam Shah, son of the famous Afghan, Sher Shah, was an able ruler. When Hemu reached Agra, the Sultan was planning a campaign against the rebel governor of the Punjab. 

"My father drove Humayun out of India. He could come back if rebels weaken the unity of my empire. This rebel must be crushed," the Sultan declared. 

"It should. But with the famine there, our success will depend on our supplies," a general noted. 

"Your majesty, I know an enterprising young man called Hemu who could get us all the grain we'd need!" the officer suggested. 

When Hemu was conducted to the Sultan, he was asked, "Is it true that you can get food for an army on the march, in a famine-stricken region?" 

"Certainly, your majesty. Our loyal farmers would be honoured if they could be suppliers to your majesty's troops," Hemu replied. 

"Very well. I hereby appoint you Superintendent of Bazars," the Sultan declared. 

Hemu undertook a whirlwind tour of the kingdom. "Remember, this grain is for the government troops. So quote me a fair price," he told the farmers. Hemu proved to be a resourceful man, and the Sultan took him with the army to the Punjab, paying him generously. 

"The grocer, Hemu, no doubt feeds the army. But he fleeces the Sultan," the jealous nobles murmured at the Sultan's tent. "Instigate the tribesmen there against him." 

A few days later, the tribesmen of Kashmir captured Hemu. "The tribesmen of Kashmir have killed the rebel and his men. We may safely return now," the nobles lied to the Sultan. 

But Hemu survived. So Islam Shah stayed in the Punjab. Sometime later, a message arrived from Mirza Kamran, Humayun's brother. "He seeks asylum in your kingdom," the messenger announced. 

"It's strange that a Mughal should seek asylum in our camp," the Sultan mused. 

"An enemy's foe is a friend, your majesty. We could turn it to our advantage," Hemu suggested. "Your majesty, Mirza Kamran is fleeing from Humayun. By helping Kamran you can upset Humayun's plans." 

The Sultan was intrigued by Hemu's remark. "You are shrewd, Hemu. You shall lead my secret service." 

Having strengthened his frontier, Islam Shah came to Delhi, where he fell ill. Now seriously ill, Islam Shah went to his favourite resting-place—Gwalior. 

"The Sultan is on his death-bed and the prince is but a minor. The courtiers have no unity. Humayun is only waiting for a chance to capture Delhi," Hemu observed astutely. 

Hemu was proved right. On the death of Islam Shah, the young Sultan was murdered by his own uncle who later ascended the throne assuming the title of Sultan Muhammad Adil Shah. 

"I have decided to take up the reins of the empire founded by Sher Shah, to provide efficient administration and to win greater glory," Adil Shah announced. He appointed Hemu as his minister. 

"I know you will be loyal. Besides, you, a humble grocer, can never be a threat to me," the Sultan reasoned. 

But the Afghan nobles resented Hemu's growing influence. "This grocer is no statesman. His advice to the Sultan, to seize the jagirs from the powerful nobles for redistribution, is dangerous," they grumbled. 

Rebellions broke out. Junaid Khan of Ajmer rebelled. "Your majesty, entrust me with a small force. I'll put Junaid Khan in his place," Hemu volunteered. 

"Bravo, Hemu! But you are no soldier," the Sultan laughed. 

"My common sense and intelligence can lead the soldiers to victory. Give me a chance and I'll prove it." 

At Ajmer, Hemu used defensive tactics that made Daulat Khan, Junaid's general, believe that victory was within his grasp. Then, with a mighty roar, Hemu attacked. Daulat Khan was slain. 

When Junaid Khan learnt about the defeat of his men, he was furious. "Prepare to march at once!" he ordered. 

At Hemu's camp, Hemu noted, "They have more men than I do and my men lie tired and wounded. But I must win! My future depends on it." 

And at night, when Junaid Khan's forces were asleep, he attacked. The enemy was taken by surprise, and a triumphant Hemu returned to the court at Gwalior. 

Meanwhile, at court, Taj Khan Kermani, another noble, left Gwalior with his men in defiance. Adil Shah sent forces in pursuit. The two armies met at Chhibramau, 50 miles from Agra, but Taj Khan fled to Bengal. 

Soon, Ibrahim Khan also rebelled in Agra and Delhi, proclaiming himself Sultan. Adil Shah sent envoys, but Ibrahim refused. Hemu marched against him, defeated Ibrahim's forces, and Taj Khan fled to Bengal. 

At about the same time, the governors of Bengal and Lahore declared their independence. The empire began to fall apart. 

A year later, Humayun died. "Your majesty, Humayun is dead. He fell from a staircase while hurrying to say his evening prayers," a messenger reported to Hemu. 

"Akbar, his successor, is only a child. Your majesty, now is our chance to wipe out the Mughals from the land," Hemu urged Adil Shah. "You are right, Hemu! You shall lead the expedition." 

When Hemu reached Agra, he found the fort deserted by the Mughals. The cowards were too scared to face him and had taken to their heels. 

At the Mughal court in Delhi, Tardi Beg, the governor, held consultations with his generals. "Hemu is on his way with a huge army. He is out to crush us," Tardi Beg panicked. "We cannot defend Delhi. We must retreat to the Punjab and join Prince Akbar." 

The Mughals were fully prepared when Hemu attacked, but Hemu was in the thick of the battle, encouraging his men. His charge routed the Mughals. They had to flee, leaving a large number dead, and all the Mughal wealth fell into Hemu's hands. 

Hemu made a triumphant entry into the palace in Delhi. On ascending the throne, Hemu assumed a new name—Vikramaditya. 

"I shall proclaim myself King and found a new dynasty!" he decided, as the Afghans were fighting among themselves and the Mughals were without a leader. 

Hemu's first concern was to defend his new-found kingdom against the Mughal army despatched by Akbar. "The veteran soldier, Bairam Khan, is leading the Mughal soldiers. This is going to be a decisive battle. It's victory or death." 

The two armies met on the historic plains of Panipat, near Delhi. 

Just as the armies were about to attack, the leading elephant in Hemu's army was struck by lightning. "A bad omen. We shall be destroyed!" his men cried. 

"No power can defy our will. Victory goes to those who fight for it. Take courage and fight!" Hemu rallied them. 

The spirited charge of Hemu and his men scattered the Mughal forces. Bairam Khan was uneasy. "Only a miracle can save us now. Or is this the end of the Mughal rule in India?" 

Just then a chance arrow... pierced Hemu's eye. He swooned. 

"The King has been hit! He's dead!" his soldiers panicked. Believing their leader to be dead, Hemu's forces scattered in panic. That which was certain victory turned into defeat. 

Hemu's elephant slowly wandered into the shelter of the nearby jungle, carrying the unconscious and unfortunate hero who, but for this stroke of fate, might have become the Emperor of India and changed the course of history.
