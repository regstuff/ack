# True Friends

Long, long ago, during the reign of King Magadha, a wedding was being celebrated in Rajagriha. 

"What a sumptuous feast! And such pomp and ceremony!" the guests exclaimed. "And why not? After all, the groom's father is the richest merchant in town." 

The bride was demure and beautiful. At first, she was treated with tenderness. "No! Don't do any work. Your lovely hands will be spoilt," her mother-in-law would say. 

But a few months later... "Don't sit there dreaming. Can't you lend a hand in the housework?" the mother-in-law scolded. "Lovely, my foot! Two whole years she has been married to my son and still they have no child..." 

The worried daughter-in-law confided in her maid. "I will not get any respect in this house unless I am with child." 

"Why don't you pretend to be so?" the maid suggested. "With the advice and help of the maid, the daughter-in-law pretended to be an expectant mother and once again she began to be fussed over." 

In due course, she said, "I think it's time to go to my parents' house for the delivery." 

She set out on her journey. "There's a caravan ahead of us! Let's try to go with them," she told her maid. 

They accompanied the caravan which camped in the evening near a grove of trees. Next morning, the caravan had moved on! And as the lady and her maid woke up, "What's this? A new-born baby!" 

"Oh! The caravan people must have abandoned it!" the maid guessed. "Are you thinking what I'm thinking? Yes! Let's pretend it is your baby." 

They hurried back with the baby boy. "An heir at last! What a lovely boy! We must celebrate his birth with great pomp," the family rejoiced. 

At the naming ceremony, he was named Nigrodha Kumar. 

Just then, two more boys were born in the city on the same day. In the merchants' house, the boy was named Shakha Kumar. In the weaver's house, there was a new-born who was named Pottik. 

"Let them be brought here! They will be good company for my son," the merchant declared. 

The three boys played together all day. When they were about eleven years old, they went to Takshashila for studies. "You two can afford the two thousand-coin fee... but my poor father is just a weaver. I cannot go with you," Pottik said sadly. 

"I spoke to my father and he has agreed to pay for your studies too," Nigrodha offered generously. 

While roaming from one place to another after their studies, they reached the outskirts of Varanasi. They spent the night under some trees. Early at dawn, Pottik woke up hearing the cackle of a hen. 

"Why are you cackling so loudly over an egg?" the hen's mate asked. 

"Because this one is not an ordinary egg. The one who eats the yolk of it is destined to be a king... the one who eats the white of it will become commander of the army... and the one who eats the shell will become a treasurer," the hen explained. 

When the hens moved away, Pottik quietly picked up the egg. "I must try to find out whether there is any truth in what the hen said." 

Carefully he separated the parts of the egg and mixed them in the breakfast porridge. "Here's your porridge, Nigrodha, with the yolk. And this is yours, Shakha. The white is in this one. I'll eat the one with the shell," Pottik thought to himself. 

When they had finished eating, Pottik repeated what he had heard. "Now if what the hen said is true, you will become a king soon, Nigrodha. Shakha will become the commander of the army and I the treasurer." 

Shakha was unhappy but kept silent. "Who is he to decide? He should have given the yolk to me. I'll teach him a lesson one day," Shakha fumed inwardly. 

They were warmly welcomed at the first house they visited in Varanasi. "It's been seven days since our childless king died. Now who will rule here?" the host asked. 

"Don't you know the tradition? A flower-decorated chariot will be sent out today. It will have no charioteer. But the new king will be the one in front of whom the chariot stops." 

The three young men set out. Nigrodha slept on a stone-slab in a park while the others slept on the steps outside. 

An excited mob followed the decorated chariot. "The horse has stopped! At the entrance of the park!" 

"Ah! What a noble face this young man has," the royal priest said, looking at Nigrodha. "His feet have the signs of an emperor." 

The hubbub awoke Nigrodha. "Oh, sire! You are our chosen king. Accept this crown and the kingdom," the priest said. 

Nigrodha Kumar was promptly installed as the king. "I name Shakha Kumar as my Commander-in-Chief. Pottik, you must stay with me as my constant companion," the new king declared. 

One day, Pottik said, "I miss my father and mother. I will go and fetch them here." 

With a small retinue, Pottik set off for Rajagriha. He brought his parents back. On his way to Varanasi, Pottik stopped at Shakha's house. "Tell your master that his friend Pottik is here." 

But Shakha was arrogant. "Who's Pottik? I don't know anyone by that name. Beat him and throw him out. He must be a madcap." 

The guards threw Pottik out. "What an ungrateful wretch. I'm sure my friend Nigrodha will not behave thus," Pottik thought. 

Pottik reached the palace late in the evening. "O King! Your friend Pottik desires to see you," a guard announced. 

"Bring him in right away," Nigrodha smiled. "Come, you must be tired after your journey." 

Nigrodha welcomed him warmly, gave him clothes and ornaments, and made him the royal treasurer. "You will have the authority to determine the taxes payable by the members of the various guilds. You will be in charge of the treasury." 

Shakha's silence infuriated the king. "O you wicked liar! Take him away! Kill him," King Nigrodha roared when he learned what Shakha had done. 

"No! Please don't, O King! It is not easy to bring anyone back to life... therefore, I don't want him killed. Please forgive him," Pottik intervened. 

"If you insist. But I want him banished from my kingdom," the king declared. 

"A favour done to a deserving noble person is like a seed sown in fertile soil. It will reap rich rewards. Indeed, any favour done to an ungrateful man is like a seed burnt by fire. Nothing ever comes out of it," the king concluded. 

With Pottik by his side, Nigrodha Kumar ruled wisely and well for many years.
