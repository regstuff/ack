# Chennama of Keladi: The Queen Who Defied Aurangzeb

In the year 1663, Somashekhara Nayaka, the ruler of the kingdom of Keladi in the Malnad region of Karnataka, attended the annual Rameshwar fair in his capital city of Bidanur. There, amid the bustling stalls, he caught sight of Chennamma, the beautiful and intelligent daughter of a local merchant. 

Fascinated by her grace, the King resolved to marry her, ignoring his ministers' advice to form a political alliance with neighboring royal houses.

The royal wedding brought great joy to the citizens, but it foiled the dark ambitions of Bharama Mahuta, the powerful chief of the royal elephant force. Bharama had secretly plotted to usurp the throne, believing the King would remain unwed and without heirs.

Over the next few years, the young queen transformed herself into a formidable leader. Under royal tutors, Rani Chennamma mastered horse riding, archery, statecraft, and administration, taking a keen interest in the welfare of her subjects.

However, as years passed without an heir to the throne, King Somashekhara fell into deep melancholy. Seizing upon the King's sadness, the treacherous Bharama Mahuta offered him a slow-acting poison, claiming it was a rare potion to cure childlessness. The poison drove the King into fits of delirium, and within months, Somashekhara passed away.

The morning after the King's death, Bharama Mahuta approached Rani Chennamma, arrogantly offering to take the reigns of government. But Chennamma had already uncovered his treachery. 

" Don't move!" guards shouted as Chennamma signaled. 

Looking coldly at the traitor, Chennamma declared, "By poisoning the King who trusted you, you have proved yourself the blackest of traitors!" She ordered Bharama executed for high treason.

Following the King's death, Keladi descended into turmoil as rival ministers plotted against the Queen, dismissing her as an inexperienced merchant's daughter. 

Before order could be restored, alarming news reached the court: a massive army from the Sultanate of Bijapur, led by General Jannopant, was marching to invade Keladi!

As panic erupted in the Durbar and senior ministers threatened to desert, Rani Chennamma stepped onto the royal dais. Unclasping her gold necklaces and bangles, she placed them into the treasury chest.

"You have just heard the news—Keladi is under attack!" Chennamma's voice echoed through the hall. "Who is to rule is not important; our first job is to repel the invader. These hands that once wore gold bangles now take up the sword to fight along with you! Will you fight?"

"We will fight!" the assembled soldiers and citizens roared back.

Realizing that Bidanur could not withstand a direct siege, Chennamma executed a brilliant strategic retreat. She ordered the capital evacuated and moved her army to the impregnable jungle fortress of Bhuvanagiri. When the Bijapur forces entered Bidanur, they found only an empty city with an empty treasury.

At Bhuvanagiri, Commander-in-Chief Thimmanna bowed before her, begging forgiveness for his earlier doubts. Chennamma embraced his loyalty and reappointed him to lead the army. 

Utilizing expert hit-and-run guerrilla tactics in the dense, rain-drenched Malnad forests, Chennamma's forces decimated the Bijapur supply lines, routing the invaders and liberating Bidanur. In 1671, Rani Chennamma was formally crowned Queen of Keladi.

To secure the kingdom's future, Chennamma adopted a bright young boy, Basava Nayaka, personally training him in administration, ethics, and military command. She expanded trade with Arab, Portuguese, and Dutch merchants, bringing immense prosperity to Keladi, and successfully repelled an invasion by King Chikkadevaraya Wodeyar of Mysore.

In 1689, a historical crisis confronted the realm. Following the brutal execution of Chhatrapati Sambhaji by Mughal Emperor Aurangzeb, Sambhaji's younger brother, Rajaram, fled south toward the fortress of Jinji. Disguised as a simple monk, Rajaram entered Keladi and sought an audience with Rani Chennamma.

"Mother, I am Rajaram, son of Chhatrapati Shivaji," the prince revealed in her private chamber. "Mughal armies are hunting me. I seek safe passage through your kingdom."

Ministers warned Chennamma in panic: "Highness, granting passage to Rajaram will bring the wrath of Aurangzeb upon Keladi! We cannot stand up to the Mughal Empire!"

Rani Chennamma looked at her council with unflinching courage. "You mock me by putting your request as a question. It is the sacred tradition of Keladi rulers to protect anyone who seeks refuge. I shall face the wrath of Aurangzeb!"

She granted Rajaram shelter and arranged an armed escort to guide him safely to Jinji.

Learning of her defiance, Aurangzeb dispatched a formidable Mughal army under General Jaan Nisar Khan to crush Keladi. Chennamma sent a dignified diplomatic reply while ordering her forces to prepare for battle.

Before Chennamma's letter reached the Emperor, the Mughal army charged into the dense Malnad rainforests. As heavy monsoon rains broke out, the forests turned into soggy, impassable marshes. The Mughal troops, unaccustomed to the terrain, fell victim to dysentery, leeches, and fever, while Keladi's soldiers struck from the thickets with deadly accuracy.

Harassed day and night in the torrential rains, the Mughal army was forced to sue for peace and retreat, allowing Rajaram to reach Jinji safely! 

Deeply moved by her heroism, Rajaram sent a letter of gratitude: *"The rulers of larger kingdoms looked away in fear, but you stood up to the Mughals. I shall ever stand in awe of your courage."*

Rani Chennamma ruled Keladi for twenty-five glorious years, noted for her justice, religious tolerance, and efficient administration. On her deathbed, she passed the crown to her adopted son, Basava Nayaka, who built the fort of Chennagiri in her honor and authored the famous Kannada encyclopedic work *Shivatattvaratnakara*.
