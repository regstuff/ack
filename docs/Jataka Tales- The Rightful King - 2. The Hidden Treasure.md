# The Hidden Treasure

One day, long long ago, a woman came walking dejectedly down the road that led away from the city of Mithila. Her steps were slow and her feet dragged. She wore the garb of a maid but seemed to be of noble birth. 

"I cannot walk a step farther. What shall I do? Perhaps that carter will..." she thought. 

"How far are you going, sir? Can you take me to Kalachampa?" she asked. 

"You are very kind, sir! I... I don't know how to thank you," she said as he offered her a ride. 

As the cart jogged along, the exhausted woman fell asleep. When she woke up it was evening. 

"Which city is this?" she asked. 

"Why, it's the city of Kalachampa! We have arrived." 

After alighting from the cart, she walked into the city. "I came here because this was the only city I'd heard of. Where shall I go now?" 

A little later, a teacher came by with his pupils. "Who are you? You seem to be in distress," he asked. 

"I... I am... I am the Queen of Mithila. My husband was killed in battle. I had to flee the city for the sake of my unborn child." 

"Please don't hesitate. Tell me the truth. I may be able to help you. You may stay at my house. My wife will look after you." 

The Brahmana and his wife looked after her with love and care. After a few days, a son was born to her. "What shall we call him, brother?" she asked. 

"We'll call him Mahajanaka," the Brahmana replied. 

The Queen and her son stayed on in the house. When Mahajanaka grew a little older, he made friends with the boys in the neighbourhood. But they often quarrelled, and Mahajanaka, who was the strongest, would beat up the others. 

"He has no father!" the boys mocked him. 

Mahajanaka ran to his mother. "Oh, mother! Who is my father?" 

"How can I, son? Our enemies shouldn't know that we are alive! Oh, what shall I do?" she thought, but eventually she told him the truth. "You are the son of King Aritthajanaka of Mithila! A few days before you were born, your father had to do battle with his enemy, Poljanaka. We lost, and our king is no more!" 

Years passed. Mahajanaka grew up to be a handsome and learned man. "Mother, do you have any money with you?" 

"I have a few gold coins and ornaments. They are all yours." 

"With my half I shall buy some merchandise, carry on trade overseas and earn the money I'll need to raise an army. I wish to go to Mithila to claim my throne." 

Soon the day of departure dawned. "Mother, bless me! And don't worry. May God be with you, my son." 

Meanwhile, in Mithila, Poljanaka was alive but seriously ill and he had no son. "I have given the matter deep thought and made my decision. The man my daughter chooses for a husband, shall be the king," he told his ministers. 

"Your majesty, Princess Shivali will choose a good man, but how can we be sure that he will make a good king?" 

"I have thought about that too. Her suitor will have to perform three tasks to prove his intelligence, strength and wisdom," the king replied. "First, he must point out the head of the Princess' square bed. Then he must string a bow which would ordinarily require the strength of a thousand men. Lastly, with the help of sixteen clues, he will have to find the sixteen pots of gold I've hidden in and around the palace." 

A few days later the King died. 

The ministers tried to find a husband for Shivali. The General tried, but failed to please her. The Treasurer tried, and failed. All the others who tried to woo her also failed. 

Meanwhile, on board Mahajanaka's ship, a storm broke. Wave after mighty wave lashed at the ship. "I must remain calm! I must not panic. I have a mission to accomplish. I must not let death rob me of the chance to regain the throne of Mithila," Mahajanaka resolved, holding on to the mast. 

As the ship sank under the onslaught of the waves, Mahajanaka tightened his grip on the mast. A few days later, Mahajanaka was washed ashore. 

Later, in a garden outside the eastern gate of the city of Mithila, he overheard two men talking. "...and so Mithila is still without a king!" 

"I am in Mithila! And Mithila is without a king! But I have neither weapons nor money. I must move cautiously," he thought, falling asleep on a stone slab. 

At the palace, the ministers consulted the royal priest. "Send out the sacred chariot. It will lead us to the right man." 

The chariot, accompanied by the royal priest and ministers, rolled past the houses of the general and the treasurer, and out of the eastern gate. It came to a halt near the spot where Mahajanaka lay sleeping. 

"He's a total stranger," the ministers observed. "Let the drums be beaten. If he's a coward, he will flee. If he is brave, he won't be frightened." 

When the din awoke him, there was no sign of panic in Mahajanaka's eyes. He went back to sleep calmly. 

The priest came forward. "Rise, O noble one. The kingdom awaits you!" 

When Mahajanaka arrived at the palace, Princess Shivali tested him. "The Princess is waiting for you in her chamber," a maid informed him. He deliberately took his time. 

When he finally entered, Shivali thought, "How serene and confident his manner! How majestic! He is the man for me!" 

"May I relax here for a while?" he asked, sitting on her square bed. "Give it to me," he said, holding out a pin. 

Shivali handed it to him, and he placed it on the bed. "That—the place where Shivali would naturally preserve her pins when she removes them—is the head of the bed," the ministers marvelled. "It is. He is right." 

Next, he was taken to the armoury. There, Mahajanaka lifted the great bow with ease and strung it. "He's strung it! It's incredible!" 

"The last test. You have to find the sixteen pots of gold hidden in and around the palace. Are there any clues?" he asked. 

"Yes. ...where the sun rises; and the second where the sun sets. The third..." 

That night Mahajanaka thought hard. "...sun... sun... That's it! The sun also symbolizes light—the light of knowledge and truth. Now I know!" 

The next morning, he asked, "Did your king honour ascetics and scholars? Where did he receive them when they came?" 

The ministers took him outside the palace. "This is where the king received ascetics and scholars." 

"Have this place dug up and you will find the first pot." 

The place was dug up, and a huge pot of gold was found! He deciphered the remaining clues one after the other, and all the sixteen pots were found. 

"You have proved yourself on every score, my lord. You must now marry the Princess, and make us happy," the ministers bowed. 

"First, I want my mother and my uncle to be brought here from Kalachampa." 

After a few weeks, the Brahmana arrived with Mahajanaka's mother. Mahajanaka and Shivali were married. 

When Mahajanaka was crowned King of Mithila, he made an announcement: "I declare that the gold found in the pots will be distributed among the needy." 

The declaration was received with great joy. "Long live King Mahajanaka!!"
