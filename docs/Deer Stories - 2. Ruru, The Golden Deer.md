# Ruru, The Golden Deer

Mahadhanaka was the son of a rich merchant of Varanasi. His father, instead of sending him to school, spoilt him with all the pleasures of the world. "Enjoy yourself, my son! I can give you everything," his father would say. 

"I know, father!" Mahadhanaka replied happily. 

Since Mahadhanaka had never handled money before, he did not know how to use it responsibly. "Use the most expensive cloth you have, for our clothes," he commanded the tailors. "Let us have some more liquor and play another round of cards," he told his freeloading friends.

When he came of age, Mahadhanaka was married to a beautiful girl. "Your mother has cooked this for you, my lord," his wife said gently. 

"Hmm... smells delicious!" he remarked, living a life of utter luxury. 

Soon his parents died, and he was left alone to manage the estate. "Alas! Who will look after me now?" he wept. But his friends were only after his wealth. 

"Come on Mahadhanaka, cheer up," they urged. "Let us have a game of dice and some wine to warm us up."

It didn't take long for him to squander his inheritance. One day, he opened his treasure chest and found it empty. "What! No money! If I don't do something, I'll soon have no friends either." Then an idea struck him. "Yes, that's it. I'll borrow some money and pay it back later."

He went from one money-lender to another and promised them all the same thing: "I'll return your money as soon as I become rich again."

Months passed. When Mahadhanaka showed no sign of returning the money, the lenders grew furious. "You had better pay up soon, Mahadhanaka, or else..." they threatened.

"Have patience. I will," he stammered. But he knew he would never be able to repay the money. "I would rather die than face the money-lenders," he thought in despair.

Then one day, Mahadhanaka led all his creditors to the bank of a river. "Where are you leading us?" they asked suspiciously. 

"I have some treasure hidden in the river bed. I will return all that I have borrowed from you," he lied. As they reached the edge, he thought, "Now is my chance to slip away, and drown myself. That will be the end to all my sorrows." With a loud *Splash!*, he jumped into the raging river. "Help! Glub! Gasp! Help!" he panicked, regretting his decision as the water swallowed him.

Not very far away, on the opposite bank, lived Ruru, a magnificent golden deer. "Somebody's drowning. I must save him," thought Ruru when he heard the cries. Ruru dove into the water and swam to him. "Ho! Man. I will save your life. Hold on." He dragged the heavy man to the safety of the shore. 

"How do you feel?" Ruru asked. 

"Much better. Thank you," Mahadhanaka coughed, amazed by the talking golden deer. 

"Before parting, promise me you will tell no one about me and where I live," Ruru requested. 

"I promise, friend," Mahadhanaka swore. 

Meanwhile, that very night in Varanasi, Khema, the Queen, had a vivid dream. She woke up the King in a flurry. "What is it, my queen?" 

"I dreamt that a golden deer preached dharma to me," she said. "If such a deer exists, I would like to hear its discourse." 

"If there is such an animal, you will," the King assured her. The King sent for his councillors the next morning. "Are there such animals as golden deer?" 

"Yes, your majesty. There are," they replied.

The King announced a massive reward: a sack of gold coins placed in a gold pot on an elephant's back. A town crier was sent to tell the people of the reward. Just then, Mahadhanaka strolled by and heard the announcement. "What did you say, my friend?" he asked the crier. 

"The king is offering a large reward to any man who can tell him of the whereabouts of a golden deer." 

Mahadhanaka's eyes widened with greed. "Hmm, I shall soon be rich again," he schemed, completely forgetting his promise to Ruru. He hurried to the palace. 

"Your majesty, this man says he knows where a golden deer may be found," a courtier presented him.

"Is that so? When can you lead us to him?" the King asked eagerly. 

"At the break of dawn, your majesty."

At dawn, the royal hunting party set off. "I hope you know the way," the king warned. 

"There, your majesty," Mahadhanaka pointed, "in that grove of mango and sal trees, lives the golden deer you want." 

"Surround the grove," the King ordered his soldiers. 

"I'll stand a little way off and see what happens," Mahadhanaka cowardishly thought, slinking away.

When Ruru heard the noise of the beaters, he realized what was happening. "That's a hunting party, I'm sure. How did they find this grove?" Instead of running, Ruru stepped out and confronted the king. 

The king was astonished to hear Ruru's calm, human-like voice. "I promise I won't shoot. Come out of your hiding place," the King said, lowering his bow. 

"What is it you want to know?" Ruru asked gracefully. "Your majesty, who told you about me and where I live?" 

The King pointed to Mahadhanaka, who was hiding behind a tree. "He did." 

Ruru sighed deeply. "I thought as much. It would have been better to save a log of wood from the river than a man like him." 

"What do you mean by that?" the King asked, puzzled. 

"Your majesty, I once saved this man from drowning in the river, and this is how he has repaid me." 

The King's face turned red with anger. "Is that so? He shall be hanged for it!" the King declared, glaring at the trembling Mahadhanaka. 

But Ruru intervened. "No, don't do that. I don't want him to die because of me." 

The King was moved by the deer's compassion. "You are lucky," the King told Mahadhanaka with disgust. "But for him I would have had you hanged. Now go away." 

The King then turned to Ruru with folded hands. "Ask for anything, and I'll grant it to you." 

"Your majesty, from now on, let no animal or bird be harmed by anyone in your kingdom," Ruru requested. 

The King immediately sent around a proclamation throughout his lands: "No living creature big or small shall henceforth be harmed. Should anyone harm them he shall be severely punished." 

Ruru then went with the king to the palace, where he discoursed dharma to the queen just as she had dreamt, and then peacefully returned to his forest.
