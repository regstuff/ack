# Elephanta

Beyond Bombay harbour, across the blue waters, lies the green isle of Elephanta. A short walk uphill takes you to the magnificent cave temples, cut out of solid rock by the hand of man. Inside the main cave may be seen some of the finest sculptures of Indian art, carved about 1300 years ago. 

These caves were carved patiently and laboriously by artisans during the reign of the Mauryas of the Konkan. Later, the Mauryas were defeated in a great naval battle off Elephanta by the Chalukyan King, Pulakesin II. 

"Hail Emperor Pulakesin!" his soldiers cheered. 

After being ruled by several Hindu dynasties, the island fell to the Sultans of Gujarat and eventually passed from them to the Portuguese. Unfortunately, the Portuguese soldiers had little respect for the ancient art and damaged many of the sculptures, using them for target practice. "Ha! Ha! I have hit the nose this time," one soldier laughed. "I have done better! I have knocked off an arm!" another boasted. 

The island was later taken by the Marathas. "Hail, Emperor Sambhaji! We shall make this island an invincible fort," his men declared. 

The early names of this island were Gharapuri and Sripuri. When the Portuguese first landed, they saw a massive stone elephant. "This is unique! A stone elephant to receive us!" they marvelled. "The island should be called Elephanta!" 

The stone elephant was later removed by the British to the Victoria Gardens in Bombay (now Veermata Jijabai Bhonsle Udyan). 

Of the seven caves at Elephanta, the main cave is the most important. It is dedicated to Lord Shiva, the great god of the Hindu Trinity. 

In the main cave, the first figure at the right is that of Shiva as Nataraja, the Lord of Dancers. His dance symbolises the eternal motion of the universe. The story behind this goes back to ancient times. 

Long, long ago, in a forest, there lived ten thousand rishis who by virtue of their penances had become very powerful. "We are greater even than the devas! We are invincible!" they boasted arrogantly. 

At Kailasa, Shiva turned to Vishnu. "You and I must subdue these arrogant sages." 

Shiva took the form of a handsome, young yogi and Vishnu took the form of his charming wife. As soon as they appeared before the rishis, the sages' wives were completely captivated by the handsome yogi. "He's sure to choose me!" one gasped. "He will not! He will choose me," another argued. 

Leaving the sacrifice unfinished, the women walked towards the yogi's wife, entranced by her beauty. "You will marry me. No, she shall be mine," the sages fought over Vishnu's female form. 

Suddenly, the rishis came to their senses and realised they were being deluded by some supernatural power. "May you and your magical powers be cursed!" they shouted at the yogi. "And cursed be your wife, the serpent in a woman's guise!" 

Their curses, however, were of no avail against the supreme gods. So they prepared a sacrificial fire. "We will destroy them for all time," they declared, pouring ghee and chanting mantras. 

Suddenly, a ferocious tiger sprang from the fire. "Our curses may have failed, but this tiger will not. Destroy him!" 

Unperturbed, Shiva caught the tiger in an iron grip, flayed it, and draped its skin over his body. 

The next moment, a giant snake emerged from the fire and darted at Shiva. He calmly picked it up and wore it as a garland around his neck. 

Hardly had he done so when, from the fire, there emerged an evil dwarf holding a club. "Now try and overpower him!" the rishis mocked. 

"You cannot! He is the evil goblin himself! He will destroy you!" 

In reply, Shiva dashed the evil creature to the ground, assumed his own divine form, and began to dance upon the dwarf's back. Good had triumphed over evil! The rishis and the devas in heaven gazed in awe at the powerful dance in which all creation and the cosmic universe was set in motion. 

"He has taught us a lesson in humility!" the rishis bowed. "Glory to the great Shiva!" 

Another magnificent panel at Elephanta is the Lingodbhava Murti. One day, Brahma, the Creator, and Vishnu, the Preserver, were arguing about which of them was the greater. Suddenly, there appeared an endless fiery pillar, a Lingam, without beginning or end. 

Vishnu, in the form of a boar, burrowed into the earth. "I will find the origin of this Lingam," he said. Brahma, in the form of a swan, flew higher and higher into the heavens. "And I shall find the end!" 

After countless years, the boar returned exhausted. "I have failed. I could not find its beginning." 

Neither did Brahma succeed in his mission, but he refused to admit it. He saw a Ketaki flower falling from the heavens. "You must come with me to Vishnu and say that I picked you up from Shiva's head," Brahma instructed the flower. 

Brahma assumed his real form and lied to Vishnu. "I saw the end of the fiery Lingam. And I was a witness to it," the flower lied. 

Suddenly, Shiva emerged from the centre of the fiery Lingam. "Brahma, you have been dishonest! From now on, none shall honour you. Nor will you have your own temple or festival," Shiva cursed him in anger. 

But to Vishnu, Shiva said, "Dear Hari, you are a lover of truth! I am pleased with you. You will always be honoured as I am. You will have separate temples and festivals." 

One of the most exquisite panels at Elephanta is that of the marriage of Shiva and Parvati. Parvati (Uma) had undertaken severe penances to win Shiva's love. An old Brahmana appeared before her and tried to dissuade her. "Why have you renounced the world? To win the love of Shiva? I wish to marry him." 

"That mendicant?" the old man scoffed. "He has matted hair and decks himself with serpents! He is uncouth..." 

"How dare you speak of my loved one so! Go away!" Parvati shouted angrily. 

Suddenly, the old man changed his form. "It is you, my Lord!" Parvati gasped as Shiva revealed himself, having merely tested her devotion. And so they were married. 

The next panel is of Shiva as Gangadhara, showing the descent of the river Ganga to the earth. King Sagara had sixty thousand sons who were reduced to ashes by the angry Sage Kapila. Sagara's descendant, Bhagiratha, undertook severe penances to bring the celestial river Ganga down to earth to wash their ashes and grant them salvation. 

"I shall send Ganga down to earth to redeem your ancestors," Brahma finally granted. "But Shiva must break her fall, or the earth will be washed away." 

As Ganga hurtled down from the heavens, proud of her mighty force, Shiva caught the turbulent river in his matted locks. "The force of Ganga's fall has been broken!" Bhagiratha rejoiced, and the holy river flowed gently onto the earth. 

In the centre of the northern wall is the gigantic, iconic figure of Shiva as Mahesha (the Trimurti). It combines in himself the qualities of Creator, Preserver, and Destroyer. 

Other panels depict Ardhanarishwara (Shiva and Parvati as one entity, half-male and half-female), and a scene of Ravana attempting to uproot Mount Kailasa. As the demon king lifted the mountain, Parvati trembled with fear, but Shiva merely smiled and pressed the mountain down with his toe, imprisoning the arrogant Ravana beneath it. 

The last panel in the main cave shows Shiva as the Great Yogi. He sits on a lotus in deep meditation. Though the lotus grows in muddy waters, it remains pure and clean. Similarly, Shiva's face reflects the calm and spiritual strength of the supreme yogi, forever detached from worldly illusions.
