# Jayadratha

In the capital of Sindhu, Queen Duhshala was anxiously waiting for the return of her husband, Jayadratha, from Hastinapura. 

"I hope all is well, my lord," she greeted him. 

"All is well, Duhshala. In fact things couldn't be better... The Kauravas, your brothers, have found a supremely clever way of humbling your cousins, the proud Pandavas! They invited the Pandavas to play a game of dice... The losers would surrender all rights to their kingdom and go into exile for thirteen years," Jayadratha explained. 

"The Pandavas fell for that? Didn't they know my uncle, Shakuni, would help my brothers to win?" 

"Naturally, Shakuni won the game for Duryodhana with his tricks. Ha! Ha! Ha!" Jayadratha laughed. 

Meanwhile, the Pandavas—Yudhishthira, Bhima, Arjuna, Nakula, and Sahadeva—with their wife, Draupadi, had reached the Kamyaka forest. 

One day, while the Pandavas had gone away as usual in search of game, leaving Draupadi at home, Jayadratha happened to pass by. He saw Draupadi and was captivated. 

"I am Draupadi, the wife of the valiant Pandavas. My husbands have gone out hunting. They will be pleased to have your King as their guest," she offered graciously. 

But Jayadratha had evil intentions. "Why should you suffer with the Pandavas? Forsake them. Be my Queen and live in comfort," he proposed. 

"To lay hands on me is to invite the wrath of Arjuna and Bhima upon yourself. It is like kicking a sleeping lion," Draupadi warned fiercely. 

Disregarding her anger, Jayadratha carried her to his chariot and drove away. 

When the Pandavas returned and discovered Draupadi missing, they found chariot tracks and immediately set out in pursuit. In the battle that followed, the Pandavas scattered Jayadratha's army. 

"Jayadratha fled for his life, leaving Draupadi behind." 

Bhima and Arjuna pursued the scoundrel. Before leaving, Yudhishthira had told them, "For the sake of our cousin, Duhshala, do not slay Jayadratha, though he is so wicked!" 

Bhima caught Jayadratha, pulled him by the hair, and kicked him hard. "You coward! You dare to carry away a lady by force yet you run away from a man!" 

"Bhima, don't kill him. Remember what Yudhishthira told us," Arjuna cautioned. 

Instead of killing him, Bhima shaved Jayadratha's head, leaving five tufts of hair, to humiliate him. "If you wish to live you must publicly declare that you are the slave of the Pandavas," Bhima ordered. 

Jayadratha was forced to agree and was brought before Yudhishthira. "Let him go! He is our slave and you have shaved his head. That is enough for me," Yudhishthira pardoned him. 

Jayadratha left, burning with shame and anger. "I will have my revenge..." 

He went to the banks of the Ganga and performed severe penances, imploring the protection of Lord Shiva. When Shiva finally appeared, Jayadratha asked, "Lord, I want to be able to defeat all five of the Pandavas in battle!" 

"That is not possible. No one can defeat the Pandavas. However, you will be able to check the advance of the Pandavas once. But even so you won't be able to stop Arjuna," Lord Shiva granted him. 

Years later, the great war of Kurukshetra broke out between the Kauravas and the Pandavas. On the thirteenth day of the battle, Drona was the Kaurava commander. He formed the impenetrable Chakravyuha formation. 

The Kauravas lured Arjuna away from the main battlefield using the Samshaptakas, a tribe of warriors sworn to fight to the death. While Arjuna was busy fighting them, the young Abhimanyu, Arjuna's son, penetrated the Chakravyuha. 

However, Jayadratha used his boon to hold back the other four Pandavas, preventing them from following and assisting Abhimanyu. Trapped inside, Abhimanyu fought valiantly but died a hero's death against overwhelming odds. 

When Arjuna returned and heard of his son's death, his grief turned to fury. "Arjuna, we could not follow Abhimanyu into the Chakravyuha. Jayadratha held us back," Yudhishthira explained. 

Arjuna took a terrible vow. "Tomorrow, before sunset, I will kill Jayadratha on the battle-field—and if I fail I will enter fire." 

The next day, Drona arranged a massive formation to protect Jayadratha, placing him twelve miles behind the front lines. 

Arjuna, with Krishna as his charioteer, launched a fierce attack, releasing volleys of arrows and breaking through the Kaurava ranks. He defeated many warriors, including Shrutayudha, whose mace recoiled and killed him because he threw it at the unarmed Krishna. 

As they fought their way deep into the enemy lines, Arjuna's horses grew tired. Arjuna created a pool of water with an arrow and built a protective wall of arrows around them. Krishna unharnessed and refreshed the horses. 

As evening approached, the Kauravas grew jubilant, thinking Arjuna would fail his vow. The sun was getting low. 

"Look! Krishna and Arjuna are here again!" but the Kauravas kept a careful watch on Jayadratha. 

Suddenly, it became dark. "Krishna! The sun has set!" Arjuna cried. 

"No, Arjuna. This is an illusion. You have nothing to fear," Krishna assured him. 

Believing the sun had set and he was safe, Jayadratha came out to look. "Darkness at last! I am safe!" he cheered. 

"At that moment the darkness was dispelled and the sun was visible again. 'The sun has not set! It was only an illusion wrought by Krishna!'" the Kauravas realized in horror. 

"Then Arjuna struck." With a powerful arrow, he severed Jayadratha's head, fulfilling his vow just before the sun truly set. 

Victorious, Krishna and Arjuna blew their conches to announce their victory, avenging the death of brave Abhimanyu.
