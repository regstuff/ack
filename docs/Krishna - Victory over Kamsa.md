# Krishna: Victory over Kamsa

On the auspicious day of Shivaratri, Krishna, Nanda, and other cowherds visited Ambikavana, a forest near the river Saraswati. Abstaining from any food to observe the fast, they went to sleep on the river bank. 

Suddenly, a huge python pounced on the sleeping Nanda. "Help, Krishna! Help! Hurry! The python is swallowing me," Nanda cried. The cowherds woke up in alarm and beat the python with sticks and burning logs, but in vain. 

Then Krishna came and gave a gentle kick to the python. With the mere touch of his foot, the python vanished. In its place stood a youth in all his radiance. "I am a Vidyadhara named Sudarshana... Proud of my wealth and good looks, I laughed at seeing their ugly faces. They cursed me to become a python," he explained. Bowing to Krishna, the youth disappeared, freed from his curse. 

One moonlit night, Krishna and Balarama were roaming in the forest accompanied by Gopas and Gopikas, filling the air with melodious music. Just then, Shankhachooda, a devotee of Kubera, appeared and ran off with the Gopikas. 

"Oh Rama! Oh Krishna!" they cried. Krishna and Balarama chased him. Shankhachooda ran, but Krishna pursued him and with a single stroke of his hand, knocked Shankhachooda's head off. Krishna took the shining gem from the demon's head and presented it to Balarama. 

One day, a huge bull confronted Krishna. "It was the demon Arishtasura in the form of a bull. As he stamped the ground with his enormous hooves the very earth trembled." Krishna stood calmly. The enraged creature leapt towards him. Krishna caught hold of his horns and drove the Asura back forcefully. With a deft movement, Krishna pulled out its sharp horns and killed the demon. 

Meanwhile, Sage Narada visited King Kamsa in Mathura. "Krishna, who lives in Vraja, is the son of Devaki... The two boys in the care of his friend Nanda... All the Asuras sent by you to kill them have been killed themselves," Narada revealed. 

Kamsa trembled with rage. He immediately plotted to kill Krishna and Balarama. He summoned his wrestlers, Chanura and Mushtika, and the keeper of his mighty elephant, Kuvalayapeeda. He planned a grand bow sacrifice and a wrestling match, inviting the boys. Kamsa sent Akrura, a Yadava kinsman, to fetch them from Vrindavana. 

While Akrura was on his way, a demon named Keshi assumed the form of a mighty horse and galloped into Vrindavana, terrorizing the people. "Keshi rushed at Krishna with his enormous mouth wide open as if he would devour the entire sky." 

Krishna effortlessly thrust his left arm into the open mouth of the demon. "At the touch of Krishna's hand Keshi's teeth began to fall. Then Krishna's arm began to swell inside the mouth of the demon... till it choked the demon's throat." Keshi collapsed and died. 

A few days later, while Krishna was playing hide-and-seek with his friends, the demon Vyomasura appeared, disguised as a cowherd. He joined the game as one of the thieves and started kidnapping the boys, hiding them in a cave. 

"As soon as Vyomasura tried to take away another 'sheep', Krishna pounced on him, like a lion attacking a wolf." Krishna caught him roughly, pinned him to the ground, and killed him. Then Krishna freed all the trapped boys from the cave. 

Akrura arrived in Vrindavana and delivered Kamsa's message. Early next morning, Krishna, Balarama, and the cowherds left for Mathura, leaving the Gopikas heartbroken. On the way, while bathing in the Yamuna, Akrura had a vision of Lord Vishnu resting on the serpent Shesha, realizing Krishna's divine nature. 

In Mathura, Krishna and Balarama explored the city. They encountered a rude washerman of Kamsa. When the washerman insulted them, Krishna slapped him, killing him instantly, and distributed the fine clothes among his friends. 

They then met a tailor who fitted the garments, and Sudama, a flowerman who worshipped them and presented them with garlands. 

Walking further, they met Trivakra, a hunchbacked woman carrying special sandalwood paste for Kamsa. Touched by Krishna's gentle smile, she offered him the paste. "Krishna placed his two feet on the toes of the hunchback. He raised his hands and with his fingers, he pushed up her chin with a slight jerk." Her hump vanished, and she stood erect, a beautiful woman. 

Finally, they reached the site of the bow sacrifice. Ignoring the guards, "Krishna reached for the mighty bow. With his left hand he deftly lifted the bow, strung it, and in a matter of minutes, split the bow into two." They vanquished the guards and returned to their camp. 

The next morning, Kamsa inaugurated the festival of wrestling matches. As Krishna and Balarama arrived at the arena gates, the mahout provoked the elephant Kuvalayapeeda to attack them. 

"For long, Krishna teased and taunted the elephant. Then with one hand he pulled at the trunk and floored him. Krishna then pulled out the tusks, and with them, he finished off both, the elephant and its keeper." 

Carrying the tusks, the brothers entered the arena. The wrestling began. Krishna fought Chanura, while Balarama fought Mushtika. "Unperturbed, Krishna caught hold of his arms and flung him on the ground after turning him around several times in the air." Chanura was dead. Balarama defeated Mushtika, and they also killed the other wrestlers who stepped forward. 

Seeing this, Kamsa panicked and ordered his men to drive the brothers away and kill Vasudeva, Ugrasena, and Nanda. 

But before anyone could act, "Krishna leapt up to his high seat... Catching Kamsa by his hair Krishna threw him down. Krishna then jumped on Kamsa, killing him that very instant." 

Kamsa's eight younger brothers rushed to take revenge, but Balarama killed them all. 

Thereafter, Krishna and Balarama hurried to the prison and freed Vasudeva and Devaki, bowing before their true parents.
