# The Blue Umbrella

In a serene, sunlit village nestled in the pine-scented hills of Garhwal, lived ten-year-old Binya. The mountains were her home, and her days were spent herding her two cows, Neelu and Gori, through the mountain pastures.

One afternoon, while searching for her cows near a clearing, Binya came upon a group of wealthy picnickers from the city. Resting under the shade of a pine tree was a lady holding the most beautiful umbrella Binya had ever seen—a bright, twirling parasol made of sky-blue silk. 

Binya stood entranced, unable to take her eyes off the blue flower-like umbrella. As she drew closer, the city lady noticed the small pendant hanging around Binya's neck—a polished leopard's claw set in brass, a lucky charm given to her by her mother.

"What a lovely necklace!" the city lady remarked. "Is that a real leopard's claw?"

"Yes," her husband replied. "They wear them in the hills to ward off evil spirits."

"I must have it!" the lady declared. Turning to Binya, she offered, "Little girl, will you trade your necklace for two rupees?" Binya shook her head. "Five rupees then?" Binya shook her head again.

Frustrated, the lady looked at her blue umbrella. "What if I give you this blue umbrella in exchange for your leopard-claw necklace?"

Binya's eyes lit up with joy. Without a moment's hesitation, she unclasped her necklace, handed it to the lady, and took the brilliant blue umbrella into her hands.

From that day on, the blue umbrella was Binya's constant companion. She carried it everywhere—through the forest, down the steep hillsides, and across the stream, keeping it open even when the sun was mild just to gaze at its vibrant blue silk.

The umbrella became the wonder and envy of the entire village. The schoolmaster's wife wished she had one, the temple priest admired its bright shade, and the village children gathered around Binya just to hold it for a moment.

However, no one coveted the blue umbrella more than old Ram Bharosa, who ran a small tea and sweet shop on the Tehri road. Every day, as the Tehri bus stopped near his shop, travelers would admire the blue umbrella twirling in Binya's hand.

"Binya, that umbrella is of no use to you," Ram Bharosa called out one afternoon as she walked past his shop. "The sun isn't that hot, and silk isn't made for heavy rain. Sell it to me for five rupees."

"No," Binya replied firmly.

"I'll give you ten... twelve rupees!" Ram Bharosa persisted.

Binya shook her head with a smile and walked away, twirling her umbrella.

Unable to buy the umbrella, Ram Bharosa grew sullen and bitter. When Binya's older brother, Bijju, came to the shop, Ram Bharosa refused to give him sweets on credit. He hired a boy named Rajaram from the neighboring village to clean dishes and run errands at the shop.

Noticing his master's obsessive longing, Rajaram approached Ram Bharosa. "Babuji, why are you so sad?"

"It's all due to that girl Binya and her wretched umbrella," Ram Bharosa sighed.

"What will you give me if I get the blue umbrella for you?" Rajaram whispered.

Ram Bharosa hesitated, then whispered back, "I'll give you three rupees... but do not let anyone know!"

A few days later, while Binya was resting in a grassy meadow, Rajaram quietly crept up, snatched the blue umbrella, and ran into the forest. Waking up, Binya spotted the thief running away. "My umbrella! He's stolen my umbrella!" she shouted, sprinting after him.

Hearing her cries, Bijju joined the chase. Together, they cornered Rajaram in a shallow mountain stream. As Bijju grabbed him, Rajaram dropped the umbrella and confessed in terror, "Let me go! It was Ram Bharosa—he paid me three rupees to steal it!"

News of the theft spread like wildfire through the hills. Disgusted by the old man's dishonesty, the villagers boycotted Ram Bharosa's shop. Schoolchildren walked past without buying toffees, and no one stopped for tea. Ram Bharosa sat alone in his empty shop, watching his livelihood crumble. "Not from Ram, the umbrella thief," the children would whisper.

Kindhearted Binya watched the old man's misery with growing sorrow. She felt responsible for his ruin. *An umbrella is just a pretty thing,* she thought to herself. *It isn't worth a man's livelihood.*

One afternoon, Binya walked into Ram Bharosa's empty shop, placed her blue umbrella on the counter, and bought a single toffee. Leaving a coin, she turned and walked away, leaving the blue umbrella behind.

"Binya! Wait!" Ram Bharosa called out, running to the doorway. "You forgot your umbrella!"

"You keep it," Binya called back with a warm smile. "I don't need it anymore."

Ram Bharosa was moved to tears by her generosity. He placed the blue umbrella outside his shop for anyone in the village to borrow whenever it rained. Villagers returned to his shop, and laughter echoed once again along Tehri road.

A few weeks later, a mountain bear strayed near the shop during the night and left behind a sharp, curved bear's claw stuck in a wooden pillar. Ram Bharosa retrieved the claw, took it to a silversmith in town, and had it set in a beautiful silver locket.

The next day, as Binya walked past with her cows, Ram Bharosa called her over. "Binya, take this! You gave me the umbrella—now take this silver bear's claw necklace from me!"

Binya beamed as she put on the silver pendant. As she walked home through the golden afternoon light, her cows bells chimed, and her happy song echoed across the hills of Garhwal.
