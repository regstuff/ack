# Aniruddha

Usha was the beautiful daughter of the noble Asura king, Bana of the thousand arms. Bana was a fierce devotee of Lord Shiva. One day, with his thousand arms, he played various musical instruments as Shiva danced the Tandava. Shiva was immensely pleased and granted him a boon, assuring him of his protection. 

But after some time, Bana grew vain. He complained to Shiva, "My thousand arms are becoming a useless burden to me. Even without them, there is none equal to me, save you." Shiva, to put an end to this pride, cursed him: "O vain and foolish one, in a future encounter, one equal to you will humiliate you." But Bana welcomed the curse, looking forward to meeting a peer in fight.

A few days later, Usha and her friends were picking flowers in their favorite grove. As the day grew warm, a tired Usha drowsed under the trees and had a vivid dream. She woke up with a start, startling her friends. She had dreamt of a handsome prince who won her heart, only to disappear, leaving her in grief.

Her close companion, Chitralekha, the daughter of one of Bana's ministers, sent the others away to console her. "Do not grieve, my princess. If this prince exists, I can and shall bring him to you. But first, you will have to identify him for me from among the many portraits I shall paint for you." 

Usha only knew that his skin was dark blue and he wore yellow garments. Chitralekha began to paint. She painted a number of gods, Gandharvas, and Yakshas, but Usha kept saying no. Then, among the various portraits, she painted Krishna and his son Pradyumna. Usha was abashed, for they closely resembled the prince of her dream. Finally, Chitralekha drew the portrait of Aniruddha, son of Pradyumna. 

"This is he!" Usha cried, casting her eyes down in sudden shyness. 

"He shall soon be here with you," promised Chitralekha. 

With the help of her yogic powers, Chitralekha conveyed herself through the night sky to Dwaraka, and into the palace of Aniruddha. She found him fast asleep on his bed. Using her magic, she transported the sleeping prince, bed and all, back to the kingdom of Bana and into the presence of her dear princess. 

When Aniruddha woke up, Usha explained what had happened. But Aniruddha, captivated by the lovely Usha, was not able to take his eyes off her. "I cannot dream of leaving you. I would rather stay here and delight in your company," he told her. Thus imprisoned by the tender attentions of Usha, Aniruddha married her in secret. She entertained him with song and dance, and they spent many happy days together hidden away in her chambers.

But such stolen happiness could not last forever. The palace attendants grew suspicious because the princess hardly ever stepped out of her rooms. They spied on her and discovered the Yadava prince. 

They went straight to Bana. "O King, forgive us. We are afraid that your daughter has secretly married the Yadava prince, Aniruddha."

"What!" Bana thundered, "Enough! Let me see this for myself."

Bana stormed into Usha's room with his guards. "Seize him! Kill him!" he ordered. 

But Aniruddha was quick. He grabbed a mace from one of the attendants and slew all of them before they could even realize what was happening. Bana, livid with rage, stepped in. Using his thousand arms, he overpowered Aniruddha and bound him from head to foot using live serpents for ropes. Aniruddha was taken away and imprisoned in the court of Bana.

Meanwhile, all of Dwaraka was upset about the disappearance of Aniruddha. Four months passed with no trace of him. Then, Sage Narada came to see Krishna with news. "Aniruddha is in the court of Bana. Bana has bound him with live serpents and imprisoned him."

Krishna immediately called a council of all his men. "Get your horses, chariots, and arms ready for battle. Aniruddha is alive, but in danger. I have just had news of him." 

Krishna, Pradyumna, and Satyaki set out for Bana's kingdom with hundreds of warriors to rescue Aniruddha. When they reached the city gates, Krishna gave the command to attack, and his army surged forward, laying waste to Bana's defenses.

Bana's rage knew no bounds. He came out with an equally large army, and a great battle ensued. Shiva, who had promised Bana his protection at all times, came to his aid with his son, Kartikeya. 

Krishna fought with Shiva. In no time at all, Krishna destroyed Shiva's weird hordes and unleashed the Jrimbhanastra weapon, which kept Shiva completely stupefied. Elsewhere on the battlefield, Pradyumna vanquished Kartikeya with ease.

Blind with anger, Bana rushed at Krishna. With his thousand arms, he drew five hundred bows at the same time and set two arrows in each. But Krishna was too quick for him. With his glowing discus, Krishna cut off nine hundred and ninety-six of Bana's arms, killed his charioteer and horses, and destroyed his chariot. Krishna blew his conch in triumph and was about to follow the fleeing Bana to finish him when Shiva intervened.

"Krishna, Bana is devoted to me, and I have promised him my protection. I plead with you to spare him," Shiva said.

Krishna smiled at Shiva. "It is only to humble him that I have cut off all except four of his arms. And to relieve the earth of her burden, I have destroyed his army. He still has four arms left. Besides, I grant him immortality and everlasting youth."

Shiva turned to his followers. "Bring Bana to us."

When Bana arrived, Shiva told him, "Krishna has pardoned as well as blessed you. Bring his grandson and your daughter to him." 

Bana bowed and returned with Usha and Aniruddha. The whole city of Dwaraka was anxious for the return of their prince. They were overjoyed when, placing Usha and Aniruddha in the front, the victorious Krishna, Pradyumna, and Satyaki returned safely home.
