# Kesiya

Long ago, there lived a terrible miser named Kesiya. He was the King's treasurer, a man of immense wealth, but he absolutely hated spending a single coin. 

One day, as he was walking home, he saw a man eating a plate of hot, syrupy sweet pancakes called *poodas*. 

"It's such a long time since I have eaten poodas," Kesiya thought, his mouth watering. "I could ask my wife to make some... but the cost of the ingredients!"

When Kesiya reached home, he looked so utterly depressed that his wife immediately noticed. 

"You look sad. What's the matter?" she asked. 

"Nothing," he sighed. 

"Tell me what's wrong. Is the King angry with you for some reason?" 

"No."

"Have any of the servants asked for a raise?" she guessed, knowing his nature. 

"Oh, no! They're all good people," Kesiya groaned. 

"What's the matter, then?" 

Kesiya finally broke down. "Well, if you must know... I have a craving for poodas."

His wife smiled in relief. "Is that all? I'll make some for you right away. In fact, I'll make enough for the whole neighbourhood!"

Kesiya gasped in absolute horror. "Have you taken leave of your senses, woman! What! All right, I'll make just enough for us."

"For us?" he frowned. 

"For the children, for you and for me," she explained. 

"Leave the children out," Kesiya ordered strictly. "They may not care for poodas."

"Then I'll make just enough for the two of us."

Kesiya glared at her. "You don't *really* want to eat poodas, do you?"

"Er... well..." she stammered, realizing what he meant. 

"I thought as much," Kesiya nodded in satisfaction. "Make just enough for me."

As his wife turned to go to the kitchen, Kesiya suddenly stopped her. "Wait! We must be careful. If you make the poodas in the kitchen, the sweet aroma will attract the neighbours... and then we'll have to give them some."

"I'll tell you what we'll do," he plotted. "We'll take everything we need up to the terrace on the roof, and you can make the poodas up there where no one can smell them."

So Kesiya and his exhausted wife carried all the heavy cooking utensils, the firewood, the batter, and the frying pan up the steep stairs to the terrace. 

"...up to the terrace. Nobody here... good!" Kesiya grinned, locking the terrace door behind them. "Now you can get to work."

Sometime later, the sweet smell of frying poodas filled the terrace. 

"Are they ready?" Kesiya asked eagerly. 

"Almost," his wife replied, flipping the hot pancake. 

Suddenly, a strange shadow fell across the terrace. Kesiya looked up. To his utter horror, a Buddhist monk was calmly hovering in the air above them. 

"But let them cool... Oh, we have a guest," his wife noticed. 

"A guest?" Kesiya snapped. He turned to the monk. "What do you want? Go away!" he yelled, waving his arms. 

But the monk calmly descended and hovered just above the terrace floor. 

"Do you think you can get a pooda by hovering up there?" Kesiya sneered. "I wouldn't give you one even if you were standing on this terrace."

The monk gently landed, standing perfectly still on the terrace. 

"Do... do you think I'll give you a pooda just because we are standing on the same terrace?" Kesiya stammered, taking a step back. "I wouldn't give you one even if you were standing beside me."

The monk smoothly floated across the floor, appearing right beside the terrified miser. 

"I won't give you one! I mean it!" Kesiya shrieked, backing away. He turned to his wife in a panic. "Give him a small one and let him go away."

His wife poured a tiny drop of batter into the hot oil. But magically, the drop instantly expanded, swelling into a massive, thick pooda that completely filled the entire pan! 

"Not that one! That's too big!" Kesiya yelled. "Make a small one for him."

His wife tried again, pouring an even smaller drop. But again, it swelled into an enormous pancake. "I can't help it! That's turned out even bigger," she cried. 

"The paste keeps on spreading over the whole pan. There's something very strange going on here," she warned. 

"You're just a bad cook, that's all!" Kesiya scolded her. He looked at the massive pile of giant poodas. "Give him that one on top and let him go away."

His wife tried to lift the top pooda, but it was firmly stuck to the one below it. "They are all stuck together," she grunted, pulling hard. 

"Pull!" Kesiya yelled, grabbing the other end of the pile. 

They both pulled with all their might. Suddenly, the entire pile of poodas flew into the air, and Kesiya tumbled violently backwards, crashing onto the hard stone floor. 

He sat up, rubbing his bruised head, and stared at the serene monk. "It is clear that we are not dealing with an ordinary man," Kesiya finally realized, feeling a strange sense of shame. "You can have all the poodas, respected sir."

He respectfully carried the entire tray to the monk. "Sit and eat," he offered. 

"I want them not for myself..." the monk smiled gently. "...but for my Lord Buddha and his disciples. They are waiting for me."

"Buddha!" Kesiya gasped. "F-forgive me! I did not know..." He fell to his knees in deep reverence. 

"Come with me if you wish to meet him," the monk offered. 

The monk raised his hand, and a glowing, magical staircase appeared in the air, leading all the way from the terrace down into a distant forest. Kesiya and his wife carried the heavy tray of poodas down the miraculous stairs, till finally, they reached a clearing where Lord Buddha was teaching his monks. 

Buddha accepted the offering of poodas, and they were distributed among all his hundreds of monks. Even Kesiya and his wife got their share and sat down to eat with the disciples. 

"This is the first time I have shared my food with strangers," Kesiya whispered to his wife. "And strangely, I feel incredibly happy... er, what are you staring at?"

"The poodas," she pointed. 

Kesiya looked at the tray. Everyone in the massive gathering had eaten their fill, but the tray was still as miraculously full as before! 

"Never have I seen such a wonder! This is the greatest day of my life!" Kesiya wept in joy. 

Having witnessed the true miracle of generosity, Kesiya returned home a completely changed man. The very next morning, the once-miserly treasurer woke up with a bright smile. 

"Wife, tomorrow you will make poodas for the whole neighbourhood!" he announced happily.
