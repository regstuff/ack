# Nandi Vishala

Once upon a time, a kind Brahman received the gift of a beautiful little bull-calf. He named him Nandi Vishala. He looked after the calf extremely well, and it grew into a fine, massive, and sturdy bull. 

*The kind Brahman has brought me up with loving care, as if I were his own son. I must repay him for all his trouble,* Nandi Vishala thought. 

One day, the magical bull spoke to his master. "Master, go to a rich merchant in town. Tell him that your bull can easily draw a hundred heavily loaded carts. Make a bet on that for a thousand pieces of gold."

The Brahman could not believe his ears. "Am I dreaming or have I gone mad? I heard my bull talking!"

"You are not dreaming, master," Nandi Vishala assured him gently. "Do as I say. It will bring you good fortune."

"Are you sure I'll win such a bet?" the Brahman asked nervously. 

"Of course! Or I wouldn't have suggested it."

So the Brahman went to a rich merchant in the town. "Sir, whose bull do you think is the strongest in this town?"

"There are many strong bulls in this town, but mine are the strongest," the merchant boasted proudly. 

"Well, I have a bull who can easily pull a hundred heavily loaded carts," the Brahman challenged. "Can yours do better than that?"

The merchant laughed out loud. "You must be joking! I'm not! It's impossible! No bull can draw a hundred loaded carts."

"I'm willing to make a wager on that," the Brahman smiled. 

"Agreed! The stake shall be a thousand pieces of gold!" the merchant laughed, absolutely certain of an easy victory. 

On the appointed day, the Brahman was ready with a hundred heavy wooden carts, all loaded to the brim with sand, gravel, and heavy stones. He yoked Nandi Vishala to the very first cart. 

*A thousand pieces of gold will soon be mine!* the Brahman thought greedily. *Add to that the thousand I've saved over the past few years, and I'll have two thousand pieces of gold! With that money, I'll buy many more bulls and make many more wagers till I become the richest man in town! Everyone will have to bow to my wishes!*

These greedy thoughts made the Brahman incredibly arrogant and quite unlike his usual kind self. He grabbed a heavy whip. 

"Come on, you rascal! Pull! Be quick, you rascal!" the Brahman screamed, cracking the whip. 

The bull was absolutely shocked by his beloved master's cruel words and terrible behaviour. *Well, I'm not the rascal he calls me! I won't move!* Nandi Vishala decided, planting his hooves firmly into the dirt. 

No matter how much the Brahman yelled, the bull refused to pull the carts. 

The merchant was jubilant. "Ha! Ha! Come on, sir! You've lost a thousand gold pieces!"

Unyoking Nandi Vishala, the heartbroken Brahman sadly walked away. He had to go and bring the gold he had kept hidden at home to pay his debt. "All my life's savings gone!" he wept. 

Later, at home, the Brahman threw himself onto his bed, crying bitterly. 

"Why did you call me a rascal?" Nandi Vishala asked gently, walking up to the bed. "Have I ever broken a pot or gored anyone or...?"

"No, never, my child!" the Brahman sobbed, deeply ashamed of his arrogant outburst. 

Nandi Vishala immediately felt incredibly sorry for the Brahman. "All is not yet lost," the bull comforted him. "Go and make the wager again. Let the stake be two thousand pieces of gold this time. Only remember, don't ever call me a rascal again!"

The Brahman immediately went to the merchant and offered to make the exact same wager as before. 

"The man seems to be a fool!" the merchant thought, laughing. "You are not content with losing a thousand pieces of gold?"

"I am so certain of winning that I'm willing to bet two thousand this time," the Brahman insisted. 

"Two thousand! All right, I accept!" the merchant agreed. 

When the loaded carts and the bull were ready once again, the Brahman gently patted the magnificent animal. "Now then, my fine fellow! Pull the carts along."

With a single, mighty tug, Nandi Vishala effortlessly pulled the entire train of a hundred heavily loaded carts, marching forward until the very last cart stood exactly where the first had been! 

"Amazing!" the merchant gasped in pure shock. 

The merchant happily handed over the gold. "You deserve every one of these gold pieces!"

The Brahman took the two thousand pieces of gold and walked home a much happier, richer, and far wiser man.
