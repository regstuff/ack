# The Queen's Necklace

One sunny day, the queen and her attendants were enjoying a bath in the royal pool. A young maid was tasked with sitting on the banks to watch over the queen's beautiful clothes and precious jewellery. As the afternoon wore on, the warm sun made the girl drowsy, and she soon drifted off to sleep.

High up in a nearby tree, a female monkey had been watching the sparkling jewels with great interest. "That pearl necklace would look wonderful on me," she thought mischievously. "How can I get it without being seen? Oh, she's dozing. This is my chance!"

The monkey quietly climbed down, snatched the gleaming pearl necklace, and scrambled back up the tree. She hid the stolen treasure in a hollow branch and sat over it, waiting to see what would happen. 

Soon, the maid awoke. She gasped in horror. "The queen's necklace is gone! Thief! Thief! A thief has stolen the queen's necklace!"

The palace guards came rushing to the scene. "Did you see which way he went?" the captain demanded.

"No!" the terrified girl cried. 

"He must have jumped over the wall. Let's look outside!" The guards poured out of the palace gates. The very first person they spotted was a poor, innocent woodcutter carrying a bundle of sticks. 

"There he is! Catch him!" they shouted. 

Seeing the angry, armed guards charging at him, the woodcutter panicked. *They say these soldiers are cruel, heartless fellows,* he thought. *I wonder why they want me, but I'd better not stop to ask!* He dropped his wood and ran for his life, but he tripped and fell with a heavy thump. 

"Where's the necklace?" the captain roared, hauling the woodcutter to his feet. "Don't pretend to be innocent! I'll bang your head against a tree!"

"Oh, my God! These ruffians are just looking for an excuse to beat me," the woodcutter thought frantically. "No, no, don't hurt me! I'll tell you everything! I gave the necklace to... to a priest!"

The guards immediately hauled the woodcutter into town and dragged a bewildered priest out of his home. "Where's the necklace?" they demanded.

The priest, seeing the angry guards, thought the exact same thing: *This is no doubt happening to me because of some sin I have committed in my past life.* "Please don't touch me," he begged. "I'll tell you everything. I gave it to that musician."

The guards dragged the musician from his house. Terrified, the musician pointed at a passing palanquin. "I gave the necklace to... to that dancer!"

The dancer was stopped and pulled from her carriage. "Where's the necklace?" they demanded.

"I've never seen him before in my life!" the dancer protested indignantly, glaring at the musician. "Why are you looking at me like that? Stay away from me! Stay away from me, you big bully!"

Confused and angry, the guards arrested all four of them—the woodcutter, the priest, the musician, and the dancer—and dragged them before the King and his wise Dewan. 

The King interrogated them. The woodcutter swore he gave it to the priest, the priest swore he gave it to the musician, and the musician swore he gave it to the dancer, who vehemently denied it all. 

"Lock them all up!" the King commanded in frustration. "He's lying!"

But the clever Dewan stroked his chin thoughtfully. "Yes, your majesty," he agreed quietly. But later, he went to the King privately. "The palace is well-guarded," the Dewan reasoned. "The woodcutter could not have come in and gone out without being seen. He's lying to save himself from a beating. The other two must be lying for the exact same reason. But if they are innocent... who is the guilty one?"

The Dewan took a walk through the palace gardens. He noticed the monkeys playing in the trees above the pool. "Who could have... Oh! I think I know. Only a monkey could have taken that necklace without being seen."

The Dewan quickly hatched a plan. "Catch some of those monkeys," he ordered the guards. "Put cheap bead necklaces around their necks and let them go."

The guards did as they were told. The monkeys, thrilled with their new shiny beads, swung through the trees, proudly showing them off to everyone. 

Deep in her hollow, the female monkey who had stolen the real necklace saw her friends showing off. "They are so happy with their glass beads," she thought smugly. "Let them see my pearls. Their eyes will pop out!"

She proudly pulled the queen's pearl necklace from the hollow and draped it around her neck. 

"There's a monkey wearing the queen's necklace! Catch it!" the guards yelled. 

Using the bead necklaces, they tricked the monkey into coming closer and swapped the beads for the pearls. The Dewan triumphantly returned the necklace to the King. The King, realizing his mistake, immediately released the four innocent prisoners. 

"We must apologise to the dancer," the Dewan smiled. "As for the others, let them go with a warning to be more truthful in future!"
