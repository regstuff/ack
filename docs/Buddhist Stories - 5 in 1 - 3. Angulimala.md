# Angulimala: The Bandit Who Took Refuge in Buddha

In the kingdom of Kosala, governed by King Prasenajit from his capital at Shravasti, a child was born to the royal priest, Garga. On the midnight of the infant's birth, a strange phenomenon occurred—every weapon in the royal armory and across the houses of Kosala blazed with a brilliant, supernatural light.

Perturbed by the omen, Garga consulted a renowned astrologer, who read the stars with a heavy heart. "I regret to inform you that your newborn son is destined to become a notorious murderer—a terror to all people."

Shaken, Garga rushed to the palace and offered to sacrifice the infant to save the realm. But King Prasenajit refused. "Lord Buddha has taught us to believe in the innate goodness of every living soul. Educate your son well under your guidance, and he will grow up to be a good citizen."

Garga named his son **Ahimsaka** ("The Harmless One"). True to the King's hope, Ahimsaka grew into a brilliant, gentle, and obedient youth, devoted to his studies. 

At the age of fourteen, Ahimsaka traveled to the famous university city of Takshashila to study under a renowned master. Intelligent and humble, Ahimsaka quickly outshone all his peers, winning the deep affection of his guru.

His rapid success ignited bitter jealousy among the older disciples. Plotting to destroy him, the disciples staged a deception. As the guru walked past the courtyard, they loudly whispered, "Ahimsaka has become arrogant. Indeed, he thinks he is more learned than our master... and our master's wife agrees!"

Suspicions taking root in his mind, the guru walked into his house and found Ahimsaka sitting near his wife, absorbed in academic discussion. Misinterpreting the scene, the enraged guru shouted, "Ahimsaka! Get up! You have become arrogant and ungrateful! You have no place here—leave at once!"

Baffled and heartbroken, Ahimsaka tried to explain, but his guru turned him out into the street. 

Ahimsaka returned to Shravasti, but when he confessed his expulsion to his father, Garga disowned him in rage. "Don't show me your accursed face! Go away!" Garga roared, slamming the door.

Shunned by society and rejected by his family for a crime he had never committed, bitterness turned Ahimsaka's heart to stone. *I was innocent, yet they brand me a criminal,* he thought bitterly. *I shall take revenge on the society that rejected me!*

Retiring to the wild Jalini forest along the trade route to Kaushambi, Ahimsaka became a bloodthirsty highwayman. He swore a grim oath to slay one thousand human victims, cutting off a finger from each victim to keep count and wearing them as a grisly garland around his neck. The people began calling him **Angulimala** ("He with the Garland of Fingers").

Angulimala struck terror across Kosala. Merchants, travelers, and villagers avoided the forest. He accumulated nine hundred and ninety-nine fingers in his bloody garland, needing just one final victim to complete his thousand.

Desperate citizens appealed to King Prasenajit, who dispatched a large detachment of forest guards to capture the murderer. But Angulimala ambushed the soldiers from high cliffs, hurling massive boulders and scattering the royal forces. 

Hearing of the terror in Jalini forest, Lord Buddha set out alone on foot toward Angulimala's haunts. Herdsmen warned him, "Monk, do not enter that forest! The murderer Angulimala spares no one!" But Buddha walked calmly onward.

From a rocky ledge, Angulimala spotted the solitary monk walking down the trail. *My final victim!* he chuckled, unsheathing his curved scimitar and charging down the hill.

"Halt, monk! Halt!" Angulimala roared, sprinting after the yellow-robed figure.

Yet, to his bewilderment, no matter how fast Angulimala ran, he could not close the distance. Lord Buddha walked at a slow, peaceful pace, yet remained forever a step ahead of the gasping murderer.

Exhausted and drenched in sweat, Angulimala stopped, leaning against his sword. "Monk! Why don't you stop? Stop moving!"

Buddha turned and looked at him with eyes of infinite compassion. "I have stopped, Angulimala. I am at rest. It is you who are in perpetual, restless motion."

Angulimala blinked in confusion. "What do you mean? You were walking, yet you say you have stopped!"

"My mind is at peace because I have ceased all violence against living beings," Buddha replied gently. "You, my child, are trapped in perpetual motion because your heart is consumed by anger and fear."

The soft, divine address—*"my child"*—shattered the wall of hatred around Angulimala's soul. No one had called him a child or shown him love since his fall. 

"Don't you know who I am?" Angulimala gasped, raising his blood-stained scimitar. "I am Angulimala! I want your fingers and your life!"

"Take them, my son, if it brings peace to your troubled mind," Buddha offered serenely.

Overwhelmed by the Master's boundless love, the scimitar slipped from Angulimala's trembling fingers. He collapsed at Buddha's feet, sobbing bitterly. "Master! Forgive me! Henceforth I shall never kill! Admit me into your fold!"

Buddha raised him up gently. "Get up, my child."

Buddha brought Angulimala to his monastery on the outskirts of Shravasti, ordaining him as a monk.

The following morning, King Prasenajit arrived at the monastery with his cavalry, preparing for a fresh expedition against the killer. "Master, I have come for your blessings to exterminate the monster Angulimala," the King announced.

"Supposing Angulimala gives up violence and lives as a peaceful monk, what would you do?" Buddha asked softly.

"I would salute him, Lord, but I cannot imagine such a miracle," Prasenajit replied.

Buddha pointed to a bald-headed monk quietly watering plants nearby. "He has become one. There he is."

The King stared in awe, saluting the transformed murderer before departing in deep reverence.

As a monk, Angulimala bore the heavy weight of his past karma with quiet fortitude. One morning while seeking alms, he heard a woman screaming in labor agony inside a cottage, her life in grave danger. 

Deeply moved, Angulimala returned to Buddha. "Master, how can I ease her misery?"

Buddha instructed him: "Go to her and say: 'Sister, since I was born anew into the spiritual life, I have not intentionally taken a single life. By the truth of this declaration, may you and your child be safe!'"

Angulimala ran back and recited the solemn blessing. Instantly, the woman's agony ceased, and she gave birth to a healthy baby boy.

Days later, while walking through the markets of Shravasti, an angry mob recognized the former murderer. "It's Angulimala!" someone yelled. "Kill him before he kills us!"

Stones, sticks, and mud rained down upon the monk. Blood poured from his forehead, and his robes were torn to shreds. Yet Angulimala did not raise a hand or utter a word in defense, accepting the blows as the natural fruition of his past deeds.

Crawling back to the monastery bleeding profusely, Angulimala collapsed at Buddha's feet. 

"Do you feel anger toward those who beat you, my son?" Buddha asked gently, cradling his head.

"No, Father," Angulimala whispered with a peaceful smile. "When I killed people, I did not know what I was doing. They too did not know what they were doing today. I am at peace with the world."

Reciting *"Buddham Sharanam Gacchami"*, Angulimala breathed his last in profound serenity. Sitting beneath the Bodhi tree, Lord Buddha declared to his assembly: *"Angulimala was a great soul. He conquered vice, cruelty, and anger, and has attained Nirvana."*
