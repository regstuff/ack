# Satyakama

Gautama was one of the foremost sages in ancient India. Many seekers of truth found their way to his peaceful forest school. 

One day, a boy of eight years approached the great sage with folded hands. "Salutations to you, O master. Accept me, Satyakama, as your disciple."

Gautama looked kindly at the boy. "Tell me, child, to which family do you belong?"

Satyakama lowered his head. "I don't know to which family I belong, nor does my mother. I have no father, sir. My mother, Jabala, has asked me to call myself 'Satyakama Jabala'. Please take me as your disciple."

A senior disciple scoffed. "Sir, only a Brahmana is entitled to sacred knowledge. Satyakama may not be one."

But Gautama shook his head. "He who speaks the truth without fear or hesitation is a Brahmana. Satyakama, I accept you as my disciple. What goal have you set for yourself?"

"Sir, I want to experience Brahman—the supreme reality," Satyakama declared. 

The master became thoughtful. "This child is truthful and sincere," he thought. "I will help him in achieving his goal." 

Gautama took Satyakama to the cattle pen. "There are four hundred weak and sickly cows and bulls here," the sage instructed. "Take them to a distant forest. Come back only when they are fat and healthy and number over a thousand."

Satyakama obeyed at once. Driving the cattle into the deep, isolated forest, he built a small hut and settled in. At first, he was confused. *I told the guru that I want to experience Brahman. I wonder why he sent me to the forest? Is Brahman to be found in the forest?*

But soon, Satyakama stopped worrying. "How peaceful it is here! And everywhere I look, I see the beauty of nature! The sun, the sky, the river, the trees, the flowers..." 

For years, Satyakama took excellent care of the cattle. At dusk, while the animals rested, he meditated deeply. *Where should I look for Brahman? Where is he?*

Under his loving care, the cows improved in health, and their numbers multiplied until they surpassed a thousand. 

"Now that the cattle number over a thousand, I have fulfilled the task given to me by my guru," Satyakama realized. 

As he was preparing to return, he noticed a tiny calf was missing. He searched frantically in the north, south, east, and west until he finally found it. He hugged the calf tightly. 

That evening, as he gazed at an aged bull, a strange peace washed over him. Suddenly, it seemed as if the bull was speaking to him. "Brahman is in the North. He is in the South, the East, and the West."

"That's true!" Satyakama gasped, his eyes wide. "Why search for Him? He is present everywhere." 

The next day, Satyakama began the long journey back to his guru's ashram, driving the massive herd before him. At dusk, he built a campfire and sat beside it. As he watched the flames dance, he felt as if the fire was speaking to him. 

"Brahman pervades the earth, the sky, the atmosphere, and the ocean," the fire seemed to crackle. 

"Yes, Brahman is endless like the sky," Satyakama realized.

The next morning, as the sun rose over the river, a beautiful swan flew past. "Brahman is in the sun, in the moon, in the lightning, and in the fire," the swan's graceful flight seemed to say. 

"Of course! It is Brahman who makes the sun shine!" Satyakama smiled.

Satyakama stood completely still as a flood of new thoughts rushed into his mind. "Then who makes me breathe? Who makes me see, hear, and think?" 

A small bird fluttered past his face, chirping sweetly. "It is Brahman, it is Brahman, it is Brahman," the bird sang. 

When Satyakama finally reached the forest school, he radiated a profound, inner peace. Sage Gautama looked at him in awe. 

"Satyakama, you glow like one who has attained knowledge of Brahman. How did this happen?" the sage asked.

Satyakama described his profound experiences in the forest—how the bull, the fire, the swan, and the bird had revealed the truth to him. "Nature taught me," Satyakama smiled. "But knowledge imparted by the teacher helps the most in attaining one's goal. Please instruct me, sir."

Gautama smiled warmly and imparted the final, sacred knowledge to his deserving disciple, who later became a great and revered sage himself.
