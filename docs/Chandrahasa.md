# Chandrahasa: Faith Cannot Be Vanquished

In the ancient kingdom of Kerala, King Medhavi and his queen prayed fervently to Lord Vishnu for an heir. Their prayers were answered, and a baby boy was born to them. But tragedy struck a month later when enemy forces besieged the fort, and King Medhavi was slain in battle.

Knowing the fort would fall, the Queen Mother entrusted her infant son to her faithful old nurse. "Take him through the secret tunnel and flee!" she wept, before performing Sati on her husband's funeral pyre.

The nurse carried the prince to the town of Kuntala, living in poverty and begging for alms to feed the child. When the boy turned five, the nurse passed away. Moved by the child's plight, the neighborhood women fed the sweet orphan boy.

One day, while playing with other children in the woods, the orphan longed for a marble. Praying to Lord Vishnu, he found a smooth, round black stone in the riverbed. Using it in their games, the boy won every match. Unknown to him, the sacred black stone was a divine *Shalagrama*—an emblem of Lord Vishnu's grace.

As the orphan boy grew, his gentle charm made him the leader of the village children.

One afternoon, Dushtabuddhi, the ambitious Chief Minister of Kuntala, stood on his balcony with a learned Brahman. The King of Kuntala had no sons, and Dushtabuddhi secretly plotted to seize the throne for his own son, Madana.

Pointing to the orphan playing in the street, the Brahman astrologer declared: "Mark my words, Dushtabuddhi! That boy is destined to become the King of Kuntala! Take him under your care and educate him."

Alarmed by the prophecy, Dushtabuddhi resolved to eliminate the boy. He hired two ruthless assassins, ordering them to carry the five-year-old child into the deep forest and slay him.

In the forest, as the killers raised their axes, the boy looked up innocently and began chanting *"Hari! Hari!"* Touched by his purity, the assassins hesitated. 

"We cannot kill this innocent child," one assassin said.

"Neither can I," the other agreed. "Look, he has six toes on his left foot. Let us cut off the extra toe to show the Minister as proof of his death!"

They cut off the extra toe, presented it to Dushtabuddhi, and left the child in the forest. Wild deer and monkeys fed the injured prince, until King Kalindaka of Chandanavati—a vassal ruler of Kuntala—came to the forest to hunt. Finding the abandoned child, the childless king adopted him as his son, naming him **Chandrahasa** ("One whose smile is like moonlight").

Chandrahasa was educated in scriptures, archery, and statecraft. By his eighteenth birthday, King Kalindaka formally crowned Chandrahasa as his successor.

Soon after, Dushtabuddhi visited Chandanavati. When Kalindaka proudly introduced his adopted son, mentioning that he had found him alone in the forest thirteen years ago, Dushtabuddhi realized with horror that Chandrahasa was the same street child he had ordered killed!

Determined to complete the murder, Dushtabuddhi handed Chandrahasa a sealed letter addressed to his son Madana in Kuntala. "Ride immediately to Kuntala and deliver this letter to my son. It is highly confidential."

The letter contained a secret instruction in Sanskrit: *"Dear Madana, the bearer of this letter is destined to become the King of Kuntala. Therefore, without waiting for my return, give him visha (poison)."*

Chandrahasa mounted his horse and rode toward Kuntala. Feeling exhausted as he approached the capital, he stopped at a royal pleasure garden and fell asleep under a tree, with the letter tucked in his sash.

It so happened that Vishaya, Dushtabuddhi's beautiful daughter, was gathering flowers in the garden with her maidens. Seeing the handsome prince sleeping beneath the banyan tree, she picked up the letter out of curiosity.

Reading her father's handwriting (*"...give him visha..."*), Vishaya gasped. *Father must have mis-spelt my name in his haste! He obviously meant Vishaya! What a handsome man he has chosen to be my husband!*

Using a plant stalk and dark kajal from her eyes, Vishaya cleverly added the letter *"ya"* to *"visha"*, altering the command to read: *"give him Vishaya"* (marry him to Vishaya). She tucked the letter back into his sash and departed.

When Chandrahasa woke up and delivered the letter to Madana, the dutiful son read his father's command. Believing his father had found the perfect groom, Madana arranged a grand wedding that very evening, marrying Chandrahasa to Vishaya!

When Dushtabuddhi returned a few days later, he was shocked to find Chandrahasa married to his daughter! Madana showed him the letter (*"...give him Vishaya..."*). 

Realizing his daughter's intervention had foiled his plot, Dushtabuddhi seethed with rage. He immediately planned a third assassination. Sending for Chandrahasa, he instructed: "Go to the temple of Goddess Kali outside the city gates tonight at midnight to offer prayers." 

Dushtabuddhi secretly instructed royal killers to hide at the Kali temple and slay whoever arrived alone at midnight.

Meanwhile, the old King of Kuntala had a vision of his impending death. He consulted his priest, who advised: "Marry your daughter to Dushtabuddhi's noble new son-in-law, Chandrahasa, and crown him King!"

Madana rode out to find Chandrahasa. Seeing Chandrahasa heading toward the Kali temple, Madana stopped him. "The King wishes to see you immediately at the palace! Take my horse and go—I will carry your offerings to the Kali temple in your place."

Chandrahasa took Madana's horse, rode to the palace, was married to the Princess of Kuntala, and crowned King of Kuntala!

Meanwhile, at the Kali temple, Dushtabuddhi's hired assassins mistook Madana for Chandrahasa and killed him in the dark.

When news reached Dushtabuddhi that Madana had gone to the temple instead of Chandrahasa, the minister rushed to the shrine, found his own son lying dead in blood, and stabbed himself in grief and guilt.

Arriving at the temple shortly after, King Chandrahasa was horrified to find both Madana and Dushtabuddhi dead. Falling before the image of Goddess Kali, Chandrahasa prayed intensely to Lord Vishnu, drawing his sword to offer his own life to restore theirs.

Suddenly, Lord Vishnu appeared in a flash of divine light. "Hold, Chandrahasa! I will not allow a pure soul like you to die!" 

With a wave of his hand, Lord Vishnu restored Madana and Dushtabuddhi to life, cleansing Dushtabuddhi of all envy and hatred. 

Tears of repentance flowing down his face, Dushtabuddhi embraced Chandrahasa. The noble prince returned to the capital, where he ruled over Kuntala and Chandanavati with his two wives, Vishaya and the Princess, in peace, prosperity, and righteousness.
