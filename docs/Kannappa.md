# Kannappa

It was the custom of the hunters of Uduppur, in ancient Tamil Nadu, to begin the day with prayers to Lord Murugan. Their chief, Nagan, invoked the blessings of the Lord for his tribe. 

Nagan and his wife Tattai were childless. "Tattai, have faith in Lord Murugan. You too will be a mother one day," Nagan consoled her. They arranged a festival to propitiate Lord Murugan, praying for a son. 

Their prayers were answered. A son was born to them. "He is strong-limbed. We'll name him Tinnan," Nagan declared. The boy later came to be known as Kannappa. 

Tinnan grew under the loving care of his parents into a youth of extraordinary strength. He practised hard and became an expert archer, learning how to wield the spear and the sword. 

When Tinnan was about sixteen years old, some villagers approached his father. "Nagan, wild animals are destroying our crops and cattle. Please come to our help." 

"I am getting old. I will send my son, Tinnan," Nagan replied. 

Tinnan and his companions accompanied the fear-stricken villagers. In the forest, Tinnan valiantly fought and killed a tiger that attacked him. Later, they caught a boar in a snare. The boar broke loose, but Tinnan pursued and killed it at the foot of the Kalahasti hill. 

"I am thirsty, Nanan. I'll go with you to fetch some water," Tinnan said to his companion while another, Kadan, lit a fire to roast the boar. 

As the two climbed the hill, Tinnan felt a strange sensation. "Nanan, something is happening to me. Why does my heart feel so light and joyful?" 

Suddenly he broke into a run towards a temple. When Tinnan entered the temple, he was overwhelmed by devotion upon seeing the Shiva Linga. "Lord!" he cried, shedding tears of joy. "Lord, I have found you. Lord, how lonely you must be! From now on, I'll remain with you." 

Nanan told him that a priest used to come and worship there. Tinnan decided to bring food for the Lord. "You two may eat. I have to take food for my lord," he told his companions. 

Tinnan carefully chose the tastiest piece of roasted meat. "Hmm. This is delicious. My lord will like it." 

He also wanted to take flowers and water. "How can I carry both these things at the same time? The flowers will get squashed! Of course! It is simple!" he thought. 

With the meat in his hands, he filled his mouth with water from the stream, and gathered flowers in his hair. 

When he reached the shrine, he gave the deity a ritual bath with the water from his mouth, decorated the Linga with the flowers from his head, and placed the meat before the Lord. "Lord, I have brought you the tastiest piece of meat. Please accept it." 

That night, Tinnan stood guard outside the temple to protect the Lord from wild animals, while his companions returned to the village to inform Nagan of the change in Tinnan. Nagan came to fetch him, but Tinnan refused to leave. "Father, I cannot leave. This place is my home now." 

The next morning, while Tinnan was out hunting, the temple priest arrived. He was shocked to see the offerings of meat. "Lord of all Lords, who has defiled your shrine? He must be punished!" 

The priest cleaned the shrine, bathed in the lake, brought water in a clean vessel, gathered flowers, and completed his formal devotions. 

But as soon as he left, Tinnan returned with his offering of roasted meat, water in his mouth, and flowers in his hair. He removed the priest's flowers and performed his own worship. 

This happened every day. Tinnan and the priest alternately offered worship. The priest grumbled about the defilement, while Tinnan worshipped with pure, innocent love. 

Meanwhile, at Kailas, Lord Shiva told Parvati to "wait and see" who was the true devotee—the holy Brahman or the uncouth hunter. 

On the morning of the sixth day, when the priest came to the shrine, he saw that one of the Linga's eyes was bleeding! "Wh... what! The eye is bleeding! This is something unheard of! Some disaster is likely to happen! I'd better go," the frightened priest fled. 

A few minutes later, Tinnan arrived. "My Lord, your eye is bleeding! Who has hurt you? You must be in pain!" 

He ran to get herbs and applied a paste to the affected eye. "There, this will make you well again." 

But the bleeding didn't stop. "What shall I do to relieve your suffering, Lord?" Tinnan wondered. Then he had an idea. "I know what to do. I will give you my eye." 

With an arrow, Tinnan gouged out one of his own eyes and replaced the Lord's bleeding eye with it. "Lord, you are cured! I am so happy." 

But Tinnan's joy was short-lived. "Oh! The left eye has begun to bleed!" 

"I know what to do now. I will give you my other eye, Lord," Tinnan decided without hesitation. 

He raised the arrow to his left eye, but paused. "But, Lord, when I take out my other eye, how will I know where to place it?" 

He found a way out. He placed his foot on the left eye of the Lord to mark the place, and got ready to take out his other eye. 

At that moment, Lord Shiva appeared and stopped him. "Tinnan, stop! Many have prayed to me for sight. But only you have offered me your eyes." 

The next moment, Tinnan's sight was fully restored. He stood with folded hands before his beloved master. 

"Peerless Kannappa, come with me to Kailas, where you shall ever be by my side," Lord Shiva blessed him. 

"Lord, I am truly blessed," Kannappa, the Eye-Saint, replied with devotion.
