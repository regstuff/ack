# Subhas Chandra Bose

Subhas Chandra Bose was born in Cuttack, Orissa, on January 23, 1897. He was a brilliant, inquisitive boy, the son of Prabhavati and Janaki Nath Bose, a famous lawyer. 

When Subhas was five, he was admitted to the Protestant European School at Cuttack. He was an incredibly bright student and quickly became a favorite among the teachers. However, as he grew older, he began to notice the stark differences between the Indian students and their British teachers. 

"Have you heard? Bengali has been made compulsory for Bengalees at the matric level," a classmate told him one day. "But Bengali is not taught at our school."

Realizing the importance of his roots, a twelve-year-old Subhas transferred to the Ravenshaw Collegiate School. "So you have never learnt Bengali," the headmaster noted. 

"No. But I soon will, sir," Subhas promised. 

The headmaster, Beni Madhav Das, was a true Indian nationalist. "Never forget, we are Indians and India is a great country," he would tell his students. "While you learn your lessons, learn to love your country too."

Inspired by his teacher, Subhas began to organize his fellow students. On the eve of the first anniversary of the martyrdom of Kshudiram Bose (a young Indian revolutionary hanged by the British), Subhas proposed a secret fast. "I propose that we fast tomorrow. What do you say?" The students enthusiastically agreed. 

The British government, threatened by the rising nationalism in the school, quickly transferred Headmaster Beni Madhav Das away to Bengal. But the seeds of patriotism had already been sown in Subhas's heart.

At fifteen, Subhas discovered the writings of Swami Vivekananda. "How noble and inspiring," he thought, spending hours in deep meditation and yoga. But his soul was still restless. "I need a guru to guide me to my destiny."

After passing his matriculation exams and standing second in the entire university, Subhas traveled across India visiting famous pilgrim centers in search of a true spiritual guide. But his search proved futile. "These so-called saints are interested more in worldly matters than in spiritualism," he realized in disappointment. 

He returned to Calcutta and joined the Presidency College. However, the racist behavior of the British professors deeply angered him. Professor Oten, in particular, was notoriously abusive. 

"You natives have nothing in your brains but sawdust!" Oten yelled at them in class. "You are barbarians! It is we English who have civilized you."

The Indian students were appalled. When Oten physically assaulted a student, Subhas took charge. He organized a massive strike, rallying over a thousand students to boycott classes. It was the first organized student strike in modern India. 

Though the strike forced Professor Oten to apologize, the British Principal retaliated by rusticating Subhas from the college. Undeterred, Subhas returned home and spent his time serving the poor and sick, eventually gaining readmission to the Scottish Church College with the help of supportive Indian leaders. 

He graduated from Calcutta University at the age of 22 and traveled to England to appear for the prestigious Indian Civil Service (ICS) examination. He passed with flying colors, ranking fourth in the entire British Empire. 

But to everyone's utter shock, Subhas refused the highly coveted position. 

"I plan to resign from the service," he announced. "I appeared for my I.C.S. examination to educate myself—not to become a part of the bureaucratic machine of the British Empire."

His resignation created a massive sensation. Returning to India, he met with Mahatma Gandhi, who advised him to work with Deshabandhu Chittaranjan Das, the "uncrowned king of Bengal." Subhas quickly became Das's right-hand man, dedicating his life to the freedom struggle. 

The British government, fearing Subhas's immense popularity and brilliant organizing skills, constantly harassed and arrested him. He was thrown into horrific jails, from Calcutta to the infamous Mandalay Jail in Burma, where the inhuman conditions caused him to contract tuberculosis. 

When the British offered to release him on the condition that he leave for Europe, Subhas fiercely refused. "I refuse conditional freedom. I would rather die here." Forced by massive public protests, the government finally released him unconditionally. 

As the years passed, Subhas's vision for India's freedom began to clash with Gandhi's path of strict non-violence. Subhas believed that the British would never leave India peacefully and that a massive armed struggle was necessary. 

"The country is ready to fight," Subhas declared. 

"My differences with the Mahatma will split the party. I must resign," he eventually decided, stepping down as Congress President and forming his own radical faction, the Forward Bloc. "We demand Indian independence, here and now."

The British, terrified of a violent uprising during World War II, placed Subhas under strict house arrest in Calcutta. But Subhas was a master of disguise and evasion. 

On the midnight of January 7, 1941, a bearded Muslim man walked out of Subhas's house and got into a chauffeured car. He then boarded a train as an insurance agent named Moulvi Jiauddin. He traveled to Peshawar, posing as a deaf and dumb Pathan, crossed the rugged mountains into Afghanistan, and finally made his way to Berlin, Germany. 

The man in disguise was none other than Subhas Chandra Bose. 

In Germany, he met with Adolf Hitler to seek military help to liberate India. Realizing Germany was too far away to mount a direct attack on India, Subhas made a perilous journey. He traveled halfway across the world in a German U-boat (submarine), secretly transferring to a Japanese submarine in the middle of the ocean, and finally arrived in Southeast Asia. 

There, he took command of the Indian National Army (INA), raising a massive fighting force from Indian prisoners of war and civilian volunteers. He formed the Azad Hind Government and established the Rani of Jhansi regiment, an all-women combat unit. 

His fiery speeches electrified Indians across Asia. "Give me blood, I promise you freedom!" he roared to massive crowds. "Chalo Delhi! (Onward to Delhi!)" 

The people responded by pouring out their wealth, donating their entire life savings and jewelry to fund the INA's war effort. They gave him the title "Netaji" (Respected Leader).

The INA marched bravely alongside the Japanese army, fighting their way through the dense jungles of Burma and finally entering Indian soil at Kohima and Imphal. They raised the Indian tricolor flag, fighting ferociously against the British. 

However, the terrible monsoon rains and the eventual retreat of the Japanese forces cut off the INA's vital supply lines. Heavily outgunned and starving, the INA was forced into a tragic retreat. 

When Japan officially surrendered in 1945, the INA was forced to lay down its arms. With the British and American forces hunting him, Netaji's life was in grave danger. 

On August 17, 1945, Netaji boarded a Japanese plane for an unknown destination. What became of Netaji on that fateful trip remains a profound mystery. But his colossal sacrifice, unyielding courage, and his immortal cry of "Jai Hind!" awakened a fire in the Indian soul that ultimately drove the British out of India forever.
