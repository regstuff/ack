# Krishna: The Lord of Dwaraka

After killing Kamsa, Krishna and Balarama went to meet their true parents, Vasudeva and Devaki. To spare them from awe, Krishna used his Maya to make them forget his divinity for a while. "Overwhelmed by Krishna's words, Vasudeva clasped Krishna in a fond embrace." 

Krishna then comforted his grandfather Ugrasena and restored him to the throne. "You are now the King of the Yadavas," Krishna declared. Kamsa's cruel reign had caused many subjects to flee, but now they welcomed them back and settled them in their own homes. 

After a joyful reunion with Nanda, his foster father, the Upanayana (thread ceremony) of Balarama and Krishna was performed. The boys then set out for the ashrama of Sage Sandipani in Avanti for their formal education. 

"Within sixty-four days, they mastered all the sixty-four arts and crafts." For their gurudakshina, Sage Sandipani asked them to restore his son, who had drowned in Prabhasa. 

Krishna went to the ocean and learned that a mighty Asura named Panchajana, who dwelt in a conch shell, had taken the boy away. Krishna dived into the sea, killed the Asura, and took the shell, which came to be known as Panchajanya. He then went to the abode of Yama, the Lord of Death, and brought the boy back. 

With the blessing of their guru, the two boys returned to Mathura. 

Knowing how much his foster parents and the Gopikas missed him, Krishna sent his friend Uddhava to Vrindavana with a message. "Tell them I am well... and eagerly awaiting my return," Krishna said. 

Uddhava found Nanda, Yashoda, and the Gopikas deeply engrossed in their love for Krishna. He delivered Krishna's message: "I am the eternal soul... Be assured." Though moved by the message, they still longed for his physical presence. After staying for many months, Uddhava returned to Mathura and described their devotion to Krishna. 

Krishna then fulfilled a promise to visit Sairandhri, the woman whose hunch he had cured. Later, he visited Akrura and sent him to Hastinapura to check on the Pandavas. Akrura returned with worrying news: "Dhritarashtra favours the Kauravas and the Pandavas are not treated fairly... They even tried to poison them." 

Meanwhile, Kamsa's two queens, Asti and Prapti, had returned to their father, Jarasandha, the King of Magadha. When they told him how Kamsa was killed, Jarasandha was enraged. "I will punish him," he roared. 

Jarasandha attacked Mathura seventeen times with a huge army. Seventeen times, his army was destroyed by Krishna and Balarama. But each time, Krishna let Jarasandha go to collect another army, slowly lightening the burden of evil from the earth. 

"On the eighteenth occasion, just before Jarasandha reached Mathura, the Asura Kalayavana, heard of the might of Krishna and Balarama." Kalayavana, whom even intrepid warriors feared, attacked Mathura with an army of 23 Akshauhinis. 

Faced with two formidable enemies, Krishna decided to build an invincible fortress city in the ocean. "The city was named Dwaraka." With his divine power, Krishna transported all the Yadavas safely to the new city overnight. 

Leaving the rest of his subjects safe, Krishna came out of the main gate of Mathura, unarmed. Kalayavana saw him and gave chase. Krishna led him on a long run, always staying just out of reach, until they came upon a hillside cave. 

Krishna ducked into the cave. Kalayavana followed. In the dim light, Kalayavana saw a sleeping form covered with Krishna's yellow garment. Thinking it was Krishna, he gave it a swift kick. 

The sleeping man slowly woke up. When he opened his eyes fully and looked at Kalayavana, the mighty Asura caught fire and was reduced to a heap of ashes! 

The sleeping man was King Muchukunda. He had helped the Devas in a long battle against the Asuras. Weary, he had asked for a boon to sleep. The Devas granted it, adding: "If any fool disturbs your sleep, he will be reduced to ashes the instant you look at him." 

Krishna appeared before King Muchukunda in his resplendent form. Muchukunda was overwhelmed with devotion and bowed respectfully at Lord Krishna's feet.
