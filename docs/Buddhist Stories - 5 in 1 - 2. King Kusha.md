# King Kusha: Inner Beauty Above All

In the ancient kingdom of Kushavati, King Okkaka and his chief queen, Sheelavati, lived in deep sorrow for they had no children to inherit the throne. Queen Sheelavati performed intense prayers until Indra, the King of the Devas, appeared before her in a vision.

"I grant you a boon of two sons, Queen Sheelavati," Indra announced. "One shall be wise and master of all arts, but physically unattractive. The other shall be exceptionally handsome, but a complete fool. Which will you have first?"

"The wise one, my Lord," Sheelavati replied without hesitation.

In due course, Sheelavati gave birth to her eldest son, whom they named Kusha. Two years later, she bore her second son, Jayampati, who grew up to be remarkably handsome.

From an early age, Prince Kusha became conscious of his dark complexion and rough features. *I must excel in every art and branch of learning to make up for my looks,* he thought to himself. Through relentless effort, Kusha mastered martial arts, statecraft, music, sculpture, and painting, outshining every prince of his era.

When Kusha reached sixteen, King Okkaka wished to crown him and find him a bride. But Kusha was reluctant. *Would any beautiful princess marry an ugly fellow like me?* he worried. 

To avoid hurting his parents, Kusha devised a clever plan. Using his supreme sculpting skills, he carved a life-sized statue of a maiden out of pure gold—a figure of breathtaking, flawless beauty. 

Bringing his mother to the studio, Kusha said, "Mother, if you can find me a princess as beautiful as this golden image, I shall marry her."

Queen Sheelavati dispatched royal ministers across the subcontinent with the golden statue mounted on a chariot. "Exhibit this statue in every capital," she instructed. "When you find her living likeness, ask for her hand in marriage for Prince Kusha."

After months of searching, the envoys arrived at Sagala, the capital of the Madda kingdom. They placed the golden statue near the royal bathing ghat. 

Soon after, the old hunchback nurse of Princess Prabhavati walked down to the river with her maidservants. Spotting the golden figure through the trees, the nurse mistook the statue for Prabhavati herself! "Prabhavati! What are you doing here alone?" the nurse scolded, rushing forward and slapping the statue's cheek. 

Pain shot through the nurse's hand as it struck solid metal. "Ouch! It's a statue!" she gasped. Looking closely, the nurse laughed, "Ha! I mistook this cheap metal figure for our princess! Why, this image is ugly compared to my darling Prabhavati!"

Overhearing the nurse's words, the delighted envoys sought an immediate audience with the King of Madda, who happily accepted the marriage proposal for his daughter Prabhavati.

Back in Kushavati, Queen Sheelavati was overjoyed. But fearing Prabhavati would reject Kusha if she saw his face before marriage, Sheelavati instituted a strict royal condition: "According to our royal house tradition, a bride is not permitted to see her husband's face until she has conceived." Princess Prabhavati accepted the condition.

The wedding and coronation were celebrated with royal pomp. Living in darkness during their nights together, Prabhavati grew deeply attached to her husband's gentle voice, noble character, and soul-stirring music on the veena, without ever having seen his face.

Longing for a glimpse of his bride, King Kusha disguised himself as a royal elephant keeper. As Sheelavati escorted Prabhavati through the palace gardens, Kusha watched her from the stables. Overcome by playful mischief, he tossed a small piece of mud at her. 

Prabhavati was outraged. "How dare you, ugly fellow!" she shouted at the disguised king. "I will have my husband cut off your hands!"

Determined to see her husband's face, Prabhavati pleaded with Queen Sheelavati until the Queen arranged a procession. Handsome Prince Jayampati was dressed in royal robes to ride the lead elephant, while real King Kusha rode behind disguised as the elephant driver.

Watching from her balcony, Prabhavati admired the "handsome king", but noticed the ugly driver behind him waving at her boldly. "What an impudent elephant driver!" she complained to her nurse.

Suspicious, the old nurse ran down to the palace courtyard to inspect the royal party. To her horror, she saw the ugly driver step down first while all the ministers bowed low before him! 

The nurse rushed back to the balcony. "Princess! The man sitting behind was King Kusha! Your husband is the ugly elephant keeper!"

Heartbroken and feeling betrayed, Prabhavati refused to live with an unattractive husband. Ignoring all pleas, she boarded her chariot and fled back to her parents' palace in Sagala.

Though crushed by her departure, Kusha refused to force her. "Do not stop her," Kusha told his ministers. "I will go after her alone and win her heart through true merit."

Renouncing his royal comfort, Kusha traveled to Sagala in disguise. He took up residence near the palace and spent his nights playing soul-stirring melodies on his veena beneath Prabhavati's window. Hearing the music in the dark, Prabhavati wept, recognizing her husband's touch.

Next, Kusha apprenticed himself to the royal potter. He crafted exquisite porcelain vases depicting scenes of Prabhavati and her nurse. When the potter brought the vases to the palace, Prabhavati recognized Kusha's handiwork and smashed the vase on the floor in anger: "I don't want it!"

Undeterred, Kusha became an apprentice to the royal wicker-worker, weaving a palm-leaf fan depicting Prabhavati's likeness. When the fan was presented to her, Prabhavati threw it down, realizing Kusha was constantly near her.

Finally, Kusha gained employment as an assistant cook in the royal kitchen. His culinary skills were so extraordinary that when the King of Sagala tasted a dish cooked by Kusha, he ordered that the new cook prepare all meals for the royal princesses.

Every day, Kusha personally delivered food trays to Prabhavati's chambers. Seeing him toil as a humble cook out of sheer devotion to her, Prabhavati's heart melted with secret admiration, yet her stubborn pride kept her from admitting it. For seven long months, Kusha served her faithfully without receiving a single warm word.

Seeing Kusha's endless patience and Prabhavati's stubbornness, Lord Indra decided to resolve the impasse. Indra sent royal envoys to seven powerful neighboring kings, falsely informing each ruler that King Okkaka had cast off Princess Prabhavati, and offering her hand in marriage.

All seven kings arrived at Sagala simultaneously with their armies! Discovering they had been offered the same bride, the seven monarchs were furious and delivered an ultimatum to the King of Sagala: "Give Prabhavati to all seven of us, or prepare for war!"

Terrified of total destruction, the desperate King of Sagala declared in despair, "I have no choice! I must cut Prabhavati into seven pieces and send one piece to each king!"

Hearing her father's gruesome decree, Prabhavati was terrified. She ran to her mother's quarters weeping, "Mother! If only King Kusha were here to save me!"

"He IS here, child!" her mother revealed. "He has been working in your kitchen as a cook for seven months, enduring humiliation just to be near you!"

Prabhavati ran to the royal kitchen and fell weeping at Kusha's feet. "My Lord, forgive me! I have been vain and cruel. Save me from this terrible fate!"

Kusha lifted her up gently, wiping her tears. "Will anyone dare touch my beloved wife while I am alive? Dry your tears and return to your chambers."

Stepping into the royal assembly with his golden bow, King Kusha revealed his true identity to the King of Sagala and challenged the seven invading kings. Awed by his majestic presence and martial aura, the seven kings bowed before him. 

Displaying master diplomatic skill, Kusha arranged the grand weddings of Prabhavati's seven younger sisters to the seven kings, turning potential war into nationwide celebration.

Her pride completely dissolved by his valour, wisdom, and devotion, Queen Prabhavati proudly took King Kusha's hand, returning with him to Kushavati where they ruled together in peace and harmony for many prosperous years.
