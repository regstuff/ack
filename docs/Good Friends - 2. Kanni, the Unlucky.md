# Kanni, the Unlucky

During the reign of Brahmadatta in Varanasi, there lived a prosperous merchant called Anathapindika. 

One day, a servant came to him. "Sir! A man called Kalakanni is here to see you!" 

"Show him in!" Anathapindika said. "Kalakanni" was a name that meant 'unlucky'. 

The man walked in hesitantly. "But, sir! He looks very poor and wretched!" the servant whispered. 

"Never mind! It must be my old friend. After all, not many people have a name like his!" Anathapindika said. "Ah! It is you. How good to meet you after all these years!" 

Kalakanni explained his plight. "My luck, I guess. Whoever chose my name was right. I am really unlucky. But, truly, I have been so miserable." 

"Hush! Don't ever say that!" Anathapindika comforted him. "Not any more! Now you will stay with me and share my life. No buts! I will not let you go. After all how can I ever forget a friend like you? Those days we spent together at the Gurukula were the happiest of my life." 

"Well, I will stay if I can be of any use," Kalakanni agreed gratefully. 

Kalakanni helped Anathapindika in his work and was treated like a member of the family. "I have a big business here. I need a trustworthy friend like you to help me." 

But Anathapindika's business associates were perplexed by his loyalty. "How can you trust a stranger so much? And what an inauspicious name—Kalakanni," they muttered. "Why, even uttering such a name repeatedly might bring you bad luck." 

"Bah! I don't believe in such things," Anathapindika dismissed their superstitions. "He is no stranger! He is my childhood friend." 

"They say that a man who walks seven steps with you becomes a friend. After a fortnight of being together, he is like a family member..." Anathapindika defended his friend. "I had spent years with Kalakanni in the Gurukula. And he is a real gem, whatever be his name." 

After a few months, Anathapindika had to go on a journey. "I'll be away for a few days. We are going to our native place for a wedding." 

"But who will look after your house?" a friend asked. 

"Oh! Kalakanni will be there. I can trust him with my life. Here are the keys! Kalakanni! Take care." 

Some thieves, knowing the master was away, plotted to rob the house. "Ah! The rich Seth Anathapindika is leaving town. Let's burgle his house tonight." 

By chance, Kalakanni overheard the conversation. "Oh! How can I fight these stout rogues? The servants in the house are feeble and old. They will not be able to resist those robbers." 

But Kalakanni was clever. "But what if I can fool the thieves?" he thought. 

At dusk, he gathered the servants together. "Here! You blow loudly on the conchshell," he instructed one. "You must play on the drum. You have a sweet voice, you must sing. The rest of us will clap with the rhythm. Make as much noise as you can." 

At night, the dacoits arrived outside the house. "Come, let's enter the empty house." 

But as they crept closer, a loud cacophony of drums, conch shells, singing, and clapping erupted from inside. *AAA THUMP! THUMP! CLAP CLAP* 

"You said it was empty! It sounds as if there's a celebration going on," one thief whispered in alarm. 

"Why, there must be a real crowd inside. The seth must have returned," the leader deduced. "Let's run before anyone sees us. Leave your weapons. Let's flee." 

The next morning, the servants found the abandoned weapons. "And here are footprints! Some thieves must have got in," they realised. 

"Yes, we were to be robbed. But we made such a din that the thieves must have been scared off," Kalakanni smiled. 

When Anathapindika returned, he was told about Kalakanni's clever trick. His business associates were finally convinced. "Your Kalakanni is really clever," they admitted. 

"Of course, he is! Now do you realise why I value him so much?" Anathapindika beamed, proud of his loyal, 'unlucky' friend.
