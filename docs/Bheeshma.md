# Bheeshma: Selflessness Personified

One afternoon, as King Shantanu of Hastinapura walked along the banks of the river Ganga on a hunting trip, he beheld a woman of otherworldly beauty. Enchanted by her presence, Shantanu approached her. "O divine maiden, whoever you may be, I desire to make you my queen!"

The maiden turned to him calmly. "O King, I shall willingly become your wife, but on one condition: you must never question who I am, nor criticize anything I do, no matter how strange it may seem. The moment you speak an unkind word or question my actions, I shall leave you forever."

Deeply in love, Shantanu agreed. "So be it!"

They were married and lived in happiness. But soon, the Queen began to behave in a terrifying manner. Every time a child was born to her, she carried the newborn to the river bank and drowned it in the flowing waters, whispering softly, "This is for your good."

Horrified by her actions, Shantanu suffered in silence, holding his tongue lest he lose his beloved wife. Seven sons were born, and seven times she drowned them in the river. 

When the eighth child was born, Shantanu could restrain himself no longer. As she walked toward the river, he sprang forward. "Stop! Who are you, O wicked woman? How can you murder your own children?"

The Queen paused, her eyes filled with sorrow. "Alas! You have broken your promise. I am the Goddess Ganga. Our first seven children were the Vasus, cursed by a sage to take mortal birth. By drowning them immediately, I freed them from earthly suffering. But this eighth child was destined to live a long life on earth. He shall be called Gangeya."

Holding the infant, Ganga added, "Because you broke your word, I must leave you now. I shall take the child with me, raise him in the heavens, and return him to you when he comes of age." With those words, Goddess Ganga vanished into the river with the baby.

Bereft of his divine wife and son, Shantanu returned to his palace. For years he ruled Hastinapura wisely, but his heart remained heavy with longing.

Sixteen years passed. One day, while hunting along the Ganga, Shantanu noticed that the river's current had become strange and subdued. Walking along the bank, he discovered a handsome youth shooting a barrage of arrows with such astonishing skill that he had constructed a dam across the rushing river!

As Shantanu marveled at the youth's prowess, Goddess Ganga emerged from the waters. "Shantanu, this is Gangeya, the eighth son I bore you," she declared proudly. "He has mastered the Vedas under Sage Vasishta and weaponry under Parashurama. He is known as Devavrata. Take him home and proclaim him your heir."

With great joy, Shantanu brought Devavrata back to Hastinapura and crowned him Prince Regent. Devavrata's nobility, wisdom, and unmatched martial skill quickly won the hearts of the nobles and commoners alike.

Four years later, while walking along the banks of the Yamuna, King Shantanu breathed in a divine fragrance. Following the scent, he met Satyavati, the beautiful daughter of the chief of fishermen. Overcome with desire, Shantanu approached her father, Dasaraja, to ask for her hand in marriage.

The fisherman chief bowed respectfully. "O King, it is an honor. But I will give my daughter to you only on one condition: the son born unto Satyavati must inherit the throne of Hastinapura after you."

Shantanu was dismayed. *How can I disinherit Devavrata, my noble and rightful heir?* he thought. Unwilling to commit such an injustice, Shantanu turned away and returned to his palace, falling into a deep, silent melancholy.

Noticing his father's declining health and sorrowful demeanor, Devavrata approached him gently. "Father, you possess everything a king could desire. Why are you so sad?"

Shantanu offered vague excuses, but Devavrata questioned the King's old minister and learned the truth about Dasaraja's condition.

Without a moment's hesitation, Devavrata rode to the fisherman's cottage accompanied by the elder statesmen of the realm. "Dasaraja," Devavrata announced, "I renounce my claim to the throne of Hastinapura. I vow before all present that the son born of your daughter Satyavati shall reign as King!"

The fisherman chief bowed, but raised another concern. "O Prince, I have no doubt that you will keep your word. But what of the sons that may be born to you in the future? They may challenge my daughter's children for the kingdom."

Understanding the depth of the sacrifice required to secure his father's happiness, Devavrata raised his hand to the heavens and uttered a terrifying, solemn oath: *"Chief of fishermen, from this day forth, I vow to embrace lifelong celibacy! I shall never marry, nor father any children!"*

As the words echoed across the river, the heavens opened, and celestial flowers showered upon the prince. Voices of the Devas resounded from the clouds: *"Bheeshma! Bheeshma!"*—meaning *He of the terrible oath!*

Bheeshma escorted Satyavati to Hastinapura. When King Shantanu learned of his son's supreme sacrifice, tears welled in his eyes. Embracing Bheeshma, the King granted him a divine boon: *Ichha-Mrityu*—the power to choose the exact moment of his own death, ensuring that death could never touch him without his own consent.

Shantanu married Satyavati and fathered two sons, Chitrangada and Vichitravirya. After Shantanu's death, Chitrangada was killed in battle, leaving the young Vichitravirya to ascend the throne under Bheeshma's protective guidance.

When Vichitravirya came of age, Bheeshma rode alone to the Kingdom of Kashi, where a Swayamvara was being held for the King's three daughters—Amba, Ambika, and Ambalika. Challenging the assembled suitors, Bheeshma defeated all the rival kings in battle and carried the three princesses to Hastinapura to be brides for Vichitravirya.

As the wedding preparations began, the eldest princess, Amba, approached Bheeshma privately. "In my heart, I had already chosen King Salva as my husband, and he had accepted me," she confessed. "Knowing this, please do what is right."

Honoring her feelings, Bheeshma permitted Amba to depart with dignity to Salva's kingdom. Meanwhile, Vichitravirya was wedded to Ambika and Ambalika.

However, when Amba presented herself before King Salva, he rejected her coldly. "I cannot accept a woman who was won in battle by another man. Return to Bheeshma."

Spurn by Salva and unable to return to Hastinapura, Amba's grief turned into consuming hatred for Bheeshma. "Bheeshma is the cause of all my humiliation!" she vowed. "I shall live only to destroy him!"

Amba sought out Sage Parashurama, Bheeshma's martial guru, who vowed to aid her. Parashurama ordered Bheeshma to marry Amba. When Bheeshma respectfully refused, citing his unbreakable vow of celibacy, Parashurama challenged his former disciple to battle.

A colossal duel ensued between the two mighty warriors, lasting twenty-three days. Weapons of mass destruction shook the earth until Parashurama, unable to overcome Bheeshma's divine defense, conceded defeat. 

"I have done my best, child," Parashurama told Amba. "Seek Bheeshma's protection."

"Never!" Amba declared. Retiring to the deep forest, she performed severe penance for twelve years until Lord Shiva appeared before her. 

"I seek the power to slay Bheeshma!" Amba demanded.

"You shall do so in your next life," Lord Shiva granted. "You will be reborn as Shikhandin, the child of King Drupada, and bring about Bheeshma's fall."

Satisfied, Amba built a funeral pyre and leaped into the flames, her soul reborn as Shikhandin.

Years passed. Vichitravirya died childless, and through the divine intervention of Sage Vyasa, Ambika gave birth to the blind Dhritarashtra, Ambalika to Pandu, and a maidservant to Vidura. Bheeshma raised the princes with fatherly care, and later supervised the upbringing of their sons—the one hundred Kauravas and the five Pandavas.

When the great Kurukshetra War broke out, Bheeshma was appointed Commander-in-Chief of the Kaurava army. Though his heart lay with the righteous Pandavas, his oath bound him to defend the throne of Hastinapura. Before the battle began, the eldest Pandava, Yudhisthira, approached Bheeshma to seek his blessings.

"May victory be yours, my son," the grand patriarch blessed him warmly. "My body belongs to the King, but my blessings are with righteousness."

For nine days, Bheeshma fought like a lion, annihilating thousands of Pandava troops each day while ensuring that the five Pandava brothers remained unharmed. Seeing their armies decimated, the Pandavas visited Bheeshma's tent at night to ask how he could be defeated.

Bheeshma smiled gently at his grandsons. "I will never strike a woman, or anyone who was once a woman. Place Shikhandin before Arjuna in battle tomorrow."

On the tenth day of war, Arjuna rode into battle with Shikhandin standing at the front of his chariot. Seeing Shikhandin, Bheeshma lowered his bow, refusing to shoot. Seizing the moment, Arjuna loosed hundreds of arrows from behind Shikhandin. 

Arrow after arrow pierced the patriarch's body until, at last, Bheeshma fell from his chariot. So closely spaced were the arrows lodged in his flesh that his body did not touch the earth—he lay suspended upon a bed of arrows (*Shara-Shayya*).

Both armies halted their battle and gathered in reverence around their beloved grandsire. As Bheeshma lay on the arrows, he looked up at the warriors. "My head hangs down. I need a support for my head."

Kaurava princes brought soft silk pillows, but Bheeshma turned them away. Arjuna stepped forward, drew three arrows from his quiver, and shot them into the earth beneath Bheeshma's head, creating a warrior's pillow.

"A fitting pillow for a Kshatriya!" Bheeshma smiled. "Now I am thirsty."

Arjuna shot a divine arrow deep into the earth beside Bheeshma's head. A jet of crystal-clear Ganga water sprang forth, flowing directly into Bheeshma's lips. 

Invoking his boon of *Ichha-Mrityu*, Bheeshma chose to remain on his bed of arrows until the sun entered the auspicious northern solstice (*Uttarayana*). After the war concluded with the Pandavas' victory, the dying patriarch delivered his final, legendary discourses on statecraft, ethics, and duty (*Rajadharma*) to King Yudhisthira before peacefully releasing his soul to the heavens.
