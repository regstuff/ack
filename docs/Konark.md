# Konark

King Narasimha Deva I was an ambitious ruler, a conqueror, as well as a generous king. There was only one thing he lacked—an heir. 

"O Surya, if my devotion has pleased you, I pray to you, grant me an heir," he prayed to the Sun God. 

Soon after, his prayer was answered. "My Lord, you have been blessed with a son. He is as radiant as the sun," the Queen announced. 

"We will name him Bhanudeva," the King decided. He summoned his ministers. "To commemorate my victories in battle, and as a mark of gratitude to the Sun God for giving me an heir, I will build a temple... right here, at Konark, where it will greet the first rays of the fiery sun as they fall on the earth." ('Konark' means 'Corner of the Sun'). 

Under the orders of the chief minister, Sibai Santra, workers started throwing stones into the water to lay the foundation in a gorge. But the strong current carried the stones away. 

Tired and frustrated, Sibai stopped at a cottage. An old woman gave him some hot khiri (a thin gruel). Sibai was so hungry that he plunged his fingers right into the middle of the bowl, burning them. 

"Ha ha! Why, you're just like that foolish Sibai Santra! Throwing stones in the middle of the water instead of starting from the sides," the old woman laughed. 

Realisation dawned on Sibai. He started filling up the sides of the gorge first, and the foundation was successfully laid. 

The King appointed Bisu Maharana as the chief architect. "I have brought 1200 of the best craftsmen in the kingdom... The King wants to build a great temple... You know I can't disobey the King's orders. I'll be back as soon as the temple is completed," Bisu told his pregnant wife before leaving. 

Bisu started to plan and build the temple in the form of Surya's vehicle—a chariot drawn by seven golden horses, with twenty-four wheels representing the hours of the day. 

Meanwhile, in Bisu's home, his little son, Dharmapada, was growing up. While other children played, Dharmapada spent all his time reading his father's old manuscripts on architecture. When Dharmapada turned twelve, he set out for Konark to meet his father. 

On his way, he rested near the temple complex and ate some berries. He shared them with a man passing by. "These berries are delicious! Bisu, you must taste these berries," the man said to his companion. 

"My son!" "Father!" Dharmapada and Bisu were joyously reunited. 

But Bisu and the craftsmen were in deep distress. "What do we do now? If we don't fix the crown stone by Magha Sukla Saptami, the King will have us all beheaded," Bisu lamented. They had tried many times, but the crown stone refused to hold up due to complex magnetic calculations. 

"May be I can help. I can do it. I can fix the crown stone," Dharmapada offered. Bisu reluctantly gave the boy a chance. 

Dharmapada set to work, and his calculations worked! He successfully placed the stone, saving the day. 

At first, there was great relief and happiness among the craftsmen. But then, a dark thought arose. "What will happen when the King finds out that it was a 12-year-old boy who fixed the crown stone and not us? We will be beheaded!" they worried. "Bisu, you must send him away... The lives of those 1200 craftsmen are more important than mine," some argued cruelly. 

Dharmapada overheard this. Unnoticed by his father, he climbed right to the top of the temple. Bisu looked everywhere and finally saw him. "Dharmapada! Son! Where are you going?" 

"This should save her from the first shock," Dharmapada thought of his mother. He stood at the very top of the temple... and jumped into the river below, sacrificing his own life to save the 1200 craftsmen from the King's potential wrath. 

"I have built a temple but lost a son," Bisu wept bitterly. 

The grand temple of Konark stood for many years, a testament to the skill of the artisans and the sacrifice of a young boy. Till, gradually, it fell prey to vandals and natural disasters. But what remains is still breathtaking, and the stories around this ancient monument will continue to be told for ages to come.
