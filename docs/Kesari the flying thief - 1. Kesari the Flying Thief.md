# Kesari the Flying Thief

Kesari, the incorrigible thief, had been banished from the city with the warning that if he returned and was caught, he would be hanged. 

"I dare not go back to the city. At least not for a few days! If they catch me..." he thought, hiding in a forest outside the city. 

Suddenly, he saw a strange sight. A man was floating down from the sky! "He's coming down! But how did he get up there?" Kesari watched in amazement. 

The man landed, removed his sandals, and went to bathe in a nearby stream. "It's the sandals! He's removing them! He can't touch earth without removing them!" Kesari realized. 

As the man bathed, Kesari crept out and snatched the magic sandals. Putting them on, he instantly rose into the air. 

"Hey! Come back! Those are my sandals!" the man shouted. 

"Were, my friend, were!" Kesari mocked, flying away. "Who says thieving is bad? The gods themselves approve. If they didn't, would they have sent me this gift to make my job easier?" 

Kesari flew back to the city. He found a deserted temple dedicated to Goddess Kali. "A deserted temple! It would make a fine hiding-place for my booty!" he decided. "O Goddess, make me prosperous! Give me wealth!" he prayed before setting out. 

That night, Kesari stole precious gems from a jeweller's shop. The next morning, the jeweller complained to the King. The King summoned the Kotwal (chief of police). 

The Kotwal explained, "I saw a man prowling in the jeweller's shop. I ran out and tried to chase him... But he hovered about for a while, just to tease me... and then rose high into the sky... and vanished. I'd have to have wings for that, Maharaj!" 

"A flying thief may be hard to trace and catch. But as king it is my duty to bring him to book. I myself will hunt him down," the King resolved. 

The King dressed as a common soldier and, with a small party of men, searched the forest. "Let us each search a different part of the forest. We'll be able to work faster that way," the King suggested. 

While resting, the King smelled a familiar fragrance. "Mm-m-m! The fragrance of musk and... sandalwood? It's coming from that direction. There must be a temple somewhere there." 

He found the deserted temple and saw Kesari worshipping the Goddess with the stolen gems. "Ah! I was right. There's somebody worshipping the Goddess!" 

When Kesari left to fetch more offerings, the King hid inside the dark temple. When Kesari returned and offered the food to the deity, the King stepped out and captured him. "Don't try to struggle! Let me go!" Kesari cried. 

The King was about to arrest him when he noticed Kesari's calm and peaceful expression while he had been meditating earlier. "I have never seen such a peaceful expression before," the King marvelled. "He has no wealth. No wonder he has peace." 

The King's men arrived. "Ah! There's the scoundrel pretending to be a monk! Ha!" they shouted. 

"No! This is not the Kesari I set out to catch. Forgive me, O enlightened one," the King said, falling at Kesari's feet. "What force brought over the change I do not know... but I do know that I am in the presence of a man who has found peace." 

"I salute you, noble one," the King bowed. Kesari the thief had, indeed, transformed into Kesari the enlightened one, realizing that true peace comes not from stolen wealth, but from inner devotion.
