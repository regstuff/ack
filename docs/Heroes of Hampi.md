# Heroes of Hampi

In the early fourteenth century, the armies of the Delhi Sultanate attacked the Deccan hoping to expand their territories. Over the next twenty years, pitched battles were fought, as the kingdoms of the south defended their lands. 

Once, in a break between battles, two brothers, the warriors Hakka and Bukka, went hunting on the banks of the river Tungabhadra. 

"We need a new strategy, Bukka. Our army is being beaten back every single time. All we can do for now is to try and hold the fortress of Anegundi, and worry about feeding the army," Hakka sighed. 

As they rode, their hunting dogs chased a rabbit. "The rabbit is tiring! We've almost got it now," Bukka cried. 

Suddenly, the rabbit stopped, turned around, and snarled fiercely at the dogs. *GRRR!* The dogs yelped in terror and backed away. 

"The rabbit is snarling! What could make a rabbit act so brave?" Hakka marvelled. "I've never seen a rabbit act like that!" 

They met an ascetic sitting nearby. "It is the land here—a land of bravery. This is where you should set up your kingdom of victory. The rabbit is a sign," the ascetic told them. "The battles are not over. Now you should build a kingdom. What better place than here, in this land of heroes?" 

"But we are just local chieftains, Sangama's sons. How could we hope to build an entire kingdom?" Bukka asked. 

"I don't think he knows what he's saying. Maybe we should leave," Hakka whispered. 

"No! Send your dogs back and hear me out. Let me tell you about this place. Then you can decide if I'm right or wrong," the ascetic said. "Below you flows the river Pampa, or Tungabhadra. She was deeply in love with Shiva. First, Shiva married Sati..." 

The ascetic began his story. Daksha held a great yagna. He invited all the gods and goddesses, but Shiva and Sati were left out. Hurt, Sati decided to attend the sacrifice anyway, but she was humiliated. Sati invoked the god of fire and burst into flames. 

Filled with sorrow and anger, Shiva tore off a strand of hair and threw it on the ground. From the hair emerged a terrible god, fearsome in his rage, and filled with a thirst for destruction—Veerabhadra. 

Veerabhadra led his army down from Mount Kailasha, bent on destruction. They destroyed the yagna, smashed the altar, and crushed the offerings. Then, Veerabhadra beheaded Daksha. The other gods tried to stop him, but no one could withstand his fury. 

The devas went to Vishnu. "How can we stop Veerabhadra? He will destroy the world!" 

Vishnu worshipped Veerabhadra with offerings of lotuses, hoping to pacify him. He did this 999 times. "The thousandth time... I'm one lotus short! I can't stop now!" Vishnu plucked out his own lotus-shaped eye and offered it to Veerabhadra. 

Veerabhadra was touched, and his anger vanished. "Your devotion is unmatched. I will call off my army and stop destroying the world. I will also restore your eye. Take this Sudarshan Chakra. No being in the world can stand against it," Veerabhadra blessed him. 

"The place where this happened is now called Chakratirtha. It is not very far from here, on the banks of the river Pampa," the ascetic explained. 

"Well, Sati was reborn as the beautiful river Pampa. In fact the word Hampi is a corruption of her name, Pampa. This time her father was Brahma," the ascetic continued. Pampa did penance until Shiva appeared before her and agreed to marry her. 

"So the ascetic began another story. 'Kishkindha, the kingdom of monkeys, was ruled by the powerful Vali, with help from his brother Sugriva.'" 

One night, a demon called Mayavi appeared at the gates of Kishkindha and challenged Vali to a fight. Vali rushed forward, and Mayavi fled into a cave. 

"Sugriva, I'm going in after him. You stay here. If you see blood coming out, then it means I'm dead. Just block the entrance and go home," Vali ordered. 

Sugriva waited an entire year. Finally, blood flowed out. Hearing Mayavi's roar of victory, Sugriva thought his brother was dead. He blocked the mouth of the cave and went home sadly. Shortly after, a reluctant Sugriva was crowned king. Hanuman was his minister. 

But it was Mayavi's blood. Vali returned, furious that Sugriva had blocked the cave. "Traitor! Murderer! Fratricide!" Vali roared, driving Sugriva out. 

"We should go to Rishyamukha," Hanuman advised the fleeing Sugriva. "Rishyamukha is the hill on which Vali once threw a demon's corpse. It fell near the dwelling of a rishi who was furious with Vali. 'If you ever step near my ashram again, you will die!' the rishi had cursed Vali." So Sugriva and Hanuman lived safely in a cave on Rishyamukha hill. 

A few days later, two strangers came by. It was Rama and Lakshmana, searching for Sita, who had been kidnapped by Ravana. Sugriva showed them Sita's ornaments, which she had dropped as a trail while being abducted. Rama helped Sugriva defeat Vali and regain his kingdom, and in return, Sugriva and Hanuman helped Rama rescue Sita. 

After the war in Lanka, Hanuman went back to his penance in Hampi and concentrated on Veerabhadra. When Veerabhadra appeared, Hanuman asked, "I want a deity and a temple of my own. A place for me to pray and derive strength from." 

"I shall erect an idol on this very spot for you," Veerabhadra granted. An idol appeared, but it started growing taller and taller. Hanuman started building a temple around the idol, but it kept growing. Finally, the idol stopped growing. 

"The idol is still here in the Uddana Veerabhadra temple," the ascetic finished his story. "This is a place steeped in valour. It is a sacred place. This is a good place for a fortified city. The river will act as a barrier against invaders, in addition to supplying a city with water. Now do you still think I am a madman?" 

Hakka and Bukka were spellbound. "You are very wise, sir. Won't you be our advisor in setting up this kingdom?" 

"I will. But now I must go and complete my prayers for today," the ascetic said, and disappeared amongst the trees. 

Hakka and Bukka rode home filled with determination. They stopped at a small Hanuman temple. "Do you by any chance know a sage who lives in the forest?" they asked some locals. 

"That's Vidyaranya! Haven't you heard of him? He is a very learned man," the people told them. 

With the sage's advice, Hakka and Bukka built a real Vidyanagara, a kingdom of learning, which could also be Vijayanagara, a kingdom of victory. 

With Vidyaranya as their advisor, Hakka and Bukka did set up the vast kingdom of Vijayanagara. Its capital was a sprawling city, home to painters, sculptors, dancers, musicians and writers. Hakka became its first king, Harihara Raya I. Though the kingdom of Vijayanagara is long gone, the remains of its magnificent palaces and ornate temples still stand testimony to a glorious epoch in the history of India.
