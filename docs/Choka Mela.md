# Chokha Mela: The Boy Who Heard God

Toward the close of the thirteenth century, in the village of Mangalvedhe in Maharashtra's Solapur district, a young Mahar boy named Chokha was playing with his friends at dusk. As the twilight bell of the village temple began to chime, a strange divine resonance stirred within Chokha's soul. The ringing bells seemed to call out directly to him: *"Come, Chokha, come!"*

Driven by an irresistible spiritual yearning, Chokha stopped playing and ran toward the temple. His playmates called after him in alarm, "Chokha! Where are you going? You are a Mahar! You cannot enter the temple!"

Ignoring their warnings, Chokha pushed through the crowds into the temple courtyard right as the evening Arati began. Absorbed in prayer, the boy gazed wide-eyed at the glowing deity. 

Suddenly, a temple priest spotted him. "Chhi! It's Chokha Mela, the son of a Mahar!" the priest bellowed. "How dare you enter this sacred place, you dirty fellow!"

"I am not dirty, sir," Chokha pleaded softly. "I bathe every day and my clothes are clean."

"Insolent wretch!" the priest shouted, seizing the boy and flinging him out onto the dusty street. "One more step inside and I'll beat the life out of you!"

Sitting by the roadside as the worshippers filed past, Chokha wept bitterly. Indignation gave way to deep reflection. *Why do they call me dirty?* he wondered. *I scrub my body clean, yet because I am born a Mahar, they bar me from seeing the Lord.*

For years, Chokha traveled from village to village, but at every temple, the doors were slammed in his face. Overcome by despair one afternoon, he sat weeping beneath a tree. A kindly trader stopped and took him home, offering him a bowl of warm gruel. But when the trader's wife questioned Chokha's caste, Chokha quietly slipped away without eating, refusing to cause domestic strife for his host.

One morning, a procession of Varkari pilgrims passed through Mangalvedhe, singing devotional hymns to Lord Vithoba of Pandharpur: *"Vithala! Vithala! Vithala!"*

Entranced by their ecstasy, Chokha joined the procession. After days of barefoot travel, they reached the holy town of Pandharpur on the banks of the Chandrabhaga River. 

In Pandharpur, Chokha bathed in the sacred river and walked to the famous Vithoba temple. Remembering his status, he did not try to enter the main gates. Instead, he prostrated at the outer entrance, standing near the threshold every evening during Arati to catch a distant glimpse of the shrine.

A senior temple priest noticed Chokha standing at the outer gate day after day. "Lowly Mahar! Why do you stand here?" the priest mocked.

"I am grateful to stand at this gate and look toward the Lord," Chokha replied humbly.

"The sugarcane may be crooked, but its juice is sweet," Chokha added gently. "The lotus grows in mud, yet it is offered to the gods. Will the Lord reject a Mahar if his faith is pure?"

The priest's pride was stung. "How dare an illiterate Mahar lecture me on faith! If your Lord loved you, wouldn't He take you into His temple?"

The priest's taunts pierced Chokha's heart. Wandering back to the riverbank, he sat in silent agony, questioning his worthiness. 

As night fell, a gentle, luminous voice echoed within his heart: *"Chokha, do not let your faith be shaken by human words. The Lord is not bound by temple walls or human prejudice. Come to the gate tonight."*

At midnight, Chokha walked down the quiet streets to the main gate of the Vithoba temple. As he stood before the heavy wooden doors, the iron bolts slid back automatically with a soft clank! 

The heavy doors swung open. Drawn by an overwhelming spiritual pull, Chokha walked through the deserted corridors into the inner sanctum. Falling before the golden idol of Lord Vithoba, Chokha embraced the deity's feet, weeping tears of pure devotion.

At dawn, the head priest entered the sanctum to prepare the morning worship—and froze in horror. Sitting at the feet of the idol was Chokha Mela!

"You filthy Mahar!" the priest shrieked in fury. "How dare you enter the sanctum and defile the Lord!"

"How can an untouchable pollute God?" Chokha asked calmly, rising. "Can a sinner walking on mother earth destroy her sanctity? God is above all pollution."

"Quiet, madman!" the priest roared. Lunging forward, he struck Chokha hard across the face with his open palm, bruising his cheek, and dragged him out of the temple. "Get out of Pandharpur before the townspeople stone you to death!"

Without a word of anger, Chokha crossed the Chandrabhaga River to the opposite bank, built a small mud hut, and immersed himself in composing devotional *Abhangs* (sacred poems).

Meanwhile, back in the inner sanctum, the priest turned to perform the ritual bath of Lord Vithoba. As he looked at the golden idol, the pitcher slipped from his hands in terror. 

The right cheek of the sacred idol was visibly swollen and bruised—in the exact spot where he had struck Chokha!

The priest rubbed his eyes in disbelief, but the swelling remained. Panic and deep remorse seized his heart. *By striking the innocent devotee Chokha, I struck the Lord himself!* the priest realized in horror. *God dwells within His true devotee, above all caste and pride!*

Full of repentance, the priest ran out of the temple, crossed the river, and fell at Chokha's feet outside his humble hut. "Chokha! Forgive me! I have sinned against the Lord by persecuting you! For my sake, please come back to the temple!"

Chokha raised the priest up gently. "Arise, priest. There is no anger in my heart."

Together, they recrossed the river and entered the sanctum. The moment Chokha bowed before the idol, the swelling on Lord Vithoba's cheek vanished completely.

Rani Chennamma's contemporary and fellow saint-poets hailed Chokha's divine devotion. Chokha Mela spent the remainder of his life at Pandharpur, composing immortal *Abhangs* that continue to resonate across Maharashtra as a timeless beacon of equality, devotion, and spiritual liberation.
