# The Hidden Meaning

There was once a farmer named Buddhuram. He was hard-working but not too bright, and he was a contented soul with absolutely no ambitions in life. 

His wife, however, was exactly the opposite. 

"Our neighbour's son-in-law has bought two buffaloes!" she yelled, slamming a pot of dough onto the floor. "Everyone is buying buffaloes or land... except us. You must make money somehow. Did you hear me?"

"Yes, yes! Now please let me eat in peace!" Buddhuram groaned. 

*Sometimes I feel like breaking a pot over his thick skull!* his wife fumed. 

Matters came to a head when one day, their neighbour excitedly burst into the house. "You won't believe it! My husband has been honoured by the King!"

"What! Are you all right? It's the heat!" Buddhuram's wife gasped, fanning her friend. 

"Yes, yes! Don't worry about me. Oh, what marvellous news... er, for what did the King honour him?" 

"For his poetry!" the neighbour beamed. "Yes. He recited one of his poems to the King. The King was so pleased with it that he presented my husband with two gold coins!"

"How marvellous!" Buddhuram's wife forced a smile. As soon as the neighbour left, her smile vanished. *From now on she'll become even more insufferable. And it's all because of that dim-witted husband of mine! If only he would do something spectacular!*

When Buddhuram returned from the fields, she was waiting for him with a broom. 

"Imagine! Two gold coins!" she screamed, hitting him with the broom. "People around us have become rich and famous... and all you can think of is food! Well, I have had enough! You are going to sit down and write a poem too!"

"A poem! But I don't know how to read and write," Buddhuram protested. 

"I'll teach you! If her husband can write poetry, so can you! I'll teach you the alphabet first. We'll begin right now!"

"What about food?"

"You'll get your food as soon as you have learnt the first letter of the alphabet!"

Forced into learning, Buddhuram sat in the dirt as his wife drew symbols. "The first letter of the Sanskrit alphabet is 'A'. It's written like this! Now go to the riverside and practise writing it in the sand!"

Every day, his wife would teach him one letter, and then send him to the riverside to practise writing it. But poor Buddhuram had a terrible memory. 

"Now what did she say the third letter was? Eeee? Beee? Ehh?" he muttered, staring blankly at the sand. 

Two travellers walking by noticed him struggling. "What are you doing my good man?"

"I am... er, learning the alphabet so that I can write poetry," Buddhuram explained. 

The traveller laughed. "You want to write poetry, do you? And do you think everyone who knows the alphabet can write poetry? You need inspiration to write poetry, my friend! Inspiration!"

"Who's that?" 

"Not who, what. Inspiration is a feeling! When you get inspired a thrill runs through your body and... oh! Never mind!" the traveller gave up and walked away. 

When Buddhuram returned home, he had completely forgotten the letters. "Then you don't get any food!" his wife yelled, slamming the door in his face. 

Life became utterly miserable for Buddhuram. "Why doesn't she leave me alone? I was happy just being a farmer," he wept. Hungry, tired, and desperate, he walked back to the river. "I can't go on like this! I'll end my life in the river!"

He bravely dove into the deep water. But the river was icy cold, and Buddhuram immediately began to shiver violently. 

"What's happening? Why's my whole body trembling?" he chattered, his teeth clacking together. Then, his eyes widened. "Perhaps this is what the pandit meant by inspiration! What a stroke of luck! But now that I have got inspiration I must write a poem! But about what? About whom?"

Just then, he noticed a crow sitting on a rock near the bank, sharpening its beak on the stone. 

"Ah, that crow... he is sharpening his beak in expectation of a feast," Buddhuram observed. He watched the crow dip its beak in the water, rub it on the rock, and repeat the process. 

"You dip it in water and sharpen it... again and again. O Kalia, I can read your mind," Buddhuram said aloud. 

He gasped. "There! I have composed a poem!" 

He scrambled out of the icy water and ran all the way home, dripping wet. "Wife! Wife! I have composed a poem! Yes! I entered the river and suddenly I had... INSPIRATION! Seizing the opportunity, I immediately composed a poem. Listen..."

He struck a dramatic pose. "*You dip it in water and sharpen it again and again. O Kalia, I can read your mind.*"

His wife stared at him. "I... er... is that a poem?" she asked, thoroughly confused. *Perhaps it is a poem,* she thought to herself. *Who can tell? Perhaps the goddess had pity on my poor husband and on me and inspired him the way she inspired Kalidasa.*

"Well, now that you have a poem, you'd better go to the palace and recite it before the King," she declared proudly. 

So Buddhuram set off for the palace. When he got there, he boldly took his place among the kingdom's greatest poets. He waited impatiently as famed scholars held the audience spellbound with brilliant epics and sonnets. 

"Is there anybody left?" the King finally asked. 

"Yes!!" Buddhuram shouted, jumping up. 

"You may begin, then."

Buddhuram cleared his throat, threw out his chest, and began to recite his composition in a loud, booming voice. 

*"You dip it in water and sharpen it again and again. O Kalia, I can read your mind."*

There was a moment of stunned silence. Then, the entire audience burst out laughing. *HAHAHA! HOHOHO! KHI KHI!* 

But the King maintained a discreet, serious silence. *No one would dare to come here and recite such nonsense unless there was some meaning in the words,* the King thought cleverly. *And the man appears so confident. He's surely not a fool.*

The King turned to his Prime Minister. "Well, what did you think of the poem?"

The Minister, noticing the King wasn't laughing, quickly adjusted his expression. "I... well... He didn't laugh with the rest of us. Perhaps the words have some significance which the rest of us missed. It is obvious that the poem is... er... pregnant with meaning, Maharaj."

"Just as I thought," the King nodded sagely. He turned to the crowd. "Can anyone interpret the poem?"

The laughing poets fell silent, looking at each other in confusion. 

"If anyone can interpret the poem, he shall be rewarded with ten gold coins!" the King announced. He ordered the poem to be written on a huge board and set up in the royal courtyard. 

"What about me, Maharaj?" Buddhuram asked. 

"You too will get your reward... but in due course," the King promised. 

For days, poets and scholars puzzled over the rhyme on the board. But no one could see any special meaning in the strange words about a crow. 

Then one morning, as the King was being prepared for a shave by his royal barber, Kalia. 

"What's the matter, Kalia? You seem rather nervous today?" the King noticed. 

"I... I'm all right, Maharaj," the barber stammered, his hands shaking. 

The barber picked up his razor, dipped it into a cup of water, and began to sharpen it on a whetstone. 

Outside the window, a guard was reading the board aloud to himself. *"You dip it in water and sharpen it... again and again. O Kalia, I can read your mind."*

Inside the room, the barber froze in absolute terror. The poem! It knew his name, and exactly what he was doing! 

"Forgive me, Maharaj!" Kalia screamed, dropping the razor and falling to his knees. "It was not my idea at all! They asked me to do it!"

The King frowned in confusion. "Do what?" 

"Cut your throat!" the barber confessed, sobbing uncontrollably. 

The King's eyes widened in realization. "Oh, I see it all now," he gasped. He immediately called for his guards. "Arrest this man and take down his statement! And summon the poet here! He is a true poet. He sees all!"

So it was that Buddhuram the simple farmer inadvertently uncovered an assassination plot and became a great hero. The King rewarded him with immense wealth, and he returned home to his astounded wife, rich and famous forever.
