# Prajapati

Long ago, there were three kinds of beings in the universe: the Devas (celestial gods), the Manavas (men on earth), and the Asuras (demons). 

In heaven, the Devas had all the comforts they could possibly aspire for and led a life of endless pleasure. They drank divine nectar, watched beautiful dancers, and lived in luxury. Yet, one day, they grew restless. 

"Here in heaven we are free from hunger, thirst, old age, and death," one Deva noted. "Yet, why aren't we happy?"

"It's true," another agreed. "We seem to have everything, yet we feel the lack of something."

"Let us go to Prajapati, our father and the creator of all, and seek his guidance," their leader suggested. 

Unlike their celestial brothers, the Manavas on earth were leading a hard life. They plowed the fields, carried heavy pots of water, and chased away birds who tried to steal their grain. 

"A good harvest! Our future is secure. Go away, you wicked bird! Stealing my grain! On no account will we part with what we have gained," a wealthy farmer shouted, throwing a stone at a bird. 

Yet, despite all the riches they had earned and stored away, the Manavas, too, were far from happy. 

"We have no contentment," a wealthy merchant sighed. 

"What we lack is peace of mind," another agreed. 

"Let us approach Prajapati, our father, for guidance," they decided. 

Deep in the underworld, the Asuras inspired fear among both the Manavas and the Devas. They were brutal and merciless warriors. 

"Spare my life. Show mercy," a terrified victim begged an Asura warlord. 

"Mercy? What does that mean? Prepare to die!" the Asura laughed, swinging his massive sword. 

But the Asuras, too, were overcome by a feeling of deep dissatisfaction. "Although we are powerful and feared by all, we are not happy," an Asura admitted as they sat around a fire. 

"Let us go to Prajapati, our father, and seek his guidance," the warlord suggested. 

The Devas, the Manavas, and the Asuras all travelled to the divine abode of Lord Prajapati. Thousands of beings gathered before the four-faced creator of the universe. 

"O father, tell us what we should do to achieve contentment and peace," they prayed in unison. 

Prajapati looked at his children thoughtfully. Then, he uttered just one single syllable. 

"DA...."

Everyone was completely perplexed. They looked at each other in confusion. "DA? What could that mean?" 

"I think it is the first syllable of a secret word which will tell us what to do," one of the men guessed. 

"I think HE wants us to discover the word for ourselves," a Deva realized. 

Accepting the puzzle, the three groups left for their respective homes to ponder over the meaning of "DA."

On returning to their celestial world, the Devas were just about to resume their life of endless pleasure when their leader suddenly raised his hand. 

"I've got it! I know what 'DA' means!" he shouted joyously.

"Tell us what it means," the others asked.

"'DA' means 'Damyata' (Restrain yourselves). Yes, the father has asked us to restrain ourselves from endless indulgence."

All the Devas put aside their cups of wine and stepped away from the dancers. They began to practise self-control, and for the first time, they experienced a profound satisfaction they had never known before. 

Meanwhile, down on earth, the Manavas had gone back to their fields. The wealthy farmer was just about to fling a heavy stone at a flock of birds when he froze. 

"I've got it! 'DA' means 'Datta' (Give)!" he cried out. "The father wants us to give away, not to hoard!"

"Only now do I realise that there is joy in giving," another man smiled. 

"How right you are! Let the birds share our grain," the farmer agreed happily, tossing seeds to the birds. The men began to practise charity, and their hearts were filled with true peace. 

Down in the underworld, the Asuras were about to resume their violent raids. An Asura warlord had his sword raised high to execute a prisoner when he suddenly froze. 

"Wait! 'DA' means 'Dayadhvam' (Be compassionate)," the warlord gasped. "Yes, the father wants us to show compassion."

He lowered his sword. "Release him. Go without fear, friend. No harm will come to you."

"You are kind! Thank you," the prisoner wept in relief. 

As the Asuras began to practise compassion and mercy, their leader smiled warmly. "My heart is filled with a kind of joy I've never experienced before," he admitted. 

And so, the Devas learned *Damyata* (self-control), the Manavas learned *Datta* (charity), and the Asuras learned *Dayadhvam* (compassion). 

To this day, to remind all people of the three cardinal virtues needed for a happy and peaceful life, the heavens thunder now and again: *DA... DA... DA...*
