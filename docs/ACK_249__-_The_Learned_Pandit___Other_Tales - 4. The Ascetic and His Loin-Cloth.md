# The Ascetic and His Loin-Cloth

In a quiet forest near a small village, there lived a wise Guru and his young disciple. They spent all their time in deep prayer and meditation, living a life of absolute simplicity. 

Then, one day, the Guru stood up. "My son, I have to leave you for a while. I am going on a pilgrimage," he announced. "Remember all that I have taught you. And above all, lead a simple life."

"I will, Gurudev," the disciple promised humbly. 

The disciple continued his simple life. One morning, as usual, the ascetic washed his only spare loin-cloth in the river and hung it out on a tree branch to dry. 

"I will now go to the village to beg for alms," he thought, walking away. 

But when the ascetic returned to his hut later that afternoon, he was shocked. "Hey! That looks like a piece of my loin-cloth!"

He examined the shredded fabric hanging from the tree. "It is! The rats have torn it to shreds! I will have to beg for another."

The next day at the village, he approached a kind woman. "Mother, I need a loin-cloth. Can you spare me one?"

"A loin-cloth? I have a new one. I'll give it to you, holy sage," she smiled, handing it over. 

The ascetic thanked her, washed it, and hung it high up on the roof of his hut. "Today, I'll hang my cloth high up here on the roof," he thought smartly. 

But that afternoon, when he returned, it was destroyed again. "Oh no, the rats have torn up this one too! I'll have to get myself a cat."

The ascetic found a stray cat and brought it home. And soon enough, the rats stopped troubling him. 

"I must keep my cat well fed," he realized a few days later as the cat meowed hungrily. "I know what. I'll get myself a cow."

And so he got himself a cow. "My cat has as much milk as it needs and more," he smiled happily. But then he scratched his head. "But I need straw to feed my cow. How shall I go about getting that? Ah! I could cultivate the land around my hut. And when the grain is harvested, I'll have all the straw my cow would need."

Accordingly, the ascetic began to till the land around his hut. Soon, he had a massive, golden harvest. 

"What a fine crop I have harvested!" he cheered. "The straw my cow will have, but what shall I do with the grain? I'll need a barn to store it in."

So the ascetic hired labourers to build large brick barns for him. "Make it taller. There's plenty of grain to be stored," he ordered the masons. 

With so much grain and property, the ascetic had now become a wealthy landlord. 

"I need a wife to help me manage all this. I think I will marry," he decided. 

And so, he married a beautiful woman, had children, and lived a life of absolute luxury, smoking a hookah in his grand mansion. 

One fine day, the Guru returned from his long pilgrimage. Walking down the forest path, he was completely bewildered by the sprawling estate before him. 

"Am I on the right road?" the Guru muttered, scratching his head. "Where is my disciple's little hut? Has some rich man driven my poor disciple away and built his estate here?"

When a servant came out of the grand house, the Guru went up to him. "My good man, an ascetic used to live here once. Do you know where he is now?"

"Er... I... he..." the servant stammered, pointing behind him. "My lord, you!"

The master of the house stepped out. It was the disciple! 

He rushed forward and fell at his Guru's feet in shame. 

"My son, what is all this?" the Guru gasped. "I left you leading the simple life of an ascetic. I return to find you surrounded by wealth and possessions. How did this happen?"

The disciple wept, burying his face in his hands. "Oh Gurudev, it all began with a single loin-cloth. To protect it from the rats I needed a cat, and then there was no end to my needs. Forgive me, Gurudev."
