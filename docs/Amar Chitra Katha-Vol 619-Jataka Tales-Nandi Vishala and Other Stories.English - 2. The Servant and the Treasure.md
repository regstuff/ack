# The Servant and the Treasure

Once, there were two old landowners who were very close friends. One of them had a very young wife who had recently borne him a son. 

"You are very lucky," his friend smiled as they sat together. "You have a son and heir to whom you can bequeath your entire fortune."

"Yes, I'm indeed lucky," the old man sighed. "But I, too, have worries. If I die, my son may never get the money. His mother is young, and she may squander it all away."

"Wouldn't the safest course be to bury your money deep in the forest?" his wise friend suggested. 

"It would certainly be a wise thing to do," the landowner agreed. 

So, taking his most loyal and trusted household servant, an honest man named Nanda, into his absolute confidence, the landowner went to a nearby forest and secretly buried his massive treasure at a certain spot. 

"My good Nanda, when my son comes of age, show him this treasure," the old man instructed. "No one else should be told about it."

The old man died soon after. Many years later, his son, now a grown young man, met his father's wise old friend. 

"My father was a rich man. But there doesn't seem to be enough money left to manage the estate," the young man explained. 

"But he did leave a lot of money for you, son. Didn't you know that?" his father's friend revealed. "It's buried somewhere in the forest. Ask your servant, Nanda. He knows all about it."

The young man immediately rushed back home and went to Nanda. "Do you know where my father put his treasure?"

"Yes, master. It's buried in the forest," Nanda nodded truthfully. 

"Well then, let's go and get it. I need it now," the young man said. 

"I'll take you there, master."

Soon they were on their way to the forest, carrying pickaxes. Nanda was a deeply honest man. Over all these years, he could easily have taken the massive treasure for himself, but he had remained fiercely loyal to his master's dying wish. 

When they finally reached the middle of the deep forest, the young man stopped. "Well, Nanda, where is the money?"

Suddenly, Nanda, who had been perfectly docile and respectful for years, turned inexplicably arrogant and highly insolent. 

"What makes you think there is money buried here for you?" Nanda yelled, throwing his pickaxe to the ground. 

The young man was completely taken aback by the sudden outburst. *What's wrong with him? I don't understand. What shall I do?* he thought in shock. *I know! I'll pretend I didn't hear a word!*

He turned calmly to Nanda. "All right. Let's go home then."

*What a relief!* the young man thought as they walked back. *He's following me quietly.*

A few days later, they returned to the forest. *Strange! He didn't seem to hesitate,* the young man observed. 

But the very moment they reached the exact same spot among the trees, Nanda's insolence exploded once again. "You idiot! What do you hope to find here?"

Once again, the young man ignored Nanda's shocking insult. "Nothing! Let's go home," he said calmly, and they walked back in silence. 

*When we set out, I am certain that Nanda genuinely means to show me where the treasure is,* the young man reasoned to himself. *But, later, the moment we arrive, he begins to aggressively abuse me. What could the reason for this be? My father's wise old friend should be able to help.*

So he went to the old man and described Nanda's incredibly strange, shifting behaviour. 

"It's simple!" the old man laughed heartily. "You say he starts abusing you when you reach a particular spot in the forest? Then that's exactly where the treasure is buried!"

"How do you know?" the young man asked in amazement. 

"Money can corrupt even an absolutely honest man," the wise old friend explained. "The moment Nanda stands precisely on the spot where the treasure is buried, the overwhelming presence of all that wealth under his feet corrupts his mind. He feels like a master, and he becomes a completely different, arrogant man."

"If that is so, why didn't he simply take it for himself?" 

"Because he didn't dare! He is honest until he stands right on top of it!" the old man chuckled. He then told the young man exactly what he should do. 

The next day, the young man went to the forest with Nanda again. The very moment they arrived at the spot, Nanda began to violently abuse him. "You fool! You idiot! You..."

But this time, his young master knew exactly what to do. He stepped forward, grabbed Nanda firmly by the collar, and glared at him. 

"Who do you think you are talking to, you knave?" the young master roared fiercely. 

Nanda instantly shrank back in sheer terror. 

The young man picked up his own pickaxe. "Don't move from there, till I order you to do so!" he commanded, and began to dig exactly where Nanda was standing. 

Within minutes, his pickaxe struck a solid wooden chest. 

"There! I've found it!" the young man smiled. He turned to his terrified servant. "Come here! Carry this home on your head."

Without the treasure directly under his feet, Nanda's temporary madness vanished, and he obeyed meekly. The son safely took his father's vast treasure home, and the honest Nanda continued to serve him faithfully till his dying day. 

*The wise one's advice worked perfectly!* the young man smiled happily. *How grateful I am to my father's friend!*
