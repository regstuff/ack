# The Hypocritical Sadhu

A cunning rascal of a Sadhu lived in a small, quiet forest hermitage on the outskirts of a village. By putting on a perfect act of supreme piety, he had entirely won the deep trust of a wealthy local Zamindar (landlord). 

Now, the Zamindar had a large pot of gold which he desperately wanted to hide from roaming robbers. As he wondered where to put it, he thought of the holy man. 

"I know what! The Sadhu is an absolute model of goodness... and dacoits would never dare attack a holy hermitage!" the Zamindar decided. 

So he took his pot of gold directly to the hermitage. 

"Holy sir! We Zamindars live in constant fear of robbers. So I am going to bury my gold right here in your hermitage," the Zamindar announced. 

The Sadhu's eyes secretly gleamed. *There's enough gold there to keep me perfectly happy for the rest of my life!* he thought. 

The Zamindar finished burying the pot. "I am leaving my gold buried here as I have complete faith in you. Please don't let me down, holy one."

The Sadhu closed his eyes piously. "It's not necessary to tell me that. We, who have given up the material world, never covet another's property!"

Satisfied, the Zamindar left. But a few days later, the rascal of a Sadhu secretly dug up the gold and buried it again at another hidden spot a short distance away in the forest. 

*Tomorrow I shall go and take my leave of the Zamindar,* the Sadhu plotted. 

The next day, the Sadhu appeared at the Zamindar's house. "I have decided to go away from here," the Sadhu sighed heavily. 

"What's the matter? Have I displeased you in any way?" the Zamindar asked in distress. 

"No, that's not the reason," the Sadhu replied. "He who has renounced the world should not live in one place for too long. It is forbidden by our vows. So I must take my leave of you."

"Well, if you must go, I cannot stop you," the sad Zamindar agreed. 

The Zamindar respectfully walked a short distance with the Sadhu, then reverently bade him goodbye. 

As the Sadhu walked away, a sudden, paranoid thought struck him. *I must make absolutely sure he does not suspect me when he eventually finds his gold has been stolen. Our paths may cross in the future, and he might accuse me!*

The Sadhu picked up a tiny piece of dry straw from the ground and stuck it carefully into his matted hair. Then, he quickly retraced his steps back to the Zamindar's house. 

A little later, as the Zamindar sat talking with a visiting merchant, the Sadhu walked back in. 

"Welcome, holy one! What brings you back? Have you changed your mind?" the Zamindar smiled. 

"No," the Sadhu replied solemnly, pointing to his hair. "But a single straw from your roof had stuck in my hair. Since we Sadhus may not take absolutely anything which is not freely bestowed on us, I have walked all the way back to return it to you."

The visiting merchant, however, was a highly shrewd and observant man. *Come to return a single straw indeed!* the merchant thought suspiciously. *This fellow has robbed the Zamindar of something incredibly valuable, I am absolutely certain of it!*

But the naive Zamindar was completely taken in by the act. "What a sensitive man! Why, he won't even take so much as a piece of straw which does not belong to him!" the Zamindar gushed. 

"Throw away the straw and go your way, holy one," the Zamindar smiled respectfully. 

As soon as the Sadhu had left, the shrewd merchant turned to the Zamindar. "Have you left anything valuable in that Sadhu's care?" 

"Not exactly. But I secretly buried my gold in his hermitage and asked him to keep an eye on it," the Zamindar explained. 

"Well, just go and see if it is still there," the merchant advised grimly. 

The Zamindar rushed to the hermitage and soon returned, pale and trembling. "It's not there! It's been stolen!"

"The thief is none other than that rascal of a Sadhu!" the merchant declared. "We can yet catch him if we're quick."

The two men sprinted after the Sadhu and quickly caught up with him on the forest path. 

"Caught you, you rogue!" the merchant yelled, grabbing the Sadhu by the throat. 

"Wh-what? I am a holy man who has renounced the world..." the Sadhu stammered. 

The merchant pulled back his fist and landed a heavy punch squarely on the Sadhu's jaw. *CRACK!* 

"Mercy, mercy! I'll tell you where it is!" the terrified Sadhu shrieked. 

The bleeding Sadhu led them into the forest and showed them exactly where he had hidden the gold. 

"So," the Zamindar glared at the bruised hypocrite, "the stolen gold didn't trouble your delicate conscience nearly as much as a single piece of straw did!" 

And giving the completely humiliated Sadhu a terribly sound scolding, they took their gold and left him in the dirt.
