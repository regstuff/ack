# Bahubali

Long ago, Nabhiraj, the father of Lord Rishabhadeva, lived in a magnificent palace in Ayodhya. He brought up Rishabhadeva with great love. When Rishabhadeva grew up and became King, he had two sons. He made his elder son, Bharat, proficient in social affairs and administration. His other son, Bahubali, learned the art of archery. 

One day, it so happened that Rishabhadeva became disillusioned with his regal splendour. Seeking spiritual liberation, he renounced the world to become a muni (sage), also known as Adinatha, the first Tirthankara of the Jains. Before renouncing the world, Rishabhadeva divided his kingdom amongst his sons. Bharat was given the kingdom of Ayodhya, and Bahubali was made the king of Podanpur.

But Bharat was not satisfied. He had a burning desire to become the sovereign king of the entire world. One day, he received auspicious news from his general. "Oh Lord! A Chakra has come into being in your armoury. Take advantage of it."

"General, state clearly what you mean," Bharat asked.

"Your majesty, a Chakra is the sign of triumph. This Chakra expects from you the victory of the universe."

"We understand," Bharat proclaimed. "Tomorrow itself our army will march to gain victory over the entire earth."

Early next morning, King Bharat's huge army was arranged in columns. Led by the invincible Chakra, the army marched forward in all four directions. They advanced east across the Ganges, where petty kings surrendered and presented precious gifts. They marched south, conquering regions from Kalinga to Kerala. In the west, they crossed the Vindhya mountains and the Narmada river, vanquishing kings up to Saurashtra. Crossing the Indus, they marched to the Vijayardha mountains, bringing under control the Himavana mountain region and subjugating the non-Aryan kings of Chilat and Avarta. 

After years of victory, when Bharat finally returned to Ayodhya, the Chakra suddenly stopped at the main city gate. Panicking, the people cried in unison, "What has happened?"

When this news was conveyed to Bharat, he summoned his priest. "Oh learned one, please explain why this Chakra has stopped."

"Your majesty, it seems the conquest is not complete," the priest explained. 

"We have conquered all the four directions. What remains to be conquered now?" Bharat demanded.

"Pardon me, my Lord," the priest said hesitantly, "your invincible brothers have not yet offered salutation to you. Amongst them, the valiant and wise Bahubali is the chief."

"Oh, then they will be vanquished," Bharat said furiously. "Defiant Bahubali shall see the fury of our weapons. Being younger, he behaves so. But unless he surrenders, you cannot be called a sovereign king."

Furious, Bharat sent his minister with a message to his brothers: "Now only Bharat is the ruler of the entire earth. If they obey us, then they too will get some share." 

When Bharat's brothers heard this, they were dismayed. They went to their father, Muni Rishabhadeva, on Mount Kailash and implored, "Father, we have bowed down only before you. In his arrogance, Bharat wants us to offer him salutation. What shall we do?"

"Strife for the kingdoms divides brothers and sons," Rishabhadeva advised. "Someday Bharat too will have to give up his kingdom. Why fight? Abandon your kingdom." Following his advice, the brothers gave up their kingdoms. 

But Bahubali, the king of Podanpur, refused. When Bharat's emissary arrived demanding his subjugation, Bahubali said, "What sort of salutation to a brother who carries a sword in his hand to snatch the kingdom given by our father? Go tell him that he cannot become the sovereign king without defeating me in the battlefield."

Soon, the two massive forces stood facing each other near Podanpur. Both brothers were ready with their bows, and the generals began arranging their armies in order.

Before the war could begin, the ministers of both sides intervened. "Lord Bharat and Lord Bahubali, please wait!" one of them shouted. "It is necessary that the ministers of both sides hold talks before starting this war." 

The masters agreed. The ministers assembled and reasoned, "This war is not motivated to remove any injustice. Why should the blood of others be shed just to determine which of the two brothers is a better warrior? It would be best if the armies do not combat. Only the two brothers should fight."

They proposed a peaceful duel: "Both the brothers should wage a sight-battle, a water-battle, and a wrestling match. Whoever wins in these will be declared the victor." Both kings agreed to this war of righteousness.

First started the sight-battle. Both brothers stared at each other, looking hard into each other's eyes. In the end, Bharat's eyes dampened and his lashes dropped; his neck also started aching. Bahubali, being taller, kept on staring without a blink and won.

Next, they descended into a pond for the water-battle. Bharat started splashing water on Bahubali with all his strength, but Bahubali stood quiet. Then Bahubali started showering blows on the water. The water he splashed entered Bharat's mouth, eyes, nose, and ears, whereas the water Bharat threw never reached Bahubali's face. Overwhelmed, Bharat turned his face away, and Bahubali won the water-battle too.

Finally, the wrestling match started. Both entered the wrestling arena. Their hands met in fierce grips. They clapped their thighs, clenched their fists, and showered blows on each other. Engaged in the fight, Bahubali suddenly changed his position and effortlessly lifted Bharat up with both hands.

"Shall I throw him on the ground?" Bahubali thought. "No, he is my elder brother. I shall make him sit on my shoulder." But as Bahubali brought him slowly and gently to the ground, Bharat's eyes reddened with humiliation. Deceitfully, in a fit of rage, Bharat threw his divine Chakra at Bahubali.

The spectators shouted in horror, "This is deceitful! Shame on Bharat!" 

But a miracle occurred. The Chakra merely went round Bahubali and then stopped peacefully in his hands. Bahubali smiled and turned to Bharat in a harsh tone. "Bharat, you forget that this Chakra cannot harm my mountain-like body. In your anger, you also forgot that this Chakra is not effective on brothers. You wish to become a sovereign king by destroying your brothers. By deceiving me, you have tarnished our father's name."

As he spoke, Bahubali calmed down and thought. "The elder brother has behaved in such a shameful manner for the greed of a kingdom. Kingdoms are a cause of misery. Of what use is this power and glory? For a kingdom even brothers come to daggers."

He turned to Bharat and said softly, "Bharat, you did me good! Take the kingdom. I am going to perform penance. I did not behave respectfully towards my elder brother, therefore, brother, forgive me." 

The sky reverberated with cheers: "Hail to the sovereign king Bahubali!"

Bahubali cast aside his royal robes and jewels. "Look after the kingdom, Bharat. I am leaving now," he said, and proceeded to become a muni. 

Bahubali gave up everything. There were neither clothes on his body nor the joys of the palace. His body became emaciated as he stood on both legs performing intense penance. Creepers wound round his arms and legs, and serpents made mounts of earth near his feet. But despite his severe austerities, he did not attain perfect knowledge.

When Bharat heard of this, he approached his father, Rishabhadeva. "Father, why has Bahubali not attained omniscience? Something still disturbs him."

"He is not free from the thought that he is standing on your domain," Rishabhadeva explained. 

"How can this be wiped, oh venerated one?"

"Bharat, go with your family. Offer him respect."

Bharat immediately went to Bahubali with his family and bowed deeply. "We convey our salutation to you, Muni Bahubali. The ground on which you stand is not mine, will never be mine... It belongs to everyone. Please show us the path."

On hearing this, the final trace of pique in Bahubali's mind disappeared. He attained omniscience and achieved liberation. 

To commemorate the memory of Bahubali, Bharat installed a life-size statue of him in Podanpur. Time passed, and creepers and snakes made their homes nearby, hiding the statue.

Centuries later, in South India, the mother of a general named Chamundaraya expressed a desire to see Bahubali's statue. "It is very difficult to see the statue of Bahubali in Podanpur," Chamundaraya told her. "I shall erect an idol of Bahubali here on the Indra Giri." 

On his bidding, artisans started hewing a huge statue of Bahubali in Shravanabelagola near Mysore in the year 983. Even today, the magnificent monolith stands as it was, its faint smile serving as a reproach to the wars waged by kings and emperors. Empires are built and destroyed, but Bahubali's spiritual empire lasts to this day.
