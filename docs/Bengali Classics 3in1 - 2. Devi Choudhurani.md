# Devi Choudhurani: The Bandit Queen of Bengal

In Durgapur, a quiet village in North Bengal, lived a poor Brahman widow and her exceptionally beautiful daughter, Prafulla. The fame of Prafulla's beauty reached Haraballabh, the wealthy but arrogant Zamindar of Bhuthnath, who selected her as a bride for his son, Brajeshwar.

Though Prafulla's mother spent all her meager savings to host a fitting wedding feast, envious neighbors spread vile rumors questioning the widow's character. Enraged by the village gossip, Haraballabh declared the marriage void of honor and ordered his son to drive Prafulla out of the house. Bound by strict filial obedience, Brajeshwar reluctantly sent Prafulla back to her mother.

For two long years, mother and daughter lived in dire poverty, spinning cotton thread to earn a pittance. Unable to watch her mother starve, Prafulla decided to seek maintenance from her in-laws. Her mother-in-law received her with sympathy, but Haraballabh remained heartless. "She cannot stay in my house!" he bellowed. "Braja, drive her out!"

Before Prafulla left, Brajeshwar met her secretly in an inner chamber. "Forgive me, Prafulla," he wept, placing his personal engraved diamond ring into her palm. "I am helpless before my father. Take this ring—sell it and live on the money until I am able to support you."

"Your name is engraved upon it," Prafulla replied softly, pressing the ring to her heart. "I will never part with it, even if I starve."

Returning to Durgapur, Prafulla arrived just in time to receive her mother's dying blessing. Left completely alone, her ordeal worsened when four thugs, hired by a lustful local zamindar, broke into her cottage at midnight and abducted her in a palanquin. 

As the palanquin was carried through the dense forest, a band of forest dacoits attacked the abductors. The terrified thugs dropped the palanquin and fled for their lives. Seizing her chance, Prafulla slipped into the dark woods and stumbled upon the crumbling ruins of an ancient mansion.

Inside the ruins lay an emaciated, dying old man. Hearing her footsteps, he gasped, "Water... please bring me water..." Prafulla fetched water from a nearby stream and cradled his head as he drank. 

Grateful for her kindness, the dying man whispered, "I have no heirs. Near that window, buried beneath the earth, lies an ewer filled with gold coins. Take it... it is yours." With those words, he breathed his last.

Prafulla dug up the pitcher of gold, buried the old man honorably, and set out toward the forest trail to buy provisions. There she encountered Bhavani Pathak, a legendary Robin Hood-like leader of a forest band who robbed corrupt British tax collectors and tyrannical landlords to feed the poor.

Noticing the gold coin in Prafulla's hand, Bhavani Pathak recognized her intelligence, courage, and desperate situation. "You possess beauty, wealth, and courage," he told her. "If you submit to my discipline for five years without touching this gold, I will train you to rule."

Prafulla agreed. For five years under the guidance of Bhavani Pathak and her instructress Nishi, Prafulla underwent rigorous discipline. She studied Kalidasa's literature, mastered the philosophy of the *Bhagavad Gita*, learned administration, and trained in unarmed combat and wrestling. 

At the end of five years, transformed into a wise, fearless, and refined leader, Prafulla assumed command of the organization under the formidable title of **Devi Choudhurani**. Moving along Bengal's riverways in a regal golden barge, her forces struck terror into oppressive landlords and corrupt officials, redistributing wealth to the needy while strictly prohibiting unnecessary bloodshed.

Meanwhile, back at Bhuthnath, Haraballabh faced financial ruin. Dacoits had repeatedly looted his revenue collections, leaving him with tax arrears of fifty thousand rupees to the British government. Facing imminent arrest, Haraballabh sent Brajeshwar to borrow the sum from his second father-in-law, the wealthy father of Brajeshwar's second wife, Sagar.

However, Sagar's father insulted Haraballabh's reputation. Outraged, Brajeshwar severed ties with the family and stormed out. Sagar ran after her husband, begging to accompany him, but Brajeshwar rebuffed her harshly. 

Observing this from the shadows, Devi Choudhurani secretly met Sagar. Recognizing Prafulla despite her regal attire, Sagar embraced her lost co-wife. Together, they devised a clever plan.

That night on the Trisrota River, Devi Choudhurani's men intercepted Brajeshwar's boat. Without firing a shot, they bound the boatmen and escorted Brajeshwar onto Devi's luxurious barge, ushering him before a veiled lady seated on a velvet divan.

"You are my slave now," the veiled lady said in a disguised voice. "Tend my feet!"

Disgusted yet polite, Brajeshwar knelt to touch her feet. The moment his hands touched her ankles, the lady threw off her veil with a triumphant laugh—it was Sagar, fulfilling her vow to make her proud husband tend her feet!

Sagar then brought Brajeshwar into an adjoining cabin where Devi Choudhurani sat. Bowing politely, Devi offered him fifty thousand rupees in gold. "Take this and pay off your father's debt," she said gently.

Brajeshwar hesitated. "I need the money, but I can only accept it as a loan to be repaid on the full moon of Baisakh."

"As you wish," Devi smiled. Slipping off a diamond ring, she pressed it into his hand. "Take this token."

Brajeshwar looked closely at the ring—it was his own engraved diamond ring! "Prafulla!" he gasped, looking up in awe. "You are Devi Choudhurani!"

Returning home, Brajeshwar handed the money to his father, explaining it was a loan from Devi Choudhurani due in Baisakh. But Haraballabh's greed overcame his honor. *Repay a dacoit?* he thought wickedly. *Nonsense! I shall inform the British Collector and claim the bounty on her head!*

When the month of Baisakh arrived, Haraballabh led British Lieutenant Brennan and five hundred soldiers to ambush Devi's barge on the river. Unaware of his father's treachery, Brajeshwar arrived on the barge to repay the loan.

Suddenly, British gunboats surrounded the barge. Realizing that Haraballabh had betrayed her, Devi acted swiftly to prevent a bloodbath. She ordered her deputy Rangaraj to raise a white flag and invite Lieutenant Brennan aboard.

When Lieutenant Brennan stepped into the cabin, Nishi and her sister, dressed in royal silk, deliberately confused the officer by claiming to be Devi Choudhurani. Moments later, Prafulla stepped forward calmly. "They are lying to protect me. I am Devi Choudhurani."

Just as Devi had anticipated from the skies, a sudden tropical gale lashed the river. Before the British soldiers could react to the tempest, Devi's master boatmen steered the heavy barge straight through the line of British gunboats, escaping into the open waters with Lieutenant Brennan and Haraballabh trapped aboard!

Inside the cabin, Nishi sternly announced to the terrified captives that they were sentenced to death. Haraballabh fell to his knees, weeping and begging for mercy. 

Devi stepped in with a smile. She granted Lieutenant Brennan his freedom on the condition that he never wage war against her people again. Turning to her father-in-law, she said softly, "Go home, father. Prepare a fitting reception for Brajeshwar and his bride."

The following morning, as the storm cleared, Devi Choudhurani handed her remaining wealth to Rangaraj, instructing him to continue aiding the poor. Stripping off her royal robes, Prafulla stepped back into the world as a humble, devoted wife.

Haraballabh received Prafulla into his home with profound gratitude and respect, while Bhavani Pathak, satisfied that his noble queen had restored justice and found peace, voluntarily surrendered to the authorities.
