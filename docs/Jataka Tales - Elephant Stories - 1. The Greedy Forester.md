# The Greedy Forester

The Bodhisattva assumed many forms through many life cycles. And so it was that when Brahmadatta reigned in Varanasi, he was born an elephant in a forest in the Himalayas. 

In a grove of deodar trees, the proud mother stood, her newborn by her side. "Have you seen the new baby? He's white all over and shines like molten silver. He looks like a full moon leaning on a dark cloud," the other elephants marvelled. 

The elephant grew up in the forest, with other little elephants. As he grew older, he noticed that most of his companions were selfish, greedy, and even cruel. The white elephant decided to leave the herd. "I don't belong here. I'd rather live alone." 

And so he dwelt apart, giving help and good counsel when needed. One day he saw a little monkey weeping. "Why are you crying?" "I'm small and weak. My friends tease me." The elephant comforted him. Many an evening found him advising an eager group of animals, young and old. Soon he came to be known as 'Good King Elephant'. 

One day, a forester from Varanasi lost his way in that forest. "I have never felt more frightened in my life. Every path seems to lead me farther away from home," he cried, exhausted and terrified. As the light grew fainter, he saw a shadow. "An elephant! I'm as good as dead! Heaven help me! I'll be crushed to pulp!" 

But every time he moved, the elephant followed. Every time he stopped, the elephant too stopped. "Why, he seems harmless! He's only following me, not chasing me as I thought," the forester realized. "I'll turn round and stop. Let's see what he does." 

To his surprise, the animal spoke to him. "I heard you scream. Is there anything I can do for you?" 

"What a kind, gentle voice! And yet, so deep and powerful!" the forester thought. "I've lost my way. I want to go to Varanasi," he replied. 

"Come home with me. I'll guide you to the city," the elephant offered. The elephant took the forester to his cave and gave him food and rest. In the evening they walked to a clearing. "Let us watch the sun go down. It fills my heart with peace," the elephant said. "I envy you, your peace of mind. We men are restless creatures," the forester replied. 

After a few days, the forester said, "I'd love to stay on, but I have work to do in Varanasi." 

"I'll take you to the edge of the forest and set you on your way to the city," the elephant agreed. The forester rode on the elephant's back. As they went, the forester thought greedily, "I must remember this clump of trees... and this hill and this winding path. I may need to come back some day." 

At the edge of the forest, he dismounted. "There lies the road to Varanasi. Thank you, my friend." 

A few days later, the forester happened to visit the ivory bazaar at Varanasi. "What beautiful things you create!" he told the craftsmen. 

"They could be even more beautiful, but good tusks are hard to come by. It's the best. But it's rare and costs much more than the tusk of a dead one," the craftsmen explained, referring to the ivory of a living elephant. 

Greed got the better of the forester. He went back to the forest and traced his way to the elephant. "What is the matter? You look unhappy," the elephant asked. 

"I am up to my neck in debt. A piece of your tusk can save me," the forester lied. 

"I will gladly give you both my tusks. But you will have to cut them off. What you hold are no ordinary tusks. They are the source of all my wisdom." 

"I came prepared. I knew you would not fail me," the forester said, bringing out a saw and eagerly cutting off the tusks. 

Back in Varanasi, the forester received a big sum of money for the tusks. But a month later, his greed flared again. "After I buy these silks and jewels, there'll be no money left. I should have cut closer to the flesh. What a waste of good ivory." 

That night he could not sleep. "If I cut any more, the elephant will suffer. But I must not be sentimental. I must get hold of those precious stumps of tusks." 

So back he went to the elephant. Greed had hardened his heart. "Your tusks did help to clear my old debts. But I need more money in order to live," he pleaded. 

"You may have what is left of my tusks," the noble elephant agreed. He crouched down to give the tusks. The forester carried out his cruel plan, pinning the elephant's trunk down with his foot and sawing them off. The elephant was left torn and trembling, but not a word of reproach escaped his lips. 

Suddenly, the forester felt the ground heave under his feet. The earth split open and a fire raged. The forester fell in, realizing he was being punished for his greed, but it was too late. 

As the flames consumed him, a voice was heard: "An ungrateful man is never satisfied—not even if he is given the whole world." 

As for the wise elephant, he lived the rest of his life in the peace and quiet of the Himalayas.
