# The Man Who Loved Vadas

One day Khandoba, the miser, felt a strong craving. "Wife! Will you make some vadas for me?" he asked. 

"Yes?" she replied. "I will, if you get me some oil and some dal." 

"You'll need this tin for the oil, won't you?" she asked, handing him a container. 

"No, I won't," Khandoba said, too lazy to carry it. He put on his large white turban and went to a shop in the market. 

"Can you show me the best oil you have?" he asked. 

"Try this one, sir. It's the best in the market," the shopkeeper offered. 

"Let me smell it and see." Khandoba bent forward, as if to smell the oil in the large drum. Suddenly, his loose turban slipped and fell right into the oil! 

"Oh, dear! My turban!" he cried. 

"Oh, no!" the shopkeeper gasped. He quickly fished out the dripping cloth. "I'm extremely sorry, sir. Your turban..." 

"Never mind. Give it to me. I'll have it washed and..." 

"No, my friend. I'm in a hurry. I must leave right away. I'll come back later for the oil," Khandoba said quickly, snatching his oil-soaked turban. 

Back at home, Khandoba squeezed the heavy turban into a vessel. "The vessel is full!" his wife exclaimed in surprise. "But I don't understand why you brought the oil in your turban." 

"Now take this bag," she offered another container. 

"What for?" 

"To get the dal." 

"I have my turban," he grinned mischievously. 

At the market, he went to a grain merchant. "I want some good dal, sir. Can you show me the best you have?" 

"Here. Have a look at the dal in these bags, sir." 

"Let me... oh! My turban!" Khandoba deliberately knocked his turban into the open sack of dal. The lentils stuck to the oily cloth. 

"I'll see you later," he said, picking up the dal-coated turban and rushing off. 

"Here you are! All the dal you need," Khandoba presented his turban to his wife. 

"Why couldn't you use a bag as everyone else does?" she sighed. 

"I have my reasons. Now I am going out. I want the vadas to be ready by the time I return." 

Khandoba's wife ground the dal, patted the paste into vadas, and began to fry them. Soon she had a plateful of crisp, hot vadas ready. 

"M-m-m-m...! Delicious!" she tasted one. "I think I'd better taste one and see... I'll have just one more, while it's hot." Before she knew it, she couldn't stop. 

Sometime later Khandoba returned. "Are the vadas ready?" 

"Ye..s. Twenty of them," she replied nervously. 

He looked at the single vada on the plate. "You ate nineteen vadas! How could anyone eat so many vadas!" he exploded. 

"Like this!" she said, popping the last vada into her mouth. 

"Hey! Wait! I...!" Khandoba lunged, but it was too late. Poor Khandoba, despite all his scheming, had to go without the vadas.
