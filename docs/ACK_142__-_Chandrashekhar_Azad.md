# Chandra Shekhar Azad: Great Indian Revolutionary

Chandra Shekhar was born in 1906, in Bhanwra, a small village in the Central Provinces of British India. From a young age, he was a robust, intelligent, and fiercely curious boy. Once, his mother caught him lighting an entire box of matches at once. 

"Experimenting, mother," he explained innocently. "A single match gives a good deal of light. I wanted to see how much light a boxful of matches could give."

"Haven't I told you that fire is dangerous?" she scolded, pulling him away.

"But it is also useful, mother," he smiled. "It chases away darkness."

As he grew older, Chandra Shekhar learned to shoot a bow and arrow with incredible accuracy from his local Bhil friends. Eager to explore the world, he caught a ride to Bombay with a traveling pearl trader. He worked as a painter's assistant in the docks for a few months, but quickly realized he wanted something more meaningful. "In what way will this sort of work benefit me?" he thought. "I'll go to Varanasi and become a Sanskrit pandit."

In 1921, the Non-Cooperation Movement against British rule was in full swing. The streets of Varanasi echoed with patriotic fervor. Fired by the zeal of his countrymen, young Chandra Shekhar joined a massive procession of revolutionaries, holding a banner high. "Inquilab Zindabad! Bharat Mata ki Jai!" he shouted.

The British police descended on the crowd. "Arrest the lot, insolent brats!" the officer yelled. Chandra Shekhar was among the very first to be arrested. 

When produced before the British Magistrate, the boy stood tall and fearless. 

"What's your name?" the Magistrate asked.

"Azad," he replied clearly. The word meant 'Free'.

"What is your father's name? Where do you live?"

"Freedom is my father's name and jail, my home."

Furious, the Magistrate yelled, "Impudent brat! But you are still a child. So I'll let you off with a good caning. Take him away."

The police dragged him to the courtyard, tied him to a wooden frame, and whipped him mercilessly. But Azad did not squirm or cry out even once. Instead, after every agonizing stroke of the cane, he shouted, "Inquilab Zindabad! Bharat Mata ki Jai!"

When news of his incredible bravery spread, the revolutionaries of Varanasi gave him a hero's welcome. "Friends! I dedicate my life to the service of my country," Azad addressed the cheering crowd. "The police will never again take me alive! I will always remain free. I am Azad."

From that day on, the name 'Azad' stuck forever. 

Two years later, in 1923, the Hindustan Republican Association established a center in Varanasi. Azad quickly became a staunch supporter alongside leaders like Ramprasad Bismil and Ashfaqullah. 

"We need funds for our work. How are we to raise it?" Bismil asked during a secret meeting.

"There is only one way—let's rob the rich," a member suggested. 

"Robbing the rich will only make us unpopular," another argued. "Let us loot the government. Then alone will the people support our movement."

On the 9th of August, 1925, Azad and his comrades executed a daring plan. They secretly boarded the 8-Down Lucknow passenger train at Kakori. Once the train reached a secluded spot, they pulled the chain, overpowered the guards, broke open the state treasury safe with sledgehammers, and vanished with the cash. The daring heist became known as the Kakori Conspiracy.

A massive manhunt followed, and sadly, many of the conspirators were caught and sentenced to death. Heartbroken but undeterred, Azad eluded the British police for years, constantly changing disguises. He lived as a wandering Hindu ascetic in a Hanuman temple near Jhansi, terrifying local police officers who dared question him. Later, he lived as a humble mechanic named 'Hari Shankar', quietly learning how to drive and repair cars while secretly organizing the party from the shadows. 

By 1928, the movement had gathered massive momentum. Azad traveled to Kanpur and joined forces with the fiery revolutionary from Punjab, Bhagat Singh. Together, they formed the Hindustan Socialist Republican Association. Azad commanded the military division, while Bhagat Singh led the organizers. 

When the British sent the Simon Commission to India, millions protested. During a massive protest in Lahore, the police ordered a brutal lathi-charge. The beloved independence leader, Lala Lajpat Rai, was severely beaten and died from his injuries. 

Azad and Bhagat Singh were furious. "I cannot bear it any longer. We must take revenge," Azad declared. 

They planned to assassinate Scott, the British police official responsible. But in the chaos of the ambush, they mistakenly shot and killed another officer, Saunders. "Never mind. Saunders too was among those who caused Lalaji's death," they realized. 

A massive dragnet was thrown over Lahore. "Close all roads leading out of the town," the police chief ordered. But Azad was a master of evasion. While Bhagat Singh escaped disguised as a British gentleman, Azad dressed as a singing sadhu and slipped right past the armed guards onto a train to Mathura. 

Azad and Bhagat Singh set up secret bomb factories in Agra and Kanpur. In 1929, to awaken the masses, Bhagat Singh and Batukeshwar Dutt threw a non-lethal bomb into the Central Legislative Assembly, intentionally getting themselves arrested to use their trial as a platform for independence. 

"I must be arrested and sentenced to death," Bhagat Singh insisted to a worried Azad. "It will inspire others to dedicate their lives to the cause."

With Bhagat Singh in prison, Azad took total command of the movement. He organized daring raids, smuggled weapons, and even planted a bomb under Lord Irwin's train (though the Viceroy narrowly escaped). But tragedy struck again when the British government sentenced Bhagat Singh to death by hanging. Despite Azad's tireless efforts to save him, Bhagat Singh was martyred on March 23, 1931. 

A devastated Azad shifted his headquarters to Allahabad. The ranks of the movement had dwindled, and traitors lurked in the shadows. 

On February 27, 1931, Azad and his comrade Sukhdevraj were walking through Alfred Park in Allahabad. A traitor spotted them and immediately informed the police. Within minutes, eighty armed policemen in four vans surrounded the park. 

Azad was caught completely unawares. Before he could even draw his famous Mauser pistol, a police bullet struck him deeply in the thigh. 

"Panditji!" his comrade screamed.

"Get away from here, Raj. I will hold them," Azad ordered, bleeding heavily. Sukhdevraj made a desperate run for safety while Azad turned and opened fire on the overwhelming forces. 

Azad was quickly shot in the right arm, rendering it useless. Unfazed, he grabbed his Mauser with his left hand and continued the ferocious gun battle. For thirty-two minutes, the wounded lion held eighty heavily armed policemen at bay from behind a single tree, returning fire with deadly accuracy. 

Finally, Azad checked his pistol. He had only one bullet left. 

"No! I won't be taken alive!" Azad whispered fiercely. True to the vow he had made as a child in Varanasi, he raised the Mauser to his own temple and pressed the trigger. 

Even as his lifeless body fell to the ground, the British police were so terrified of him that the officer ordered his men to shoot his corpse again before daring to approach. 

Chandra Shekhar Azad was only twenty-five years old when he became a martyr for the cause of Indian independence. But true to his name, he lived and died a free man.
