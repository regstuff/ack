# Dasharatha: The Story of Rama's Father

Long ago, young Dasharatha, the beloved crown prince of Ayodhya, was renowned for his incredible skill with a bow. He was so masterful that he could hit a target in the dead of night, guided only by the sound of its movement. 

One dark, rainy night, Dasharatha was hunting in the forest when he heard a deep gurgling noise nearby. *Glug... glug... glug...*

*What's that? A thirsty elephant's trunk, out for its fill!* he thought. Relying on his hearing, he aimed at the sound and let his deadly arrow fly.

But when the arrow found its mark, it was not an elephant that cried out, but a young man. "Ah! Murder! But I have harmed none," the voice wept in agony. "Ah! My poor, old, blind parents! I leave you, helpless."

Dasharatha ran to the spot, his heart pounding with dread. He had mistaken the gurgling of a hermit's water pitcher for an elephant drinking. As he reached the riverbank, he found a young ascetic dying in the mud, Dasharatha's arrow piercing his chest. 

"What have I done!" Dasharatha cried in horror. "I who am a virtuous prince, the protector of the weak!"

The dying youth looked up weakly. "O prince, my thirsty parents are waiting at the hermitage nearby. Go and tell them about me. But first, remove this arrow. Let death relieve me of this pain."

Dasharatha did as he was told. He carefully removed the arrow, and the youth breathed his last. Filled with unbearable guilt, Dasharatha filled the pitcher with water and slowly made his way to the hermitage. 

Inside the small hut, an old, blind couple sat waiting. "Our son has been away a long while. Could something have happened to him?" the father asked nervously.

Hearing Dasharatha's footsteps, the old man smiled. "Ah! There you are at last. Quick, give me the water. I cannot bear the thirst any longer."

Tears flowed down Dasharatha's face. He stood there, speechless.

"Why are you silent, my son?" the blind father asked.

"O venerable one, I am not your son," Dasharatha wept. "My name is Dasharatha. I have committed a heinous sin and stand before you, sad and repentant." He explained how he had unknowingly shot their son in the dark. 

The old couple sat stunned. Then, past his choking grief, the bereft father cursed the prince. "It was my son. Our only son. You killed him. Your son, too, shall be parted from you. And you shall die grieving for him."

A thoroughly shaken Dasharatha returned to his palace. But as the years passed, the tragic incident gradually faded from his memory. After his father, King Aja, retired to the forest, Dasharatha became the King of Ayodhya. He married Kausalya, a princess of Kosala. 

Dasharatha was a noble, virtuous king. He was so renowned for his valour that even Indra, the King of the Devas, sought his help in wars against the Asuras. But despite his greatness, Dasharatha was unhappy. Kausalya had not borne him any sons. 

Hoping for an heir, he sought the hand of the lovely Kaikeyi. In order to win her, Dasharatha made a solemn promise to her father: "The son born to Kaikeyi shall become the crown prince." 

But years passed, and Kaikeyi too failed to bear a son. Dasharatha then wed the gentle Princess Sumitra, but she also remained barren.

One day, Indra asked for Dasharatha's help in a great battle against the Asura Shambara. Kaikeyi, a bold and fiercely loyal queen, insisted on accompanying her husband to the battlefield. During a terrible clash, Dasharatha was severely wounded and lay senseless on the field. 

Kaikeyi bravely rode her chariot through the carnage, rescued the King, and nursed him day and night in her tent until he regained consciousness.

"Kaikeyi, my dearest Queen, you have brought me back to life," Dasharatha smiled. "Ask any two boons of me."

"At this moment, with you alive, I have all I need, my Lord," Kaikeyi smiled back. "I'll ask for the boons when I need them."

From then on, Kaikeyi became Dasharatha's favorite queen. Desperate for an heir, Dasharatha eventually performed a grand sacrifice. From the blazing fire, a divine being emerged holding a bowl of *payasa* (a sweet gruel). "O King, give this to your worthy queens and they shall bear you sons."

The just King first took the *payasa* to Kausalya and gave her half. He then went to Sumitra and gave her a fourth. But realizing he needed to favor Kaikeyi, he changed his mind, giving Kaikeyi an eighth, and the remaining eighth back to gentle Sumitra. 

Within a year, all three queens gave birth. Kausalya gave birth to Rama; Kaikeyi to Bharata; and Sumitra to the twins Lakshmana and Shatrughna. Though Dasharatha loved all four princes, Rama, the eldest, was his favorite. 

Years later, when the princes had grown into handsome youths, Dasharatha decided it was time to step down. "I shall see Rama crowned Yuvraja (heir-apparent), without any delay," he announced to his council. 

The kingdom rejoiced at the news. But in Kaikeyi's chambers, her wicked, hunch-backed nurse, Manthara, was furious. 

"Wake up, Kaikeyi! Your good days are about to come to a rude end," Manthara hissed. "The crafty King has sent your son Bharata away, only to install his Rama as the heir-apparent. Don't you understand? Kausalya will humiliate you!"

At first, Kaikeyi dismissed her, but Manthara’s poisonous words slowly twisted her mind. "If Rama becomes king, he will banish Bharata! You will be a slave in the royal household!" 

"Ah, Kaikeyi! Listen to me," Manthara urged. "Remember the two boons Dasharatha had offered you many years ago? Ask for them now. Ask for the installation of Bharata and the exile of Rama for fourteen years."

Kaikeyi, her mind fully poisoned by jealousy and fear, cast off her royal finery and threw herself on the floor of the 'chamber of protest'.

When Dasharatha found her there weeping, he was horrified. "O Kaikeyi, why are you in tears? Tell me what you want, and I swear by Rama that I will do whatever you want."

"Name the boons and I shall see that they are yours," he promised.

Kaikeyi slowly looked up. "Then let my son Bharata be proclaimed heir-apparent. And let Rama be banished to the forest for fourteen years."

Dasharatha was stunned. "No-o-o-o!" he screamed, clutching his chest. "I may banish Kausalya or Sumitra, but never Rama! My eldest son, whose love sustains my life!"

But Kaikeyi was immovable. "Nothing stains that fair name as a broken promise," she mocked coldly. 

Realizing he was trapped by his own vow, Dasharatha collapsed at her feet in despair. By morning, he was a broken man. "Let Rama go to the forest," he whispered weakly. "And let the preparations for tomorrow be for my funeral."

When Rama was called, he accepted the cruel decree without a moment of hesitation. "I would willingly surrender not only the kingdom but also my very life for your order alone, mother," Rama told Kaikeyi calmly. 

Despite the weeping of the entire kingdom, Rama, his wife Sita, and his loyal brother Lakshmana departed for the forest. 

The moment Rama left, Dasharatha collapsed. He refused to let Kaikeyi near him. "Keep away, O vile Kaikeyi! Your touch will pollute me," he gasped, demanding to be taken to Kausalya's palace.

On the sixth night after Rama's departure, a haunting memory from Dasharatha's youth resurfaced in his feverish mind. The image of the blind, old hermit he had orphaned so many years ago flashed before his eyes. 

"His father's curse has come true," Dasharatha wept to Kausalya. "I die yearning for my son... O my prince, my son, where are you? O Kausalya! Ah, gentle Sumitra! I..."

With those final words, King Dasharatha breathed his last, his heart broken by the very curse he had earned in his youth. 

Yet Kaikeyi's wicked plot yielded her nothing but ash. When Bharata returned and learned what his mother had done, he furiously refused the throne. Placing Rama's wooden slippers on the royal seat, Bharata ruled Ayodhya only as a humble caretaker until the true King, Rama, returned from his exile.
