# Chandragupta Maurya: The Determined Prince

In 321 B.C., the ancient kingdom of Magadha was governed from its capital, Pataliputra, by the nine Nanda brothers, headed by Crown Prince Nanda. The aged King Sarvartha Siddhi had retired from active rule, leaving power in the hands of his tyrannical sons.

Arrogant and ruthless, the Nandas grew deeply envious of their noble cousins, General Maurya and his one hundred sons. Among the Mauryan princes, the youngest, Chandragupta, was celebrated as the most brilliant marksman and hunter in the realm.

"The Mauryas are powerful, intelligent, and loved by the people," Crown Prince Nanda whispered to his brothers during a hunting expedition. "One day they will prove a threat to us. If we let them live, they will take the throne."

A few months later, while King Sarvartha Siddhi was away on a pilgrimage, Nanda prepared a deadly trap. He constructed a grand, secluded palace outside Pataliputra and invited General Maurya and his hundred sons to an exclusive royal banquet.

Once the guests arrived, Nanda feigned an awkward situation. "Maurya, our main hall is crowded. Please take your sons down to the private cellar dining room while our other guests are served above."

Suspecting no treachery, General Maurya and his sons descended into the underground cellar. The moment the last prince entered, Nanda's henchmen slammed the heavy iron doors shut and bolted them from the outside.

"You will never come out of this palace alive!" Nanda taunted from behind the sealed doors.

Trapped in the pitch-black cellar without food or water, the Mauryas struggled in vain to break the iron doors. As days passed, starvation took its heavy toll. One by one, the princes collapsed. Holding young Chandragupta's hand, General Maurya gasped with his dying breath: "Even if one of us survives... he must take revenge!"

"Without a doubt, Father!" Chandragupta swore through tears.

Days later, when the stench of death reached the upper halls, Nanda's guards reported that all the prisoners had perished except one youth. Unwilling to open the cellar, Nanda ordered Chandragupta transferred to the state dungeon and set fire to the palace, burning it to ashes to conceal his crime.

Meanwhile, news of the Mauryas' destruction reached King Parvatak of Parvata Desh. Wanting to confirm if any Mauryas survived before invading Magadha, Parvatak dispatched his master spy, Kamalapida, to Pataliputra. 

Kamalapida brought an iron cage containing a fierce lion to the public square, issuing a royal challenge: "Set this lion free without opening or breaking the iron cage! If no one in Magadha can solve this, your king must pay tribute to Parvata Desh!"

Scholars and nobles gathered around the cage in bewilderment. The cage had no door whatsoever. How had the lion entered, and how could it be freed?

When news reached the state prison, young Chandragupta offered to accept the challenge. Brought before the court in chains, Chandragupta inspected the cage closely. He noticed that when a guard brandished a staff, the lion leapt in fury, but when a blindfolded elephant was paraded past, the lion showed no reaction at all. 

Examining the guard's staff, Chandragupta realized the staff was magnetized, and the "lion" was actually a hollow iron shell filled with solid wax!

Without uttering a word, Chandragupta picked up a burning torch and set fire to the cage. As heat filled the iron enclosure, the wax lion melted away into a pool of grease, leaving behind only a bare iron skeleton.

Emperor Nanda was forced to acknowledge the youth's brilliance. Releasing Chandragupta from prison, Nanda appointed him manager of the royal guest-house, intending to keep him under constant surveillance.

One hot afternoon, as Chandragupta stood near the guest-house, he observed a bald Brahman in simple red robes walking up the path. The Brahman pricked his foot on a clump of sharp, spiky *kusha* grass. Instead of merely walking away, the ascetic sat down in the dust and painstakingly uprooted every single blade of sharp grass along his path.

Impressed by the man's extraordinary determination, Chandragupta approached him. "Revered one, please honor me by dining at the guest-house before you proceed."

The Brahman was **Chanakya** (Kautilya), the legendary scholar of Takshashila. Over dinner, Chandragupta opened his heart, recounting the murder of his father and ninety-nine brothers. 

Chanakya placed a comforting hand on the prince's shoulder. "Don't worry, Chandragupta. I, Chanakya, will help you avenge your family and win the throne of Magadha."

That evening, as Chanakya finished his meal, Emperor Nanda and his brothers walked into the guest-house after a hunt. Seeing an unknown man in coarse robes occupying the seat of honor, Nanda flew into a rage. "What impudence! A beggar in rags occupying the royal seat! Get up, you scamp!"

Nanda seized Chanakya by his shoulder and shoved him off the dais. In the scuffle, Chanakya's sacred topknot of hair (*shikha*) came untied.

Standing erect, his eyes flashing with fire, Chanakya unbound his hair completely and swore a terrible oath before the royal assembly: *"You arrogant reprobates! I shall not tie up my hair until the Nanda dynasty is completely uprooted and destroyed! If I fail, my name is not Chanakya!"*

Chanakya and Chandragupta slipped out of Pataliputra that night and traveled to Parvata Desh, securing an alliance with King Parvatak to overthrow Nanda.

To neutralize Nanda's powerful external allies, Chanakya deployed master strategy. When the aggressive King of Lampaka threatened Parvatak's borders, Chanakya dispatched a spy to Kamarupa with a forged letter, inciting Kamarupa to attack Lampaka and keeping both foreign armies occupied away from Magadha.

Next, Chanakya sent envoys to the King of Kashi—a staunch ally of Nanda—soliciting his help against Nanda. Nanda's brilliant Prime Minister, Rakshasa, advised Kashi to pretend to join Chandragupta's alliance, intending to double-cross Chandragupta during the battle.

Anticipating Rakshasa's counter-trap, Chanakya set his own master trap outside Pataliputra. He arranged a fake midnight fire-sacrifice (*yagna*) led by his spy, Sage Masopavasi. 

At midnight, expecting King Kashi to deliver a captured Chandragupta in a royal palanquin to the sacrificial fire, Nanda and his eight brothers arrived at the site. 

"Deliver the prisoner!" Nanda laughed, stepping up to the palanquin.

The curtains flew open—and out leaped Chandragupta and a band of elite Mauryan commandos with drawn swords! 

"We have been duped!" Nanda screamed as Masopavasi set the sacrificial canopy ablaze, surrounding the Nandas in fire.

Chandragupta charged into the fray, his spear gleaming in the firelight. Spotting Nanda, Chandragupta drove his spear through the tyrant's chest. "The murders of my father and brothers are avenged!" he declared as Nanda collapsed dead onto the burning sands.

The remaining Nanda brothers were slain in the battle, and their army surrendered. 

With victory achieved, Chanakya tied up his topknot of hair in triumph. Chandragupta entered Pataliputra to the deafening cheers of the citizens and was anointed Emperor of India, founding the grand Maurya Empire.

Placing the imperial crown upon Chandragupta's head, Chanakya imparted his final counsel: "Be a just king, Chandragupta. Work unceasingly for the welfare of your people, and make India strong, prosperous, and united."
