# Jasma of the Odes

Jasma was the daughter of Nayak Dalo Dhand, chief of the nomadic Ode tribe. When Jasma was born, her mother said, "She reminds me of a jasmine, fair and pretty as she is. Then let us call her Jasma." 

Unfortunately for Jasma, her mother died when she was just a toddler. "Poor motherless Jasma. I must marry again and bring a mother for you," Dalo decided. 

Dalo married a girl called Dali. But Dali soon became jealous of her stepdaughter. "Jasma—Jasma. I'm sick of that name. I wonder why she didn't die with her mother," she thought. From the day she set eyes on her, Dali disliked Jasma and often ill-treated her. 

As Jasma grew up, Dali's dislike turned into jealous hatred, for Jasma was easily the most beautiful girl in the Ode tribe. She scolded her all day. "Lazy girl. Go to the well and fetch some water if you have nothing else to do." 

"I didn't say I wouldn't, mother," Jasma replied dutifully. 

That night Dali spoke to Dalo. "We must arrange Jasma's marriage soon. She has come of age. What about my cousin, Bhalo Bhand's son, Rupa?" 

"B... but Rupa is a cripple!" Dalo protested. But Dali did not give up and forced Dalo to agree. 

When they went to Bhalo Bhand, Dalo was honest. "Cousin, you know that all I have is for Jasma. I have one head of cattle. Please accept it and look after my little flower." 

"Cattle or no cattle, Jasma shall become my daughter-in-law!" Bhalo declared warmly. 

On the day of the wedding, Jasma went through the ceremony bravely. But that night when she saw Rupa's crippled form and face clearly for the first time, she broke down. "He's ugly too! Alas! What have I done to deserve this?" 

"Jasma, forgive me. I know how you must feel. I will try to make you as happy as I can. Please don't weep," Rupa comforted her, showing his heart of gold. 

Jasma was moved by his words. Ashamed of herself, she fell at his feet. "I am sorry, Rupa. I should ask your forgiveness. I will be a loyal wife to you and will look after all your needs." 

A few months later, Jivaji Barot, a court poet of Gujarat, happened to pass by the Ode camp. "What a beautiful girl! I have never seen anyone like her," he marvelled. 

He hurried back to Patan, the capital of King Siddharaj Jaisingh. "Your majesty, I have seen the most beautiful girl in creation—Jasma, the daughter of the chief of the Ode tribe," Jivaji reported, singing verses praising her beauty. 

"A mere tribal girl! You call her a queen! If it is an exaggeration, Jivaji, I'll behead you!" the King warned. 

"If you see her, your majesty, you will call her a goddess. But tribal customs will not permit Jasma to come to the palace," Jivaji explained. 

Meanwhile, a terrible famine hit Gujarat. The Odes were badly hit and starving. Jivaji advised the King, "The Odes are badly in need of work. If your majesty can provide it, the Odes will come to Patan." 

The King agreed and announced the construction of the Sahasra-Linga lake. The Odes accepted the work and camped on the outskirts of the capital. 

The next day, as the King rode to the site, he saw Jasma carrying a load. "My God! What a beautiful creature! She must be a goddess," he gasped. 

He rode up to her. "O beautiful one, what is your name?" 

"I am Jasma of the Ode tribe, your majesty," she replied simply. 

"Delicate one, you remind me of the jasmine flower. Say you will become mine and I will take you to my palace! I will make you the Queen of Gujarat," the King offered. 

"I'd rather live in my hut. Ha! Ha! Ha! What would I do with a kingdom?" Jasma laughed, spurning him. 

Her laughter maddened the King. "If you will not come willingly, I'll take you away by force." 

"Don't you dare touch me. I am a married woman," Jasma retorted fearlessly. 

The King sent his minister with a bag of gold to buy her from Dalo and Rupa. "A bag of gold... for you, provided you leave Jasma behind for the King," the minister offered. 

"We work with earth and stone. What would we do with gold? We will not sell our honour. Leave us!" Dalo and Rupa rejected the offer angrily. 

The spurned King decided to take her by force. He sent a palanquin to fetch her. Jasma filled the palanquin with rocks and sent it back. "I'll spare the tribe but I'll kill her husband and make her mine," the furious King decided, ordering his guards to kill Rupa. 

The guards attacked the Ode camp. The Odes fought back bravely, but Rupa was killed. Then the King ordered his army to capture the rest of the tribe. 

The Odes fled in the dead of night, but the royal army pursued them. The Odes fought to the finish. The King's army was initially routed, but the commander changed strategy and surrounded them. Dalo was killed. 

When Jasma heard of the deaths, she refused to run away. "I am an Ode. An Ode never runs away! I will join my husband," she declared. 

She prepared a funeral pyre and committed Sati. As the flames enveloped her, she cursed the King: "Alas! I am doomed! My kingdom is doomed. The great King Siddharaj Jaisingh will die a leper for lusting after a chaste wife." 

As the flames consumed her, Laldev, Indra's messenger, appeared. "I am Laldev... I have come to take you to heaven." 

"I will not go with you leaving all those who died for me behind," Jasma replied firmly. 

Struck by her loyalty, Laldev granted her wish. "Then I shall bring them back to life." One by one, the dead Odes breathed again. Rupa and Dalo were revived. 

"You may stay, Jasma. The doors of heaven are ever open to you. Come when you are ready," Laldev smiled, returning to heaven. 

Many, many years later, Jasma died. After her death, songs were composed on her and temples were built in her memory. "Jasma, you were famous for your beauty, but we remember you for your virtues," her people sang.
