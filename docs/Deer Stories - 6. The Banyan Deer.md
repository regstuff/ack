# The Banyan Deer

In two different parts of a vast forest lived two handsome golden stags. One was called the Banyan Deer, and the other was known as the Branch Deer. Each led a large herd of its own.

In the nearby city of Varanasi, there lived a King who was obsessively fond of hunting deer. His love for the sport was so great that he constantly disturbed his townsfolk. "Come on, everyone, let's go on a hunt!" he would announce, dragging the villagers away from their farms and daily chores. 

Tired of the constant interruptions, the people got together and hit upon a plan. "Let us chase all the deer into the king's garden," they decided. "Then he will have all the deer he wants right in his backyard, and he will not disturb us at odd hours to go into the forest." 

They gathered sticks, pots, pans, and anything that made a loud noise. The next day, they surrounded the forest and created a massive racket. *Bang! Clang! Thump!* Startled by the noise, they successfully drove all the deer, including the herds of the Banyan and Branch Deer, right into the king's sprawling walled garden. 

"There! Now he will not disturb us with so many deer so near him," the villagers cheered. A few townsmen then went to the King. "Sir, you distract us from work when you go hunting; so we have driven all the deer into your garden. You may shoot them whenever you wish." 

The King was delighted. "I must warn my men to leave the two golden stags alone, as they are too beautiful to kill," he instructed his royal archers. 

And so, the King would simply step into his garden and shoot a deer whenever he pleased. Sometimes, the head cook would accompany him. "Look, my lord! There goes a plump one," the cook would point. But if it was one of the golden stags, the King would say, "Not him. He is a golden stag."

However, the deer were being hunted in a chaotic and terrifying manner, causing injuries and panic as the herd stampeded. Seeing his brethren being slain in such large numbers and in such fear, the wise Banyan Deer decided to do something about it. He approached the Branch Deer. 

"My friend, our deer cannot escape death in this walled garden, but let them not be killed needlessly in terrifying stampedes," the Banyan Deer proposed. "We will send them to the execution block by turns—one from my herd, one from yours—each day." 

The Branch Deer agreed. In this way, the deer whose turn it was would willingly go to the block and peacefully wait for the royal cook to take him away. "Hmmm, this is a nice plump one today," the cook would say, simply taking the waiting deer. 

One day, it was the lot of a doe from the Branch Deer's herd. She was heavily pregnant and about to become a mother. She went to her leader, the Branch Deer, and pleaded, "My lord, let me escape this time. When I have had my little one, there will be two of us to go to the block. Please spare my unborn child."

"No, no, I cannot do this. It is your turn. Go away," the Branch Deer coldly dismissed her. 

Desperate, the pregnant doe went to the Banyan Deer and tearfully told him her story. 

The Banyan Deer's heart melted with compassion. "Very well. You don't go this time. I will do something about it," he promised her. 

That day, the Banyan Deer himself walked to the block and placed his golden head upon it. 

When the cook came along, he was shocked to see the majestic leader at the block. "Oh, oh! One of the golden deer! I must turn back and tell the king." 

When the King heard the news, he was completely surprised. He rushed to the spot in his royal chariot. "Why are you here?" he asked the Banyan Deer in disbelief. "I granted you immunity. You are free to live." 

"Your majesty," the Banyan Deer replied calmly, "a doe who was about to become a mother came to me and asked to be spared so her fawn could live. It was her turn, but I could not let her die. So, I have come in her place. Please spare her." 

The King was astounded at such profound love and self-sacrifice from an animal. He was deeply moved. "Both of you shall live. You deserve it." 

"If you can spare our lives," the Banyan Deer asked respectfully, "can't you spare the lives of all the deer, your majesty?" 

The King, his heart forever changed by the stag's nobility, smiled. "I can! I will!" 

"And couldn't you spare the lives of all the creatures in your forest, your majesty?" the Banyan Deer pressed gently. 

The King thought for a while, looking out over his vast kingdom. "All right! From this day, there shall be no hunting or killing of any kind in my kingdom!" 

And so, true to the King's word, all the animals in the forest lived in peace and harmony for a long time to come, safe under the gentle wisdom of the Banyan Deer.
