# The Royal Elephant

When King Brahmadatta reigned in Varanasi, there lived, not far from the city, a colony of carpenters. Every day they would sail down the river and enter a dense forest to fell trees for timber. Thus they moved from forest to village and back again. Work was plentiful. 

One day, as they sat down to their midday meal, they saw an elephant limping towards them. "He is limping and seems to be in great pain," one carpenter noticed. 

The elephant came up to the carpenters and held out a foot. "I stepped on something—I think it's a splinter," he seemed to indicate. 

"You are right. It has entered quite deep into the flesh," a carpenter said. They removed the splinter, bathed and dressed the wound, and nursed the foot back to health. 

"You have been very kind to me. I would like to come here every day and help you," the elephant seemed to say. 

The elephant served them well for a number of years. One day he brought a young one with him. "Look! Our friend has brought a young one with him today. What a beautiful white animal!" 

"I am no longer as active as I used to be. I have brought my son to take my place," the old elephant conveyed. He then walked away, leaving the elephant torn between his old life and his new friends, but the young white elephant stayed. 

The young elephant worked hard. He helped to load and unload timber. He fetched and carried tools. And transported logs. More than that, he brought joy and laughter into the carpenters' lives, playing with their children and swinging them on his trunk. 

One day, quite by accident, the rain washed some of his droppings into the river. Later, downstream, where the royal elephants bathed, the trainers noticed something. "Our elephants are refusing to get into the water." 

"The water contains the droppings of a noble animal. He must live upstream somewhere," the trainer guessed. "Let us report the matter to the King." 

When the trainer reported the matter to the King, the King himself set out with a band of trusted men. "That must be the animal we are looking for. How nobly he bears himself!" the King exclaimed when they found him. 

"Do you think he wants us to work in the palace?" the carpenters wondered. 

"If he did, he would send for us, not come here himself," the chief carpenter reasoned. 

"We would like this elephant for our royal stables. Pay them a hundred thousand pieces of gold for his tail, the same for his trunk and for each of his four feet," the King offered. 

"You must not think me greedy, your majesty. They need clothes. And the children too must be provided for," the elephant seemed to convey. 

"We admire your loyalty. We shall more than satisfy you," the King promised. 

Upon receiving this assurance, the elephant agreed to go with the King. It was a sad leave-taking. "Who will play with us?" the children cried. 

The city wore a festive air. The elephant, regally caparisoned, was led to his gaily decorated stable. The nobles and ministers looked on in admiration. "He is the most majestic animal I have ever seen." 

The King made over half his kingdom to the elephant. "You are a brother to me and a companion for life," the King told him. The elephant became deeply attached to the King and followed him everywhere. "My Lord, he refuses to touch his food till you have first fed him a morsel," the keepers noted. 

A few months later, the Queen was expecting a baby. "The elephant has brought us great good fortune. My power as a monarch is now unchallenged. I feel our child is destined for great things," the King rejoiced. 

But their joy was short-lived. The King fell seriously ill. As he lay dying, he thought, "My elephant will be broken-hearted. Our child will never know a father!" 

"We must keep the sad news from the elephant," the Queen and ministers agreed. "I wonder how long it will be before he finds out!" 

Meanwhile, the King of Kosala was marching towards Varanasi. "The King is dead, and confusion reigns. We should ask them to suspend fighting for a week," the ministers decided. The King of Kosala acceded to their request. 

According to the astrologers, the Queen would give birth to her child within a week. If it was a son, they would fight. If not, they would surrender. "Long live the new-born King!" they cheered when a son was born. "Fight for the King! Fight to the last man!" 

They fought bravely, but the lack of leadership told on them. "We have lost heavily. What do you expect? Our leader lies in a cradle," the generals despaired. 

"I have an idea. Let the royal elephant take charge," the Prime Minister suggested. 

They went to the Queen who sent for the elephant. While he gazed at her son, she told him, "His father is dead. If the King meant anything to you, he is now your charge." 

"The King dead! Why was I not told? How can I live without my master?" the elephant grieved. 

"As I do, for his child and for the kingdom that is his by right... and which is at this very moment in grave danger," the Queen pleaded. 

The elephant knelt to receive the Queen's blessing. "I shall talk no more of death. Your son shall rule," he vowed. 

"We now have something to live for," the Queen smiled. 

As the keeper of the royal stables got him ready for the battle... the King's men felt their hopes rise. "Get the troops out! Let no man dawdle!" 

And then the elephant charged forward, the skies resounding with his mighty trumpeting. "Put courage in your hearts and strength in your arms! Follow our leader. He is divinely inspired!" the generals shouted. 

The Kosala armies were routed, the battle won. The elephant stood before the Queen, his proud head bowed before his infant master. "My grief is great for your dead father. But I live for you and for the great destiny that awaits you, my prince," the elephant seemed to say. 

And the Bodhisattva (for the child was none other than he) grew up to inherit a kingdom which he ruled wisely and well.
