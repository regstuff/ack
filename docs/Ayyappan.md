# Ayyappan

Emboldened by a boon from Brahma, the demon Mahishasura began harassing all on earth. The people cried out, "The evil one is here! O Devas, save us!"

The Devas held council in heaven. "This asura must be destroyed," they decided. "Because of Brahma's boon, only a woman would be able to do it. Then let us evoke one with our combined powers."

As soon as the resplendent woman appeared before them, she laughed, "Ha! Ha! Ha!" 

"We will call her Chandika Devi," the Devas said. 

Laughing all the while, Chandika Devi descended on earth. The whole world shook with the force of her arrival. Mahishasura, seeing her, commanded his men, "Who is that senseless woman? Go bring her to me." 

But Chandika Devi vanquished his men in no time. They returned to Mahishasura. "Lord! We had to retreat."

"Take the whole army if necessary, but bring her here!" Mahishasura roared. However, his entire army was no match for Chandika Devi and was soon routed. 

Mad with anger, Mahishasura approached Chandika Devi fiercely. "That woman has gone too far. I shall destroy her," he declared and attacked her. But he, too, was no match for her divine power and was soon slain. Her mission accomplished, Chandika Devi ascended to heaven.

When Mahishi, the wife of Mahishasura, learnt of her husband's death, she vowed revenge. "I shall teach the Devas a lesson. It was they who invoked the Devi." She undertook a deep penance to propitiate Lord Brahma. 

When Brahma appeared before her, he asked, "Mahishi! What do you desire?"

"That I should not meet my death at the hands of either Vishnu or Shiva!" she asked. Brahma granted her the boon and vanished.

Feeling invincible, Mahishi began taunting the Devas. "To whom will you turn now? Shiva and Vishnu are powerless against me."

Shiva watched and heard all. He went to Vishnu, who had taken the form of Mohini, the beautiful enchantress. 

"We must create a son who will destroy Mahishi," they decided. 

"Who will rear the baby?" asked Vishnu.

"I must put an end to Mahishi's arrogance," Shiva said. "My devotees, Rajashekhara and his queen, long for a child. I will take him there!"

As soon as the radiant infant was created, Shiva said, "I will tie this gold bell around his neck before I take him to earth." 

Meanwhile, on earth, King Rajashekhara was out hunting with his retinue. After a tiring day, while the party relaxed on the banks of the river Pampa, Rajashekhara wandered off alone. "How peaceful and quiet it is here," he thought.

Suddenly he heard a feeble wail. A baby's cry! Rajashekhara walked in the direction from which the sound came and saw a helpless baby lying all alone on a rock. 

"Poor baby! Don't cry!" he soothed, looking around. "Not a soul in sight. To whom could it belong?"

While he debated with himself whether to take the child home or leave it, a Brahman suddenly appeared before him. "Do not hesitate, O King. Take him home."

"But who is he?" 

"You will know when he is twelve years old," the Brahman replied.

"Wait! What shall I call him?" 

"Call him Manikanthan, after the gold bell which hangs round his neck," the Brahman advised before disappearing.

Rajashekhara took Manikanthan home to his queen. "A child at last! How happy I am," the queen rejoiced. "He is God's own gift."

But Rajashekhara's wicked Diwan didn't think so. "The accursed child! It has upset all my plans," he muttered.

Manikanthan soon grew up into a strong young lad. He was placed under the guidance of an excellent Guru and soon mastered all there was to learn. "Manikanthan is not an ordinary child," the Guru observed.

By the time Manikanthan's studies were over, the Guru knew that he was a divine child. When Manikanthan offered him the traditional guru-dakshina, the Guru said, "From you I want compassion. Not these." The Guru brought his blind and mute son before Manikanthan. "Manikanthan, please restore his sight and speech."

Manikanthan blessed the boy, and instantly, the Guru's son saw light and spoke. "Father! I have been given a new life!"

After this first miracle, Manikanthan performed many more, and the news spread. "I have never seen such a gifted boy," people praised. "Our future is assured. He will succeed the King."

The Diwan, who overheard these comments, got really worried. "I must get rid of the boy somehow." 

Then, the unforeseen happened. The queen gave birth to a son. 

The Diwan was jubilant. "The throne shall yet be mine!" he plotted. A few days later, he went to the queen. "Manikanthan will soon be twelve years old. He will then be crowned heir apparent. With that, your own son will lose all chances of ever becoming the ruler."

"What can I do? I am helpless," the queen worried.

"Will you do as I tell you? For the sake of your own son?"

"I will. For my son I will do anything."

"Then pretend to have a severe headache. Leave the rest to me," the Diwan instructed. The Diwan then went to the court physician and bribed him. "Do as I tell you and you will be well rewarded."

Soon, a messenger came to the physician. "The queen is in agony. The king has asked you to examine her immediately."

After the physician had examined the queen, the anxious king asked, "What is the quickest remedy? I cannot bear to see my queen in pain."

"The only remedy for this ailment is tigress's milk," the physician lied.

The king was aghast. "Tigress's milk? But how are we to get it?" The king thought about it for a while and then sent for his Diwan. "Send as many men as possible into the forest in search of tigress's milk. The one who brings it shall have half my kingdom."

Many men went but all returned unsuccessful. "We saw a number of tigers, but..." they stammered.

Then, exactly as the wicked Diwan had foreseen, Manikanthan approached his father. "Father, let me go on this errand!"

"Manikanthan, no! You are too young. How can I let you go on such a dangerous venture?"

"For my mother's sake let me go, father."

Manikanthan persisted, and Rajashekhara finally had to yield. "All right, my son. Go if you must. But take care."

Manikanthan set out for the forest. As he wandered deeper and deeper into it, he came to the domain of Mahishi, who lived in that forest. 

"Who is this creature who dares to trespass on our forest?" an asura guard demanded, running to Mahishi. "There is a boy roaming in our forests."

"How dare he, without my permission! I'll destroy him," Mahishi declared.

Mahishi went up to Manikanthan and attacked him. "Come, Mahishi. My earthly mission is about to be fulfilled," Manikanthan said calmly. A fierce battle ensued, and Manikanthan soon killed Mahishi.

As Manikanthan continued his search for a tigress, Indra, king of the Devas, appeared before him. "I shall take the form of a tigress and come with you into the city as your mount," Indra offered. The Devas who had accompanied Indra also turned themselves into tigresses. "We will follow you into the city."

As Manikanthan rode into the city on his terrifying mount, followed by an equally terrifying retinue, the citizens fled in panic. "Tigers! Help! Run!"

One of the citizens ran to Rajashekhara. "Manikanthan has returned... with a horde of tigresses." 

The king rushed out to see this unusual sight and was stunned. "Manikanthan is twelve today. Today I will know who he is," he remembered.

Just then, he heard a divine voice from the heavens. "Manikanthan is none but God himself."

Rajashekhara ran forward and fell at Manikanthan's feet. "Please send the tigresses away, Manikanthan. My people are terror-stricken."

Manikanthan smiled, and the tigresses vanished. 

"As soon as you left, the queen recovered. I became suspicious," the king told him. "My suspicions were confirmed when I overheard a conversation between her and my Diwan. I shall punish them for this."

"Spare them," Manikanthan said gently. "They hastened my mission of slaying Mahishi. And now I must leave this world."

"Lord, a last request," the king wept. "Show me the place in my kingdom where I may build a temple for you."

"I am pleased. Build it where this arrow I shoot falls," Manikanthan said, drawing his bow. "You may observe the path of the arrow."

The arrow flew high and fell at the summit of the Shabari hills. Manikanthan vanished, and the king began his trek to the mountain top. 

"It is so high... impossible," the king thought. He decided, "Let there be just eighteen steps leading to the temple at the eastern gate."

At last, Rajashekhara reached the top of the mountain. "How will I ever achieve my task?" As he stood there, dazed, wondering where to begin, a voice guided him. "I shall send Vishwakarma to help you out."

Under the guidance of Vishwakarma, the divine architect, the magnificent temple was soon completed. "Through the Lord's grace it has risen - noble and imposing," the king said. "Will I find a befitting idol now?"

Lord Vishnu divined the king's anxiety and sent for Parashurama. "Go down to earth as a stone-carver and help Rajashekhara install the idol."

Parashurama left for Rajashekhara's palace in the garb of a sculptor. At the king's court, he announced, "I have heard of your problem. I may be able to help."

"Show me your wares before you take up the challenging task," the king requested.

Parashurama showed his flawless sculptures. 

"You are no ordinary sculptor. Tell me the truth. Who are you?" the king asked in awe.

"I am Parashurama. I have already fashioned the idol," he said, assuming his own form. "Today, the day of Makara Sankranti, is auspicious. I shall install the idol straightaway." 

And thus it was that the image of Ayyappa, as Manikanthan came to be popularly known, was sanctified on the hill.

Meanwhile, the wicked Diwan was afflicted by an incurable disease. "I have sinned. Will I ever be cleansed?" he suffered. 

One night he had a dream of Ayyappa. "Come to my temple on the Shabari hill. But enter only after cleansing yourself in the Pampa." 

The Diwan wasted no time. The next morning he set out for the river Pampa. "I have come to wash away my sins in remorse, holy Mother. Save me."

The Diwan came out of the water glowing with health. "I am cleansed. The holy mother has been merciful." Then, without pausing for breath, he ran up the eighteen steps to the temple. "Ayyappa! Ayyappa!"

When he reached the sanctum sanctorum, overcome by emotion, he fell at the feet of the idol. "Swamiye Sharanam Ayyappa!"

And to this day, as millions of pilgrims make their arduous way to the temple of Ayyappa on the Shabari hills, their faces shine with devotion as they call out, "Swamiye Sharanam Ayyappa!"
