# A Monk in Need is a Friend Indeed

One day, King Brahmadatta of Varanasi and his son were busy discussing matters of state. 

"We must send more soldiers of our army to the provinces," the king said. 

"Yes, I have discussed recruiting new soldiers with the commander," his son agreed. 

Just then, a servant announced an arrival. "Oh! That is the monk Tiritavachchha! I must go and receive him," the king exclaimed, rushing out. "But, father, you don't need to go. He'll be given alms like the other monks," the prince protested. 

But the king rushed out to welcome the monk personally. "Come, you must sit here. On the throne?" he offered. 

"How odd!" the prince thought, watching his father fuss over the monk. "Your majesty, the lunch has been served for royal guests. Shall I take your guest there?" a servant asked. 

"No. He will eat with me here in my private chamber," the king insisted. 

The special treatment given to Tiritavachchha did not go unnoticed. "What a fuss over an ordinary monk. Our king treats him as if he were a brave warrior who has made a great conquest," the courtiers whispered. 

After lunch, the Chief Minister was summoned. "Send for the architect. I want him to build a suitable dwelling for Tiritavachchha in the compound. It should be spacious and comfortable." 

Soon it reached the prince's ears. "Father, why do you give this man so much importance? He is not an expert in any art. He is neither your relative nor your friend. How come he deserves such royal treatment?" 

"Do you remember, son, two months ago, I went with the army to quell a rebellion in the districts?" the king began his story. 

"While leading our soldiers, my elephant ran in a frenzy and went into the forest. I was wounded and tired. Luckily we reached an ashrama. A well at last. Ah! I must have some water!" 

"But there was no pulley to draw the water nor anyone around to help. I'll tie the rope around the elephant's leg and climb down the well," the king had decided. 

"I climbed down the well and drank my fill of cool water. But then... it's too slippery. I can't go back," he realized, trapped at the bottom of the well. 

"In the evening, the monk Tiritavachchha returned to his ashrama. 'An elephant here! Surely there must be a rider too,' the monk looked around. 'Ah! There you are. Wait! I'll help you out.' The monk took me out of the well." 

"For two days the monk looked after me treating my wounds with herbs and bringing food for me from the forest. 'You have saved my life! You must come and visit me in Varanasi,' I told him." 

"Had he not rescued me and looked after me, I would not have survived," the king finished his tale. 

"Yes, father, now I understand that you owe your life to him," the prince nodded. 

"Don't you think he deserves to be my friend?" the king asked. 

"Indeed, father. We must do all we can for him," the prince smiled, finally understanding the true value of friendship.
