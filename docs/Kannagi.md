# Kannagi

Handsome Kovalan and quiet, charming Kannagi, of a merchant clan in the Chola kingdom, were married with pomp and ceremony. They lived happily in the city of Poompuhar. 

"I will make that house a heaven for you," Kovalan promised. 

Three years later, during the spring festival, Kovalan saw Madhavi, a beautiful dancer, perform. "What an excellent dancer! Perfect! Her technique is perfect!" 

He was captivated. "She is just another dancer. I was foolish to have lost my heart to her... to have squandered all my money on her," Kovalan later realized, but only after he had lost all his wealth. He had even given Madhavi an emerald necklace that was a state tribute. 

While Kovalan stayed with Madhavi, poor Kannagi was always in tears. "He does not deserve such a good wife. The cad has taken away all her jewels and money," her friends sympathised. 

"Send him away even if he does. You are too good for him," they advised. 

"No. Don't say that. My husband is my god," Kannagi replied faithfully. 

One day, utterly disillusioned after misunderstanding a song she sang, Kovalan walked away from Madhavi forever. He returned to Kannagi. 

"I will never neglect you again," he promised. "But I'll need some capital to start my business again." 

"My lord, I still have my anklets—the last of my jewels. We can sell one of them," Kannagi offered. 

"It will fetch a better price in the markets of Madurai. Let us go there," Kovalan decided. 

They set out on foot for Madurai, the capital of the Pandya kingdom. It was a long and arduous journey. When they arrived, Kovalan left Kannagi in the care of a kind couple and went to the market to sell the anklet. 

When he reached the street of the goldsmiths, he asked the Queen's goldsmith, "Sir, do you know anyone who would like to buy this anklet?" 

"It's beautiful," the goldsmith said. But he thought, "It's exactly like the Queen's anklet! The one my wife has been yearning for... I stole the Queen's anklet and gave it to my wife. If I can fool the King, I'll be safe." 

The goldsmith went to the King. "I suspect him of stealing the Queen's anklet. I am going to the King," he told Kovalan to wait. 

"I brought back the anklet because it was not safe in my house. A thief tried to steal it but I caught him and retrieved it," the goldsmith lied to the King. 

The King, distracted by the Queen's pouting over the lost anklet, blundered. "Good work. Let the thief be taken away and beheaded," he ordered his guards, without even a trial. 

The guards found Kovalan. Despite his noble appearance, they carried out the King's orders. "He was beheaded. The King's orders. He stole the Queen's anklet." 

When Kannagi heard the news, she fainted. "My husband, a thief? Is the King mad?" she cried when she revived. 

At the market place, Kannagi sat down near the body. "Look! The severed head has joined the body!" the people gasped. 

Kovalan spoke to her. "Kannagi, why do you look so sad? You'll join me soon." Then he died in her arms. 

"Your King is a murderer. This anklet will prove Kovalan's innocence," she declared, charging towards the palace. The crowds followed her. 

"Go and tell your King that a woman seeks justice. Her husband has been killed unjustly," she demanded at the gates. 

She stood before the King and Queen. "My innocent husband tried to sell an anklet. Someone called him a thief and you, O unjust King, beheaded him without even a trial!" 

"So you are the thief's wife. He stole this, my Queen's gold anklet which has pearls inside," the King said. 

"My gold anklet has diamonds inside. Here is the second of the pair!" Kannagi challenged. She took the anklet from the King and broke it. The diamonds lay scattered on the floor. 

"What have I done! I have killed an innocent man. I can atone only by my own death," the King realized his grave injustice. Overcome by his sense of guilt, the King fell dead. The Queen soon followed him. 

But Kannagi's anger was not appeased. She reached the palace gates and cursed the city: "If I am the chaste wife of an innocent man, let this cursed city burn to ashes in the flames of my wrath. Let the old, the disabled and the children be spared." 

She tore off her left breast and flung it at the city. Instantly, Madurai was engulfed in flames. 

Later, the Goddess of Madurai came down to her. "Listen to me, O Kannagi. The King was not at fault. It is your husband's sin in a previous birth that has brought about this tragedy. Kovalan was one of the Pandya King's soldiers. He brought about the death of an innocent man... whose wife cursed him: 'If I am the pure wife of an innocent man, you and your wife shall know the pain that is now ours.'" 

Kannagi understood. "Bless us, O Goddess. Release us from the throes of this curse." 

"You will go to heaven with your husband. He will come down and take you. Madurai will be rebuilt," the Goddess promised. 

As promised, Kovalan came down in a winged chariot. "Kannagi, we are now free of all earthly pains and pleasures," he said, taking her to heaven. 

Years later, temples were built to honour the divinity of Kannagi. Even to this day, people worship her as the epitome of chastity and devotion.
