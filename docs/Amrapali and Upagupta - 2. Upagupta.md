# Upagupta

Long, long ago in ancient Mathura, there lived an incredibly famous dancer named Vasavadatta. She was renowned far and wide for her stunning beauty and her breathtaking art. 

After every performance, the wealthy men of Mathura would praise her endlessly. "What a perfect figure! What a charming appearance! What graceful movements..."

"...And what a hard heart!" another would interrupt bitterly. "She's unique, indeed!"

"You seem bitter. Have you too been spurned by her?" 

It was true. Vasavadatta was fiercely independent and entirely unimpressed by wealth or status. "I am prepared to part with all my wealth for a single smile from her," one rich admirer sighed as she rode past in her chariot. 

But Vasavadatta didn't so much as glance at her admirers. "They are all madly in love with you. Couldn't you see it in their eyes?" her companion asked. 

"I chose not to see," Vasavadatta replied coldly. "No! Not one of them possesses what I am looking for."

One evening, as Vasavadatta was standing on the balcony of her grand mansion, she saw a young, serene Buddhist monk pass by in the street below. She was instantly captivated by his peaceful aura. 

"Quick! Run down and invite that monk in," she ordered her maid. 

The maid gasped. "I can't believe it. Vasavadatta showing some interest in a man!" 

The maid ran down to the street. "Vasavadatta wishes to see you, holy one. Will you come in?"

The monk stopped, his face perfectly calm. "Not now. I will see her at the proper time."

When the maid reported this, Vasavadatta was confused. "Perhaps he hesitates to visit me because he is poor and can't bring me expensive gifts. Tell him I don't want anything from him."

The maid went back out. "O monk, my friend does not crave for gifts or riches. Please visit her."

"No, I cannot. It is not yet time to visit Vasavadatta," the monk repeated firmly, and walked away. 

The monk who spurned the invitation of the greatest dancer in Mathura was Upagupta, a devoted disciple of Lord Buddha. 

When Vasavadatta heard his final rejection, she was absolutely stunned. "Why should the only man I choose to love, shun me?" she wept bitterly. 

Heartbroken, she completely stopped giving dance performances, much to the annoyance of the wealthy people of Mathura. "Life in Mathura has lost its charm. How could she be so cruel to us!" they complained. 

Meanwhile, her friend was deeply worried. *She sits alone, brooding over that heartless monk. It is not good for her health and looks. I must find something to distract her.*

"A famous sculptor is holding an exhibition of his works. Let us go and see it, Vasavadatta," her friend suggested. "All right, if you insist," she agreed listlessly. 

At the art show, looking at the beautiful statues, Vasavadatta finally forgot her sorrow for a while. She stopped before a particularly beautiful statue. "Such exquisite workmanship! Will you sell this to me?" she asked the handsome Chief Sculptor. 

"Whatever it may cost, I am prepared to buy it. Quote your price," the Sculptor smiled smoothly. "It's yours, if you agree to dance again."

Vasavadatta hesitated. "You can't go back on your word," her friend whispered. "You have agreed to pay whatever price he asks."

Vasavadatta reluctantly agreed. 

The next evening, people flocked to the town hall. At the end of the breathtaking performance, Vasavadatta received thunderous applause. But instead of making her happy, the adoration only made her brood all the more. *Why did that monk shun me when thousands of people long for a sight of me?* she thought sadly. 

In the days that followed, the Chief Sculptor began to visit the dancer at her mansion to capture her image in stone. "My art will die with me. But yours will last for centuries," she smiled as he worked. 

"My talent, which brings so much happiness to you, only makes my envious rivals hate me," the Sculptor sighed. 

A few days later, the Sculptor suddenly vanished without a trace. "Why doesn't he come? The work is yet to be completed. It's three days since he last came!" Vasavadatta worried. 

Meanwhile, his friends and relatives were frantically searching for the Chief Sculptor. "He was last seen entering the house of Vasavadatta, three days ago," one noted. "Have his rivals done away with him?"

The next day, a horrifying discovery was made. The dead body of the missing Sculptor was found buried in the dirt not far from Vasavadatta's house. 

The royal guards immediately arrested her, and she was brought before the King, charged with the brutal murder of the Sculptor. 

"Do you have anything to say in your defence?" the King asked. 

Utterly shocked and grief-stricken, Vasavadatta had nothing to say. She stood in completely stunned silence. 

"Vasavadatta is guilty," the King decreed firmly. "Confiscate all her property and have her permanently banished from Mathura."

As Vasavadatta was violently turned out of her own house, an angry mob jeered at her and pelted her with heavy stones. "Take this, you murderess!" they screamed. 

Bleeding profusely from the stones, Vasavadatta barely managed to reach the outskirts of Mathura, finally finding refuge in a dark, lonely crematorium. 

Her loyal friend found her there, weeping. "Vasavadatta, I know you are innocent! They have deliberately done this to you."

"Friend, let us not blame anyone," Vasavadatta sighed weakly. 

As the days passed, people coming in and out of Mathura looked upon Vasavadatta, who was now seriously ill with painful, festering wounds from the stoning, as an absolute untouchable. 

"She doesn't deserve any sympathy," they sneered, spitting at her as they walked past. "Leave her to die. Why do you waste your time on her?" They would sometimes even stone her again as they passed by. 

Then, one evening, came Upagupta, the serene Buddhist monk. 

"Friend, cover my body," Vasavadatta whispered weakly, hearing footsteps. "Let him not see me in this terrible state."

Upagupta knelt beside her. "Vasavadatta, I have come to you."

Vasavadatta looked up at him, tears in her eyes. "O monk, when all Mathura admired me, you alone spurned me. Why have you chosen to visit me now when I am a mass of festering flesh, shunned by all?"

Upagupta smiled with infinite compassion. "At that time you did not need me, Vasavadatta. You do, now. Come, let us go to my monastery."

Upagupta gently took her with him and nursed her back to health. Though she survived, she was heavily scarred. 

"I am no longer beautiful," Vasavadatta sighed sadly, looking in a mirror. "No one will look at me now."

Upagupta shook his head. "Vasavadatta, you are yet to discover a beauty far greater than the one you have lost—the true beauty of the self."

Curious to know more about this inner beauty, she began to attend the peaceful discourses of Lord Buddha. 

"You can't call even this physical body your own," Lord Buddha taught the monks. "When the body is cast away it merely becomes food for others. Light the lamp within you, and only then will you truly find eternal peace."

*Light the lamp within... peace...* Vasavadatta thought, feeling a profound sense of clarity wash over her. 

Vasavadatta walked forward and fell humbly at the feet of Lord Buddha. "Lord, admit me into your order."

"So be it, my child," Lord Buddha smiled warmly. "May you finally find peace of mind."
