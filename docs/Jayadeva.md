# Jayadeva

Jayadeva was born in Kendubilva, a small village on the eastern coast of India, nearly eight hundred years ago. His father was a poor Brahman named Bhojadeva, and his mother was a pious woman named Bhama Devi. 

Humming the name of Lord Hari, Bhama Devi would churn curds. "Hari bol... Hari bol..." 

"What's that you said?" she asked her young son. 

"Hari bol..." 

"Very good, my son. Always sing of Hari," she smiled. 

When Jayadeva was six years old, he began to study at the village school. As the years passed by, Bhojadeva found it increasingly difficult to make ends meet and had to borrow money from a neighbor, Niranjan Babu. 

"Don't worry. Jayadeva will soon be able to help you," his wife comforted him. But Jayadeva was lost in the world of the Bhagawat Purana, especially the exploits of Krishna. 

"Father, permit me to go and spend some time in the forest. I want to be away from books and from people. I want to be alone to meditate," Jayadeva asked one day. With his parents' blessings, he went to the forest, where he sang songs in praise of Radha and Krishna in a state of ecstasy. 

A few weeks later, Jayadeva returned to his village. Niranjan Babu met him. "Jayadeva, where have you been? In your absence both your parents have died. Your father had taken a loan from me... which he failed to repay. So I have occupied his house instead." 

"Bhaja Radhe Govindam!" Jayadeva chanted peacefully. 

Suddenly, smoke billowed from the house. "Smoke! A house in the village is on fire!" Niranjan Babu cried. It was Jayadeva's house. 

Shocked, Niranjan Babu stood rooted to the ground, but Jayadeva ran towards the burning house. "Jayadeva! Come back!" 

"Look! The fire has gone out! Nothing has been burnt! How strange!" Niranjan Babu marvelled. "I see the hand of God in this. Forgive me, Jayadeva, and accept me as your disciple!" 

Jayadeva did not stay for long. He left the village and set out for Puri to worship Lord Jagannatha. Walking for many miles in the scorching summer heat, overcome by thirst, he fell down unconscious. 

A cowherd, carrying water and curds, gently placed Jayadeva's head on his lap. "Here, have some curds. You will feel better." 

"You are a good, kind child. What are you doing here in the forest?" Jayadeva asked as he revived. 

"I am looking after my cattle. I am a cowherd," the boy replied. Then he pointed to the temple of Puri. "There's the temple! Lord Jagannatha!" 

Such was his eagerness that Jayadeva broke into a run. Later, he realized, "So it was you, Lord Krishna! It was you who revived me with cool curds. And I didn't recognize you!" 

Later, when he returned to the temple, a Brahman named Sudeva Sharma spoke to him. "I want you to marry my daughter, Padmavati." 

"But that is not possible! My life is dedicated to the Lord!" Jayadeva protested. 

"Lord Jagannatha appeared to me in a dream and asked me to arrange this marriage," the Brahman explained. 

"I will leave my daughter with you. You must decide whether or not you will carry out the Lord's will." 

Accepting it as divine will, Jayadeva married Padmavati. They returned to Kendubilva, where he built a hut, installed an image of Krishna, and began to write his famous Sanskrit poem, the Gita-Govinda, about Radha's immortal love for Krishna. 

One day, he wrote: "O Radha, place your tender feet upon my head as a diadem divine..." He hesitated, thinking it disrespectful to portray God humbled by a woman. "No! Would God let himself be humbled in this way by a woman?" He crossed out the lines and went to the river for a bath. 

While he was away, Lord Krishna took Jayadeva's form and came to the hut. He asked Padmavati for food, ate his meal, and then completed the verse in the manuscript: "Place those tender petals, your delicate feet, upon my head as a diadem divine." Then he vanished. 

When the real Jayadeva returned and saw the completed verse, he was bewildered. Padmavati explained that he had just eaten and written it. "Now I see the light, Padma. The Lord loves Radha so much that he is prepared to become her slave," Jayadeva smiled, realizing the Lord had visited them. 

When Jayadeva completed the Gita-Govinda, it won immediate recognition. However, the King of Utkal had also composed songs about Lord Krishna, which he called "Gita-Govinda". The King wanted his version to be more popular, but the people preferred Jayadeva's. 

To settle the matter, the King placed both versions before the image at the Jagannatha temple. The door was shut. After some time, it was opened. Jayadeva's work was on top of the King's! 

"I accept your verdict, Lord Jagannatha!" the King bowed. The Lord then instructed the King in a dream to include verses from his work in each chapter of Jayadeva's. 

After some time, Jayadeva left for a pilgrimage. The King of Jeypore, a generous man, hosted him and gave him a large offering of gold coins. As Jayadeva passed through a forest, three men joined him. 

The three men were dacoits. When night fell, they cut off Jayadeva's hands, threw him into a dry well, and ran away with his gold coins. 

Lakshmana Sena, the King of Gaud, happened to be passing by and heard singing from the well. His men rescued Jayadeva. Impressed by his saintliness despite his suffering, the King appointed Jayadeva as his spiritual guide. 

Following Jayadeva's advice, the King built a rest-house for travellers. One day, the three dacoits came there, disguised as sadhus. Jayadeva recognized them but welcomed them warmly. 

"Accept these gold coins. My guards will see you safely to our borders," Jayadeva offered. 

On the way, the guards asked the 'sadhus' why the saint was so kind to them. The dacoits lied, "He was to be hanged. But we came to his aid. So only his hands were cut off!" 

Suddenly, there was a loud rumbling noise and the dacoits fell into a pit that opened beneath their feet, swallowed by the earth for their terrible lie. 

The guards reported this to the King. Jayadeva then disclosed the truth about the dacoits. "Though they treated you so badly, you were willing to forgive them?" the King marvelled. "You are indeed a saint!" 

The next moment, as Jayadeva prayed, his hands were miraculously restored. 

Some time later, Padmavati joined her husband at the palace. The Queen befriended her, but one day, to test Padmavati's devotion, the Queen falsely told her that Jayadeva was dead. 

"My lord!" Padmavati gasped, and fell dead from the shock, for her love was so powerful she died the moment she believed her husband had passed away. 

When Jayadeva arrived and saw the tragedy, he began to recite from his Gita-Govinda. A few moments later, Padmavati sat up, alive again, and joined her husband in singing the praises of Krishna. 

Jayadeva and Padmavati lived long. When they were quite old, they went to Brindavan, where they spent the last days of their lives with other devotees, singing of the glory of Lord Krishna.
