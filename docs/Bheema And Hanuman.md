# Bheema and Hanuman: The Meeting of the Sons of the Wind

During the twelve years of exile in the forest, the five Pandava brothers and their wife Draupadi lived in the deep wilderness accompanied by their preceptor, Sage Dhoumya. When Arjuna, the third Pandava, departed for Mount Indrakeela to perform severe penance and obtain celestial weapons from the Devas, five years passed without his return. The Pandavas missed him deeply, and the forest felt empty without his presence.

One morning, the revered Sage Lomasha arrived at their encampment bearing welcome news. "Arjuna's mission has been successful!" Lomasha announced. "The Devas require his presence a little longer, but he has asked me to take you on a pilgrimage to holy shrines, across Mount Gandhamadana, to Kubera's celestial gardens. He will join you there."

Joyfully, the Pandavas and Draupadi set out with the sages. As they climbed the steep, narrow trails of Mount Gandhamadana, a violent dust storm broke out, followed by torrential rain. Bheema, the strongest of the Pandavas, protected Draupadi, holding her close beneath a shelter of trees until the tempest abated.

However, the strenuous ascent took a heavy toll on Draupadi. As the sun came out, her knees buckled and she fainted from exhaustion on the rocky path. Nakula ran forward to support her, while Yudhisthira wept in distress. "She was born a princess, yet hardship and sorrow have been her lot," Yudhisthira mourned. "Bheema, how will she cross the snow-bound peaks ahead?"

Bheema's face lit up with an idea. "Brother, do not despair! I shall summon my son, Ghatotkacha."

Closing his eyes, Bheema focused his thoughts on his demon son, born of his union with the demoness Hidimba. In a distant forest, Ghatotkacha instantly felt his father's call. Within moments, Ghatotkacha and his Rakshasa companions materialized before the Pandavas, bowing reverently. 

"Father, command me!" Ghatotkacha offered.

"Carry Draupadi safely to the mountain top," Bheema instructed. "Fly low so she feels no unease."

Ghatotkacha and his strong followers lifted Draupadi and the Pandavas into the air, flying swiftly above the clouds until they alighted at the sacred hermitage of Badrikashrama on the mountain summit, where the ancient sages Nara and Narayana had meditated. There, the inhabitants received the Pandavas with high honor.

Some days later, as Bheema and Draupadi strolled through the charming woods near the hermitage, a gentle mountain breeze wafted an exquisite flower to Draupadi's feet. It was a golden *Saugandhika* lotus, possessing an intoxicating, celestial fragrance. 

Picking up the lotus in delight, Draupadi showed it to Bheema. "Look at this exquisite flower! What a sweet fragrance it has! I will give this one to Yudhisthira, but oh Bheema, please get me some more to take back with us!"

"I would go to the ends of the earth to fulfill your wish, Draupadi," Bheema replied warmly.

Grasping his heavy golden mace and bow, Bheema marched deeper into the uncharted forests of Gandhamadana, following the direction of the scented breeze. As he strode through the jungle, a rogue wild elephant charged at him with a deafening roar. Undaunted, Bheema seized the beast by its tusks, wrestled it to the ground, and slew it with a single blow. Lions and tigers fled in terror as the prince forged ahead, clearing a path through the wilderness.

Reaching the foot of a high peak, Bheema discovered a crystal-clear lake bordering a lush banana grove. After taking a refreshing dip, Bheema raised his conch to his lips and blew a blast so powerful that the mountainside reverberated.

Deep within the banana grove sat **Hanuman**, the immortal monkey god of the *Ramayana*. Like Bheema, Hanuman was born by the grace of Vayu, the wind-god, making the two mighty heroes spiritual brothers.

Hearing the conch blast, Hanuman smiled in recognition. *Only one being on earth can sound a conch so loudly—my brother Bheema!* Hanuman thought. Sensing Bheema's approach, Hanuman noticed the prince's proud demeanor. *Bheema has always been proud of his strength. For his own good, I must teach him humility.*

Hanuman stretched himself across the narrow mountain path, lashing his long tail against the ground and letting out a roar that shook the mountain peaks. 

Hearing the deafening sound, Bheema hastened into the grove, only to find an old, frail monkey lying lazily across the trail. "Impudent monkey!" Bheema bellowed. "Get out of my way or I shall thrash you!"

Hanuman looked up with sleepy eyes. "I am old and ill, and you woke me from my sleep. Being a human, you are wiser than I and ought to be more considerate. Who are you?"

"I am Bheema, son of Pandu, born to Kunti by the grace of Vayu!" Bheema declared chest out. "Now move aside!"

"I have no energy to move," Hanuman replied calmly. "If you must pass, simply leap over me."

"Leaping over a living creature is against the code of Dharma," Bheema countered. "If it were not a sin, I would leap right over you and this mountain, just as my brother Hanuman leaped over the ocean to find Rama's wife!"

"Who is this Hanuman?" the old monkey asked innocently.

"He is my handsome, strong, and intelligent brother!" Bheema boasted. "I am as strong as he! Now get up, or I will send you to the abode of Yama!"

Hanuman concealed his smile. "O mighty Bheema, have pity on an old weakling. Just push my tail aside with your hand and go across."

Smirking at the weakling monkey, Bheema reached down with his left hand to flick the tail out of the path. But to his utter shock, the tail did not move an inch. 

Frowning, Bheema gripped the tail with both hands and pulled with all his might. Straining every muscle, his face turning red and sweat pouring down his forehead, Bheema heaved again and again. Yet the monkey's tail remained rooted to the earth as firmly as a mountain!

Gasping for breath, Bheema released his grip and fell to his knees, his arrogance shattered. "What is the matter with me?" he whispered in disbelief. "I, Bheema, am unable to move the tail of a mere monkey!"

Realizing he was in the presence of a divine being, Bheema folded his hands reverently. "O mighty one, forgive my harsh words! Pray tell me who you really are."

The old monkey rose, his eyes shining with affection. "I am your brother, Hanuman!"

Overjoyed, Bheema fell prostrate before him. "My brother! I am blessed beyond measure to behold you! Please, show me the colossal form in which you crossed the ocean!"

Hanuman smiled and began to expand his body. Right before Bheema's eyes, Hanuman grew taller and grander until his form towered higher than Mount Gandhamadana itself, radiating a blinding, golden light that dazzled the mountain. 

"Enough, brother!" Bheema gasped, shielding his eyes. "The light emanating from your body dazzles me! I feel faint!"

Resuming his normal size, Hanuman embraced Bheema warmly. As their arms clasped, a surge of divine energy flooded Bheema's body, dispelling all fatigue. 

"My strength has returned manifold!" Bheema exclaimed. "With your blessings, we are sure to conquer our enemies!"

"I shall do even more," Hanuman promised. "When you charge into battle against the Kauravas, I shall reside upon the flagstaff of Arjuna's chariot, and my battle roar will strike terror into the hearts of your foes!"

Hanuman then pointed to a hidden mountain path. "Follow that trail to the Saugandhika forest and Kubera's gardens, where the lotuses grow." With tears of affection in his eyes, the immortal monkey blessed his brother and vanished into the grove.

Invigorated, Bheema followed the path until he reached the pristine lake in the Saugandhika forest, covered in radiant pink and gold lotuses. As he bent to drink from the waters, a legion of heavy-armored Rakshasa guards, sent by Kubera, blocked his way. "Mortal, this is Lord Kubera's private garden! You cannot take these flowers without his permission!"

"This lake on the mountain belongs to all!" Bheema asserted, plunging into the water. When the guards attacked him with spears and maces, Bheema wielded his mace in a fury, slaying over a hundred Rakshasas and scattering the rest in terror.

The fleeing guards rushed to Kubera's palace. "Lord! Yudhisthira's brother has entered the lake by force!"

To their surprise, Kubera smiled. "I know why Bheema is there. Return to your posts and let him gather as many flowers as he desires."

Meanwhile, Bheema gathered armfuls of the golden Saugandhika lotuses. Filled with joy, he began to frolic in the lake, splashing the waters with such immense power that massive waves and stone-like spray whirled into the sky, shaking the mountain.

At the hermitage, Yudhisthira heard the roaring mountain tempest and grew alarmed. "Brothers, arm yourselves! A great catastrophe may be upon us!"

Only Draupadi smiled serenely. "O King, do not fear. Bheema must have found the celestial lotuses I requested."

Ghatotkacha carried Yudhisthira, Sage Dhoumya, and the Pandava brothers to the lake, where they found Bheema happily wading ashore with a mountain of glowing lotuses. Yudhisthira embraced his brother with relief and gentle reproach. "O Bheema, what have you done? Never commit such a rash act again!"

Bheema smiled warmly and placed the fragrant Saugandhika lotuses into Draupadi's hands. Moments later, Kubera's guards arrived and bowed before Yudhisthira, granting Kubera's permission for the Pandavas to dwell near the holy lake. There the Pandavas rested in peace and prosperity until Arjuna returned in triumph from the heavens.
