# Kochunni

Kochunni lived with his parents at Kayamkulam, a little-known town in central Kerala. His parents were poor, and his father was a petty thief. Young Kochunni went hungry most of the time when his father couldn't steal anything. 

"Why should I stay here and starve? I'll run away and try to earn an honest living," Kochunni decided one day. 

Tired and hungry, Kochunni came to a town called Evoor. An old Brahman named Annavi saw him. "That old Brahman looks a kind sort. I'll ask him for some food." 

Annavi was indeed kind-hearted. He fed Kochunni a bowl of steaming rice gruel. "I'll try to persuade a friend of mine to hire you," Annavi promised, and got Kochunni a job in a shop. 

Kochunni worked hard in the shop during the day and at night taught himself to read and write. Within a few years, he was keeping accounts and managing the shop single-handed. 

One day, an unusual teacher came to Kayamkulam—an expert in the art of warfare, hypnotism, and mesmerism. Kochunni's master hired the teacher. Kochunni also wanted to learn. 

"No, I cannot. How do I know you will not misuse what you learn? After all, your father was a thief," the teacher initially refused. But when he found out Kochunni was secretly watching and learning anyway, he agreed to accept him as a pupil. 

One day, a customer came to the shop for molasses. Kochunni remembered he had sent the last of it to his master's house. Wanting to please the customer, he ran to his master's house, found it locked, squeezed through a gap in the wall, and brought the molasses. 

The following day, his master confronted him. "Sir, Kochunni is not to be trusted. We saw him break into your house..." people had reported. 

"Still, we feel that you should dismiss him. He might rob you. After all, his father was a thief," they insisted. 

The master dismissed Kochunni. Hounded by society for the sins of his father, Kochunni had but one choice. "Well, if that's the way they want it, I shall be a thief—but an honest one. I'll steal from the rich and give to the poor." 

He soon gathered a small band of loyal followers, making Mammad and Kochupillai his lieutenants. Soon the name of Kochunni came to be dreaded by the rich and loved by the poor. 

He loved making a fool of pompous men. Once, he robbed a rich money-lender named Nair who boasted that no one could break into his house. Kochunni not only broke in but also took all of Nair's wealth, leaving him bewildered. 

In contrast, he rewarded those who were kind. Once, disguised as a poor man, he was fed by a kind Brahman. Later, he rewarded the Brahman handsomely with stolen wealth. He also retrieved a precious ruby ring from an arrogant aristocrat and returned it to the poor old man it belonged to. 

However, tragedy struck Kochunni's personal life. His mother-in-law was an ill-tempered woman who constantly nagged him and his wife. One day, during a heated argument, she insulted him and his friend Krishna Pillai. In a fit of rage, Kochunni struck her with a stick. To his horror, she fell dead. 

"I have killed her! I'll surrender myself to the police," he told his grieving wife. He allowed himself to be arrested, but on the way, he managed to overpower the police and escape. 

The Tahsildar, determined to catch him, cleverly played on the greed of Kochunni's lieutenants, Mammad and Kochupillai. "I will make you rich beyond your wildest dreams... if you catch him," he bribed them. 

The treacherous lieutenants threw a party and got Kochunni drunk on a special brew. When he was fast asleep, they tied him to a cot and called the police. 

Kochunni woke up and, though outnumbered and tied, put up a gallant fight. "Traitors! All of you! Take this—and this!" he yelled. But he was finally overpowered and carried out. 

Kochunni was kept in a solitary cell, under a heavy guard. "Alas! Too late I realise the truth. Justice is one thing, law another. My aims were noble and just but law does not take these things into consideration. My methods were unlawful and for that I pay; for that I repent," he reflected bitterly. 

Three months later, even before his trial was going on, Kochunni died. His spirit roamed free once more. It was almost as though he had willed himself to die. The memory of this gallant outlaw lingers on amidst the graceful palms and sparkling lagoons he loved so well.
