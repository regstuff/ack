# The Acrobat and Other Buddhist Tales

This collection of parabolic Buddhist and Jataka tales illustrates how wisdom, detachment, and selflessness overcome human weakness, vanity, and sorrow.

### The Acrobat

In the ancient city of Rajagriha, crowds gathered in the square to watch a troupe of visiting acrobats. High above the ground, balanced on a bamboo pole, a beautiful young maiden performed astonishing aerial feats, flipping and twirling like a bird.

Standing in the crowd was Ugrasena, the handsome son of Rajagriha's wealthy royal treasurer. Smitten by her grace, Ugrasena went home and declared to his parents, "I want to marry the acrobat girl! If I cannot marry her, I shall starve myself to death!"

Fearing for their son's life, his parents offered a box of gold to the acrobat chief. But the proud chief refused. "I am not selling my daughter! If your son wishes to marry her, he must travel with our troupe."

To his parents' dismay, Ugrasena abandoned his luxurious life and joined the acrobats. He married the maiden, but since he possessed no acrobatic skills, he made himself useful by driving bullock carts, carrying firewood, and pitching tents.

Soon a son was born to them. As the troupe traveled from city to city, Ugrasena's wife would rock the baby in her arms, singing teasing songs: *"Sleep, my son, son of a wood-fetcher... son of a cart-driver..."*

Hearing her mock him, Ugrasena was deeply hurt. "Woman, are you referring to me?"

"Yes," she replied nonchalantly. "It is my acrobatic skill that makes me famous; you are just a helper."

Driven to win her respect, Ugrasena went to his father-in-law. "Teach me acrobatic feats!" 

Under strict training, Ugrasena made astonishing progress. In a few months, he could turn seven somersaults in the air—outperforming even his wife!

When the troupe returned to Rajagriha, posters announced that Ugrasena would attempt a legendary feat: turning fourteen somersaults in mid-air from a thirty-foot pole! Thousands gathered in the city square, including the King and his ministers.

Climbing to the top of the high pole, Ugrasena launched himself into the air, completing the first somersault. But as he turned in mid-air, his eyes caught sight of Lord Buddha sitting serenely under a tree at the edge of the square, surrounded by peaceful monks.

Looking into Buddha's tranquil face, a profound realization struck Ugrasena: *What am I doing? I am risking my life merely to gain human applause and impress a vain woman!*

Sliding down the pole, Ugrasena walked past the cheering crowd straight to Lord Buddha, fell at his feet, and requested, "Venerable Master, admit me into your Sangha." Buddha blessed him, and Ugrasena found the true peace he had sought all his life.

### The Harvest

Once, a hard-working farmer was clearing and ploughing his field. Lord Buddha happened to walk past the field every morning, inquiring kindly about the farmer's progress. 

Touched by the Master's interest, the farmer bowed. "Sir, you take such great interest in my work! When my crop is harvested, I consider you my partner and will share half the grain with you."

Buddha smiled gently and continued on his way. 

Weeks passed, and the farmer's field flourished with golden wheat. "Tomorrow I shall call the reapers," the farmer thought happily as night fell.

That night, black clouds gathered, thunder split the sky, and an apocalyptic hail storm lashed the valley. By morning, the entire wheat crop lay flattened and destroyed in mud.

Devastated, the farmer returned home, threw himself onto his bed, and refused to eat or speak. 

Hearing of his grief, Lord Buddha walked into his room. "Tell me why you grieve, O farmer."

"Master, last night's storm destroyed my crop," the farmer wept. "I am ruined, and I cannot keep my word to give you your share."

Buddha sat beside him and spoke softly: "You grieve not because the crop died, but because of your attachment—attachment to your crop, to your word, and to me. From attachment springs sorrow; from attachment springs fear. He who is free from attachment is free from the burden of grief."

Understanding the Master's wisdom, the farmer rose from his bed, his heart relieved of sorrow.

### The Golden Maiden

In Shravasti lived Kumara, the son of a wealthy merchant. When his parents pressed him to marry, Kumara carved a statue of a maiden out of solid gold, possessing matchless beauty. 

"Father, Mother," Kumara declared, "if you find a maiden as beautiful as this golden image, I shall marry her; otherwise, I shall remain single."

After months of searching across many kingdoms, royal messengers located a maiden in Sagala whose beauty far surpassed the golden statue. Delighted, Kumara's parents sent rich gifts to her family to bring her to Shravasti.

Impatietly awaiting her arrival, Kumara stood on his balcony every evening. On the tenth night, he saw his father entering the courtyard with a solemn face.

"Mother, Father, why has she not arrived?" Kumara asked anxiously.

"Son," his mother wept, "she fell ill on the journey and passed away."

Kumara collapsed onto the floor in unbearable agony, weeping for his lost bride.

At that moment, Lord Buddha stepped through the doorway. "Arise, youthful one," Buddha said gently. "You do not grieve because a woman died; you grieve because the object of your intense desire was taken away. From desire springs sorrow; from desire springs fear. He that is free from desire neither sorrows nor fears."

As the Master's calm voice enveloped his heart, Kumara prostrated before Buddha and was admitted into the Sangha.

### The Perfectionist

In a bustling city lived a master carpenter who took immense pride in his craftsmanship. 

One day, a young expectant father visited his workshop. "Sir, my wife is blessed with a child. I need a cradle."

"You have come to the right man!" the carpenter boasted. "I shall build you the most perfect cradle you have ever seen. Come back in a week."

A week later, the man returned. "Is the cradle ready?"

"A work of perfection cannot be rushed!" the carpenter replied pompously. "Give me another week."

Week after week, month after month, the father returned, but the carpenter kept polishing, carving, and refining, insisting it was not yet perfect. Soon, the child grew too old for a cradle, and the father gave up visiting the shop.

Years rolled by. The child grew up, married, and became a proud father himself! 

Remembering his father's story, the young man walked into the carpenter's workshop. "Sir, the cradle my father ordered for me when I was born should be ready by now!"

The old carpenter looked up from his bench with a frown. "It isn't ready yet! And look here, young man—I take pride in my work! I will not permit you or your father to rush a true perfectionist!"

The young man stared at the old carpenter in disbelief, shook his head, and quietly walked away, realizing that perfectionism without utility is mere vanity.
