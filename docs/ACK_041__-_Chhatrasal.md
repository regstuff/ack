# Chhatrasal: The Founder of Bundelkhand

In a dense, darkening jungle, a young boy sat alone under a tree, sad and forlorn. 

An old man passing by stopped in surprise. "Who are you, son? What are you doing here in this jungle?"

The boy looked up with tear-filled eyes. "I am Chhatrasal, the younger son of Champatrai, the Bundela warrior. My parents are dead."

"I am Mahabali, the oil man," the kind stranger said. "I was once their servant. I am sorry to hear that. What happened? How did they die?"

"There was a surprise attack by the Mughal troops. They were completely surrounded," Chhatrasal explained, his voice hardening. "They preferred death to the humiliation of being captured, and stabbed each other. I escaped. I will avenge the fate of my parents."

Mahabali took the boy in. "Come home with me. Spend the day at my place. Rest for a while before you proceed to your uncle's village."

After a brief rest, Chhatrasal rode out. On the way, he spotted two Mughal soldiers mercilessly harassing innocent devotees outside a temple. Blind with rage, young Chhatrasal charged his horse forward, his sword flashing in the sun, and killed both soldiers instantly. 

The crowd cheered. "Who are you?" they asked.

"I am Chhatrasal, a son of the Bundela warrior Champatrai!"

"Long live Chhatrasal! Glory be to Chhatrasal!" the people roared.

But in the nearby village, his uncle Sujanrai was pacing worriedly. "No word from Chhatrasal. His whereabouts... could the Mughals have captured him?"

Suddenly, a guard announced, "Sir, look! Someone is coming."

Chhatrasal rode in, safe and sound. "You—Chhatrasal! Thank God you are safe," his uncle gasped in relief. "Why did you take so long?"

"I had to pass through Mughal territory," Chhatrasal replied grimly. "My parents must be avenged. I must found a Bundela kingdom."

"A worthy ambition," Sujanrai nodded. "But it is not easy to fight the Mughal armies. You must first learn the art of warfare."

Sujanrai arranged for Chhatrasal to be trained by Amar Singh, the commander of their forces. For four years, Chhatrasal trained rigorously in swordsmanship, archery, and strategy. 

"We are now ready, equipped to fight the Mughals," Chhatrasal told his commander. "But my uncle does not seem to be very enthusiastic." Hearing that his elder brother Angadrai was gathering forces elsewhere, Chhatrasal left without telling his uncle. 

"I am bent on avenging our parents' death and establishing a Bundela kingdom," Chhatrasal told his brother. "I do not want to delay anymore."

Angadrai was hesitant. *It would take time to prepare our forces, but Chhatrasal is getting impatient,* he thought. *Ah! That's it. I'll get him married. It will divert him.*

Soon enough, Chhatrasal was married to Devkunwar, the daughter of a feudal lord. But the fire in Chhatrasal's heart had not died down. 

"We have already wasted quite some time. We must now get busy," Chhatrasal insisted to his brother. "Before going to war, we have to collect enough funds and forces, and also study the war strategy of the Mughals."

"How is it possible for us to study their strategy?" Angadrai asked.

"Let us go to Raja Jaisingh. He is the chief of the Mughal forces," Chhatrasal suggested. "But remember, no one should guess our real intentions."

They travelled to the Mughal camp and presented themselves. "What? You, the sons of Champatrai, here to enlist in the Mughal army? Couldn't you get any other job?" the officers mocked.

"We are warriors. You are a warrior, too. We came in the hope that you would appropriately honour us," Angadrai replied smoothly.

"Champatrai was no doubt a brave warrior, but he was not a friend of the Mughals," the officer warned. 

"We are here to make amends. Give us a chance," Chhatrasal said. 

They were accepted into the army under the command of Bahadur Khan. Soon, Kurmamal, the ruler of Deogarh, rose in rebellion against the empire. The Mughal army, with the Bundela brothers in its ranks, marched to suppress it. 

"The fort is strong indeed. A deep moat on three sides... and just one gate, closed and well-guarded," Bahadur Khan observed. "But there must be some entrance at the back."

That night, Chhatrasal scouted the fort walls. Spotting a faint light bleeding through a crack across the moat, he realized it was a secret entrance. The next morning, he led a swift, stealthy strike through the moat, breaching the secret entrance and throwing the gates open for the main army. 

The Mughal army easily conquered Deogarh. Bahadur Khan was thrilled. "The emperor has sent me his congratulations. He has called us to Delhi. You too will come."

"Why should I? Deogarh was won by you," Chhatrasal deferred.

"No Chhatrasal, it was won by you. I will not take any undue credit," the honest commander insisted. "I will convince the emperor that such brave men should be honoured and befriended—not antagonised."

In the grand court of Delhi, Emperor Aurangzeb sat on his throne. "Bahadur Khan, I congratulate you on your victory over Deogarh."

"If it may please your majesty, congratulate Champatrai's son, Chhatrasal," Bahadur Khan corrected him respectfully. "It was he who won Deogarh."

Aurangzeb's eyes narrowed maliciously. "O! The son of Champatrai should be happy that I have not ordered him beheaded. Arrest him!"

Before the guards could move, Chhatrasal drew his sword and slashed his way out of the court. "The one to arrest Chhatrasal is yet to be born!" he yelled, escaping on his horse.

Realizing the Mughals could never be trusted, Chhatrasal headed south to meet the legendary Maratha leader, Chhatrapati Shivaji.

"I want to establish an independent kingdom. How shall I go about it?" Chhatrasal asked the great king.

"The Mughal army is strong in numbers but it always fails to withstand surprise attacks," Shivaji advised wisely. He pointed to a portrait of his own guru, Swami Ramdas. "He is my patron saint. I draw my strength and inspiration from him." 

Shivaji presented Chhatrasal with a magnificent sword. "I am sure you will never bring dishonour to this gift of mine."

Inspired, Chhatrasal returned to Bundelkhand. The local leaders rallied behind him. "Maharaj Champatrai was our leader. We are indebted to him. We will do whatever you command us to do," they swore. 

"If we rise in rebellion we may have to pay for it—probably with our lives," Chhatrasal warned.

"We will! We will!" the men roared.

Despite the support, Chhatrasal was overwhelmed. His army was tiny and poor. Wandering the forest one night, he prayed, "I need guidance. I wish I had a guru."

As if in answer, he spotted a light in the uninhabited forest and found a sage sitting by a fire. "I've been waiting for you, Chhatrasal," the sage, Pran Nath, smiled. 

"Amazing! I've never met him, yet he knows me," Chhatrasal thought, falling to his knees. "I am desperate for a guru. Sir, I need guidance... I wish to fight the Mughals, but to raise an army, I need plenty of money. What shall I do?"

"That is my concern, not yours," Pran Nath declared. "Tonight, on your horse, you will cover as wide an area as you can. And all that region will produce diamonds. Go with my blessings."

Empowered by the sage's incredible blessing, Chhatrasal soon possessed immense wealth. Men enlisted in his army by the hundreds, and they began liberating areas of Bundelkhand.

Furious, Aurangzeb dispatched a massive force under Syed Bahadur. The Mughals surrounded Chhatrasal's camp in the jungle. But Chhatrasal was prepared. His trusted lieutenant, Nathoo Singh, slipped through the enemy cordon at night. 

"Welcome back, Nathoo Singh. Our men are ready. They await your signal," Chhatrasal whispered the next morning. 

As the unsuspecting Mughals closed in on the camp, Nathoo Singh's forces screamed, "Har Har Mahadev!" and attacked brutally from the rear. Caught in a deadly trap, the Mughal army was completely routed. 

Aurangzeb sent another massive force under Ruhalla Khan. "Don't lose courage," Chhatrasal told his generals. "This will be a battle of wits." 

Chhatrasal feigned a retreat, hiding his troops in the hills. "Tomorrow morning we will attack," the Mughal commander laughed in his tent, watching dancers perform. 

Suddenly, a sentry rushed in. "Run... run... Chhatrasal has attacked! That's a big battalion carrying torches!"

The Mughals scrambled out in a panic, only to realize the "torches" were tied to the horns of a herd of cattle storming down the hill. While the Mughals were distracted by the stampede, Chhatrasal's real forces struck from behind, claiming another stunning victory.

Chhatrasal continued his run of victories, officially establishing his capital in Panna. But years later, an enormous Mughal army under Mohammad Khan Bangas descended upon them. 

"Bangas is getting the better of us," a general reported. "He has lost only 4,000 men to our 12,000."

Realizing his kingdom was on the brink of collapse, the aging Chhatrasal wrote a desperate letter to the Maratha Peshwa, Baji Rao. "As the elephant was overpowered by the crocodile, so are the Bundelas losing out today. Save us, Baji Rao."

Baji Rao's massive army arrived just in time. Together, the Marathas and Bundelas besieged Bangas in the fort of Jaitpur. With their supply lines cut, the Mughals began starving. "Kill the camels and horses and eat them... if that's not enough, eat one another!" Bangas yelled in despair, before finally surrendering unconditionally.

To commemorate the ultimate victory, Chhatrasal ordered grand celebrations. He ruled Bundelkhand until a ripe old age, bringing an era of deep peace, justice, and prosperity to Central India. The grateful people of Bundelkhand cherish his memory and his magnificent sword to this day.
