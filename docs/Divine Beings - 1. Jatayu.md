# Jatayu

Many stories are told about the great vulture, Jatayu. He was the son of Aruna, the Sun God's charioteer, and he was the nephew of Garuda, the mount of Vishnu. He was a bird of majestic proportions, and when he spread his wings, it seemed as though they covered the whole sky. Jatayu lived with his older brother, the magnificent Sampati, who was the King of Birds, in a deep forest full of tall trees and wondrous creatures. 

Even as hatchlings, both the brothers had been fond of flying at dizzying heights, challenging themselves and each other. "Let's see who reaches that peak first!" 

As they grew older and stronger, their races became more frequent and danger-ridden. "Slow down, Jatayu!" Sampati would warn. "They may be rash and foolhardy, but how splendid they look!" Aruna, their father, would think. 

One day, Jatayu challenged his brother. "I bet I can fly higher than you, brother Sampati!" 

"Ha ha, we'll see," Sampati laughed. 

"My sons, you must not come near us," Aruna warned them as they approached the Sun God's chariot. "It's dangerous. You must tell your sons not to fly so close to us unless they want to be scorched." 

"Don't worry about us, father," Sampati assured him, but Aruna's advice went unheeded. 

The two brothers rose higher and higher, their wings beating faster than even the wind. "Our forest looks like a speck of dust from up here," Jatayu marvelled. 

Suddenly, Sampati realized what was happening. "I'm starting to feel a little tired... I wonder if Sampati's feeling the same... no, he seems to be all right," Jatayu thought, unaware of the imminent danger as he inched towards the sun. 

"Jatayu is gaining speed! On the other hand, I feel quite tired and breathless. At this rate, I won't be able to stay ahead of him for much longer," Sampati thought. 

Soon, the heat became unbearable. "It's blazing hot! Where are we? My throat is parched. Every inch of my body seems to be on fire. My brother looks exhausted too. What is happening to us?" 

It was Sampati who realized what was wrong. "We are too close to Surya! It must be noon now. No wonder the sun god is shining so brightly," Sampati panicked. "I can hear the sound of Surya's horses. The chariot will be here any moment... it's too late to fly away... we'll be burnt to death!" 

"If I stop the chariot, the universe will be thrown out of balance and there will be utter havoc," Surya thought, helplessly watching the birds approach. 

"My sons... oh no!" Aruna cried out in horror. 

"I must continue on my path. Forgive me, my children," Surya said. 

Surya's chariot was dangerously close now. "Oh, how do I save him? Jatayu, no!" Sampati yelled. "There's only one way..." 

With a final burst of speed, Sampati flew over Jatayu, stretching his wings wide to shield his beloved brother. The flames ate up Sampati's wings, and he hurtled to the ground in pain, having lost the power of flight. "Aaaaaargh!" 

"Sampati, my brave son! What a great sacrifice you have made!" Aruna wept. 

Jatayu also fell, exhausted and broken. "Sampati, where are you?" 

Sampati had fallen on Mount Mahendra. "I am unable to move... and the pain... it is unbearable," he groaned to a sage who found him. "Let me tend to your burns," the kind sage Nishakara offered. Sampati remained there for the rest of his days, under the care of the sage. The two brothers never met again. 

Many years later, in the kingdom of Mithila, King Dasharatha of Ayodhya was getting his son Rama married to Sita. Dasharatha met Jatayu, and they became close friends. "I must bid you farewell now, but I give you my word, I will come to your aid whenever you need me," Jatayu promised as Dasharatha left for Ayodhya. 

Soon after, Dasharatha consulted his royal priests. "Why are we being punished like this? Have I offended some god?" 

"Lord Shani's evil eye is upon Ayodhya," the priests explained. "You must be very careful, your majesty. If Lord Shani's eye falls on you, you will be killed." 

"Then I will fly to the heavens and seek Shani out. I will beg him to put an end to our suffering," Dasharatha resolved. 

Dasharatha set out in his flying chariot. Shani sensed Dasharatha approaching him. "Dasharatha is coming to meet me! I cannot let him get close to me. I promised Brahma I would not cast my eye on any living creature." Shani flew faster to evade him. 

"He's evading me. But I cannot give up. My kingdom is at stake," Dasharatha thought, giving chase. "I'm getting closer." 

Jatayu was watching the chase from a distance. "That's Dasharatha, the King of Ayodhya, chasing Shani! It will surely get him into trouble. Let me help him." 

Shani turned around. "He's still following me! I have to stop him." Shani cast his burning gaze upon Dasharatha's chariot. "My chariot is on fire!" Dasharatha yelled as the fire spread like lightning. "Aaaah!" he screamed, falling from the sky. 

"I managed to catch your father as he fell," Jatayu later recounted to Rama. 

"You are safe now," Jatayu had told Dasharatha. 

"Who are you, O great bird?" Dasharatha asked. 

"My name is Jatayu. I am the brother of Sampati, the King of Birds, and the son of Aruna, the Sun God's charioteer. My uncle is Garuda, the mount of Vishnu." 

"I have heard many stories about your courage and wisdom, Dasharatha," Jatayu said. "I could not have let you plummet to your death." 

"I'm indebted to you for life," Dasharatha thanked him. 

Jatayu took Dasharatha to Shani. "Shani Dev! My kingdom has been struck by a great famine. My people are starving. It is all because Shani's evil eye is upon Ayodhya," Dasharatha pleaded. 

"Dasharatha, you almost lost your life pursuing Shani. Your chariot is in flames. Climb on to my back, and I will take you home," Jatayu offered. 

"I have never witnessed such valour. I promise you, Ayodhya will not suffer anymore," Shani declared. 

"Hail King Dasharatha! He has saved us from death!" the people of Ayodhya rejoiced when they returned. 

"Thank you, noble friend," Dasharatha told Jatayu. 

Years later, Rama, Lakshmana, and Sita were exiled to the Panchavati forest. "I have never seen a bird of such gigantic proportions. Why, it's as big as a hillock!" Rama exclaimed when they encountered Jatayu. 

"Do not be afraid. My name is Jatayu. You must be the sons of Dasharatha... you look so much like him," Jatayu greeted them. "I promised Dasharatha that I would always be there for him and his family as long as I had breath in my body. You do not have anything to fear in this forest. We are grateful to you, Jatayu." 

"Our father is no more," Rama told him sorrowfully, recounting the events that led to their exile and Dasharatha's death. 

"I cannot believe my dear friend is gone," Jatayu mourned. 

One day, when Rama and Lakshmana were out hunting, Ravana came to their hut in disguise. "Will you give me something to eat?" he asked Sita. When she stepped out, he revealed his true form. "Ha ha! You are mine now!" 

"No! Let me go!" Sita screamed as Ravana dragged her into his flying chariot. 

Jatayu heard her cries. "That's Sita's voice! Help! Somebody, please save me!" 

"Stop, Ravana!" Jatayu intercepted the chariot in the sky. "I promised Dasharatha I would protect his family. I have to save Sita. You are committing the greatest folly of your life." 

"Out of my way, bird!" Ravana roared. 

"Fool! I warned you. Let her go, Ravana. Sita is Rama's wife," Jatayu declared, attacking Ravana fiercely. 

"Aaaaaargh!" Ravana screamed as Jatayu's talons tore into him. 

But age was catching up with the great bird. "I am losing my strength. My limbs are failing me. Ravana is too powerful, and I have become weak with age," Jatayu realized. 

"Ha ha! You foolish thing! You may be a mighty bird, but you are no match for me," Ravana sneered. Deaf to Sita's pleas, Ravana slashed at Jatayu without mercy. With a cruel swipe of his sword, he cut off Jatayu's wings. "My wing!" Jatayu cried as he fell to the earth. 

Later, Rama and Lakshmana returned to find Sita missing. While searching the forest, Rama found the fallen bird. "Jatayu! What happened? Who did this to you?" 

"Forgive me, Rama. I tried my best to save Sita. But he was too powerful for me... R...Ravana. He cut off my wings and talons. I'm useless without them," Jatayu gasped, his life slipping away. 

"Oh Jatayu! You put your own life in danger to save Sita," Rama wept. 

"You must go now... rescue Sita from that evil demon's clutches. They went south," Jatayu breathed his last. 

"We were not able to perform the last rites for our father, but we will give Jatayu that honour," Rama told Lakshmana. 

The two brothers built a pyre out of fragrant wood for the great bird. "I will bring the water of the seven sacred rivers," Rama said, shooting an arrow into the ground. Water sprang up from the spot, and Jatayu's soul was granted salvation.
