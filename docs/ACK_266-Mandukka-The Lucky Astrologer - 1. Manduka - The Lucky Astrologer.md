# Manduka: The Lucky Astrologer

One day, a poor, down-on-his-luck Brahmana was passing by the house of a wealthy merchant when he heard the sounds of joyous celebration. 

"Hey! There's a wedding going on there!" he realized, feeling a pang of sadness. "No one even bothered to tell me about it. Who tells me anything? Even when I was a child, everybody ignored me... except father, of course, who never failed to tell me one thing every so often."

He remembered his father's harsh words: *"You are as dull as a Manduka (frog) in a well."*

"That's what I've become, a Manduka! And I'm tired of it," he sighed. "I wish I could do something... anything... which would make everyone sit up and take notice of me."

Suddenly, as he walked past a beautifully decorated white horse tied to a post near the wedding, an idea struck him. 

"Hey, I've got it!" he grinned maliciously. 

He waited until no one was looking, snuck up to the post, and quietly led away the horse on which the bridegroom had arrived. He hid the horse deep in the forest near a stream. 

The next morning, the merchant's house was in an absolute uproar. 

"Master, the bridegroom's horse has disappeared!" a servant cried. 

"Disappeared? How could it disappear? Go and look for it!" the merchant yelled. 

"We already have, master. It has vanished without a trace."

The merchant paced nervously. "Oh, this is terrible! What am I to do now?" 

"Master," another servant interrupted. "There's a woman outside who says we should seek the help of her husband. She says he's an astrologer."

"Go fetch him at once!" the merchant ordered desperately. 

Sometime later, the Brahmana, dressed formally with charts under his arm, arrived at the merchant's house. *How well my plan is working!* he thought gleefully. *Already I'm being treated as an honoured guest.*

"Your servants tell me you have lost a horse," the Brahmana said sagely. 

"That's right," the merchant pleaded. "I shall reward you handsomely if you can help us find him."

"Give me a few moments and I shall tell you exactly where he is," the Brahmana promised, unrolling his astrological charts. He pretended to study them intently, muttering to himself. "I'll have to study these charts and... and... Ah! You'll find the horse near the stream in the forest. But make haste, he is in danger of being stolen."

"Rush to the stream!" the merchant ordered his men. 

Sure enough, the servants returned leading the white horse. The merchant was overjoyed to see the horse again. "I can't believe it! So the astrologer was right. You have helped me save my face. I can never thank you enough," the merchant smiled, handing the Brahmana a heavy bag of gold coins. 

"It was nothing... all in a day's work!" the Brahmana shrugged modestly. 

The merchant's servants quickly spread the word about what the incredible astrologer had done. "And he told us the exact spot at which we could find the horse!"

"You don't say! I had always thought he was a half-wit."

"You should never go by appearances. What a great astrologer he is!"

Soon, people everywhere began to show him deep respect. The Brahmana's fame spread far and wide. *Everyone bows to me now,* he smiled to himself. *At last the Manduka has jumped out of the well!*

Then, one day, royal guards arrived at his doorstep. "Are you Pandit Bhojraj? Your presence is requested at the palace. Please come with me."

At the palace, the King looked incredibly worried. "The Queen's necklace has been lost... or stolen," the King explained. "I have heard that you once told a merchant where he could find his lost horse. I am sure you can tell us where the necklace is."

The Brahmana swallowed hard, cold sweat breaking out on his forehead. "I... er... I need time."

"Well? You can take as long as you want," the King said firmly. "But you must not leave the palace till the necklace is found."

Guards escorted the Brahmana to a private chamber. *This is the end of the road for me,* he panicked. *I wonder what the King will do to me when he discovers that I am not a real astrologer.*

The Brahmana shut himself up in his chamber and prepared for the worst. "Why did I claim to be what I am not? Now I will just have to pay for it," he cried, pulling his hair. "It's my tongue which has got me into all this trouble!"

Just then, outside the heavy wooden door, the Queen's maid was listening nervously. *He is a great astrologer,* the maid panicked. *He will soon know that it is I who stole the necklace. Why, oh, why did I do such a foolish thing! Let me see what he is doing.*

She pressed her eye to the keyhole. 

Inside, the Brahmana, unaware that the maid was peeping, began to angrily scold his own tongue (which in Sanskrit is called *Jihvah*). 

"What have you done, Jihvah? Why did you do it?" the Brahmana yelled at himself. 

Outside, the maid gasped in pure terror. *He knows!* 

She burst into the room and threw herself at the Brahmana's feet. "O holy sir, have mercy on me! I am doomed!" she sobbed. 

The Brahmana jumped back in shock. "Did what? Who are you?" 

"I am Jihvah, the maid. I heard you," she cried, pressing her hands together. "I did it in a moment of weakness. I don't know why I stole the necklace. Please don't tell the King!"

The Brahmana's eyes widened in realization. *She thought I was talking to her!* he realized, quickly composing himself. 

"Er... well... you have committed a great sin," he said sternly. "But I shall forgive you this once. Now, where is the necklace?"

Later that day, as the King was strolling in the garden, the Brahmana approached him confidently. "I know where the necklace is, Maharaj. Please follow me."

He led the King to a specific tree and pointed. "Here."

A gardener quickly dug up the earth and found the glittering necklace. 

"You are without doubt the greatest astrologer I have ever met!" the King gasped. "Now tell me who hid the necklace here?"

The Brahmana smiled mysteriously. "That must remain a secret, Maharaj. There are some things which even I may not know."

But the King's Prime Minister was suspicious. "There is some mystery here, Maharaj," he whispered. "If he is such a great astrologer, why can't he tell us who the thief is? He must have found the necklace in the palace and hidden it here himself. Let us test him once more."

The King agreed. After some time, the Brahmana was summoned to the King's chamber. 

"I am amazed by your power, O Pandit, and you shall be well rewarded for your services," the King said. He pointed to a small, sealed clay jar on a pedestal. "Can you tell us what is in that jar?"

The Brahmana stared at the jar, his heart sinking. *How neatly he has trapped me!* he thought miserably. *Now I'll have to tell him that I am not really an astrologer... and that I fooled him and everybody else. He'll surely put me to death!*

In absolute despair, he struck his forehead and cried out his childhood nickname. "O Manduka, you were better off in the well!" 

The King's eyes widened in sheer shock. "Well done! I should never have doubted your powers," the King gasped. 

The Minister lifted the lid off the jar, and a small green frog—a *manduka*—hopped out onto the floor! 

"There is indeed a manduka in the jar," the Minister admitted, thoroughly amazed. 

The King rewarded the incredibly lucky Brahmana with costly gifts and a permanent position at court, and he returned home to a hero's welcome, never to be called a dull frog again.
