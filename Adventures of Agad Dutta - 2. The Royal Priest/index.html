    The Royal Priest

    The Raja of Simhapur loved to hear of the grand adventures of merchants who sailed to distant, exotic lands.

    One day, an adventurous merchant named Bhadramitra of Padminikhet found himself at the Raja's court, regaling the King with tales of his voyages.

    "I do envy you!" the Raja smiled. "That was an exciting adventure!"

    "And this has been an interesting game. I must thank you for your hospitality, Maharaj," Bhadramitra bowed. "When will you be setting sail again?"

    "Tomorrow," Bhadramitra replied. "That reminds me—I need your help. I have some gold with me which I want to keep with someone trustworthy while I am at sea."

    "You can safely leave it with my royal priest, Shribhuti," the Raja suggested warmly. "A more honest man would be hard to find!"

    So Bhadramitra confidently left a heavy casket containing all his gold with Shribhuti, the royal priest, and set sail in search of trade in other lands.

    Over the years, Bhadramitra bought and sold a vast variety of exotic goods, amassing a great deal of wealth. Finally, he was on his homeward voyage back to Simhapur.

    "The good King of Simhapur and his honest priest will be pleased with the gifts I have for them," Bhadramitra mused happily, watching the horizon.

    But as he mused, the skies suddenly darkened, and a violent storm broke out. The ship was mercilessly tossed about by mountainous waves, and, in spite of all the crew's desperate efforts to save it, the ship sank.

    Bhadramitra barely managed to grab a floating plank of wood. Clinging to the plank for dear life, he floated on the rough seas for several agonizing hours. Miraculously, he was finally washed ashore, exhausted and half-dead, not far from the city of Simhapur.

    Hours later, late at night, the royal priest Shribhuti heard a feeble knock at his door.

    "Who could it be at this hour?" the priest muttered, opening the door.

    A soaking wet, shivering man in rags stood there. "It's me, Bhadramitra. I have... come for my... casket."

    Shribhuti stared at the beggar. Knowing the merchant had lost everything, a wave of wicked greed washed over the priest.

    "Away with you!" Shribhuti yelled, violently shoving the exhausted merchant back into the street. "I don't know any Bhadramitra. You've come to the wrong place."

    "No! No! You can't do this to me!" Bhadramitra cried in sheer despair. "I've lost... everything. I'm... hungry... thirsty. I need my gold. Please give me my casket..."

    Ignoring his pitiful cries, Shribhuti slammed the heavy door shut and locked it.

    The next morning, Bhadramitra squatted outside the royal palace. "Then I'll squat here and demand justice till I get it," he vowed.

    When Shribhuti arrived for his daily duties, Bhadramitra raised his voice and continued to loudly accuse him. "The royal priest has swindled me of my wealth! I want justice!"

    The cries became louder and louder until even the Raja inside the palace was able to hear them. "What's all this? What is he shouting about?" the Raja asked the priest.

    "Ignore him, Maharaj!" Shribhuti lied smoothly. "He is a madman. You know that whatever I earn each day, I give away in charity. Would I swindle anyone?"

    But Bhadramitra persisted in crying for justice day and night, refusing to leave the palace steps. Finally, the Raja could no longer ignore him and turned to his clever Prime Minister for help.

    The next morning, the Minister walked out to Bhadramitra. "Justice... I want justice. The royal priest has..." Bhadramitra croaked, his throat completely hoarse.

    "Why don't you come home with me, my good man?" the Minister offered kindly. "You could bathe, change your clothes—and then tell me your story."

    At the Minister's house, Bhadramitra gratefully ate a hot meal and narrated the entire tragedy.

    "So that's it," the Minister frowned. "Do you have any proof? Any document or receipt to support your story?"

    "I'm afraid I don't, good sir. I didn't think it necessary when the man was recommended by the Raja himself."

    The Minister took the matter back to the King. "... Shribhuti flatly denies that he took any gold from the merchant. And we have absolutely no proof!"

    "Then what can we do?" the Raja sighed.

    The Minister smiled slyly. "I have an idea. Why don't you invite Shribhuti to a game of dice tomorrow, and..." The Minister laid out a brilliant plot to trap the greedy priest.

    "Excellent! I'll do as you say," the Raja laughed.

    The next day, Shribhuti arrived at the palace, completely unaware of the trap. "Ah, Shribhuti! There you are! Come, let's begin right away," the Raja greeted him cheerfully.

    They sat down on the floor cushions to play dice. After a while, the Raja wiped his brow. "It's rather warm, isn't it? How clammy my palms are! I'd better remove my signet ring." The Raja took off his heavy royal ring and placed it on the floor.

    "It is warm. I'll remove mine too," Shribhuti agreed, slipping his own distinct gold ring off his finger and placing it next to the Raja's.

    As they continued to play, the Raja purposely bumped his hand against the rings, scattering them across the board. "Oh! Oh! How clumsy of me! I'll..."

    "Don't worry, Maharaj, I'll pick them up," Shribhuti offered eagerly.

    Just then, right on cue, the Prime Minister walked into the room. "Maharaj, pardon me for interrupting your game, but I have an urgent matter to discuss with you in private."

    "You will excuse me, Shribhuti. After all, royal duties must come first," the Raja said, getting up and leaving the room with the Minister.

    Once out of sight, the Minister pulled something from his pocket. "Here's the ring! I did manage to secretly switch them while he was distracted. I leave the rest to you."

    The Raja smiled and went back to finish the game.

    Meanwhile, a few minutes later, a royal attendant knocked on the door of Shribhuti's house across the city. Shribhuti's wife answered the door.

    "The priest has asked for the casket of gold coins to be brought to the palace," the attendant demanded officially.

    "But... but..." the wife hesitated, knowing the dark secret of the stolen casket.

    "Why do you hesitate?" the attendant asked sternly. "If you don't trust me, take a look at this. He asked me to show it to you if you were hesitant."

    The attendant held up Shribhuti's personal gold ring!

    "His ring!" the wife gasped. "He wouldn't want anyone to see it! Of course, he wouldn't!"

    Panicking, she rushed inside, grabbed the heavy casket, and handed it to the attendant. "Please deliver it safely. And... and cover it with your cloth. He wouldn't want anyone to see it."

    Back at the palace, the attendant carried the covered casket straight into the game room and stood before the Raja and Shribhuti.

    "Do you recognise that casket, Shribhuti?" the Raja asked coldly as the cloth was pulled away.

    Shribhuti's jaw dropped in absolute horror. Before he could stammer a lie, the Minister walked into the room, leading the merchant Bhadramitra.

    "Perhaps you do recognise Bhadramitra, the merchant of Padminikhet," the Minister smiled sharply. "Is your memory refreshed, or does it need another shock?"

    The Raja glared at his disgraced priest. "Come, take your casket, Bhadramitra," the Raja said kindly. "While I am truly sorry for the misery we have caused you, I am deeply grateful to you for exposing this total hypocrite."

    The Raja turned his fury on Shribhuti. "Shribhuti, I don't know who is more embarrassed. You, the royal priest who could act like a common cheat... or I, the Raja who recommended you for your honesty! Go away! And never set foot in my kingdom again."
