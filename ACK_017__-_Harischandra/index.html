    Harischandra: The King of Truth

    In the era of Treta Yuga, King Harischandra ruled the grand kingdom of Ayodhya. A righteous and deeply moral king, he was loved and respected by all his subjects. His name was entirely synonymous with truth.

    One day, while hunting in the dense forest, Harischandra heard a desperate cry. "Help! Help!"

    Thinking it was a woman in distress, the king rushed toward the sound, breaking through the thicket. He burst into a clearing, only to find the fearsome Sage Vishwamitra sitting in deep meditation.

    "What have I done?" Harischandra thought, his heart sinking. "This is Sage Vishwamitra! What can I do now?"

    He bowed deeply. "I am sorry I disturbed you, sir! I heard someone crying for help!"

    Vishwamitra’s eyes snapped open, blazing with anger. "It was the spirit of the sciences I am trying to master! I was about to succeed! You have spoiled everything by coming here! My curse will destroy your kingdom!"

    Terrified of the sage's wrath, Harischandra pleaded, "Ask for anything and I shall give it to you!"

    "Anything?" Vishwamitra challenged, raising an eyebrow.

    "Yes, anything."

    "All right! I shall remember your promise!" the sage declared.

    A few days later, Harischandra was seated on his throne when a loud voice broke the silence of the royal court. "Harischandra!"

    It was Vishwamitra. He marched down the aisle, pointing a stern finger at the king. "I have come for what you promised me! I want your kingdom!"

    "My kingdom?" Harischandra gasped.

    "Yes! If you fail to keep your promise, my curse will come upon Ayodhya!"

    The hushed courtiers watched in absolute horror as Harischandra poured sacred water into the sage's hands. "I hand over my kingdom to you," the king said softly.

    "Your majesty, how can you leave like this?" his ministers wept.

    "If I break my promise, my subjects will suffer the sage's curse," Harischandra replied resolutely. Stripped of their royal robes, King Harischandra, Queen Saivya, and the young Prince Rohitaswa walked out of the city gates. The whole city came to the road to watch them go, weeping for their beloved royal family.

    But as they walked away, Vishwamitra called out, "Wait!"

    "Haven't you forgotten something?" the sage demanded. "You have made a gift of the kingdom, but where is the dakshina (honorarium)? With every gift given, one has to give a dakshina!"

    Harischandra looked at him helplessly. "I have given away everything! I have nothing left."

    "You must fulfil your promise! Your dakshina is due!" Vishwamitra insisted.

    "Please give me a month!" Harischandra begged. The sage agreed.

    The king, the queen, and the young prince roamed from land to land, journeying through harsh forests and scorching deserts. "One month is nearly over!" Harischandra thought in despair. "I have not been able to get any work. How shall I pay the sage?"

    At last, they reached the holy temple city of Varanasi on the banks of the river Ganga. The tired family approached a grand temple. "Let us go and meet the high priest!" Harischandra suggested. "I hope I shall get some work here!"

    The high priest looked at them closely. "Who are you? Your face shows marks of nobility!"

    "We are weary travellers! Can you give me some work?" Harischandra asked.

    "What work can a Kshatriya do in a temple? No, there is no work! But take whatever food you need," the priest offered kindly.

    On the final day of the month, as the trio walked through a crowded street, Harischandra heard a familiar, terrifying voice. "Harischandra!"

    It was Vishwamitra. "One month is over today!" the sage declared.

    "I know, but... the day is not yet over! Please wait till nightfall," Harischandra pleaded. As Vishwamitra walked away, Harischandra's mind raced. In a few hours the sun will set! How shall I pay the dakshina?

    Back at their small shelter, young Prince Rohitaswa cried, "Mother! I am very hungry! Please give me some food!"

    Saivya fed him some rice, turning to her husband. "Do we have to beg for our food now?"

    Harischandra looked at his family with tears in his eyes. "There is only one way to fulfil my promise! I can sell myself!"

    "Oh, no! No!" Saivya wept.

    But Harischandra went to the marketplace, standing on the auction block. "Who will buy me? I can do any work!" he shouted to the crowds. Man after man looked at his frail, starved body and shook their heads. "No, you look weak! Look at that frail man! What work can he do?"

    I must sell myself! I must sell myself! those words beat constantly in his head.

    "Nobody will buy you!" a merchant laughed. "But someone may buy your wife for house-work!"

    Hearing this, Queen Saivya approached her husband. "No one will buy you! But you have something else for sale. I have given you a son! My life is fulfilled! Sell me and pay your debt!"

    "What do you mean?" Harischandra choked. "Do you know what you are saying? Sell my own wife?"

    "Truth is more important than human beings! You must uphold truth!" she insisted firmly. "Do you remember what the sage said?"

    With a heavy heart, Harischandra led his wife to the auction platform. "Who will buy a woman for household work? My wife is for sale!" he yelled, his voice cracking.

    "What kind of a man are you to sell your wife?" a disgusted onlooker spat.

    "I am no man but a heartless monster! But I must do what I must do!" Harischandra replied, tears streaming down his face.

    An old, wealthy Brahman stepped forward. "I will take her! She will be my wife's servant. What is her price?"

    "How can a man fix the price of his wife, the mother of his son?" Harischandra whispered.

    The Brahman, delighted that he could offer whatever he liked, tossed a purse. "Then take these five hundred gold coins and give up all claim on her!"

    As Harischandra tied the purse to his waist, young Rohitaswa realized his mother was being taken away. "Mother! Mother!" he screamed, running after her.

    "Please let me take him with me!" Saivya begged the Brahman. "He can't live without me!"

    The Brahman shrugged. "Here's another two-hundred and fifty! I shall buy the boy as well!"

    Harischandra stood completely alone, watching helplessly as his wife and son were dragged away. "I've nothing else to live for now except to pay my debt!" he sobbed.

    Vishwamitra appeared right on cue. "Have you got the money? The day is almost over!"

    Harischandra handed over the purse of seven-hundred and fifty coins. Vishwamitra counted it and frowned. "What is this? This is not enough! You have to pay me two-hundred and fifty more!"

    "But I've nothing else left!"

    "Get it somehow. I shall come at nightfall!" the sage demanded, walking away.

    Driven by desperation, Harischandra returned to the market. "Who will buy a man as his slave?" he yelled.

    The people recognized him and laughed cruelly. "You sold your wife! Is your need for money so great? You will be a burden to the one that buys you!"

    Just then, a dark, terrifying man with a skull in one hand and a staff in the other stepped through the crowd. He was a Chandala, a keeper of the cremation grounds—the lowest, most impure caste in the kingdom.

    "I am strong!" Harischandra pleaded to the man. "It's only that I've not eaten for a few days! Who will buy me?"

    "I shall buy you, if you wish to be bought!" the Chandala grunted. "You'll have to pay me two hundred and fifty gold coins!" Harischandra blurted out.

    "All right! Follow me!" the Chandala agreed.

    Harischandra paid off Vishwamitra and followed his new master to the dark, smoky, and terrifying cremation grounds. "What work do I have to do?" Harischandra asked.

    "Whatever work a Chandala does!" his master sneered. "Your duty is to wait on cremators and demand the cloth, rice and money. A sixth part goes to the king, three to me and the rest will be yours!"

    Harischandra’s days passed in a living nightmare, collecting money from grieving families and watching corpses burn. The heat and smoke shrivelled his skin, turning him into a skeletal, unrecognizable figure.

    Meanwhile, Queen Saivya was forced to work day and night in the cruel Brahman's household. One evening, her son came to her weeping. "The Brahman beat me! I was playing in his room!"

    "Go to the garden! You can play there!" she consoled him.

    But when the queen went to fetch him after her chores, she couldn't find him. Holding a lamp, she searched the dark garden until she stumbled upon a horrific sight. Young Rohitaswa lay cold and lifeless under a tree. "He is cold! What are these marks? Oh, my son! A serpent has bitten him!" she screamed.

    Her eyes filled with tears, the Queen carried the dead child to the Brahman. "My son is dead. Please give me some money for his funeral!"

    "Money? Isn't it enough that we feed you?" the Brahman yelled, slamming the door.

    Carrying her son's lifeless body, Saivya walked alone through the deserted streets to the cremation ground. At the gates, a bearded, shrivelled Chandala blocked her way.

    "What do you want? Have you brought the rice and cash?" he demanded gruffly.

    "No!" Saivya wept, her face hidden by a veil.

    The Chandala looked down at the dead boy. Suddenly, his heart began to pound. The child's face bears the mark of royalty! Who is he? Why is my heart beating so fast?

    "He is indeed a royal child!" the Chandala gasped. "His father was Harischandra!"

    Saivya looked up in shock. "What did you say? My son! My son!" She peered closely at the Chandala's dirty, ruined face. "Why did you cry out! Let me look at your face... My husband!"

    Harischandra fell to his knees. "Yes! I'm that wretched man! But... you will have to bring the cloth and rice!"

    Saivya stared at him in disbelief. "My husband who was a king is now a Chandala? You are asking the payment for your son's funeral?"

    "I have no choice!" Harischandra wept. "I cannot deprive my master of his dues!"

    "But how can I pay you?" she sobbed.

    Harischandra choked back his tears, firmly adhering to his duty. "Give me half of your upper garment!"

    "Yes! It is my duty to my master!" Saivya agreed, reaching to tear her only piece of clothing.

    Suddenly, a heavy hand grabbed Harischandra's arm, stopping him. Harischandra turned around. It was his master, the Chandala.

    "Harischandra! Your test is over!" the Chandala smiled.

    Before Harischandra could speak, the dark sky erupted with blinding light. The Devas (heavenly beings) descended from the clouds, led by Lord Indra.

    "Harischandra! We were testing your devotion to truth! You have passed the supreme test," Indra declared.

    "But, my lord, did you have to test me by taking away my only child?" Harischandra cried.

    Indra simply smiled and pointed. Harischandra turned to see young Rohitaswa standing there, rubbing his eyes and smiling, completely alive and well.

    Sage Vishwamitra stepped out from the crowd of Devas. "Harischandra! My friend, forgive me for testing you so harshly!"

    "But how can I go back? I have sold myself to the Chandala," Harischandra said, ever mindful of his promise.

    The Chandala stepped forward, his dark form shimmering until he transformed into Yama, the God of Virtue and Death. "No Harischandra! You are a free man! The Chandala was Yama himself. You have proved your virtue! You have earned your place in heaven!"

    "I cannot go to heaven without my subjects!" Harischandra insisted.

    "Your subjects will get what they deserve!" Yama argued.

    "If so, then let me share my blessings with them on the earth!" Harischandra requested humbly.

    "You are truly a great king! Your request will be granted!" Indra proclaimed.

    Word reached Ayodhya that their beloved king was returning. The streets were lined with weeping, joyous crowds. Riding a magnificent chariot alongside his queen, with young Rohitaswa leading the procession on horseback, King Harischandra returned to his throne, his name forever etched in history as the ultimate symbol of unwavering truth.
