    Bappa Rawal: The Great Rajput Hero

    In the eighth century, as foreign invaders launched successive attacks on the western borders of India, the native Rajput rulers wasted their strength fighting among themselves for petty, selfish ends. Divided and weakened, their kingdoms faced constant peril.

    It was during these turbulent times that Nagaditya, the ruler of Idur, was slain by his own Bhil subjects. His grieving queen fled into the dense wilderness with her three-year-old son, Prince Shailadhish, accompanied only by a few fiercely loyal followers and the royal priest, the Rajguru.

    For years, the royal family lived in hiding, enduring hardship and constant fear. "Rajguru, I am tired of this hard life," the Queen Mother confessed one day, her eyes heavy with exhaustion.

    "You will have to bear it for the sake of the young prince, Rajmata," the Rajguru replied gently.

    "Will he have no respite from our enemies?" a loyal follower asked anxiously.

    "He will," the Rajguru reassured them. "We plan to give him another name. Since the destiny of Mewar rests in the young prince's hands, his name shall now be Bappa Rawal."

    "But he would still be easily recognized here!" the Queen Mother pointed out.

    "We will take him to Parashar village in the Trikoot range, in the Abu hills," said the Rajguru. "Nobody will recognize him there."

    "Must you take him away from me?" she wept, cradling her son.

    "Yes, in the interests of Mewar, you must bear this separation," the Rajguru urged.

    One afternoon, the Rajguru brought the young prince to Nagda, placing him in the care of a trusted Brahman family. "Bappa is hardworking and honest," the Rajguru assured the householder. "He will serve you well."

    Turning to the boy, the Brahman's wife gave him his duties. "Every morning, you will have to take my cattle to the hills to graze."

    "It will be done, sir," Bappa replied respectfully. That evening, as Bappa watched his protectors depart into the sunset, he swallowed his sorrow. They have gone, he thought to himself. I am all alone.

    Bappa toiled faithfully every day, quickly earning high praise from the Brahman and his wife. While herding the cattle in the nearby hills, he met other local boys. "Hey! Who are you?" a boy called out one morning.

    "I am Baliya, and he is Dev," another answered. "We are learning to shoot arrows. You can join us if you like."

    Bappa accepted eagerly. While the cattle grazed peacefully, Bappa practiced archery until his skill eclipsed all others. "You've hit the target again, Bappa!" Baliya cheered. "He's never missed a single shot!"

    Moving from archery to sword-fighting and wrestling, Bappa's strength and agility grew astonishingly. Defeated in every match, the village boys gathered around him in admiration. "Bappa, you have gotten the better of us in sword-fighting as well as in wrestling," they declared. "Henceforward, you are our leader. You command, and we obey."

    However, Bappa's rapid physical development began to arouse suspicion in the Brahman's home. "Bappa is not honest anymore," the Brahman's wife complained to her husband. "Don't you see how hefty he has suddenly become?"

    "He's just a growing boy," the Brahman reasoned.

    "I have my doubts. The milk yield of our grey cow has decreased," she insisted.

    "You don't suspect him of drinking the milk, do you?" the Brahman asked, shaken. "No, it cannot be! Bappa is honest. My wife must be mistaken."

    Overhearing their conversation, Bappa felt deeply wounded. When he stepped into the courtyard, the Brahman confronted him gently. "For several days, the grey cow's milk yield has been low, Bappa. Why?"

    "I too have wondered why, master," Bappa replied, his voice choked with emotion. "The grey cow has not been giving enough milk, and I know you suspect me. You will have to let me prove my honesty to convince your wife."

    Determined to uncover the truth, Bappa and his friends, Baliya and Dev, kept a vigilant watch on the grey cow as it grazed in the hills. "Could someone be milking it while we are busy wrestling or shooting?" Baliya wondered.

    "Quite possible," Bappa said. "We must keep an eye on that cow."

    Deep in the forest, they followed the grey cow to a secluded rocky grove. To their astonishment, the cow stopped over a natural stone formation and stood perfectly still. White milk began to flow spontaneously from its udders, trickling over a sacred Shiva Linga nestled among the rocks.

    "So that's where the milk goes!" Dev gasped.

    "You must inform your master," Baliya added.

    Nearby, sitting atop a high rock in deep meditation, was an austere hermit. "Who's that?" Bappa whispered.

    When the boys returned home and related the miraculous scene, the Brahman released a sigh of relief. "I knew Bappa was honest!" he exclaimed.

    "Thank God! The mystery is solved," his wife agreed remorsefully.

    Returning to the forest, Baliya cautioned, "That is Mahatma Harit. Quick! Let's run away before he sees us—he is known to have a terrible temper!"

    "No," Bappa insisted. "Let's sweep away the dry leaves from his hermitage and then go."

    From that day forward, the boys quietly swept and cleaned the grove every afternoon while the saint meditated. In time, Mahatma Harit opened his eyes and wondered who was tending to his hermitage with such devotion. Catching the boys one afternoon, he called out softly, "Who are you, my children? Come to me. Don't be afraid. Are you the ones who clean up the hermitage every day?"

    "Yes, Mahatmaji," Bappa replied, bowing. "I am Bappa, and these are Baliya and Dev."

    Pleased by their humility and devotion, Mahatma Harit smiled warmly. "I am very pleased with you. You must come to me every day, and I will instruct you in the sacred lore." As Bappa bowed, the sage mentally noted, He has the refined manners of a true prince.

    During this time, the region of Nagda was ruled by a Solanki king. As autumn approached, the king instructed his prime minister, "Celebrate the Jhool Jhoolni fair with great pomp and please the Goddess. The Princess has to be married this year."

    When the fair commenced, Bappa, Baliya, and Dev joined the festive crowds. The Solanki Princess also visited the jungle temple with her maidens. After offering prayers at the shrine, the maidens walked toward the swings. "Rajkumari, we forgot to bring ropes for the swings!" a maiden cried. "How will we swing?"

    Noticing the young cowherds nearby, another maiden suggested, "Those boys have ropes. They might help us out." The Princess called out to Bappa and his companions.

    "What can we do for you?" Bappa asked.

    "We forgot to bring our ropes. Will you lend us yours?" a maiden requested.

    Nudged by a spirit of mischief, Bappa whispered to his friends, "Let me tease her a little." Turning to the maidens, he smiled boldly. "Sure—but on one condition!"

    "Name it," the maiden replied.

    "You will have to marry me," Bappa declared, pointing playfully to the Princess.

    "What! How dare you?" the maidens gasped in shock. "Aren't you ashamed of yourself? Her father is the King! You could be punished for this!"

    Baliya interjected quickly, "Our friend did not suggest a real wedding! He meant a mock one—like that of dolls!"

    "The young man seems to be courageous and fearless," the Princess observed quietly to her maid.

    Bappa turned to leave. "Let us go, friends. Why waste our time here?"

    "Wait! I accept your condition," the Princess called out unexpectedly. Standing beneath a wide banyan tree, Bappa Rawal and the Solanki Princess went through a lighthearted mock marriage ceremony, looping garlanded vines around each other.

    After the ceremony, Bappa chuckled, "You will soon forget me, Rajkumari."

    "Never!" the Princess replied earnestly. "I will remember this mock marriage all my life."

    Months passed, and preparations were underway for the Princess's formal betrothal to a neighbouring prince. Scholarly astrologers gathered in the royal court to match their horoscopes. Suddenly, the chief astrologer gasped in alarm. "My Lord, your daughter is already married! She is not worthy of our prince."

    "My daughter married without my knowledge? Impossible!" the Solanki King roared. "There must be some mistake!"

    "Astrology can never be wrong, sir!" the sage insisted.

    The proposed alliance was promptly cancelled. Furious, the King summoned the Princess. "Who is this person you have married?" he demanded.

    "I didn't ask his name, Father," the Princess confessed. "We went through a mock wedding at the Jhool Jhoolni fair."

    A few days later, sitting in her chambers, the Princess drew a detailed portrait of the young cowherd from memory. "Aha! How handsome he is!" her maid admired. "Why, your picture is a perfect replica of that boy!"

    "I wish we had asked his name!" the Princess sighed.

    Just then, the King entered the chamber and caught sight of the painting. "Who is this?" he asked sharply.

    "The same boy I was talking about, Father," she answered quietly.

    The King turned to his guards in fury. "The young man in this picture, wherever he may be, shall be captured and brought before me!"

    When news of the King's manhunt reached the hills, Bappa realized he was in danger. "The King's men are looking for the boy who married the Princess in fun," he told his companions. "It is not advisable for me to stay in this place."

    "We must not let them know that it was you, Bappa," Baliya said.

    Taking a heavy stone, Bappa threw it into a deep canyon. "Let us take an oath of secrecy," Bappa said solemnly. "If you don't keep my identity a secret, may all the good deeds of your ancestors be buried in a launderer's well, like this stone!"

    "Where will you go?" Dev asked.

    "To Mahatma Harit," Bappa replied.

    "We will not let you go alone, Bappa," his friends declared firmly. "The three of us will stick together."

    "I am fortunate to have friends like you," Bappa said gratefully.

    For months, Bappa remained in hiding in the sacred grove, immersing himself in spiritual and military scriptures under Mahatma Harit's tutelage. However, yearning to see his mother, Bappa decided to visit her. "You are taking a great risk," Baliya warned. "The King's men are still searching everywhere."

    "I am no coward!" Bappa replied, setting off for his mother's dwelling.

    Reaching her humble cottage, Bappa fell at her feet. "Where did you disappear, my son?" the Queen Mother wept, embracing him tightly.

    "I was at the feet of Mahatma Harit, learning the scriptures," Bappa explained. Introducing Baliya and Dev, he added, "Mother, here are my inseparable friends. Now I will always live with you."

    Seeing the prince grown into a formidable youth, the old Rajguru approached the Queen Mother. "The Solanki's soldiers are still looking for Bappa, and everyone has seen his portrait," the Rajguru noted. "Now is the time for us to reveal the full truth to him."

    The Queen Mother sat Bappa down and disclosed his true lineage. "Son, I am the sister of Raja Mori of Chittor—a true princess! All our family's suffering was endured for your sake, Bappa. Now that you have grown up, do your duty."

    Hearing the story of his ancestors, Bappa's heart swelled with pride and determination. "I promise that I will win back the territories of my forefathers and restore honor to them!" he vowed.

    "May God bless you! Bappa Ki Jai!" his followers cheered.

    Set on fulfilling his destiny, Bappa and his friends rode toward Chittor. Along the way, they spotted a remote hut where the renowned sage Baba Gorakhnath sat in deep meditation. "Hush! Take care not to disturb him," Bappa instructed his companions.

    As they waited respectfully, Baba Gorakhnath opened his eyes and gazed intently at the youth. Sensing the divine royal aura around Bappa, the sage smiled. "Brilliant young man, a time will come when you will unite Rajasthan and lead her to victory! Take this magic sword—it will help you in your grand mission."

    Accepting the blessed blade, Bappa bowed low before continuing his journey.

    Upon reaching Chittor, the fifteen-year-old Bappa presented himself at the royal court of his uncle, Raja Mori. Impressed by the boy's noble bearing, Raja Mori welcomed him warmly. "Come, sit by my side," the King offered.

    For several days, Raja Mori observed Bappa's keen intelligence, martial prowess, and polite conduct. Delighted with the youth, the King made a surprise declaration: "I bestow a small estate on Bappa Rawal and appoint him to my Council of Ministers!"

    The unexpected elevation of a young newcomer infuriated the veteran ministers of Chittor. "This is unfair and unjust!" they murmured bitterly behind closed doors. "Our King trusts him beyond reason! He is hardly sixteen years old, completely inexperienced, and inferior to us in every way. To work under a mere boy—what a humiliation!"

    Unfazed by their resentment, Bappa continued to serve with distinction. Recognizing his capability, Raja Mori approached him privately. "The commander of my forces is too old. If I appoint you in his place...?"

    "I shall not disappoint you, uncle," Bappa assured him. "You honor me by calling me brave. I will stake my all to heighten your glory."

    Meanwhile, news of Bappa's rising fame reached the Solanki ruler of Nagda. Investigating the youth's origins, the Solanki King realized that Bappa Rawal was indeed the brave young man from the portrait. "He is the most eligible youth for my daughter!" the King declared. He immediately dispatched a messenger to Chittor with a formal marriage proposal.

    When the Solanki envoy delivered the proposal, Bappa asked, "And what of the Princess?"

    "She worships your portrait, my Lord," the envoy smiled.

    With Raja Mori's blessing, Bappa accepted the proposal. He traveled to Nagda, where he was united in marriage with the Solanki Princess in a grand royal ceremony. After spending a few days in Nagda, Bappa returned to Chittor with his bride.

    Upon his return, Bappa addressed the disgruntled old ministers directly. "My ministers resent my appointment," he told Raja Mori. "I will give it up if you wish."

    "No," Raja Mori advised him. "Use your innate abilities and win them over."

    Before long, a crisis struck the realm. A massive Arab army, led by foreign invaders, advanced rapidly upon Chittor. Standing before the royal court, Bappa rallied the nobles: "A foreigner has invaded Chittor. It is our sacred duty to protect the motherland!"

    Resentful and cowardly, the old councillors refused to mobilize. "We will not fight," they announced coldly.

    "Your reason?" Bappa asked, controlling his anger.

    "We are too old," the councillors replied sarcastically. "We return the lands and gifts bestowed upon us. Dispatch your most trusted lieutenant to the battlefield—he is young, braver, and far more experienced than we are!"

    The taunt ignited Bappa's spirit. "Most certainly, I will!" he roared. "Let the aged go home and rest. Bappa will take care of the enemy, unaided!"

    "Then why delay? Get to the battlefield!" Raja Mori commanded.

    Bappa led his forces to the front lines, charging into battle with his blessed sword. Seeing Bappa in action, the Solanki ruler of Nagda dispatched his army to attack the invaders from the rear. Caught in a crushing double-pronged assault, the Arab forces broke rank and retreated in chaos.

    As the battle ended, a soldier ran up to Bappa. "What is it, my man?" Bappa asked.

    "The councillors are ashamed," the soldier reported. "They are on their way to the battlefield."

    "Tell them it's too late," Bappa smiled. "The enemy has been routed!"

    Ashamed of their initial reluctance, the councillors bowed before Bappa upon their arrival. "We are glad that Chittor has such a brave son in you!" they proclaimed. "We are with you!"

    Rather than returning immediately to Chittor, Bappa pursued the fleeing foreign forces all the way to Ghazni. Storming the enemy stronghold, he captured Salim, the foreign ruler, and liberated the ancestral territories of his forefathers. Appointing a trustworthy representative to govern Ghazni, Bappa returned victorious to Chittor, where the citizens showered him with flowers and jubilation.

    Recognizing Bappa's extraordinary leadership, the aging Raja Mori gathered his council. "Councillors, of my free will, I appoint Bappa Rawal as my successor," he announced.

    At an auspicious hour, Bappa Rawal was crowned King of Chittor amid grand celebrations. Shortly after his coronation, Bappa summoned all the chieftains of the neighboring Rajput kingdoms. "Friends, if only we were united, no one from across the borders would dare to attack us," Bappa urged passionately.

    Moved by his vision, the chieftains agreed unanimously. "We look to you for guidance!" they declared. "We willingly accept you as our King. Rajasthan shall be one united kingdom!"

    Though most rulers submitted peacefully, Isphagul, the ruler of Bandar, resisted. Bappa led his army and defeated him swiftly. "Let this be a lesson to anyone who stands in the way of Rajput unity!" Bappa proclaimed.

    Returning home, Bappa bowed before his mother. "Mother, the states of Rajasthan have merged to form one united whole."

    "His promise fulfilled, Bappa returned to his mother," she wept with joy. "Bappa is their chosen king, and I am proud to be your mother."

    Under King Bappa Rawal's visionary leadership, the united Rajput forces launched bold campaigns abroad, expanding their influence across Ispahan, Kandahar, Iran, Turan, and Khorasan, bringing vast territories under Indian rule.

    After ruling Chittor for many prosperous years, ensuring his subjects lived in peace and contentment, Bappa Rawal handed the throne to his son. Retiring to the serene tranquility of the remote Himalayas, the legendary warrior-king lived to the ripe old age of one hundred, leaving behind a legacy of unity, courage, and honor that endured for centuries.
