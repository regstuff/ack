    Animal Tales from Arunachal Pradesh

    The folklore of Arunachal Pradesh has been handed down by word of mouth across generations. These tales mirror the joys, sorrows, triumphs, and anxieties of its diverse people, seeking to explain the deeper aspects of life and the way animals came to be.

    The Pig and the Dog

    A man once had a pig and a dog. One day, he suddenly realized that the two animals did no work at all.

    "Get up, you lazy creatures!" he yelled, kicking them out of the house. "Go and do something useful! Whichever of you does some work might find a place in my house."

    The pig and the dog went to their master's field. There, the pig ploughed the field the whole day long, toiling in the mud, while the dog simply slept under a tree.

    In the evening, the dog awoke, stretched, and approached the exhausted pig. "Have you finished?"

    "Yes... and I am exhausted," the pig panted.

    As the pig wearily set off for home, the dog jumped into the freshly ploughed field, ran up and down several times to leave his footprints everywhere, and then followed the pig home.

    That night, the master asked, "What work did you do today?"

    "I ploughed the field, master," the pig said proudly.

    "And you?" the master asked the dog.

    "Master... it was I who ploughed the field," the dog lied smoothly.

    "I will have to find out the truth for myself," the man thought. He got up early the next morning and went to the field. When he returned, he was furious with the pig. "Are you satisfied with my work, master?" the pig asked.

    "What work? I saw only the dog's footprints in the soil. It was he who ploughed the field," the master declared. "You liar! Go away, you lazy creature."

    He turned to the dog with a smile. "Come, dog. You have done some hard work. You may come into the house with me."

    Ever since that day, dogs have stayed in their masters' houses, and pigs have not been allowed inside.

    The Owl and the Tiger

    One day, the birds and animals of the earth held a grand meeting. They chose the tiger to be the king of the jungle, and the owl to be his minister. The owl was very proud of his new position.

    But the king and his minister had quite different natures. The tiger was rash and quick-tempered. One day, they had an argument which quickly turned into a violent quarrel.

    The angry tiger slapped the owl so hard across the face that the owl's face was pushed in and became completely flat!

    Furious, the angry bird picked up a burning log from a nearby fire and struck the tiger with it, leaving long, scorched black stripes all over the tiger's golden body.

    Since that day, owls have had flat faces, and tigers have black stripes on their bodies.

    Why Crabs Have Flat Bodies

    There once lived a crab near a river. One day, as he was looking longingly at the ripe fruit high up on a tall tree, his friend the mouse came along.

    "Would you like to eat some of those fruit?" the mouse asked.

    "I would love to," the crab sighed.

    "I'll climb up and throw some down for you," the mouse offered. He scampered up the tree and began to pluck the fruit, tossing them down to the waiting crab.

    Unfortunately, one of the falling fruits hit a nest of red ants with a loud thud.

    The ants were furious and rushed out of their nest. "Who did that? It must be that boar!" they cried, seeing a wild boar passing by. "The scoundrel! Let's teach him a lesson."

    They swarmed the poor boar, biting him all over. "Ooooh! Aaah!" the boar squealed, running blindly into the forest. In his panic, he crashed into a tree where a bat was sleeping.

    "That boar is after me!" the terrified bat shrieked, flying away frantically. Seeking shelter, the bat flew straight into the ear of a passing elephant.

    "A bat in my ear! Help! Help!" the elephant trumpeted in distress. He thrashed about wildly and ended up knocking down the small hut of an old woman.

    "Come back, you brute!" the old woman yelled, chasing the elephant with a stick. "How dare you knock my hut down."

    "I am sorry... but there's a bat in my ear," the elephant pleaded.

    The bat flew out and said, "He knocked down the tree in which I was sleeping. I was trying to get away from a boar."

    "Let's find him," the old woman declared.

    When they found the boar, the old woman scolded him. "I am homeless because of you. You frightened the bat and he frightened the elephant and he knocked down my house."

    "It's not my fault," the boar protested. "Some ants attacked me and I was trying to get away from them."

    When the old woman questioned the ants, they replied, "We had good reason to attack him. He broke our nest."

    "I didn't!" the boar snorted. "A mouse was throwing that crab some fruit and one of them hit the nest."

    Finally, the old woman turned to the crab. "Is this true?"

    "I... er... yes. One of the fruit did hit the nest," the crab admitted nervously.

    "So you are to be blamed for the destruction of my hut," the old woman said sternly. "You'll have to give me something to make up for it."

    "All right. I'll get you something from my house," the crab promised. He crept under a large stone... and stayed there, refusing to come out.

    "Come out! Come out!" the old woman shouted.

    Finally, the woman and the animals lost their patience. "Let's press the rock down on him," they decided. The elephant pushed his heavy weight onto the rock, crushing it down.

    "Oh-oh! Help!" the crab cried out, but the old woman and the animals just walked away.

    Sometime later, the crab crawled out from under the rock. But the heavy weight had crushed him, and he had become as flat as a pancake. Crabs ever since have had flat bodies.

    The Otter and the Wildcat

    An otter and a wildcat had become good friends. One day, the otter accompanied the cat to a human village.

    "I'll steal a chicken for you and one for me," the wildcat whispered. "But keep an eye out for men. Shout a warning if you see any of them coming towards me."

    The cat crept into a hut and successfully caught a fowl. But the fowl squawked so loudly that the inmates of the house awoke and began to chase the cat with sticks.

    The otter, who was watching from the bushes, was terrified. "After those people catch him, they'll come after me. I'd better get out of here," he thought, and ran away as fast as he could without warning his friend.

    The cat narrowly escaped his pursuers and eventually caught up with the otter in the forest. "You coward!" the cat hissed.

    "I... I am sorry. I am not used to this sort of thing," the otter mumbled.

    The cat ignored him, greedily ate the entire fowl himself, and threw the bare, picked-clean bones to the otter. "There, that's your share."

    The otter felt deeply hurt but didn't say anything.

    Some days later, as they were passing by a pond, the otter said, "Let's catch some fish."

    "But I can't swim," the cat replied.

    "You go into the pond, catch the fish and throw them to me," the cat ordered arrogantly.

    The otter dove into the pond, caught and ate several delicious fish himself, and then surfaced, tossing a handful of fish bones onto the bank. "There! That's your share."

    "You rascal! I'll tear you apart!" the wildcat roared. In his blind anger, the cat forgot that he did not know how to swim. He leapt into the deep water to catch the otter, thrashed about helplessly, and was drowned.

    The Bulbul and the Hornbill

    The mighty hornbill was once the king of the birds. But he was a cruel tyrant, known to kill smaller birds if they made even the slightest mistake.

    So one day, all the birds got together in secret and decided they must have a new king. Their choice fell on the gentle bulbul. "He has a regal appearance," the heron observed, "and he could not hurt anyone even if he wanted to."

    "But how do we break the news to the hornbill? He won't be pleased," the crow pointed out.

    The clever owl spoke up. "I have an idea. I'll need your help, woodpecker. Come with me."

    Sometime later, the owl approached the hornbill. "O king, we feel that you should undergo a test and prove your worth to continue ruling us. You will have to sit on a thick branch and break it. If you don't succeed, whoever does shall be deemed worthier of ruling us."

    The arrogant hornbill scoffed. "Tell me which branch I should sit on. If I cannot break the branch, who can?"

    "That's the branch," the owl pointed to a sturdy-looking limb on a tall tree.

    The hornbill flew high into the air and landed on the branch with all his massive might. But the branch did not break. He bounced and clawed, but it remained firm.

    "Remember, anyone who enters the contest must sit on an equally thick branch," the hornbill declared, slightly embarrassed.

    "The bulbul is the next contestant," the owl announced. "Is the branch over there thick enough?"

    The hornbill inspected a nearby branch. "That branch is even thicker than this. If the bulbul can break that branch, he certainly deserves to be king. Go on!"

    What the hornbill didn't know was that the thick branch the bulbul was to sit on had been secretly bored through from the inside by the woodpecker, leaving it entirely hollow.

    When the tiny bulbul landed on it, there was a loud CRACK, and the thick branch snapped in two, sending the bulbul fluttering safely into the air.

    "He... he has broken the branch," the hornbill muttered in shock. Acknowledging defeat, the disgraced hornbill flew away, and the bulbul became the beloved new king of the birds.

    The Porcupine and the Elephants

    A herd of elephants used to bathe in a particular, pristine pool deep in the jungle. One day, the leader noticed the water was murky. "Why is our pool so muddy today? Somebody has been playing in the water. Who?"

    He spotted a dragonfly hovering nearby. "Dragonfly, do you know who has dirtied our pool?"

    "No!" the dragonfly buzzed.

    "If you find the culprit, let us know," the elephant ordered.

    Sometime later, the dragonfly saw a porcupine covered in wet mud. "Did you by any chance bathe in the pool the elephants use?"

    "I did," the porcupine grunted.

    "They are annoyed because you made it so muddy," the dragonfly warned.

    "I can't help it if there's so much mud in that pool. Anyway, that pool doesn't belong to them. I'll use it whenever I want to," the prickly creature snapped.

    The dragonfly flew straight back to the elephants. "I have found the animal that muddied your pool. He is not at all repentant. He says he will use this pool again. He doesn't know who he is dealing with!"

    The leader of the elephant herd reached up with his trunk, plucked a single, thick hair from his head, and gave it to the dragonfly. "Show this to that stupid creature. Tell him he is picking up a quarrel with one whose hair is so thick."

    The dragonfly took the heavy elephant hair back to the porcupine. "The elephants say you do not know whom you are dealing with. Is each hair of his so thick?" the dragonfly asked, struggling to carry the large strand.

    The porcupine scoffed. "You call that thick? Here, take one of mine to them." He pulled out one of his sharp, solid quills.

    The dragonfly carried the porcupine's quill back to the elephants. "He has sent you a hair of his own."

    "WHAT!" the elephant leader gasped, staring at the thick, sharp, spear-like quill. "Is each hair of his so thick?"

    "He must be a giant of a creature," a smaller elephant whimpered.

    "Let's not quarrel with him, brothers. We'll find another pool tomorrow," the leader decided quickly. The terrified elephants scrambled out of the pool and ran away into the jungle, never to return.

    The Frog and the Monkey

    Long ago, there was a Raja who had a monkey and a frog as his servants. One day, the Raja commanded, "Go to the forest and bring me some fruit. And you get me some fish from the stream."

    The frog and the monkey set out to do their master's bidding. But when they reached the forest, the monkey greedily ate every fruit he picked. And when his stomach was completely full, he curled up on a tree branch and went to sleep.

    He awoke only towards evening, realizing he had nothing to show for his day. "The Raja will beat me for not bringing him fruits. Now what should I do?"

    Finally, he got off the tree, rolled himself thoroughly in the mud several times, and set off for home.

    On the way, he met the frog. The frog had worked hard all day and had caught a large bundle of fish.

    "This creature will get me into trouble," the monkey thought enviously. "Why did he have to catch so many fish, and I don't have so much as a berry to give the Raja? This won't do at all!"

    The monkey ambushed the frog, snatching the bundle.

    "Hey! What are you doing?" the frog cried.

    The monkey tossed all the fish back into the stream.

    "See what you've done! I've lost all the fish!" the frog wept.

    "It doesn't matter. Why are you so afraid of the Raja? He won't remain our master much longer, you know," the monkey boasted. "I'm going to rebel against him. And when I become the new Raja, I'll make you my minister."

    The frog was naive and agreed. "Oh! Now come, let's go home. Tell him you didn't catch any fish."

    The Raja was furious when he saw the servants returning empty-handed. "What could I do, master?" the monkey whined. "I scoured the whole jungle for fruit-bearing trees... but I could not find any. See how dirty and dishevelled I am. Does that not show that I have been working?"

    He pointed an accusing finger at the frog. "He, on the other hand, looks so clean and fresh. He must have spent the whole day playing in the water."

    "You know that's not true!" the frog protested.

    "You useless creature!" the Raja roared. He struck the frog on the head so hard that the top of his head became completely flat. "I sent you to catch fish, not to play in the water!"

    "I did catch fish. But he threw all of them into the stream!" the frog cried, pointing at the monkey.

    But the Raja wouldn't listen. "I can't stay here anymore," the frog said sadly. "There's no justice here." The frog left the palace forever and went to live in the river. And frogs, since then, have had flat heads.

    The monkey was glad to get rid of the frog. A few days later, he tried to make good on his boast and rebelled against the Raja, gathering his monkey relatives to attack the palace.

    "The traitor! I must act fast if I am to save myself," the Raja thought. He made a thick, black paste and covered his entire face with it. Then, he sat on his throne and waited for the monkeys to appear.

    When the monkey entered, he stopped in surprise. "Where did you get that paste, master? I would like to put some on my face too."

    "My servants put me in the hollow of a large tree and lit a fire around it," the Raja lied cleverly. "The smoke made my face black. It looks beautiful now."

    "Please ask your servants to do the same thing for me, and my friends," the vain monkey requested.

    "Gladly," the Raja smiled.

    The Raja's guards rounded up the monkey and all his friends, shoved them into the hollow of a large, dry tree, and set fire to it. Only one of the monkeys escaped—a female—and her face had been permanently blackened by the thick smoke. Her descendants are the black-faced monkeys we see today.

    The Frog and the Tiger

    A frog and a tiger were once good friends. The frog was a frequent visitor to the tiger's den, and whenever he came, the generous tiger always gave him fresh meat to eat.

    One day, the frog said, "I have come to your house so often. But you have never come to mine. Please come to my house tomorrow."

    "I would be delighted to come," the tiger said warmly. "But... I am a meat-eater."

    "As if there's no meat in my house!" the frog boasted. "Please do come."

    "Oh, all right," the tiger agreed.

    The frog hopped home feeling rather worried. "What have I gone and done! From where shall I get meat for him?"

    As he sat near the river, he saw a wild horse drinking water. "Aha! A horse! I'll bite off some of his flesh."

    The brave little frog leaped into the air and landed squarely on the horse's back. The horse felt the sudden prick and viciously swished its long tail. THWACK! The heavy tail struck the frog, sending him flying through the air. He hit the ground with a sickening THUMP.

    "Ouch! Both my legs must be broken," the frog groaned, dragging himself painfully back to his hole.

    The next day, the tiger arrived. "Welcome, my friend, welcome!" the frog called out, hiding his injured legs. "Please make yourself at home while I get you something to eat."

    The frog painfully hopped up to the loft of his small burrow.

    After some time, the tiger heard a loud "OOOOOOH!" from above. "What is he doing?" the tiger wondered, peeking into the loft.

    He was shocked to see the frog biting into his own mangled leg. "What are you doing, my friend? Why are you biting off flesh from your own leg?"

    "I... uh... I had no meat to offer you..." the frog stammered in shame.

    "So you wanted to give me your own flesh?" the tiger asked gently. "Do you think I would have eaten it? Why did you do such a thing?"

    The frog felt deeply ashamed of his foolish pride. When the tiger eventually left, the frog dragged himself to the riverbank and jumped into the deep water. "I could never show my face to the tiger again. I shall stay in the water for the rest of my life."

    And frogs to this day live mostly in the water.
