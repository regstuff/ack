    Drona: The Master Archer and Teacher

    Drona, the son of the great sage Bharadwaja, was named so because he was born in a 'drona', a sacred vessel. As a young boy, he proved to be exceptionally brilliant.

    One day, Bharadwaja’s friend, King Prishata of Panchala, visited the ashram with his own son, Drupada. "Drona is a clever boy," the king observed. "Let him be a companion to my son, Drupada."

    "As you wish, sir," the sage replied.

    The two boys began their studies together. Drupada often looked at his friend in awe. "Drona, how I wish I were clever like you," he said.

    "But you are stronger," Drona smiled back.

    Eventually, Bharadwaja sent Drona to the great Rishi Agnivesha to master the science of arms. Drupada followed him there. "The great Rishi for his guru? Certainly! Drupada is fortunate," King Prishata remarked.

    In the new ashram, their bond deepened. "Drona, let me wash our clothes," Drupada offered warmly. "You recite shlokas while I do it."

    "You are kind to me, Drupada. Listen then..." Drona replied, sharing his vast knowledge.

    When their studies ended and it was time to part ways, Drupada gripped Drona’s shoulders. "Drona, when I become king after my father, my palace shall be your home. I promise."

    "I shall always remember you and your kind offer," Drona replied gratefully.

    Years passed. Drupada ascended the throne of Panchala. Drona, however, remained in the austere ashram even after his father's death. Honoring his father's wish, he married Kripi, the pious sister of Kripa. When their son was born, a heavenly voice proclaimed, "The child shall be called Aswatthama!"

    Aswatthama became Drona’s greatest joy, even distracting him from his lifelong pursuit of arms. But they lived in stark poverty. One day, hearing that the legendary warrior Parashurama was giving away all his wealth, Drona rushed to him.

    "O great one, I have come for a share of the wealth," Drona panted, bowing before the fierce sage.

    "You are late," Parashurama sighed. "I have nothing left but this body of mine and my weapons to offer. Quick! Tell me! Which will you have?"

    Drona’s eyes gleamed. "I choose your weapons with the mantras for their use."

    "They are yours," Parashurama granted, making Drona supreme in the skill of arms.

    Elated, Drona hurried home. "Kripi! Aswatthama! I have returned! I have the best of Parashurama's wealth," he announced. But his joy quickly faded. He saw young Aswatthama playing with some wealthy boys.

    "What are you drinking?" Aswatthama asked the boys.

    "Milk!" they taunted.

    "Give me some," Aswatthama asked innocently.

    "The beggar's son wants milk!" the boys sneered. Cruelly, they mixed powdered rice with water and handed it to the boy. Aswatthama drank it eagerly and danced around, crying, "I too have tasted milk!"

    The men nearby laughed. "Fie upon Drona! His poor son drinks that stuff and thinks it is milk!"

    Drona was devastated and deeply ashamed. Gathering his family, he said, "I shall take Kripi and Aswatthama to Drupada. He will welcome us and he can share my new wealth. In Drupada's palace, Aswatthama will drink the richest of milk. Drupada loves me as his own brother."

    But when Drona entered the grand court of Panchala, he was met with cold stares. "Drupada, I have come," Drona smiled. "Remember your promise to me, my friend? Now you can fulfil it."

    The courtiers gasped. "No measly Brahman can be a king's friend!" a guard hissed.

    King Drupada looked down at Drona with haughty disdain. "And what is this about a promise? I recall nothing. But I can give you food and shelter for a night if that is what you seek."

    Cold with anger and humiliation, Drona pointed a trembling finger at the king. "I shall avenge this insult, dear Drupada, and also show you what friendship is!"

    Drona left Panchala and took his family to Hastinapura, where his brother-in-law, Kripa, was the royal tutor. "Dear Kripa, for certain reasons I want you to keep my prowess in the science of arms a secret," Drona requested.

    One afternoon, the young Kuru princes were playing outside when their ball tumbled into a deep, dry well. "Oh! What ill luck! The ball has fallen in," they groaned. "I can see it. But it is deep down."

    Drona happened to pass by. "Shame on your princely might and your skill in arms!" he chuckled. "I will bring your ball out with mere blades of grass." Using mystical mantras, Drona shot blades of grass into the well, linking them into a chain to pull the ball out.

    Astonished, the princes folded their hands. "We bow to you, sir. We have not seen anything like this. Who are you?"

    "Go to Bheeshma, your grandsire, and describe what you have seen," Drona instructed. "He will know."

    When Bheeshma heard the tale, he immediately recognized the master archer. "It is none but Drona. You are fortunate. Because in him you will have a teacher beyond compare."

    Bheeshma personally invited Drona to the palace. "Tell me, what brings you to our city?" he asked.

    "I come seeking obedient pupils who will avenge my insult," Drona declared.

    "You may consider it as already done," Bheeshma promised, granting him a comfortable home and entrusting the Kuru princes to him.

    "My sons, I have a certain task for you," Drona told the princes. "Promise me that you will do it when your studies are over."

    While the others remained silent, young Arjuna stepped forward. "I promise!"

    Arjuna's dedication quickly made him Drona's favorite. One night, a gust of wind blew out Arjuna's lamp while he was eating. Realizing he could eat in the dark purely out of habit, a thought struck him: I can make shooting in the dark a similar habit! Drona heard the twang of Arjuna's bow in the pitch dark and embraced the boy. "I shall train you till no bowman in the world is a match for you," Drona vowed.

    News of Drona's unmatched academy spread far and wide. One day, a tribal boy approached him. "I am Ekalavya, the Nishada prince. I want to become your pupil."

    Drona hesitated. A mere Nishada! Supposing one day he excels my royal pupils? "I am sorry," Drona refused. "I cannot accept you as my pupil."

    Undeterred, the devoted Ekalavya went into the forest, built a clay statue of Drona, worshipped it, and practiced relentlessly. Months later, the Kuru princes were hunting in that very forest. Their hound began to bark at Ekalavya. Before the dog could even close its mouth, Ekalavya fired seven arrows in rapid succession, sealing its jaws seamlessly.

    "Who are you? Who is your guru?" the stunned princes demanded.

    "I am Ekalavya, a Nishada prince. Dronacharya is my guru," the boy answered proudly.

    When Arjuna heard this, he was crushed. "How could you allow a Nishada to excel me? You promised..." he told Drona.

    "Come, let us go and see the wondrous performer," Drona said, his face unreadable.

    When Ekalavya saw the master, he fell at his feet. "Arise, my boy," Drona said coldly. "If you take me for your guru, give me my fee!"

    "O illustrious guru, what shall I give? There is nothing I would not give my guru," Ekalavya offered with pure devotion.

    "I want the thumb of your right hand!" Drona commanded.

    Without hesitation, and with a smile on his face, Ekalavya severed his right thumb and placed it at Drona's feet. His archery career was ruined, and Arjuna remained unequalled.

    Years later, the princes completed their training. Drona gathered them. "The time has come for me to exact my fee. Capture Drupada, the Panchala king, and give him to me."

    The Kaurava princes charged first, eager to show off, but Drupada's fierce counterattack sent them fleeing in terror. Then, Arjuna and the Pandavas rode out. "Bhima, lead with your mace!" Arjuna ordered. Piercing through the defenses, Arjuna shattered Drupada's bowstring, seized the king, and dragged him before Drona.

    Drona looked down at the bound king. "This was the moment I had patiently waited and worked for," Drona sneered softly. "Your kingdom now belongs to me. Do you wish to revive our friendship?"

    Drupada glared in silence.

    "Brave king, we brahmans are ever forgiving," Drona continued. "I ask for your friendship again, and in the name of friendship, I will give you half the kingdom. The other half I will retain. Now you need not hesitate to own me as a friend—for I too am a king."

    "You are a noble soul," Drupada said through gritted teeth as he was untied. "I am grateful to you and wish to be your lifelong friend..." But internally, Drupada vowed: ...in which time I shall have my revenge.

    Consumed by bitterness, Drupada sought out sages with exceptional spiritual powers. From a grand sacrificial fire, two divine children emerged: a warrior son named Dhrishtadyumna, born specifically to slay Drona, and a breathtaking daughter named Draupadi, destined to bring about the destruction of the Kauravas.

    Decades later, the great Kurukshetra war tore the kingdom apart. Drona, bound by loyalty, fought for the Kauravas, while Drupada and Dhrishtadyumna allied with the Pandavas. When Bheeshma fell on the tenth day, Duryodhana declared, "Let Dronacharya command our forces!"

    Drona proved invincible, cutting through the Pandava armies like a scythe. On the fourteenth day, Drupada challenged Drona directly. After a fierce, earth-shaking duel, Drona struck Drupada down, killing him.

    Dhrishtadyumna was paralyzed with grief. "Take heart, brave one," Yudhishthira consoled him. "Your father's death shall be avenged. You are born to slay Drona."

    But Drona could not be defeated in combat. Lord Krishna devised a ruthless plan. "Drona is invincible. We will have to resort to subtle means to defeat him. Tell him that Aswatthama is dead... Dronacharya will be disabled by grief."

    "No! No! That would be a lie," Arjuna protested.

    Bhima, the strongest of the brothers, had an idea. He sought out a massive war elephant named Aswatthama and crushed its skull with his mace. Running to the front lines, Bhima roared, "Aswatthama has been killed!"

    Drona froze, his bow dropping slightly. "No! It can't be true. I know how skilful my son is." He turned his chariot toward Yudhishthira, knowing the eldest Pandava never told a lie. "Is this true, Yudhishthira?"

    Yudhishthira bowed his head. "Yes. Aswatthama the elephant is dead," he replied, though the words 'the elephant' were swallowed by the din of the battlefield.

    Drona heard only that his beloved son was gone. His spirit broke. "Aswatthama! Aswatthama!" he wept, laying down his weapons in the middle of the carnage. "For your sake I took up arms to earn a living. Now that you are dead I have no use for them." Drona closed his eyes, sat cross-legged on his chariot, and entered deep samadhi, detaching his soul from his body.

    Seeing his moment, Dhrishtadyumna leaped onto the chariot and swung his sword, severing the master archer's head. But the vengeful strike meant little; unknown to the young prince, the soul of the great Dronacharya had already departed on its upward journey to the heavens.
