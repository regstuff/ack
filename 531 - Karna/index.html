    Karna: The Tragic Hero of the Mahabharata

    The Mahabharata is a gallery of heroes, and Karna is perhaps the most heroic, yet most tragic, of them all. Fate denied him his rightful dues, but he fought and achieved all that a man could aspire to have.

    His story began before the great war, when King Kuntibhoja adopted his young niece, Pritha, and renamed her Kunti.

    One day, the fiery and easily-angered sage Durvasa visited the king's palace. "The venerable sage will be our guest. Look after him well," the king instructed Kunti. For a full year, Kunti served the sage carefully, patiently, and with absolute devotion.

    Durvasa was deeply pleased. "I will give you a divine mantra," the sage told her. "It will enable you to call upon any god to come and bless you with a son who is in every way his equal."

    As soon as Durvasa left, the young and curious Kunti looked up at the sun. "I wonder if it really works. Oh! Lord Surya smiles at me. I will call him down," she thought, reciting the mantra.

    Instantly, Surya, the Sun God, appeared in his blinding glory.

    Terrified, Kunti stammered, "But I am not married. I was only curious to see if you would come. Please, please go back."

    "You shall have the son you desire," Surya gently replied. "I cannot go, as the spell of the mantra holds me."

    Thus, Karna was born. From his divine father, the radiant infant inherited an impenetrable golden armor (kavacha) and brilliant earrings (kundalas) attached to his skin.

    But Kunti, an unwed princess, was terrified of the world's judgment. Weeping bitterly, she placed the baby in a wicker basket and set it afloat on the river. "Ah! Dear son, forgive me," she cried. "If I ever see you again, will I have the strength to acknowledge you then?"

    Further down the river, a childless charioteer named Adhiratha spotted the basket. "Ah! What a beautiful child, abandoned by some heartless mother, no doubt." He took the baby home to his overjoyed wife, Radha.

    "Let us call him Vasushena," Radha beamed, cradling the golden child.

    "He shall be Radheya, dear wife," Adhiratha said proudly. "Radheya, the son of Radha."

    Karna’s early childhood was a happy one. Meanwhile, Kunti married King Pandu of Hastinapura. Because of a curse, Pandu could not father children, so he retreated to the Himalayas. Using the mantra Durvasa had given her, Kunti and Pandu's second wife, Madri, called upon the gods to bless them with five sons—the Pandava princes. When Pandu died, Kunti brought the five fatherless princes to Hastinapura, where the elders Bheeshma and Vidura welcomed them to grow up alongside their cousins, the Kauravas.

    From the beginning, a bitter rivalry sprang up. The Kauravas grew resentful of the skilled Pandavas. "Bheema torments me on the playground and haunts my dreams. Unfair!" complained Duryodhana, the eldest Kaurava.

    The great guru Dronacharya taught all the princes the arts of war. Karna, drawn to the martial arts, also became his pupil. But he noticed a bitter truth. "He is partial to Arjuna," Karna thought, watching Drona dote on the young Pandava. "He will never let me surpass him."

    Hoping to equal his rival, Karna approached his teacher. "Guru, please teach me the Brahmastra."

    Drona scoffed. "The Brahmastra is only meant for Brahmanas, and high-born Kshatriyas. I cannot teach it to you—a charioteer's son."

    Stung by the humiliation, Karna made a bold decision. He traveled to the ashram of the legendary warrior Parashurama. "Parashurama, they say, hates Kshatriyas. I will go to him in the guise of a Brahman. He is sure to accept me," Karna plotted.

    "I am a Brahman lad. Please accept me as your pupil," Karna bowed before the sage.

    "Come, my son. I am willing to teach any worthy youth, who is not a Kshatriya," Parashurama welcomed him.

    Under Parashurama, Karna mastered the highest weapons. But one afternoon, as the sage was resting his head on Karna's lap, a large, vicious insect burrowed into Karna's thigh. Blood flowed, and the pain was excruciating. Ah! How the little insect stings, Karna winced internally. But I will not disturb your rest, beloved master. Sleep on.

    When Parashurama woke and saw the pool of blood, his eyes narrowed in fury. "You have deceived me. Only a Kshatriya can bear such pain to protect another. Tell me the truth!"

    "I am the son of Adhiratha, the charioteer, a sutaputra. Forgive me, sir," Karna confessed.

    "For deceiving your guru," the enraged sage cursed, "your knowledge will fail you when you need it most!"

    Dejected, Karna wandered blindly into a nearby forest. Startled by rustling bushes, he shot an arrow on instinct, only to realize he had accidentally slain a Brahman's cow.

    "Sinner, you have killed my poor innocent cow!" the Brahman screamed, adding a second, fatal curse to the young warrior's burden: "You too will be killed when you are helpless to defend yourself!"

    Karna returned home to his mother, Radha. "Mother, I hear that an open tournament is to be held at Hastinapura... A valiant warrior will find his place among the valiant," he told her, determined to prove himself.

    At the tournament, Arjuna dazzled the crowd with his archery. But suddenly, the gates opened, and Karna strode in, his golden armor gleaming. To the astonishment of everyone, he effortlessly matched and replicated every single feat Arjuna had performed.

    "Bravo! Well done!" the crowd roared.

    Duryodhana, thrilled to see someone challenge the arrogant Pandavas, rushed forward. "Welcome, O valiant one! It is our good fortune that you are here today. What shall be your reward?"

    "Good king, all I ask is your love and the opportunity to meet Drona's favourite pupil Arjuna in single combat!" Karna declared.

    But before the duel could begin, Kripa, a royal teacher, stepped forward. "Who are you? Reveal your parentage. Our prince may fight only with his equal in birth and status."

    Karna lowered his head in shame, while high up in the stands, Queen Kunti recognized the golden armor and fainted in grief.

    "I crown Karna prince of Anga!" Duryodhana declared loudly, throwing a crown onto Karna's head to give him royal status.

    Just then, the old charioteer Adhiratha hobbled onto the stage. "My son! My blessings!" he wept, embracing Karna.

    Bhima roared with laughter. "Go sutaputra! Go drive your chariot! You are not worthy of death at Arjuna's hands."

    Duryodhana, seething with anger at the insult, took Karna by the arm. "Come Karna! It takes a hero to recognise a hero!" he said, leading him away. From that moment, a bond of absolute loyalty was forged. "Duryodhana, you have not befriended me in vain," Karna vowed silently. "I will kill Arjuna in single combat one day! I will!"

    As the years passed, the rivalry erupted into a war for the throne of Hastinapura. In the heavens, Lord Indra watched nervously. If Karna is deprived of his kavacha and kundalas, my son Arjuna need fear no mortal, Indra plotted.

    Surya, the Sun God, appeared in Karna's dream to warn him. "Lord Indra will try to take advantage of your generosity. Do not part with your kavacha and kundalas."

    But the next morning, Indra approached Karna disguised as a poor Brahman. "I beg for alms. Karna, it is said, refuses nothing to those who ask. I want your kavacha and kundalas."

    Despite his father's warning, Karna smiled and sliced the golden armor from his flesh. "My lord, I know who you are and why you are here," Karna said, handing over his invincibility. "Yet, I give to you my very life. Never let it be said that Karna refused anyone anything."

    Touched by this absolute selflessness, Indra offered a boon. "In return I grant you my master weapon Shakti, but you may use it only once."

    As war loomed, Lord Krishna went to Karna to try to avert the disaster by revealing the truth of his birth. "Come with me. I will make you a great emperor. You will have five powerful brothers and a mother who longs for you."

    Karna shook his head. "Duryodhana's love is what I value most in my life. So if you really have my interest at heart, promise me that you will keep the secret of my birth till I die."

    Desperate, Kunti herself visited Karna as he finished his prayers. Weeping, she confessed her sin and pleaded with him not to fight his brothers.

    "Mother, what you ask me to do is against my dharma," Karna replied gently but firmly. "I will fight against your sons, my brothers, but I will not kill any except Arjuna. You shall still have five sons alive, and I, my honour."

    The horrific battle of Kurukshetra began. On the tenth day, the venerable commander Bheeshma fell on a bed of arrows. Karna, who had been kept out of the battle by Bheeshma's refusal to fight alongside a sutaputra, rushed to the dying patriarch.

    "My lord! I have never been fortunate enough to be liked by you. Yet I have come to pay my respects..." Karna bowed.

    "Dear Karna! I knew who you were," Bheeshma smiled weakly. "Your firm loyalty to Duryodhana is your chosen dharma. May you lead his forces to victory! Go fight your fated enemies."

    Karna entered the fray. On the fourteenth day, he cornered Bhima but lowered his bow. Forgive me, Duryodhana. I have Bhima at my mercy but I cannot kill him. I have promised my mother.

    That night, Bhima's monstrous half-demon son, Ghatotkacha, wreaked havoc on the Kaurava army. "Kill that fellow immediately, Karna, or else our whole army will be vanquished," Duryodhana pleaded.

    With no other choice to save his friend's army, Karna unleashed the infallible Shakti weapon, killing the demon. Alas! I have killed Ghatotkacha, but I have lost the mightiest weapon Shakti which I had meant to use on Arjuna.

    On the sixteenth day, Drona fell, and Karna was made commander. The final confrontation drew near.

    The next day, Karna faced Arjuna. As their arrows collided with earth-shattering force, the wheel of Karna's chariot sank deep into the muddy ground.

    "My memory fails me. I cannot recall the mantras of the great Parashurama!" Karna gasped as Parashurama's curse took effect. He jumped down to drag the heavy wheel out of the mud.

    Seeing Karna unarmed and struggling, Arjuna hesitated. But Lord Krishna, knowing Karna was too powerful to defeat in a fair fight, commanded Arjuna to strike.

    As Karna strained against the wheel, Arjuna drew his bow and shot the fatal arrow, severing the head of the great hero. The curse of the Brahman had been fulfilled—Karna died helpless to defend himself.

    After the war, the victorious Pandavas gathered to perform the death ceremonies for their fallen kin.

    "Wait! There is one more left," Kunti said through her tears.

    "But why should I do it for Karna?" Yudhishthira asked. "Karna, our arch enemy, Karna the charioteer's son!"

    Kunti broke down and told them the tragic tale of her firstborn.

    The sun set slowly over the battlefield as the truth settled heavily upon the brothers. Each of the Pandavas remembered his own encounters with the fierce, noble warrior who had spared their lives. Thus Karna, who had longed for love, recognition, and a good name while he lived, secured them—like many great men before him—only in death.
