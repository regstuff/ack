    Ashoka

    Ashoka was the son of King Bindusara, who ruled India from his capital of Pataliputra in the third century B.C. Ashoka excelled his brothers in the art of war. Therefore, when a rebellion broke out in the distant province of Taxila, he was sent to suppress it.

    Weeks later, a messenger brought happy news to the capital. "Your majesty! We have happy news here. Ashoka has successfully put down the rebels."

    "He shall rule there as the Viceroy," King Bindusara decided. "He will keep peace there."

    However, Bindusara had a hundred sons besides Ashoka, and they were not happy to hear the news. "Ashoka has always been proud," they muttered among themselves. "The success will go to his head. It will make him more arrogant." They were unhappier still when the king recalled Ashoka from Taxila. "Ashoka back in the capital. I don't like it," one brother complained. "Especially with our old father depending on him," agreed another.

    King Bindusara, observing the tension, confided in his minister. "I am worried about the future of my kingdom. Ashoka is ruthless. He may fight with his brothers for the throne."

    "You have a hundred and one sons, your majesty," the minister replied reassuringly. "You have no cause for worry."

    Just then, a messenger arrived with urgent news. "A rebellion—this time in Ujjaini."

    "Send for Ashoka," the king ordered.

    When Ashoka arrived, Bindusara told him, "The province of Ujjaini needs an able Viceroy."

    "I would love to go South," Ashoka agreed eagerly.

    "But the Viceroy designate... will have to crush the rebels there. You alone can do it."

    "Yes, father," Ashoka accepted the mission.

    Soon afterwards, Ashoka marched towards Ujjaini. A greater part of the journey was on horseback. As they rode, Ashoka marveled at the landscape. "What a lovely place!"

    "This is Vidisha-nagar, sir," a soldier informed him.

    A little later, they decided to rest. "Let's camp here for a while. Rest the horses," Ashoka commanded. While resting, Ashoka was charmed by the beauty of the leader of a group of passing women.

    "Excuse me, lady. To which temple are you going?" he asked her.

    "We are on our way to the Chaitya. Not to a temple," she replied.

    "Then you are Buddhists."

    "Yes!"

    "Come on, Vidisha. It is getting late," her companion called out.

    "Vidisha! A name as lovely as this place," Ashoka thought to himself. "And such charming eyes! Find out who her parents are. I want to meet them."

    Ashoka's messenger soon returned. "Sir! She is the daughter of a reputed Shreshthi's family."

    "Tell the merchant I want to meet him," Ashoka said.

    Later, Ashoka met the merchant. Hesitating slightly—a rare sight for the fierce prince—Ashoka finally spoke. "I... yes. I want the hand of your daughter in marriage."

    "I am honoured, sir," the merchant replied.

    After a quick wedding, Ashoka proceeded towards Ujjaini along with Vidisha. Being a great warrior, in no time he routed the rebels, forcing them to lay down their arms. The people of the city gave Ashoka a grand welcome.

    But later, in the palace gardens, Vidisha looked sad. "This is an occasion for rejoicing, Vidisha. But you look sad," Ashoka remarked.

    "I dislike killing... even in battles... especially by you," she replied.

    "That's silly. You say it, because you are a Buddhist. I am not. I am a Kshatriya king. My duty enjoins killing if need be."

    "What about Bimbisara? He too was a king. He found peace in the teachings of Buddha," Vidisha argued softly.

    The differences between Ashoka and Vidisha persisted. Months later, Vidisha gave birth to a son.

    "A son! I am very happy," Ashoka declared. "I will name him Mahindra. He will conquer the entire world."

    "I wish he conquers the hearts of men as Prince Siddhartha did," Vidisha said quietly.

    Two years later, Vidisha gave birth to a daughter. "Lord! This time, let me choose the name," she asked.

    "As you wish."

    "I'll name her Sanghamitra."

    "Oh, no! That's a Buddhist name," Ashoka frowned, but let it be.

    One day, a messenger came from Pataliputra. "His majesty has not been keeping good health. Please return to Pataliputra immediately."

    Ashoka had hurried consultations with Vidisha. "I have received a message from the Chief Minister. My father is ill. I have to rush to Pataliputra... and you aren't yet fit to accompany me. Will you stay at Vidisha-nagar with your parents? I'll send for you later."

    "As you wish, my Lord!"

    Together they traveled up to Vidisha-nagar. As Ashoka prepared to leave, he told her, "Please take care of your health. And don't preach non-violence to my Mahindra. I want him to be a King of Kings. Before I leave, is there anything you want me to do?"

    "Yes. But give me your word that you will."

    "I will. What is it?"

    "I want you to shun violence of all kinds," she pleaded.

    At Pataliputra, Ashoka was received by the Chief Minister. "Prince! Your father is no more. And your brothers are fighting amongst themselves for the throne."

    "The throne! It is mine! I have earned the right to it," Ashoka declared fiercely. In his ambition, Ashoka forgot all about the promise he had made to Vidisha.

    Ashoka was ruthless in killing all those who challenged his right to the throne. Sumanadevi, the wife of one of his brothers, was expecting a baby at the time. Fearing for the life of her unborn child, she sought help from a loyal princess, who showed her a secret door to escape into the woods.

    Escaping from the palace, Sumanadevi wandered in the forest. An old man who was passing by took pity on her. "If you come with me to my village, I will arrange for your stay. Don't worry, daughter! My home is close by. I'll fetch my wife. You rest under this Nigrodha tree."

    "No! I cannot walk any farther," Sumanadevi wept. It was under that very Nigrodha tree that a boy was born to Sumanadevi. They called him Nigrodha Kumar. Nigrodha Kumar grew up in that village and became a monk at a very tender age.

    Meanwhile, Ashoka had become a powerful king. At his court, he asked his minister, "Is my kingdom peaceful? Are the borders quiet?"

    "Yes, your majesty. Even Taxila and Ujjaini are quiet."

    "Still we should not rest till Kalinga is conquered," Ashoka decided.

    "Why, your majesty?"

    "It is independent today and may raise its head any moment. War it will have to be. Commander! Prepare for the battle."

    Ashoka's army invaded Kalinga. The soldiers of Kalinga fought bravely, but at last, they were defeated. "We have won. Kalinga is ours. I shall visit Kalinga," Ashoka announced.

    But as he proceeded across the battlefield, he saw horrible sights. "Heaps of dead men! We have killed many," he noted grimly.

    "Over 1,00,000 men to be precise, your majesty," a soldier replied.

    As Ashoka moved farther, survivors cried out, "Mercy! We beg for mercy!"

    "We have captured 1,50,000 men, your majesty," another commander proudly stated.

    Looking around the desolate, burning land, Ashoka realized the scale of the devastation. "It looks desolate here. Lakhs have died, your majesty." Seeing the immense sufferings of the people, a wave of profound sorrow washed over him. "Oh! No! Let's return."

    Back in the palace, Ashoka was tormented. "Your majesty! Aren't you well?" a minister asked.

    "I have lost my peace of mind," Ashoka confessed.

    To add to his grief, a messenger came from Vidisha-nagar with a letter from Vidisha Devi.

    "Where is Vidisha?" he asked eagerly.

    "She plans to join the Sangha of Buddha," the messenger replied softly.

    Ashoka read the letter: "Arya putra, you have broken the promise. I am sending the children to you. Please look after them properly. Yours."

    Ashoka drew some comfort from the presence of his children, but he continued to brood over the senseless killings in Kalinga.

    One day, from his palace window, he saw a young monk passing along the road. "Who can this be? Why do I feel drawn towards him? Go! Quick! Request that monk to come here."

    When the monk arrived, Ashoka asked, "Venerable one, who are you?"

    "Don't you see, who I am? A Buddhist monk."

    "May I know your name, please?"

    "My mother called me Nigrodha Kumar."

    "Nigrodha Kumar! I know, that is the name of my brother's son."

    "Yes. I am your nephew. I can guess what is going on in your mind."

    "You hate me. Don't you?" Ashoka asked heavily. "Because you killed my father?"

    "No. I love all," the young monk replied calmly. "Hatred is destructive. It should be conquered by love."

    "Is not that reason enough to hate another? Pray, where did you learn this? At such a tender age?"

    "From the teachings of Lord Buddha. They bring peace to troubled minds."

    "My mind has not known peace," Ashoka admitted, breaking down.

    "Then seek refuge in Buddha."

    "Will you accept me as your disciple?"

    "Yes. Repeat after me... I take refuge in the Buddha."

    "I take refuge in the Buddha. I take refuge in the Sangha. I take refuge in the Dharma," Ashoka repeated, surrendering himself to the path of peace.

    A transformation came about in Ashoka. All his attention now was on bringing peace and happiness to the people. For the comfort of travelers, even in the woods, he built rest houses and dispensaries. He erected pillars and carved instructions on rocks that read, "Priyadarshi, the beloved king of god says this..."

    Years passed. One day, Ashoka said, "I have given away all things dear to me. Except..."

    "Except what, Mahindra?" a minister asked.

    "Your children," Ashoka finished softly.

    Mahindra stepped forward. "Your son wishes to join the Sangha."

    "Must you, Mahindra? It is with a heavy heart - but I give you permission."

    Sanghamitra also approached him. "Father, allow me to join the nunnery."

    "Oh! Sanghamitra!!" Ashoka cried out, heartbroken. But he could not deny her. His wife, who had raised them, also joined the nunnery. "My husband and son have renounced the world. I take no pleasure in life. You may join the nunnery."

    After a few years, Mahindra came to him. "Father, I would like to go to Lanka."

    "Go, my son. Go with my blessings."

    Two years later, Sanghamitra also came to bid him goodbye. "A message has been received from Lanka. They need nuns at the mission."

    Ashoka wept. "Sanghamitra, I have grown old. I can bear grief no more."

    "Be brave. You forget Sanghamitra is a member of the Sangha."

    "I understand. I wish you success. Go, my child. Thank you, father."

    Watching them leave, Ashoka thought of his beloved wife. "Oh! Vidisha, your children have left me just as you did for the sake of Dharma."

    A senior monk approached him gently. "Do not grieve. By sacrificing your dear ones you have proved that Dharma is dear to you. You are indeed, Dharmashoka."

    Thus Ashoka became Dharmashoka. He ruled for many years and looked after the welfare of the subjects of his vast empire as a father would look after his children. The five principles of co-existence, which he propagated, are still respected the world over. The Ashoka pillar at Sarnath reminds us even till this day of his greatness, and the 'Dharma Chakra' on the Ashoka pillar adorns the national flag of India.
