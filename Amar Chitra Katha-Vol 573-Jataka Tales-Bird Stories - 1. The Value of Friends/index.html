    The Value of Friends

    Long ago, on the peaceful shore of a large lake, there lived a solitary hawk.

    One day, a she-hawk came to live on the opposite shore. When the hawk saw her, he immediately flew over to her. "Will you be my wife?" he asked hopefully. "Together we could raise a fine family."

    "All right," she smiled. "But tell me, do you have any friends?"

    The hawk shook his head. "No. I don't need friends."

    "Then you must make some friends," the she-hawk insisted gently. "In times of need, it is friends who help."

    "But I'll do as you say," the hawk agreed. "Who shall we start with?"

    "Our neighbours, of course! Go and call on the lion, the osprey, and the tortoise!"

    The hawk agreed and flew to the tiny island in the middle of the lake where the tortoise dwelt. "O tortoise, accept me as your friend," the hawk requested.

    "With pleasure!" the tortoise smiled.

    He then flew to the osprey. "Any time you need me, send for me," the osprey promised.

    Finally, the hawk called on the lion in his den. "Now that you are my friend, no one will harm you," the mighty lion assured him.

    The hawk then returned to the she-hawk. "I have three friends now."

    "Ah! Now we can decide where to make our home," she smiled.

    "What about the Kadamba tree on the island where the tortoise lives? That's a good idea."

    So the two flew over to the island and built a sturdy nest in the branches of the Kadamba tree. Soon, two little fledglings were born to them. "This is the safest place we could find—far from the haunts of men," the proud parents thought.

    But the place was not as safe as the hawks imagined. One day, two hunters arrived at the lake. "It's been a bad day. We've caught nothing, not even a rabbit! And it's almost evening!"

    "What shall we do?" one hunter sighed.

    "Let's swim across to that island, and spend the night there," the other suggested. "At dawn we'll try our luck again. We might yet catch a fish or a young tortoise, perhaps."

    When they reached the island, night fell, and the thick swarms of mosquitoes became unbearable. "These mosquitoes are impossible! Let's light a fire. That should drive them away," the hunters decided.

    Up in the Kadamba tree, the smoke woke the fledglings, who began to cry. CHEEP CHEEP!

    "Did you hear that?" a hunter gasped, looking up. "I did! Fledglings! On this tree! They are planning to eat our children!"

    The she-hawk panicked. "Friend tortoise is asleep. Quick, my dear, fly to the osprey and seek his help."

    The hawk immediately flew to the osprey. "What brings you here at this hour?" the osprey asked in surprise.

    When the hawk told him the terrifying story, the osprey took flight at once. "Have they climbed the tree yet?"

    "Not yet. They're busy lighting a fire," the hawk explained.

    "Then fly back to your wife and comfort her. I'll take care of the hunters," the brave osprey promised.

    Under the cover of night, the osprey flew to the Kadamba tree and waited. "There! The fire is blazing merrily. Let's get the birds," the hunters cheered.

    Suddenly, the osprey dived straight into the lake, came out dripping wet, flew over the fire, and violently shook himself. The spray of water hissed and completely put out the fire!

    "What's that? Is it the fire? Perhaps the wood was wet!" the hunters groaned, starting all over again.

    But as soon as the second fire was blazing, the osprey dived into the lake and put out the fire once again. "Come down," the hunters sighed in frustration. "It's no use catching the fledglings till the fire is ablaze again."

    "Ah! My little ones, you are safe!" the she-hawk wept in relief. "Now do you see the value of having friends?"

    This went on till midnight. The she-hawk felt incredibly sorry for the exhausted osprey. "He'll lose his life trying to save our young ones. Go to friend tortoise and see if he can help."

    When the hawk flew to the tortoise and told him the whole story, the tortoise nodded. "Don't worry. I'll come there as quickly as I can."

    As the massive tortoise emerged from the water, the hunters spotted him. "Hey, look! A huge tortoise! Let's forget the birds and catch him. We'll have enough food for days."

    The hunters quickly tore their waistbands into thick strips of cloth and tightly bound the tortoise to their own bodies. "Now, pull away!"

    "I will!" the hunters grunted, digging their heels into the mud.

    But the tortoise was immensely strong. Suddenly, he pulled forward, dragging both hunters screaming straight into the deep water! Coughing and spluttering, the hunters desperately loosened the strips of cloth to save themselves from drowning.

    "The fellow is dangerous!" they gasped, scrambling ashore. "Let's leave him here and make another fire for our fledglings."

    They began to frantically collect twigs. Up in the tree, the she-hawk panicked again. "They're back! Go to the lion and seek his help."

    The hawk flew to the lion's den. A few minutes later, the fire was finally ready. "Ah! The fire is ready! Let's go and catch the..."

    Suddenly, a terrifying roar echoed through the dark island. GR-R-R!

    "EE-A-AH! A LION!" the hunters screamed in absolute terror. They abandoned everything, ran for their lives, and jumped straight into the freezing lake, swimming away as fast as they could.

    The hawks hugged their babies close. Because they had valued friendship, their little family was finally safe.
