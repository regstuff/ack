    Jagannatha of Puri

    Long long ago, King Indradyumna of the Sun Dynasty ruled over Utkal (ancient Orissa). He had conquered many lands and won great fame, but still he was not happy.

    "I have everything a king could wish for! But I yearn to do what no man has done. Something truly wonderful, truly great! Something which will make people remember me for ever," he pondered. "Yes, I know! I will build a temple—a monument to God. It shall be the most beautiful temple on earth! But my beautiful temple must have an equally beautiful image. Where will I get such an image?"

    His question was answered that night in a dream. "O King, behold this hill! It is named Nilachala. On this hill is a cave. Inside the cave you will find the most beautiful image ever seen of Nilamadhav—the blue-coloured Krishna," a voice proclaimed.

    "I've found him! Nilamadhav—the God who is blue in colour! He shall be the one to grace my temple," the king rejoiced upon waking. "But... where is Nilachala Hill? Where do I look for it?"

    The very next day he spoke to his trusted men about the dream. "Find the hill and the image and report to me. I shall then go and bring Nilamadhav to Utkal myself."

    The officers set out in different directions. Among them was a young man named Vidyapati. "Something tells me that I should ride into the jungles... that I am bound to find the hill there."

    He met people, but they had never heard of Nilachala. Undiscouraged, he searched deep into the jungle, eventually losing his way. Faint with hunger and thirst, he staggered up to a tree and collapsed.

    He was found by a cowherd and a beautiful maiden, Lalita, daughter of the Savara chieftain, Vishvavasu. "Do not be afraid, beautiful maiden. I am Vidyapati from Utkal. I lost my way in the jungle," he introduced himself.

    "Come with me, I'll take you to my father," Lalita offered. Vidyapati stayed with the Savaras, and soon, he and Lalita fell in love and were married.

    However, Vidyapati noticed that Vishvavasu left every dawn and returned at dusk. He asked Lalita about it. "Sssh! Don't speak so loudly. It's a closely-guarded secret. My father goes deep into the jungle to a cave on a hill. Yes, the cave of the great Lord Nilamadhav, on Nilachala Hill."

    "Nilachala! Nilamadhav!" Vidyapati realized. He begged Lalita to ask her father to take him there. Vishvavasu reluctantly agreed, but insisted Vidyapati be blindfolded.

    That night, Vidyapati secretly took a handful of mustard seeds from the kitchen. The next day, as he was led blindfolded, he dropped the seeds along the path. "Pour rain, pour hard. Water my mustard seeds so they will sprout and flower!" he prayed.

    When they reached the cave and the blindfold was removed, Vidyapati saw the magnificent image. "Beautiful! He is beautiful..."

    A few days later, after the rains, Vidyapati followed the trail of yellow mustard flowers back to the cave to confirm the route. Then, he took leave of Lalita and Vishvavasu and returned to King Indradyumna.

    "My lord, I have found Nilachala and Nilamadhav," he announced joyfully. The King, his Queen Gundicha Devi, and his army set out immediately.

    But as they approached, a furious sandstorm arose, turning the landscape into a desert. "Where is the hill? My cave? My Nilamadhav? The trail of mustard plants ends abruptly here. And beyond them nothing but sand!" Vidyapati cried in despair. Nilamadhav had hidden himself from the eyes of men.

    Vidyapati ordered his soldiers to drag Vishvavasu out. "It's my father-in-law, Vishvavasu! Let me go, you knaves!" the chief shouted. Vidyapati accused him of hiding the image.

    But a divine voice spoke: "O Indradyumna, you still keep my beloved devotee Vishvavasu in chains! Free him at once! Only if the two of you together lift me up shall I consent to be moved."

    The King freed Vishvavasu, embracing him. Later, Indradyumna had another dream. "O King, your tears have moved me. But I cannot appear again as Nilamadhav, in which form I am lost to the world for ever. Instead, I shall appear in the form of a Daaru (log) floating on the sea."

    The next morning, they found the Daaru on the seashore. It bore the Shankha, Chakra, Gada, and Padma—the symbols of Vishnu. They harnessed elephants and men to pull it, but it wouldn't budge until Indradyumna and Vishvavasu, the two rival devotees brought together in love, lifted it together.

    The Daaru was brought ashore, but no craftsman could carve it. Their fine axe-blades broke upon striking the wood.

    Then, an old man walked in. "I am willing to carve the Daaru, but... I will sculpt it as I like. Besides, I must be left undisturbed for fifteen days in a sealed room. On no account should you break open the door." It was the divine architect Vishvakarma in disguise.

    The King agreed. But after ten days, Queen Gundicha became restless. "My lord, why is there no sound from within? Could something have happened to the old man? Shouldn't we open the door and see?"

    Despite the King's reluctance, she insisted, and the door was broken open. "The old man has vanished! Oh, no! What have I done! We are left with three incomplete images," the King lamented.

    Just then, Sage Narada appeared. "O Indradyumna, I come from Vaikuntha. I've been sent to tell you that you should not be disappointed with these images. It is Lord Vishnu's will that the Daaru should be carved thus. I shall start work on the temple to enshrine these wooden images."

    The magnificent temple was built by the seashore, with a huge enclosure for cows to be given to the poor. The cows' hooves created a crater which filled with underground springs, becoming the Indradyumna Tank.

    "Magnificent! Only Lord Brahma is worthy of consecrating it. Let us both go and invite him to earth," the King suggested to Narada.

    They went to Brahmaloka, but Lord Brahma was deep in meditation. They had to wait for hours. "We have been here a whole day! How much longer will we have to wait?" the King asked.

    When Brahma opened his eyes, he agreed to come. But on earth, many summers had come and gone. One day of Brahma was equal to thousands of years on earth! A different king, Gala Madhav, was now ruling over Utkal.

    Gala Madhav had discovered the temple buried under the sand and installed his own deity in it. When Indradyumna and Narada descended, they were shocked by the changes.

    They found the temple. "This man is the king who originally built this temple. He reigned here a long, long ago. You must give back his temple to him," Narada told Gala Madhav.

    "Are you mad too? A king from long ago? Then how is he still alive today? Where is the proof?" Gala Madhav demanded.

    "If you are willing to believe an old tortoise then I will give you proof!" Narada said. A huge tortoise rose from the Indradyumna Tank. "My name is Akupar. I remember you, Indradyumna. I have lived for centuries in this tank... Yes, it was you who built this temple."

    Lord Brahma appeared and Gala Madhav sought forgiveness. The three wooden images were recovered from where Vishvakarma left them, placed on the Mahavedi, and Brahma performed the Pranapratishtha pooja.

    "My lord, let no one claim this temple as his after I die. Henceforth, this temple shall belong to all mankind," Indradyumna wished.

    "Noble King, your fame will live on! As you have given over this temple to the people of the world, it shall be known as the shrine of 'JAGANNATHA'—Lord of the Universe," Brahma proclaimed.

    Indradyumna is remembered in Orissa to this day, as are Gundicha Devi, Vidyapati, Lalita, and Vishvavasu, especially during the magnificent car festival of Jagannatha—the Ratha Yatra.
